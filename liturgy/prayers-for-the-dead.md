# Prayers for the Dying and the Dead

A comprehensive guide to the Catholic rites, prayers, and devotional practices surrounding death — from the final moments of life through the periodic commemorations that follow. The Church accompanies the dying and the deceased with sacraments, liturgical rites, and prayers of intercession, all grounded in the hope of eternal life through the Resurrection of Christ.

> "Eternal rest grant unto them, O Lord, and let perpetual light shine upon them. May their souls, and the souls of all the faithful departed, through the mercy of God, rest in peace. Amen."

---

## 1. When Death Is Near: The Sacraments for the Dying

When a Catholic is in danger of death, the Church provides a sequence of sacraments and rites to prepare the soul for its passage to eternal life. The faithful should **contact a priest as soon as possible** so that the dying person may receive these graces.

### 1.1 The Sacrament of Penance (Confession)

The first step is reconciliation with God through the Sacrament of Penance. If the dying person is able, they should confess their sins and receive absolution. Even if full confession is not possible, the priest will provide what the situation allows ([CCC 1420–1421](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/0583c069-d4bf-42dd-97de-c19f0b80150f/ref/1420)).

- **If the person is unconscious or unable to confess**, the priest may grant **conditional absolution** based on the person's prior act of contrition.
- A **general absolution** may be given in cases of extreme necessity when individual confession is impossible.

### 1.2 Confirmation (If Not Previously Received)

If the dying person has not yet received the Sacrament of Confirmation, the priest may confer it as part of the continuous rite of pastoral care for the sick. This completes the sacraments of initiation and strengthens the soul for the final journey ([CCC 1285–1286](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/0583c069-d4bf-42dd-97de-c19f0b80150f/ref/1285)).

### 1.3 Anointing of the Sick

The Sacrament of Anointing of the Sick (formerly called **Extreme Unction**) is the principal sacrament for those in danger of death. It provides:

1. **Union with the Passion of Christ** for the good of the sick person and the whole Church ([CCC 1532](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/0583c069-d4bf-42dd-97de-c19f0b80150f/ref/1532))
2. **Strengthening, peace, and courage** to endure the trials of illness or old age
3. **Forgiveness of sins**, if the person was unable to receive sacramental confession
4. **Restoration of health**, if conducive to the salvation of the soul
5. **Preparation for passing over to eternal life** (_transitus ad Patrem_)

**The Rite of Anointing:**

- The priest **lays hands on the head** of the sick person
- He **anoints the forehead and hands** with the Oil of the Sick, saying:

> **"Through this holy anointing may the Lord in his love and mercy help you with the grace of the Holy Spirit. May the Lord who frees you from sin save you and raise you up."** ([CCC 1499](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/0583c069-d4bf-42dd-97de-c19f0b80150f/ref/1499))

- The rite concludes with the **Lord's Prayer** and a prayer of commendation.

**Eastern Catholic Practice:** In the Byzantine tradition, the anointing extends to the forehead, eyes, nostrils, lips, ears, breast, hands, and feet, with an epiclesis invoking the Holy Spirit upon the oil and the sick person.

### 1.4 Viaticum: Holy Communion for the Dying

**Viaticum** (Latin: "food for the journey") is the final reception of Holy Communion by a person in danger of death. It is the most important of the sacraments for the dying, as it provides the Body and Blood of Christ as spiritual nourishment for the passage to eternal life ([CCC 1524–1525](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/0583c069-d4bf-42dd-97de-c19f0b80150f/ref/1524)).

> **"Receive, brother (or sister), the Viaticum of the body of our Lord Jesus Christ, which will guard you from the wicked enemy and lead you into eternal life."**

Viaticum may be given:

- **Within Mass**, as part of the Eucharistic liturgy
- **Outside Mass**, with a simplified rite including the Liturgy of the Word, the Lord's Prayer, and the communion as Viaticum

If danger continues for some time, Viaticum may be received more than once.

### 1.5 The Apostolic Pardon

At the moment of death, the priest imparts the **Apostolic Pardon** (also called the Apostolic Blessing), which carries a **plenary indulgence** applicable to the dying person:

> **"By the authority of the Apostolic Ministry, I grant you a full and remission of all your sins, in the name of the Father, and of the Son, and of the Holy Spirit. Amen."**

This is one of the most important graces the Church can offer at the hour of death.

## 2. The Commendation of the Dying

The **Rite of Commendation of the Dying** is a liturgical prayer service celebrated beside a person who is near death. It is found in the _Pastoral Care of the Sick: Rites of Anointing and Viaticum_ (1972).

### Structure of the Rite

1. **Introduction and Greeting** — The priest or minister greets the dying person and those present.
2. **Reading from Sacred Scripture** — Passages such as:
   - Isaiah 35:3–4, 6c–7, 10
   - Job 19:23–27a
   - Psalm 22; 24; 90; 113; 122
   - 1 Corinthians 15:1–4
   - 1 John 4:16
   - Revelation 21:1–5a, 6–7
   - John 6:37–40; 14:1–6, 23, 27
   - Luke 22:39–46; 23:42–43
3. **Litany of the Saints** — The faithful invoke the saints, especially the dying person's patron saints, with the response _"Pray for him/her"_ (_Ora pro eo/eā_).
4. **Prayers of Commendation** — Including the ancient prayers:

> **"Depart, Christian soul, from this world, in the name of God the Father Almighty who created you, in the name of Jesus Christ, Son of the living God, who suffered for you, in the name of the Holy Spirit who was poured out upon you. May you live in peace this day, may your home be with God in Zion, with Mary the Virgin Mother of God, with Joseph, and with all the Angels and Saints."**

> **"I commend you, dearest brother/sister, to Almighty God, and to Him whose creature you are, that, having returned to Him who formed you from the dust of the earth, may Holy Mary, the Angels, and all the Saints come to meet you as you go forth from this life. May Christ who was crucified for you bring you freedom and peace."**

5. **Prayer After Death** — If death occurs during or after the rite, a special prayer is said.
6. **Prayer for Family and Friends** — A prayer of consolation for those who mourn.

### Ejaculations at the Moment of Death

As the person is about to expire, those present are encouraged to pray more earnestly and to whisper these invocations:

- **"Into your hands, Lord, I commend my spirit."** (Psalm 31:5; Luke 23:46)
- **"O Lord Jesus Christ, receive my spirit."** (Acts 7:59)
- **"Holy Mary, pray for me."**
- **"Mary, Mother of grace, Mother of mercy, protect me from the enemy and receive me at the hour of my death."**
- **"Jesus, Mary, and Joseph, I give you my heart and my soul."**
- **"Sacred Heart of Jesus, have mercy on us."**

## 3. After Death: Immediate Prayers and Preparations

### 3.1 Prayers Immediately After Death

Once death has occurred, the following should be done:

1. **Close the eyes** of the deceased and **fold the hands**, placing a crucifix or rosary in them.
2. **Light a candle** beside the body — a symbol of the light of Christ.
3. **Sprinkle the body with holy water** — a reminder of Baptism.
4. **Recite the _Requiem Aeternam_:**

> **"Eternal rest grant unto them, O Lord, and let perpetual light shine upon them. May their souls, and the souls of all the faithful departed, through the mercy of God, rest in peace. Amen."**

5. **Pray the _De Profundis_** (Psalm 129/130):

> **"Out of the depths I cry to you, O Lord; Lord, hear my voice. Let your ears be attentive to my voice in supplication. If you, O Lord, mark iniquities, Lord, who can stand? But with you is forgiveness, that you may be revered. I wait for the Lord, my soul waits, and I hope for his word. My soul waits for the Lord more than sentinels wait for the dawn. More than sentinels wait for the dawn, let Israel hope for the Lord, for with the Lord is kindness and with him is plenteous redemption. And he will redeem Israel from all their iniquities."**

### 3.2 The Wake or Vigil

In many Catholic cultures, a **wake** or **vigil** is held in the home or funeral home on the evening before the funeral. This is a time for:

- **Praying the Rosary** for the deceased
- **Reading Sacred Scripture** — especially the Psalms and the Gospel accounts of the Resurrection
- **Sharing memories** and offering consolation to the family
- **Reciting the _Requiem Aeternam_** and other prayers for the dead

The vigil may also include the **Office of the Dead** (from the Liturgy of the Hours), which consists of Psalms, readings, and prayers for the repose of the soul.

## 4. The Funeral Rites

The Church provides a three-part funeral liturgy for the deceased Catholic, each with its own theological emphasis:

### 4.1 The Funeral Vigil (Wake Service)

- **The Reception of the Body** — The coffin is sprinkled with holy water and a pall is placed on it, recalling the baptismal garment.
- **The Liturgy of the Word** — Scripture readings, psalms, and a homily.
- **Prayers of the Faithful** — Intercessions for the deceased, the family, and all who mourn.
- **The _Requiem Aeternam_** is recited.

### 4.2 The Funeral Mass

The **Funeral Mass** is the central act of Christian funeral worship. It is a Mass offered for the soul of the deceased, with the following distinctive elements:

- **The Rite of Reception** — The coffin is received at the church door, sprinkled with holy water, and incensed.
- **The Liturgy of the Word** — Readings chosen from the Lectionary for the Dead.
- **The Homily** — Proclaiming the Resurrection and the hope of eternal life.
- **The Universal Prayer (Prayers of the Faithful)** — Including petitions such as:

> **"That He who wept at the death of Lazarus may wipe away our tears."**
> **"That He who raised the dead to life may grant our brother/sister eternal life."**
> **"That He who promised paradise to the penitent thief may lead our brother/sister into heaven."**

- **The Liturgy of the Eucharist** — The Mass is offered for the repose of the soul of the deceased.
- **The Final Commendation and Farewell** — The body is incensed as a sign of honor and a reminder that the body is a temple of the Holy Spirit. The priest says:

> **"Eternal rest grant unto him/her, O Lord, and let perpetual light shine upon him/her."**

- **The Rite of Committal** — At the graveside or place of cremation, the final prayers are said and the body is committed to the earth:

> **"Because God has chosen to call our brother/sister from this life to himself, we commit his/her body to the earth: for we are dust, and unto dust we shall return. But the Lord Jesus Christ will change our mortal bodies to be like his in glory, for he has risen, the firstborn from the dead."**

### 4.3 Eastern Catholic Funeral Practice

The Eastern Catholic Churches celebrate the **Panikhida** (memorial service) or the **Requiem Divine Liturgy**, with distinctive prayers and hymns. The Byzantine tradition emphasizes the Resurrection and the hope of the general resurrection at the Last Day.

## 5. Prayers for the Dead: The Periodic Commemorations

The Catholic tradition includes a series of periodic commemorations at specific intervals after death. These are **pious customs and pastoral disciplines**, not dogmatic requirements, but they are widely practiced and encouraged by the Church.

### 5.1 The Third Day

The **Mass on the third day** after death commemorates the **Resurrection of Christ** on the third day. It is a sign of hope that the deceased will share in the Resurrection.

- **Prayers:** _Requiem Aeternam_, _De Profundis_ (Psalm 129/130), and a Requiem Mass.
- **Theological significance:** Christ rose on the third day; the deceased is commended to the Risen Lord.

### 5.2 The Seventh Day

The **Mass on the seventh day** (the "week's mind") recalls the **completion of creation** and the **seven gifts of the Holy Spirit**. It is also associated with the tradition that the soul undergoes particular judgment shortly after death.

- **Prayers:** Requiem Mass, Rosary, _De Profundis_, and the Litany of the Saints.
- **Theological significance:** The number seven signifies completeness and perfection.

### 5.3 The Thirtieth Day

The **Mass on the thirtieth day** (the "month's mind") recalls Christ's **Ascension into heaven** forty days after the Resurrection, and the tradition that the soul's particular judgment and initial state in the afterlife are being resolved.

- **Prayers:** Requiem Mass, _Requiem Aeternam_, _De Profundis_, and the Office of the Dead.
- **Theological significance:** The number thirty is associated with maturity and completion (cf. the thirty years of Christ's hidden life).

### 5.4 The Fortieth Day

The **Mass on the fortieth day** is one of the most significant commemorations in the Catholic tradition. It recalls:

- Christ's **Ascension**, forty days after the Resurrection (Acts 1:3)
- Moses' **forty days on Mount Sinai** (Exodus 24:18)
- The Israelites' **forty years in the desert** (Numbers 14:33)
- Christ's **forty days of fasting** in the wilderness (Matthew 4:2)

**Prayers:**

- Requiem Mass offered on the fortieth day
- _Requiem Aeternam_
- _De Profundis_ (Psalm 129/130)
- Rosary (especially the Glorious Mysteries)
- Litany of the Saints
- Office of the Dead

**Theological significance:** The fortieth day marks a special transition — the soul is believed to have completed its initial purification and to be in a state of rest, awaiting the final judgment.

### 5.5 The Anniversary

On each **anniversary of death**, the family and friends offer Mass and prayers for the deceased. This is a continuing act of charity and filial piety.

- **Prayers:** Requiem Mass, _Requiem Aeternam_, _De Profundis_, Rosary, and personal prayers.
- **Theological significance:** The bond between the living and the dead is not severed by death; the Church Militant continues to pray for the Church Suffering (those in Purgatory).

### 5.6 All Souls' Day (November 2)

The **Commemoration of All the Faithful Departed** (All Souls' Day) is a universal feast of the Church dedicated to praying for all the souls in Purgatory. On this day:

- A **plenary indulgence** may be gained for the souls in Purgatory by visiting a church or oratory, praying a _Pater_ and _Credo_, and fulfilling the usual conditions (sacramental confession, Eucharistic communion, prayer for the intentions of the Holy Father, and detachment from all sin) ([Enchiridion Indulgentiarum, §29](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/b4e30e90-7ed9-4cc5-be4b-305ed07d425b/ref/Concessiones.%2029)).
- A **partial indulgence** is gained by visiting a cemetery and praying for the dead.
- The faithful may gain a plenary indulgence for the souls in Purgatory each day from **November 1 to November 8** by visiting a cemetery and praying for the dead.

## 6. The Novena for the Dead

A **novena** (from the Latin _novem_, "nine") is a nine-day period of prayer. The tradition of the novena for the dead is rooted in the Church's practice of praying for the deceased over a period of nine days, recalling the nine days of prayer of the Apostles between the Ascension and Pentecost (Acts 1:14).

### 6.1 Structure of the Novena

The novena for the dead may be prayed:

- **Immediately after death**, beginning on the day of death and continuing for nine days
- **At any time** after death, as a devotional practice
- **Before the anniversary of death**, as a preparation for the annual commemoration

### 6.2 Daily Prayers of the Novena

Each day of the novena may include:

1. **The Sign of the Cross**
2. **The _Requiem Aeternam_:**

> **"Eternal rest grant unto them, O Lord, and let perpetual light shine upon them. May their souls, and the souls of all the faithful departed, through the mercy of God, rest in peace. Amen."**

3. **The _De Profundis_** (Psalm 129/130)
4. **A Rosary** (especially the Glorious Mysteries, with the intention for the deceased)
5. **The Litany of the Saints**, with the response _"Pray for him/her"_
6. **A Scripture reading** — such as John 14:1–6, Romans 8:31–39, or 1 Corinthians 15:51–57
7. **A personal prayer** for the deceased, asking God's mercy and peace
8. **The _Eternal Rest_ prayer** (concluding)

### 6.3 Theological Basis

The novena is not a dogmatic requirement but a **pious custom** approved and encouraged by the Church. The _Directory on Popular Piety and the Liturgy_ (2002) affirms the value of novenas as a form of prayer that unites the faithful with the Paschal Mystery of Christ ([Directory on Popular Piety and the Liturgy, §155](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/4fba701f-fda0-47a5-a2de-2e210d81f770/ref/155)).

## 7. Other Prayers and Devotions for the Dead

### 7.1 The Rosary for the Dead

The Rosary is one of the most powerful prayers for the deceased. The Glorious Mysteries are especially appropriate, as they contemplate the Resurrection, Ascension, Descent of the Holy Spirit, Assumption, and Coronation of the Blessed Virgin Mary — all of which are signs of the hope of eternal life.

### 7.2 Mass Intentions

The **offering of Mass** for the deceased is the highest and most powerful form of prayer the Church can offer for the dead. The Church teaches that the Mass is a propitiatory sacrifice that can be offered for the living and the dead ([Council of Trent, Session XXII, Chapter 2](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/4a72892b-0a56-4371-a138-ff4aea2b0093/ref/); [CCC 1371](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/0583c069-d4bf-42dd-97de-c19f0b80150f/ref/1371)).

#### How to Have a Mass Offered for the Deceased

Any Catholic may request that a **Mass intention** be offered for a deceased loved one. This is done by contacting a parish priest or the parish office and making a formal request. The priest will offer the Mass on a specific date (or at the next available Mass) with the deceased person named as the intention. The faithful may also offer a **stipend** (a customary donation) to the parish, though this is not strictly required — the priest is obligated to accept Mass intentions regardless of whether a stipend is offered.

#### Attending Mass for the Deceased

You do **not** need to attend a special or "different" Mass to pray for the dead. **Every Mass** — whether a weekday Mass, a Sunday Mass, a funeral Mass, or a Requiem Mass — is the same sacrifice of Christ on Calvary made present on the altar. When you attend any Mass and offer your prayers for the deceased, you are participating in the most powerful prayer the Church possesses.

However, there are some distinctions:

- **A Mass with a specific intention** — When a priest offers a Mass with your loved one named as the intention, that Mass is offered _specifically_ for the repose of their soul. You may attend this Mass, or the priest may offer it privately.
- **A Requiem Mass** — This is a Mass offered with special prayers and rites for the deceased (see [section 7.3](#73-the-_requiem_-mass) below). It is not a "different" Mass, but rather the same Eucharistic sacrifice with additional prayers for the dead.
- **A regular Mass** — When you attend any regular Mass and mentally offer your prayers for the deceased, you are uniting your participation in the Eucharist with the intention of praying for their soul. This is a beautiful and powerful act of charity.

#### What to Do When Attending Mass for the Deceased

When you attend Mass with the intention of praying for a deceased loved one, you may:

1. **Mention their name** silently during the offertory, when the priest offers the bread and wine, uniting your offering with the sacrifice of Christ.
2. **Pray the _Requiem Aeternam_** silently during the Mass: _"Eternal rest grant unto them, O Lord, and let perpetual light shine upon them."_
3. **Receive Holy Communion** worthily, offering your communion for the repose of the soul of the deceased.
4. **Offer the Mass** — The entire Mass — from the Introit to the final blessing — is offered for the intention. You may unite your personal prayers, sufferings, and sacrifices with the Mass for the benefit of the deceased.
5. **Request a Mass intention** at your parish — Contact the parish office to have a Mass offered on a specific date for your loved one.

#### Theological Significance

The Mass is the re-presentation of the one sacrifice of Christ on the Cross ([CCC 1366–1367](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/0583c069-d4bf-42dd-97de-c19f0b80150f/ref/1366)). When offered for the deceased, it applies the merits of Christ's sacrifice to the soul in Purgatory, aiding in its purification and hastening its entry into the fullness of heavenly glory. The Council of Trent solemnly defined that the Mass is propitiatory — that is, it satisfies the temporal punishment due to sin — and that it can be offered for the living and the dead ([Council of Trent, Session XXII, Chapter 2](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/4a72892b-0a56-4371-a138-ff4aea2b0093/ref/)).

### 7.3 The _Requiem_ Mass

The **Requiem Mass** (Mass of the Dead) is a Mass offered with special prayers and rites for the deceased. Its name comes from the Introit: _"Requiem aeternam dona eis, Domine"_ ("Eternal rest grant unto them, O Lord"). The Requiem Mass includes:

- The **Introit:** _Requiem aeternam_
- The **Kyrie** (ninefold)
- The **Dies Irae** (in the traditional form)
- The **Offertory:** _Domine Jesu Christe_
- The **Sanctus** and **Agnus Dei** with special prayers for the dead
- The **Communion:** _Lux aeterna_

### 7.4 The _De Profundis_ (Psalm 129/130)

This penitential psalm is traditionally prayed for the dead, expressing the hope that God will deliver the soul from the depths:

> **"Out of the depths I cry to you, O Lord; Lord, hear my voice. Let your ears be attentive to my voice in supplication. If you, O Lord, mark iniquities, Lord, who can stand? But with you is forgiveness, that you may be revered. I wait for the Lord, my soul waits, and I hope for his word. My soul waits for the Lord more than sentinels wait for the dawn. More than sentinels wait for the dawn, let Israel hope for the Lord, for with the Lord is kindness and with him is plenteous redemption. And he will redeem Israel from all their iniquities."**

### 7.5 The _Libera Me_ (Office of the Dead)

The _Libera Me_ ("Deliver me, O Lord") is the responsory at the end of the Office of the Dead. It is a powerful prayer of intercession for the deceased, asking God to deliver the soul from eternal death:

> **"Deliver me, O Lord, from eternal death on that fearful day, when the heavens and the earth shall be shaken, when you shall come to judge the world by fire. I am trembling, and I am afraid, because of the judgment and the wrath to come. That day, that day of wrath, of calamity and misery, a great and bitter day..."**

### 7.6 Indulgences for the Dead

The Church grants **indulgences** applicable to the souls in Purgatory:

- **Plenary indulgence** — applicable only to the souls in Purgatory — may be gained by:
  - Visiting a cemetery daily from November 1–8 and praying for the dead ([Enchiridion Indulgentiarum, §29](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/b4e30e90-7ed9-4cc5-be4b-305ed07d425b/ref/Concessiones.%2029))
  - Visiting a church or oratory on All Souls' Day and praying a _Pater_ and _Credo_
- **Partial indulgence** may be gained by:
  - Visiting a cemetery and praying for the dead
  - Reciting the _Requiem Aeternam_

## 8. Latin Rite vs. Eastern Catholic Practice

| Aspect                      | Latin (Roman) Rite                                           | Eastern Catholic Churches                                 |
| --------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------ | --------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Name for the dying rite** | Pastoral Care of the Sick / Anointing of the Sick            | Holy Unction of the Sick                                  |
| **Anointing locations**     | Forehead and hands                                           | Forehead, eyes, nostrils, lips, ears, breast, hands, feet |
| **Funeral liturgy**         | Order of Christian Funerals (Vigil, Funeral Mass, Committal) | Panikhida / Requiem Divine Liturgy                        |
| **Periodic Masses**         | 3rd, 7th, 30th, 40th day, anniversary                        | Memorial services (Panikhida) at similar intervals        |
| **Key prayer**              | _Requiem aeternam dona eis, Domine_                          | _Memory Eternal_ (_Vechnaya Pamyat_)                      |
| **Office of the Dead**      | _Officium Defunctorum_ with _Libera Me_                      | Parastas (memorial service)                               |

| -------------------------------- | --------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------- |
| **1. Call the priest**           | When death is imminent            | Contact a Catholic priest immediately          |

| **3. Confirmation**              | Before death, if not yet received | Conferred by the priest                        |
