# The Order of the Mass — A Visual Walkthrough

> **Based on the video "The Mass Explained"** — a priest-led, real-church walkthrough of a typical Sunday Catholic Mass, produced by Ascension Presents. This document captures the theological explanations, spiritual insights, and practical guidance presented in the video, organized according to the fourfold structure of the Mass. Timestamps reference the source video at `https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=RS8NrJ0Y5O8` (39 min 13 sec).

---

## Introduction: Why This Matters

> "Catholics go to Mass every single Sunday, and yet, how many of us actually know what's going on?" — Opening of the video

The Mass is not a collection of arbitrary rituals. Every gesture, posture, word, and silence carries meaning rooted in Scripture, apostolic tradition, and two thousand years of lived faith. This walkthrough explains not only _what_ we do but _why_ we do it — and, crucially, _how to pray the Mass more powerfully_.

> **Note**: The priest in the video uses unconsecrated hosts and does not recite all the prayers; this is a _demonstration_, not an actual Mass. The purpose is catechetical: to show the faithful what happens and what each part means.

## Before Mass Begins: Entering into Worship

### 1. The Holy Water Font — Remembrance of Baptism (0:30)

As we enter the church, the first thing we encounter is the **holy water font** at the doorway. We dip our fingers and bless ourselves.

- **Theological meaning**: The baptismal font is placed at the entryway because _it is by baptism that we are brought into the life of Christ_. On our own, we could never approach the throne of the living God; we need His grace. Blessing ourselves with holy water reminds us that we _have_ His grace through baptism.
- **Practical tip**: As you bless yourself, recall your baptism — the day you were claimed for Christ, washed of original sin, and made a temple of the Holy Spirit ([CCC 1213](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/0583c069-d4bf-42dd-97de-c19f0b80150f/ref/1213)).

> See also: [sacraments/baptism.md](../sacraments/baptism.md)

### 2. Genuflecting to the Tabernacle — Acknowledging the Real Presence (1:06)

Upon reaching the pew, we locate the **tabernacle** — where Jesus Christ is truly present in the Blessed Sacrament — and **genuflect** (bend the knee).

The priest suggests three possible interior dispositions when genuflecting:

1. **"God, You are God and I'm not."** — An act of adoration, bending the knee because we are here to worship.
2. **"God, I'm at Your service."** — A total offering of self: whatever You want of my life, I am Yours.
3. **"Jesus, I love You."** — As a young man takes the knee to offer his life to the woman he loves, so we bend the knee to the One who loved us first.

> **Theological note**: Genuflection toward the tabernacle is an act of _latria_ — the adoration due to God alone — because Christ is substantially present in the Eucharist reserved therein ([CCC 1378](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/0583c069-d4bf-42dd-97de-c19f0b80150f/ref/1378)).

### 3. Kneeling in the Pew — Preparing the Heart (1:55)

After entering the pew, many of the faithful kneel in silent prayer before Mass begins. This is not empty time.

- **Theological meaning**: Every baptized Christian has been anointed as a **kingdom priest** — consecrated, set apart to offer the sacrifice, to give God worship (cf. [CCC 1546](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/0583c069-d4bf-42dd-97de-c19f0b80150f/ref/1546); 1 Peter 2:9).
- **Practical prayer**: "God, prepare my heart. Give me the heart that's able to lift up the right sacrifice of the Son to the Father in the power of the Holy Spirit." Ask God to prepare your heart to worship.

> See also: [sacraments/baptism.md](../sacraments/baptism.md) — the common priesthood of the faithful

## I. The Introductory Rites

### 4. The Procession — Pilgrimage to the Altar (2:34)

Mass begins with the **procession**: the priest, deacon, and servers process from the back of the church toward the altar.

- **Theological meaning**: The procession is a symbol of **pilgrimage**. The entire Christian life is a pilgrimage — a journey from somewhere to somewhere. We have the baptismal font at the doorway (our entryway into the life of Christ), and we head down the aisle toward the altar, because on that altar we will encounter the **source and summit of the Christian life** ([CCC 1324](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/0583c069-d4bf-42dd-97de-c19f0b80150f/ref/1324)).
- **Spiritual insight**: Every time we stand at the beginning of Mass, we remind ourselves: "I have a pilgrimage. I have a destination. I have a goal. I have a purpose. And that purpose is Christ."
- **Posture**: Standing — the posture of the pilgrim, of one who has a destination.

### 5. The Priest Reverences the Altar (3:16)

As the priest reaches the sanctuary, he **bends down and kisses the altar**.

- **Theological meaning**: The altar is not only the place of sacrifice — it is also a **symbol of Christ Himself** (the _altar_ is Christ, from whose wounded side flows the Eucharist). The priest reverences Christ, represented by the altar. The priest in the video shares that his mentor taught him to place his hand on the altar as he kisses it — "a way of giving Jesus a kiss and a hug."
- **GIRM reference**: [GIRM 49](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/db52efa7-5906-412d-b27c-ca92e0ce6c86/ref/49) — the priest venerates the altar with a kiss.

### 6. The Sign of the Cross and Greeting (3:47)

> "In the name of the Father, and of the Son, and of the Holy Spirit." — "Amen."

The priest highlights **three layers of meaning** in the Sign of the Cross:

1. **The Holy Trinity**: The deepest mystery of the Christian faith — that God is one, but three Divine Persons (Father, Son, and Holy Spirit).
2. **The Cross itself**: We trace the cross, which is the price that God Himself paid for you — for your heart, for your soul — to win you back.
3. **The purpose of your life**: Recalling the old Baltimore Catechism — "Why did God make you? God made me to know Him, to love Him, and to serve Him in this life, so as to live with Him forever in the next."

- **The open palm**: In the Roman tradition, the Sign of the Cross is often made with an **open palm** — the palm of blessing — reminding us that at baptism we were anointed as kingdom priests, blessing ourselves with that open palm.
- **The greeting**: "The Lord be with you." — "And with your spirit." This is an ancient biblical greeting. In Scripture, whenever an angel or prophet came to someone with a call bigger than them, they began with these words. This greeting appears **four times** during Mass, each time with increasing intensity.

> **Note on Eastern practice**: In the Eastern Catholic Churches, the Sign of the Cross is made with the thumb and first two fingers joined (symbolizing the Trinity) and the last two fingers pressed to the palm (symbolizing the two natures of Christ), and is traced from right to left. See [church-history/eastern-catholic-churches.md](../church-history/eastern-catholic-churches.md).

### 7. The Penitential Rite (5:40)

> "Let us call to mind our sins, and call upon the Lord's love and mercy."

The Church invites us to **take off the mask** — to acknowledge our wounds, our brokenness, and our sinfulness — but also to declare: "I have a Savior, Jesus Christ." I can acknowledge I'm not perfect, I'm not okay — but I _am_ loved.

- **The _Kyrie_**: "Lord, have mercy. Christ, have mercy. Lord, have mercy." This is one of the most ancient elements of the Mass, retained in Greek even in the Latin Rite.
- **Spiritual insight**: The penitential rite is not about wallowing in guilt — it is about asking God to meet us in our brokenness and give us His mercy.

> **The _Confiteor_** (one of the three forms of the Penitential Act):
>
> "I confess to almighty God, and to you, my brothers and sisters, that I have greatly sinned, in my thoughts and in my words, in what I have done and in what I have failed to do, through my fault, through my fault, through my most grievous fault; therefore I ask blessed Mary, ever-Virgin, all the Angels and Saints, and you, my brothers and sisters, to pray for me to the Lord our God."
>
> — "May Almighty God have mercy on us, forgive us our sins, and bring us to everlasting life. Amen."

> **GIRM reference**: [GIRM 51](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/db52efa7-5906-412d-b27c-ca92e0ce6c86/ref/51) — three forms of the penitential act, including the _Confiteor_.

### 8. The Gloria (6:27)

Sung on Sundays outside of Advent and Lent, the _Gloria_ is a hymn of pure praise. The priest explains:

- The Mass is an hour (or more) where we don't have to think about ourselves. It's an hour where we get to focus our attention, our hearts, our worship on **God**.
- As we sing the _Gloria_, we cry out: "God, how good You are. All the things You've done, who You are — it's worth praising."
- The _Gloria_ echoes the angels' song at Bethlehem (Luke 2:14) and is one of the most ancient Christian hymns.

> **The full text of the _Gloria_**:
>
> "Glory to God in the highest,
> and on earth peace to people of goodwill.
> We praise You, we bless You,
> we adore You, we glorify You,
> we give You thanks for Your great glory,
> Lord God, heavenly King,
> O God, almighty Father.
> Lord Jesus Christ, Only Begotten Son,
> Lord God, Lamb of God, Son of the Father,
> You take away the sins of the world,
> have mercy on us;
> You take away the sins of the world,
> receive our prayer;
> You are seated at the right hand of the Father,
> have mercy on us.
> For You alone are the Holy One,
> You alone are the Lord,
> You alone are the Most High, Jesus Christ,
> with the Holy Spirit,
> in the glory of God the Father. Amen."

> **Omitted**: during Advent and Lent, and at weekday Masses outside festal seasons.

### 9. The Collect — the Opening Prayer (7:19)

> "Let us pray."

The priest calls the altar server to bring the Missal — but more importantly, he calls _all of us_ to listen attentively. This opening prayer **sets the theme for the entire Mass**.

- **Theological note**: The Collect is not randomly chosen; it is carefully crafted to let us know what we are about to do in this particular liturgical celebration.
- **The "Amen"**: When we say "Amen" at the end, it is not a cute way to wrap up the prayer — **it is a commitment**. We are offering the Lord our "Amen" — our "so be it," our "I stake my life on this."

## II. The Liturgy of the Word

### 10. Sitting — the Posture of Receptivity (8:06)

After the Collect, we sit. Being seated is not a posture of passivity — it is a **posture of receptivity**. Knowing we are about to hear God's Word proclaimed, we want to follow wherever He leads.

The priest offers three practical ways to prepare for the Liturgy of the Word:

1. **Read the readings ahead of time** — before coming to Mass, sit down and pray through the Sunday readings.
2. **Bring something to write with and something to write on** — because every time God's Word is proclaimed, He wants to speak something to us, and writing it down makes it even more accessible.
3. **Adopt the posture of Samuel**: "Speak, Lord, your servant is listening" (1 Samuel 3:9–10).

### 11. The First Reading — The Old Testament (9:00)

> "A reading from the book of Deuteronomy..."

The first reading almost always comes from the **Old Testament** (except during the Easter season, when it comes from the Acts of the Apostles).

- **What to listen for**: The Old Testament is the story of how God interacted with His people Israel — how they turned their hearts away from Him, but He kept calling them back. Listen for: _How is God calling His people to say "yes"? How are they saying "no"? When do they ultimately surrender and give their hearts to Him?_
- **Response**: "The Word of the Lord." — "**Thanks be to God.**" This is not a reflexive afterthought. Imagine having never heard that God exists, that He knows who you are, that He loves you — and then hearing it for the first time. Every "Thanks be to God" says: "Thank You, God, for revealing Your heart to me."

### 12. The Responsorial Psalm (10:10)

> "Your words, Lord, are spirit and life."

Pope Benedict XVI observed that the beauty of the Responsorial Psalm is that this is a moment in the Mass where **we pray the words of God back to God**. The Psalms are the inspired prayer book of Israel and the Church — we take God's own words and offer them back to Him in praise, lament, thanksgiving, and supplication.

### 13. The Second Reading — The New Testament Letters (10:42)

> "A reading from Paul's letter to the Colossians..."

The second reading always comes from one of the **New Testament letters or the Book of Revelation** — writings outside the Gospels. These are the fullness of God's revelation: God speaking to us in light of the incredible revelation of His love for us in Jesus Christ.

- **Note**: On weekdays, there is typically only one reading before the Gospel.

### 14. The Gospel Acclamation — Alleluia (11:22)

Our posture changes once again: **we stand**. While all of Scripture is inspired and is the Word of God, the Gospels have a **pride of place** because of how deeply and profoundly they are about Jesus Himself. We stand to give honor to the Gospels.

- **Alleluia**: From the Hebrew _hallelu-Yah_, meaning "Praise the Lord." When we sing "Alleluia," we are literally praising the Lord with a word that transcends language. During Lent, another acclamation replaces the Alleluia.
- **The priest's silent prayer**: As the priest or deacon proceeds to the ambo for the Gospel, he pauses at the altar, bows, and prays quietly: _"Cleanse my heart and my lips, Almighty God, that I may worthily proclaim Your holy Gospel."_

### 15. The Gospel Reading (12:10)

> "The Lord be with you." — **"And with your spirit."**
> "A reading from the Holy Gospel according to Mark." — **"Glory to You, O Lord."**

This is the **second of four times** we hear "The Lord be with you" — because as we approach the words of the Gospel, we are encountering the very story of Jesus Christ, the fullness of God's revelation.

- **The triple Sign of the Cross**: As the Gospel is announced, the faithful trace a small cross with their thumb on their **forehead**, **lips**, and **heart**, praying silently: _"May the words of the Gospel be on my mind, on my lips, and in my heart."_
- **The kiss of the Gospel**: At the conclusion, the priest or deacon kisses the Book of the Gospels, praying: _"May the words of the Gospel cleanse us of our sins."_

### 16. The Homily (13:09)

The homily is a moment where the deacon, priest, or bishop **unpacks the words of Scripture** in a way meant to inspire, to educate, and to encourage us to move forward in the Christian life.

- **Purpose**: Sometimes we can hear the words of Scripture and not know what God is trying to say, or how these words apply to our lives. The homily bridges that gap. It is "an integral part of the Liturgy" ([GIRM 65](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/db52efa7-5906-412d-b27c-ca92e0ce6c86/ref/65); [CCC 1349](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/0583c069-d4bf-42dd-97de-c19f0b80150f/ref/1349)).

### 17. The Profession of Faith — The Creed (13:36)

We all stand and profess the **Nicene-Constantinopolitan Creed**. The priest reminds us not to stumble through this mechanically:

- **What we are saying**: The deepest and most profound mysteries of our faith — proclaimed for thousands of years: the Trinity, the Incarnation, the Virgin Birth, the Ascension, the resurrection of the body.
- **Who we are saying it about**: "I believe in God, _who is_ Father. I believe in God, _who is_ Son. I believe in God, _who is_ the Holy Spirit — three Divine Persons in one God." This summarizes the faith of all Christians for the last two thousand years.

> **The Nicene-Constantinopolitan Creed** (full text):
>
> "I believe in one God,
> the Father Almighty,
> maker of heaven and earth,
> of all things visible and invisible.
> I believe in one Lord Jesus Christ,
> the Only Begotten Son of God,
> born of the Father before all ages.
> God from God, Light from Light,
> true God from true God,
> begotten, not made, consubstantial with the Father;
> through Him all things were made.
> For us men and for our salvation
> He came down from heaven,
> and by the Holy Spirit was incarnate of the Virgin Mary,
> and became man.
> For our sake He was crucified under Pontius Pilate,
> He suffered death and was buried,
> and rose again on the third day
> in accordance with the Scriptures.
> He ascended into heaven
> and is seated at the right hand of the Father.
> He will come again in glory
> to judge the living and the dead
> and His kingdom will have no end.
> I believe in the Holy Spirit, the Lord, the giver of life,
> who proceeds from the Father and the Son,
> who with the Father and the Son is adored and glorified,
> who has spoken through the prophets.
> I believe in one, holy, catholic and apostolic Church.
> I confess one Baptism for the forgiveness of sins
> and I look forward to the resurrection of the dead
> and the life of the world to come. Amen."

### 18. The Prayer of the Faithful — Universal Prayer (14:29)

> "For the unity of the Church throughout the world, we pray to the Lord." — **"Lord, hear our prayer."**

While we are all standing, the lector announces the needs of the Church throughout the world. The priest offers a key insight about the response:

- **"Lord, comma, hear our prayer"**: We are actually _talking to someone_. There is Someone listening as we speak. When we say "Lord, hear our prayer," we are addressing the Lord Himself.

> See also: [liturgy/order-of-the-mass.md](order-of-the-mass.md) §3.2 for the full structure of the Liturgy of the Word.

## III. The Liturgy of the Eucharist

### 19. The Offertory — Bringing Our Gifts (15:10)

The offertory is the moment where we bring forth the gifts. The priest warns against treating this as "Catholic intermission":

- **What happens**: Ordinary gifts — bread and wine — are brought forward, which God will transform into something extraordinary.
- **The early Church**: In the early Church, people would bring their own bread and wine from home, which would become the Body and Blood of Jesus. Today, we take up a collection and purchase the bread and wine, but we are still called to offer _something_: **ourselves**.
- **Spiritual offering**: Every one of us has a person we've been trying to help, a situation that won't improve no matter what we do. This is the moment to place _those people_, _those circumstances_, and _our own hearts_ on the altar. "What God does with ordinary bread and wine, He can do with your and my ordinary hearts and ordinary people and ordinary circumstances. He can transform them into something extraordinary."

### 20. The Preparation of the Gifts — The Jewish Passover Prayers (16:54)

> "Blessed are You, Lord, God of all creation, for through Your goodness we have received the bread we offer You..."

The priest prays the very prayers that **Jesus Himself would have prayed at the Last Supper, the very first Mass** — the Jewish _berakah_ (blessing) prayers:

> **Over the bread**:
>
> "Blessed are You, Lord God of all creation, for through Your goodness we have received the bread we offer You: fruit of the earth and work of human hands, it will become for us the bread of life."
>
> **The people respond**: "Blessed be God for ever."

> **Over the wine** (the priest then pours wine and a little water into the chalice):
>
> "Blessed are You, Lord God of all creation, for through Your goodness we have received the wine we offer You: fruit of the vine and work of human hands, it will become our spiritual drink."
>
> **The people respond**: "Blessed be God for ever."

> **The priest then prays quietly**: "With humble spirit and contrite heart may we be accepted by You, O Lord, and may our sacrifice in Your sight this day be pleasing to You, Lord God."

### 21. The Mingling of Water and Wine (17:28)

A small amount of water is poured into the chalice with the wine. The priest prays quietly:

> "By the mystery of this water and wine, may we come to share in the divinity of Christ, who humbled Himself to share in our humanity."

- **Theological meaning**: When the Second Person of the Trinity became a human being, He poured out His divinity into His humanity without losing any of His divinity. The water (humanity) is absorbed into the wine (divinity) — just as we are drawn into the divine life of Christ.

### 22. The Washing of Hands — The _Lavabo_ (18:15)

At the Passover meal, the one who led the meal would always wash his hands before offering it up. The priest does the same — but the prayer, drawn from Psalm 51, reveals that this is about more than hygiene: it is about **interior purification** before offering the sacrifice.

> **The _Lavabo_ prayer** (prayed quietly by the priest):
>
> "Wash me, O Lord, from my iniquity and cleanse me from my sin."

### 23. The Invitation — "Pray, My Brothers and Sisters" (18:31)

> "Pray, my brothers and sisters, that my sacrifice and yours may be acceptable to God, the Almighty Father."

- **Theology of the priesthood of all believers**: By virtue of ordination, the priest is a **ministerial priest** — sharing uniquely in the one priesthood of Jesus Christ. But every baptized Christian is a **kingdom priest**, anointed to offer the sacrifice. You are not meant to simply _watch_ the ministerial priest pray — you are here to **offer the sacrifice as well**.
- **The people's response** highlights the **two main purposes of the Mass**: (1) to give the Father glory, and (2) to sanctify the world.

> **The full dialogue**:
>
> **Priest**: "Pray, brothers and sisters, that my sacrifice and yours may be acceptable to God, the almighty Father."
>
> **People**: "May the Lord accept the sacrifice at your hands, for the praise and glory of His name, for our good and the good of all His holy Church."

### 24. The Eucharistic Prayer — The Preface (19:57)

> "The Lord be with you." — **"And with your spirit."**

This is the **third time** we hear this greeting, and things have escalated. We are entering the apex of the Mass, where God — who is compassion and mercy, but also a consuming fire — will be worshipped.

> "Lift up your hearts." — **"We lift them up to the Lord."**
> "Let us give thanks to the Lord our God." — **"It is right and just."**

- **Theological meaning**: "It is truly right and just" — because God deserves our best. God deserves our worship. And it is this worship that, in so many ways, actually transforms and saves us.

### 25. The Sanctus — "Holy, Holy, Holy" (21:26)

> "Holy, Holy, Holy Lord God of hosts. Heaven and earth are full of Your glory. Hosanna in the highest. Blessed is He who comes in the name of the Lord. Hosanna in the highest."

- **Biblical roots**: This prayer comes directly from Scripture — Isaiah's vision of the seraphim (Isaiah 6:3) and the crowds at Jesus' entry into Jerusalem (Matthew 21:9). The angels and saints gathered around the throne of the Lamb worship Him without ceasing, and **we get to join our voices and our hearts with the worship that is happening in heaven**.

### 26. Kneeling — The Posture of Humility and Worship (22:19)

After the _Sanctus_, the congregation kneels. This is the **third liturgical posture** of the Mass:

1. Standing — the pilgrim, the one with purpose
2. Sitting — the receptive listener
3. Kneeling — the humble worshipper

Kneeling is a sign of our humility and also a sign that we are called to worship.

### 27. The Epiclesis — Invocation of the Holy Spirit (22:54)

> "Therefore, O Lord, we humbly implore You: by this same Spirit, graciously make holy these gifts we have brought to You for consecration, that they may become the Body and Blood of Your Son, our Lord Jesus Christ..."

- **Theological meaning**: We ask the Holy Spirit to come upon these gifts in a similar way that (a) the Holy Spirit sent the manna — the bread from heaven — to feed the Israelites in the wilderness, and (b) the Holy Spirit overshadowed the womb of the Blessed Virgin Mary, in whom the Second Person of the Trinity became flesh. What is now ordinary bread and ordinary wine will, **by the power of the Holy Spirit**, become the very Body, Blood, Soul, and Divinity of Jesus Christ.

- **The priest extends his hands over the gifts** during this prayer — the ancient gesture of epiclesis.

### 28. The Institution Narrative and Consecration (23:46)

> "On the night He was betrayed, He Himself took bread, and giving You thanks, He said the blessing, broke the bread, and gave it to His disciples, saying: **Take this, all of you, and eat of it. For this is My Body, which will be given up for you.**"

> "In a similar way, when supper was ended, He took the chalice. And once more giving thanks, He said the blessing and gave the chalice to His disciples, saying: **Take this, all of you, and drink from it. For this is the chalice of My Blood, the Blood of the new and eternal covenant, which will be poured out for you and for many for the forgiveness of sins. Do this in memory of Me.**"

By uttering **the very words of Jesus at the Last Supper**, in the power of the Holy Spirit, ordinary bread and ordinary wine become something extraordinary. The priest elevates the Host and then the Chalice, and **a bell is rung** — because it indicates to everyone gathered that **a miracle has just happened**.

- **Spiritual insight during the elevation**: Imagine a two-way mirror. From our side, we see our Lord incarnate now in what looks like bread and wine. From the other side, it is all of heaven looking at earth, looking at us — because we are all worshipping the Lord our God.

> **Dogmatic note**: This is **transubstantiation** — the complete change of the whole substance of bread into the substance of the Body of Christ, and of the whole substance of wine into the substance of His Blood, while the appearances (_species_) of bread and wine remain ([Council of Trent, Session XIII](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/4a72892b-0a56-4371-a138-ff4aea2b0093/ref/); [CCC 1376](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/0583c069-d4bf-42dd-97de-c19f0b80150f/ref/1376)).

### 29. The Mystery of Faith — Memorial Acclamation (25:41)

> "The mystery of faith."

Now we speak directly to **Jesus, who is truly present on the altar**. We recall what He has done for us: His death, His resurrection, and His promise to come again. The Roman Missal provides **three forms** of this acclamation:

> **Form A**: "We proclaim Your Death, O Lord, and profess Your Resurrection until You come again."
>
> **Form B**: "When we eat this Bread and drink this Cup, we proclaim Your Death, O Lord, until You come again."
>
> **Form C**: "Save us, Savior of the world, for by Your Cross and Resurrection You have set us free."

### 30. The Great Amen — The Apex of the Mass (26:09)

> "Through Him, and with Him, and in Him, O God, Almighty Father, in the unity of the Holy Spirit, all glory and honor is Yours, forever and ever."
> **"Amen."**

This is the moment. The priest takes the very Body, Blood, Soul, and Divinity of Jesus and **offers that sacrifice to the Father** — just as Jesus on the Cross said, "Father, into Your hands I commend My spirit" (Luke 23:46).

- **The Great Amen**: The priest calls it "the Great Amen" — but laments that in most parishes it becomes "the lame Amen." No — this is _the_ moment. You have **one word**: "Amen." That word sums up everything the ministerial priest has prayed at the altar. As a kingdom priest, your "Amen" sums up **your participation in the sacrifice of Jesus**. With that one word, you say: "Yes. I stake my life on this."

### 31. The Communion Rite

#### The Lord's Prayer — The "Our Father" (27:08)

> "At the Savior's command and formed by divine teaching, we dare to say:"
>
> "Our Father, who art in heaven,
> hallowed be Thy name;
> Thy kingdom come,
> Thy will be done
> on earth as it is in heaven.
> Give us this day our daily bread,
> and forgive us our trespasses,
> as we forgive those who trespass against us;
> and lead us not into temptation,
> but deliver us from evil."

The priest highlights **three reasons** why the introduction says "we _dare_ to say":

1. **We approach God as Father**: In baptism, we are made adopted sons and daughters of the Father. We get to approach the Father — at all times.
2. **"Give us this day our daily bread"**: The Greek term translated "daily" (_epiousios_) actually indicates "supersubstantial." This is the bread from heaven — the Eucharist. Every Christian who has ever prayed the Lord's Prayer has been begging the Father: "Give us this day the Eucharist — give us this day our supersubstantial bread."
3. **"Forgive us our trespasses as we forgive those who trespass against us"**: We are saying to God: "Father, only forgive me to the depth that I'm willing to forgive those who have hurt me." That is a daring thing to say.

> **The Embolism** (prayed by the priest after the Lord's Prayer):
>
> "Deliver us, Lord, we pray, from every evil, graciously grant peace in our days, that, by the help of Your mercy, we may be always free from sin and safe from all distress, as we await the blessed hope and the coming of our Savior, Jesus Christ."
>
> **The people's response**: "For the kingdom, the power, and the glory are Yours, now and for ever."

#### The Sign of Peace (28:50)

> "Let us offer each other a sign of peace."

Two insights from the priest:

1. **Jesus taught us**: "If you are bringing your gift to the altar, and there remember that your brother has something against you, leave your gift there before the altar and go; first be reconciled to your brother, and then come and offer your gift" (Matthew 5:23–24). The Sign of Peace is a symbolic gesture — we offer **peace and forgiveness** to those around us.
2. **The early Christian martyrs**: A community of believers rounded up by Roman authorities, about to be killed in the Colosseum, turned to each other and exchanged the Sign of Peace. When you offer peace to the person next to you, you are also preparing to be **martyrs together** — saying, "Lord Jesus, if by my life or by my death I can give witness to You with the people on either side of me, prepare us now."

#### The Fracturing — Breaking of the Bread (30:03)

The priest takes the sacred Body of our Lord and breaks it, then mingles a small particle of the Host with the Precious Blood.

- **Historical reason**: In the early persecuted Church, the bishop would celebrate Mass and then send a particle of his Host (the _fermentum_) via runners to house churches, where priests would place it in their chalice. This was the visible sign of **union with the bishop**.
- **Theological reason**: Mingling the Body and Blood back together is a sign that **Christ is not dead, but alive** — Body, Blood, Soul, and Divinity — the Risen Lord.

> **Prayer**: "May this mingling of the Body and Blood of our Lord Jesus Christ bring eternal life to us who receive it."

#### The _Agnus Dei_ — "Lamb of God" (31:13)

All the congregation kneels, and we cry out to Jesus as John the Baptist identified Him: **the Lamb of God who takes away the sins of the world** (John 1:29).

> **The _Agnus Dei_** (full text):
>
> "Lamb of God, You take away the sins of the world: **have mercy on us.**
> Lamb of God, You take away the sins of the world: **have mercy on us.**
> Lamb of God, You take away the sins of the world: **grant us peace.**"

#### The Invitation to Communion (32:33)

> **Priest**: "Behold the Lamb of God, behold Him who takes away the sins of the world. Blessed are those called to the supper of the Lamb."
>
> **All respond**: "Lord, I am not worthy that You should enter under my roof, but only say the word and my soul shall be healed."

Our response echoes the **centurion** of Capernaum (Matthew 8:8). We know the Lord loves us and wants to come close to us — but we also know we are not worthy. Yet we trust in His word.

#### The Distribution of Holy Communion (33:02)

The priest reminds us of **the tragedy of Christian division**: at the Last Supper, Jesus not only gave us the Eucharist — He begged the Father that we would be one (John 17:21). And yet here we are, divided as followers of Jesus. Those who are not Catholic, or not able to receive for another reason, are invited to come forward with arms crossed over the chest to receive a blessing.

- **Two ways to receive**:
  1. **On the tongue** — the most ancient and classic way. "The Body of Christ." — **"Amen."** Approach with hands folded, open your mouth, and allow the Lord to feed you — all the way back to Genesis, where God in the Garden simply wanted to feed His children.
  2. **In the hand** (by indult in the United States and other regions) — St. Justin Martyr, writing in the second century, described making a **throne for the King**: one hand placed upon the other, receiving the Lord, then consuming Him immediately and reverently.

- **The meaning of "Amen"**: When the minister declares "The Body of Christ" or "The Blood of Christ," and we respond "Amen" — that word means more than "thank you" or "uh-huh." It means **"I stake my life on that."** Because if this is Jesus, I stake my life on that; and if this is not Jesus, then I stake my life on that.

### 32. The Purification of the Vessels (35:54)

The priest carefully purifies the sacred vessels. This is so much more than "washing the dishes."

- **Spiritual parallel**: The priest invokes the image from _The Passion of the Christ_ — after Jesus is scourged, Mary and Mary Magdalene go to where His Blood was spilled, on their hands and knees, collecting as much of that Precious Blood as they can. We know that **even a particle of the Holy Eucharist, even a drop of the Precious Blood of Jesus, is enough to save the world**. So we take extraordinary care that every particle, every drop, of our Lord is preserved.

### 33. The Prayer after Communion — With Mary (36:43)

> "Let us pray."

While the priest purifies the vessels, we are invited to **pray in the place of Mary**. In Luke 1, the Holy Spirit came upon her and Jesus Christ was truly incarnate within her. Right now, after receiving Communion, we are in a similar place as Our Lady: **Jesus is truly dwelling inside of us**.

- **Mary's Magnificat** (Luke 1:46–55) is a perfect prayer for this moment: _"My soul proclaims the greatness of the Lord; my spirit rejoices in God, my Savior."_ Unite your heart with her heart in her prayer.

---

## IV. The Concluding Rites

### 34. The Final Blessing (37:41)

> "The Lord be with you." — **"And with your spirit."**

The **fourth and final time** we hear this greeting — and things have reached their peak of intensity. You are about to be sent back out into the world — but sent out **transformed**, **changed**, as someone who carries the very Holy Spirit of God, the very Body and Blood of Jesus, where He needs to go.

- **You are sent**: "You're sent out into a world that needs hope — _as hope_. You're sent out into a world that needs light — _as light_. You're sent out into a world that needs Jesus — _with Jesus_."

> "May Almighty God bless you: the Father, and the Son, ☩ and the Holy Spirit." — **"Amen."**

### 35. The Dismissal — _Ite, Missa Est_ (38:54)

> "Go in peace, glorifying the Lord by your life." — **"Thanks be to God."**

The very word "Mass" derives from the Latin _Missa_, from the dismissal _Ite, missa est_ ("Go, it is the dismissal"). The entire celebration is named after the act of **being sent** — because the Eucharist ends by sending the faithful into the world to fulfill God's will ([CCC 1332](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/0583c069-d4bf-42dd-97de-c19f0b80150f/ref/1332)).

## Summary: The Four Postures of the Mass

| Posture        | When                                                                         | Meaning                                                                      |
| -------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Standing**   | Introductory Rites, Gospel, Creed, Prayers of the Faithful, Concluding Rites | The pilgrim with a purpose; the one with a destination; honor for the Gospel |
| **Sitting**    | First Reading, Responsorial Psalm, Second Reading, Homily                    | Receptivity; listening; "Speak, Lord, Your servant is listening"             |
| **Kneeling**   | Eucharistic Prayer (after Sanctus), _Agnus Dei_, after receiving Communion   | Humility; worship; adoration before the miracle of the Eucharist             |
| **Processing** | Entrance, Offertory (bringing gifts), Communion, Recessional                 | Pilgrimage; offering; receiving; being sent                                  |

## The Four "The Lord Be With You" Greetings

| Occurrence | Context                           | Significance                                               |
| ---------- | --------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------- |
| **1st**    | Introductory Rites — Greeting     | We are gathered and about to begin                         |
| **2nd**    | Before the Gospel                 | We encounter Christ in His own words                       |
| **3rd**    | Eucharistic Prayer — Preface      | We are about to enter the apex — the Eucharistic sacrifice |
| **4th**    | Concluding Rites — Final Blessing | We are about to be sent as Christ-bearers into the world   |

Each time, "the degree of the bigness of that moment intensifies."

## Practical Takeaways for Praying the Mass More Powerfully

1. **Come early, prepare your heart**: Arrive a few minutes before Mass. Use the holy water font mindfully. Genuflect to the tabernacle. Kneel in the pew and ask God to prepare your heart to worship.
2. **Read the readings ahead of time**: Before Sunday, read and pray through the lectionary readings. You can find them in a missal, online, or through apps.
3. **Bring something to write with**: When something in the readings, the homily, or the silence strikes you — write it down. You will remember it better, pray with it deeper, and recognize God's voice more clearly.
4. **Offer yourself at the Offertory**: When the gifts are brought forward, mentally place on the paten the person you're struggling to love, the situation that won't improve, and your own heart. Let them be transformed.
5. **Mean your "Amen"**: When you say "Amen" — especially at the Great Amen, and when you receive Holy Communion — let it mean "I stake my life on this."
6. **Stay with Mary after Communion**: After receiving, don't just flip through the bulletin. Sit with Jesus inside you. Pray Mary's Magnificat. Let your soul proclaim the greatness of the Lord.
7. **Remember that you are sent**: The Mass doesn't end when you leave the church — it begins. You carry Christ into a world that needs hope, that needs light, that needs Jesus.
