# The Order of the Mass

The **Mass** is the central act of Catholic worship — the sacramental celebration of the Most Holy Eucharist in which the **sacrifice of the Cross is perpetuated on the altar** and the faithful are nourished at the Lord's table. Its ordered structure, transmitted from the apostles and gradually shaped by the Roman Church, makes present the whole work of salvation: Christ crucified, risen, and coming again. The Mass is at once the **sacrificial memorial** of the one sacrifice of Calvary and the **sacred banquet** of communion with Christ's Body, Blood, Soul, and Divinity ([CCC 1382](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/0583c069-d4bf-42dd-97de-c19f0b80150f/ref/1382); [Council of Trent, Session XXII](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/4a72892b-0a56-4371-a138-ff4aea2b0093/ref/)).

This document presents the **Order of Mass** (Latin: _Ordo Missae_) — the universal sequence of prayers, readings, and rites by which the Eucharist is celebrated in the Latin Church, and the corresponding structure of the **Divine Liturgy** in the Eastern Catholic Churches. It treats both the **Novus Ordo Missae** (the Ordinary Form promulgated in the 1969 Roman Missal of Pope St. Paul VI, revised in 2002) and the historical _Mass of the Ages_ codified after the Council of Trent (the 1962 _Missale Romanum_, retained as the _Forma Extraordinaria_).

> "At the Last Supper Christ instituted the Eucharistic sacrifice of his Body and Blood. He did this in order to perpetuate the sacrifice of the Cross throughout the ages until he should come again, and so to entrust to his beloved spouse, the Church, a memorial of his death and resurrection: a sacrament of love, a sign of unity, a bond of charity, a Paschal banquet in which Christ is eaten, the mind is filled with grace, and a pledge of future glory is given to us." ([CCC 1323](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/0583c069-d4bf-42dd-97de-c19f0b80150f/ref/1323); citing _Sacrosanctum Concilium_ §47)

## 1. The Shape of the Mass: Four Movements

The Second Vatican Council's Constitution on the Sacred Liturgy, [_Sacrosanctum Concilium_](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/f3bd930d-74b4-46ee-86ed-06d79db364a9/ref/undefined), directed that the rites of the Mass "are to be simplified, while due care is taken to preserve their substance," and that elements "which have suffered injury through accidents of history are now to be restored to the earlier norm of the Holy Fathers" — for example the homily, the prayer of the faithful, and the penitential act at the beginning of the Mass ([Missale Romanum, _Institutio Generalis_](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/6c499d17-83df-470e-8558-6c60ebcf2814/ref/)).

The Order of Mass, in its current Latin-Rite form, is organized into **four great movements**:

1. **Introductory Rites** — the gathering, greeting, penitential act, _Kyrie_, _Gloria_, and opening prayer.
2. **Liturgy of the Word** — the readings from Sacred Scripture, the responsorial psalm, the Gospel acclamation, the homily, the Profession of Faith (_Credo_), and the Prayer of the Faithful.
3. **Liturgy of the Eucharist** — the Preparation of the Gifts, the Eucharistic Prayer (with the Preface, _Sanctus_, institution narrative, anamnesis, and doxology), the Communion Rite (with the Lord's Prayer, _Agnus Dei_, sign of peace, breaking of the bread, and reception of Holy Communion).
4. **Concluding Rites** — brief announcements, the blessing, the dismissal (_Ite, missa est_), and the kiss of the altar.

This fourfold pattern — Word and Sacrament, with surrounding rites of gathering and sending — is rooted in the apostolic worship of the second century and has remained substantially stable across the Latin Church's liturgical history.

## 2. Historical Development

### 2.1 The Second-Century Witness: St. Justin Martyr

The earliest surviving description of a Christian Eucharistic celebration is given by **St. Justin Martyr** in the _First Apology_ (c. A.D. 155), addressed to the Emperor Antoninus Pius. In chapters 65–67, Justin describes the Sunday gathering of the brethren: the readings from "the memoirs of the apostles or the writings of the prophets," the prayers of the faithful, the kiss of peace, the bringing of bread and wine mixed with water to the "president of the brethren," the prayer of thanksgiving ("eucharist") pronounced by the president, the people's acclamation _Amen_, and the distribution of the consecrated gifts by deacons to those present — and to those absent.

> "And this food is called among us Εὐχαριστία [the Eucharist], of which no one is allowed to partake but the man who believes that the things which we teach are true, and who has been washed with the washing that is for the remission of sins, and unto regeneration, and who is so living as Christ has enjoined. For not as common bread and common drink do we receive these; but in like manner as Jesus Christ our Saviour, having been made flesh by the Word of God, had both flesh and blood for our salvation, so likewise have we been taught that the food which is blessed by the prayer of His word, and from which our blood and flesh by transmutation are nourished, is the flesh and blood of that Jesus who was made flesh." ([Justin Martyr, _First Apology_ 66](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/f2c979a8-871d-4f46-a059-320d3b837a76/ref/))

> "Having ended the prayers, we salute one another with a kiss. There is then brought to the president of the brethren bread and a cup of wine mixed with water; and he taking them, gives praise and glory to the Father of all things, through the name of the Son and of the Holy Spirit, and offers thanks at considerable length for the gifts which He has bestowed on us..." ([Justin Martyr, _First Apology_ 65](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/f2c979a8-871d-4f46-a059-320d3b837a76/ref/))

Justin shows a recognizable **fourfold pattern** that is still visible in the Mass today: **readings — prayers of the faithful — Eucharistic offering (with the institution narrative) — communion**. The continuity between the second-century apostolic liturgy and the present Order of Mass is one of the great witnesses to Catholic tradition.

### 2.2 The Roman Canon and St. Gregory the Great

In the centuries that followed, the Roman Rite developed its own highly distinctive Eucharistic prayer, the **Roman Canon** (_Prex Eucharistica I_ or _Canon Missae_). Its structure is recognizable in the Leonine and Gelasian Sacramentaries of the fifth and sixth centuries and reached a stable form by the pontificate of **Pope St. Gregory I ("the Great," 590–604)**. Gregory made several changes to the Mass that are widely admitted by historians: he inserted into the Canon the petition "_diesque nostros in tua pace disponas, atque ab aeterna damnatione nos eripi, et in electorum tuorum jubeas grege numerari_"; he ordered the **Our Father** to be recited in the Canon _before_ the breaking of the Host; he regulated the use of the _Alleluia_ after the Gradual during the Paschal season; and he made various disciplinary adjustments to the roles of subdeacons and deacons ([Catholic Encyclopedia, "Pope St. Gregory I"](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/f2c979a8-871d-4f46-a059-320d3b837a76/ref/)).

The Roman Canon is distinguished from the great Eastern anaphoras by the **scattered placement of its intercessions** — partly before and partly after the Consecration — and by the Roman Rite's relative reserve regarding an explicit epiclesis, the solemn invocation of the Holy Spirit upon the gifts ([Catholic Encyclopedia, "Liturgy of the Mass"](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/f2c979a8-871d-4f46-a059-320d3b837a76/ref/Liturgy%20of%20the%20Mass)). The Canon opens with the prayer **_"Te igitur"_** ("We come to you, Father") and concludes with the _embolism_ before the Lord's Prayer ([GIRM 219](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/db52efa7-5906-412d-b27c-ca92e0ce6c86/ref/219)).

### 2.3 The Council of Trent and the Tridentine Mass (1570)

The **Council of Trent** (1545–1563), in its twenty-second session (1562), defined the Mass as a true and proper sacrifice — the same sacrifice of the Cross, sacramentally offered under the appearances of bread and wine ([Council of Trent, Session XXII](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/4a72892b-0a56-4371-a138-ff4aea2b0093/ref/)). To defend the integrity of the Roman Rite against Protestant challenges and to standardize liturgical practice throughout the Latin Church, **Pope St. Pius V** promulgated the reformed _Missale Romanum_ in **1570**, with the bull _Quo Primum_. This Missal, with subsequent revisions up to 1962, is the liturgical book that defines the so-called **Tridentine Mass** — now retained as the **Extraordinary Form** (_Forma Extraordinaria_) of the one Roman Rite by the motu proprio _Summorum Pontificum_ (2007) of Pope Benedict XVI.

The Tridentine Order of Mass has the same fourfold shape (Introit, Mass of the Catechumens, Mass of the Faithful, Postcommunion and blessing) but with several features that distinguish it from the current Ordinary Form:

- **Confiteor** said at the foot of the altar at the very beginning, before the Introit psalm.
- **Last Gospel** (the prologue of St. John) recited after the blessing.
- **Communion of the priest** before the Communion of the faithful.
- Use of only the **Roman Canon** (Eucharistic Prayer I) on most days; the other Eucharistic Prayers were permitted as variants.
- Latin is the exclusive liturgical language; the readings are proclaimed in Latin only.

The 1962 _Missale Romanum_ represents the latest revision of this pre-conciliar liturgical form.

### 2.4 Vatican II and the Missal of Pope St. Paul VI (1969/2002)

The Second Vatican Council, in [_Sacrosanctum Concilium_](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/f3bd930d-74b4-46ee-86ed-06d79db364a9/ref/undefined) (1963), ordered a general reform of the liturgy: simplification of rites while preserving their substance, the use of the vernacular (with Latin retained in its proper place), the enrichment of the lectionary so that "a more representative portion of the Holy Scriptures will be read to the people over a set cycle of years," and the restoration of certain elements that had fallen into disuse ([_Missale Romanum_, Preface](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/6c499d17-83df-470e-8558-6c60ebcf2814/ref/)).

Implementing the Council, **Pope St. Paul VI** promulgated the new **Roman Missal** with the Apostolic Constitution _Missale Romanum_ on Holy Thursday, 3 April **1969**. The third typical edition (_editio typica tertia_) was issued by Pope St. John Paul II in 2000 and translated for use in the United States in 2002; it remains the current _editio typica_ of the Latin Rite. This Missal constitutes the **Ordinary Form** (_Forma Ordinaria_) of the one Roman Rite, and the faithful have the right to follow either form (Benedict XVI, _Summorum Pontificum_).

Key features of the reformed Order of Mass include:

- A new and expanded **Lectionary** with a three-year cycle for Sundays (Year A — Matthew; Year B — Mark; Year C — Luke) and a two-year cycle for weekdays, so that much more of Scripture is read over the liturgical year.
- **Three additional Eucharistic Prayers** (II, III, IV) added to the Roman Canon (Eucharistic Prayer I), permitting greater pastoral variety while keeping the Roman Canon always available.
- Use of the **vernacular** in the readings, the prayers of the faithful, and many parts of the liturgy, with the retention of Latin as the _lingua sacra_ of the Roman Rite.
- The **Prayer of the Faithful** restored to its ancient place before the offertory.
- A simplified penitential act at the beginning of Mass, with the _Confiteor_ retained as one of the approved forms.
- An optional **rite of concelebration** for bishops and priests.
- The homily given greater prominence as part of the Liturgy of the Word.

## 3. The Order of Mass in Detail — Latin Rite (Novus Ordo Missae, 2002)

The detailed sequence below follows the **General Instruction of the Roman Missal** (GIRM), the introduction prefixed to the third typical edition of the Roman Missal ([USCCB, _The Roman Missal_, Third Typical Edition](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/3f204803-62dd-4467-9edb-8b46db283e8a/ref/The%20Order%20of%20Mass)).

### 3.1 Introductory Rites (GIRM §§46–54)

The Introductory Rites have as their purpose "to ensure that the faithful, who come together as one, establish communion and dispose themselves properly to listen to the word of God and to celebrate the Eucharist worthily" ([GIRM 46](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/db52efa7-5906-412d-b27c-ca92e0ce6c86/ref/46)).

> The **Confiteor** reads in the Latin Rite: "I confess to almighty God, and to you, my brothers and sisters, that I have greatly sinned, in my thoughts and in my words, in what I have done and in what I have failed to do, through my fault, through my fault, through my most grievous fault; therefore I ask blessed Mary, ever-Virgin, all the Angels and Saints, and you, my brothers and sisters, to pray for me to the Lord our God."

### 3.2 Liturgy of the Word (GIRM §§55–71)

The Liturgy of the Word is structured by the readings of Sacred Scripture, joined to the chants and prayers that enable the assembly to receive and respond to God's word ([GIRM 55](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/db52efa7-5906-412d-b27c-ca92e0ce6c86/ref/55)). The General Instruction notes that "when the Sacred Scriptures are read in the Church, God himself speaks to his people, and Christ, present in his own word, proclaims the Gospel" (cf. [CCC 1349](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/0583c069-d4bf-42dd-97de-c19f0b80150f/ref/1349)).

| Element                                       | Minister                       | Notes                                                                                                                                                                                               |
| --------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------ | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **First Reading**                             | Lector (lay person)            | Usually from the Old Testament (Acts during Eastertide), drawn from the lectionary cycle.                                                                                                           |
| **Responsorial Psalm**                        | Cantor with people             | A psalm appointed for the day, with congregational response.                                                                                                                                        |
| **Second Reading** (Sundays & major feasts)   | Lector                         | From one of the New Testament Epistles or Revelation.                                                                                                                                               |
| **Gospel Acclamation**                        | People                         | "Alleluia" with verse (or a different acclamation in Lent).                                                                                                                                         |
| **Gospel Reading**                            | Deacon or Priest               | Stand in the presence of the Book of the Gospels; the people respond, "Glory to you, O Lord," before the reading and "Praise to you, Lord Jesus Christ" after.                                      |
| **Homily**                                    | Priest (or Deacon)             | An integral part of the Liturgy, expounding the mysteries of the faith and the Scripture just proclaimed ([GIRM 65](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/db52efa7-5906-412d-b27c-ca92e0ce6c86/ref/65)). |
| **Profession of Faith** (_Credo_)             | All                            | The Nicene-Constantinopolitan Creed recited on Sundays and solemnities (omitted in most weekday Masses).                                                                                            |
| **Prayer of the Faithful** (Universal Prayer) | Deacon, Cantor, Priest, People | A series of petitions for the Church, civil authorities, the afflicted, and the local community. Each petition concludes with the people's response: "Lord, hear our prayer."                       |

### 3.3 Liturgy of the Eucharist (GIRM §§72–89)

The Liturgy of the Eucharist is "the centre and high point of the entire celebration" ([GIRM 72](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/db52efa7-5906-412d-b27c-ca92e0ce6c86/ref/72); [CCC 1352](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/0583c069-d4bf-42dd-97de-c19f0b80150f/ref/1352)). It is structured in three great movements: the **Preparation of the Gifts**, the **Eucharistic Prayer**, and the **Communion Rite**.

In the Latin Rite, **ten** Eucharistic Prayers are now available (the Roman Canon plus nine later compositions), with the Roman Canon always permitted and required on certain days.

> The four Eucharistic Prayers of the Ordinary Form (1973 Missal):
>
> - **Eucharistic Prayer I (Roman Canon)** — the venerable prayer reaching its mature form in the sixth century, with the unique prayers _Te igitur_, _Memento of the Living_, _Communicantes_, _Hanc igitur_, _Quam oblationem_, _Qui pridie_, _Unde et memores_, _Supra quae_, _Supplices_, _Memento of the Dead_, _Nobis quoque peccatoribus_, and _Per quem haec omnia_. Required on most Sundays and major feasts when the prayer is sung aloud.
> - **Eucharistic Prayer II** — a brief anaphora based on the ancient _Anaphora of St. Hippolytus_ (c. A.D. 215).
> - **Eucharistic Prayer III** — a new composition, drawn from the Roman Rite's traditional anaphoral theology, with a fixed Preface.
> - **Eucharistic Prayer IV** — modeled on the Eastern anaphoral tradition, with a fixed Preface recalling the whole history of salvation; it includes a more developed epiclesis.

| Element                                | Minister          | Notes                                                                                                                                                                         |
| -------------------------------------- | ----------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Brief Announcements** (if needed)    | Priest or Deacon  | Practical information for the parish community.                                                                                                                               |
| **Greeting and Blessing**              | Priest            | "The Lord be with you" — "And with your spirit." Then the blessing: "May almighty God bless you, the Father, and the Son, ☩ and the Holy Spirit." The people respond: "Amen." |
| **Dismissal**                          | Deacon or Priest  | "Go forth, the Mass is ended" (_Ite, missa est_), or, in more solemn forms, "Go in peace, glorifying the Lord by your life" — answered "Thanks be to God."                    |

The dismissal is not a mere courtesy: the very word **"Mass"** derives from the Latin _Missa_, itself from the dismissal _Ite, missa est_ ("Go, it is [the dismissal]"). The Latin name of the entire celebration preserves the Church's conviction that the Eucharist ends by _sending_ the faithful into the world to fulfill God's will ([CCC 1332](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/0583c069-d4bf-42dd-97de-c19f0b80150f/ref/1332)).

## 4. Ordinary and Proper

Within both the Tridentine and post-conciliar Missals, the Order of Mass is composed of two layers:

- **The Ordinary of the Mass** (_Ordo Missae_): the parts that remain the same at every celebration — the introductory dialogue, the _Kyrie_, _Gloria_, _Credo_, Preface dialogue, _Sanctus_, Canon / Eucharistic Prayer, Lord's Prayer, _Agnus Dei_, and the dismissal. These are gathered in the front of the Missal under the title **"The Order of Mass."**
- **The Proper of the Mass** (_Proprium Missae_): the parts that change according to the day, the season, the saint being commemorated, or the Votive Mass being celebrated — the Entrance Antiphon, the Collect, the readings, the Sequence (on certain feasts), the Prayer over the Offerings, the Preface, and the Prayer after Communion.

The **Sacramentary** or **Missal** thus provides the complete liturgical book from which the priest celebrates, while the **Lectionary** (introduced as a separate book by the post-conciliar reform) provides the scriptural readings in a three-year Sunday cycle and a two-year weekday cycle.

## 5. Latin Rite vs. Eastern Catholic: The Order of the Divine Liturgy

The Catholic Church is a communion of **24 _sui iuris_ Churches**, all sharing the same faith in the Eucharist but differing in liturgical language, rite, and discipline. The **Latin Church** follows the Order of Mass described above; the **23 Eastern Catholic Churches** each follow their own liturgical tradition, the most common of which is the **Byzantine Divine Liturgy** (especially the _Divine Liturgy of St. John Chrysostom_).

### 5.1 The Byzantine Divine Liturgy of St. John Chrysostom

In the Eastern Catholic Churches that follow the Byzantine tradition (Ukrainian Greek Catholic, Melkite Greek Catholic, Ruthenian Greek Catholic, Romanian Greek Catholic, Slovak Greek Catholic, Hungarian Greek Catholic, Bulgarian Greek Catholic, etc.), the Eucharistic celebration is structured into **three great liturgies** ([_Christ Our Pascha_, Catechism of the Ukrainian Greek Catholic Church, §397](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/5c0fb401-3c71-494e-bfce-ac8641dafd3b/ref/397)):

1. **Proskomedia** (_Liturgy of Preparation_) — the prayerful preparation of the bread and wine on the side table (_prothesis_), with commemorations of the living and the dead.
2. **Liturgy of the Catechumens** (_Liturgy of the Word_) — the antiphons, the Little Entrance, the readings (Epistle and Gospel), the homily, and the prayer of the faithful.
3. **Liturgy of the Faithful** (_Liturgy of the Eucharist_) — the Great Entrance, the Creed, the Anaphora (Eucharistic Prayer), the epiclesis, the Lord's Prayer, Holy Communion, and the dismissal.

The Anaphora of St. John Chrysostom — the ordinary Eucharistic Prayer of the Byzantine tradition — culminates in a particularly solemn **epiclesis** invoking the Holy Spirit "upon us and upon these Gifts here present" so that "they may be to those who partake of them ... for the purification of soul, for the remission of sins, for the communion of your Holy Spirit, for the fullness of the kingdom of Heaven, for boldness toward you, not for judgment or condemnation" ([_Christ Our Pascha_, §401](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/5c0fb401-3c71-494e-bfce-ac8641dafd3b/ref/401)).

### 5.2 Structural Comparison

| Movement                     | Latin Rite (Novus Ordo)                                                                                              | Byzantine Divine Liturgy (St. John Chrysostom)                                                                |
| ---------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Preparation of the gifts** | Offertory (at the altar, after the Creed)                                                                            | Proskomedia (at the _prothesis_/side table, before Liturgy of the Word)                                       |
| **Entrance procession**      | Entrance procession with chant (at the beginning)                                                                    | Little Entrance (before the Gospel) and Great Entrance (before the Anaphora)                                  |
| **Creed**                    | After the homily (before the Prayer of the Faithful)                                                                 | After the Great Entrance (immediately before the Anaphora)                                                    |
| **Epiclesis**                | Before the institution narrative in the Roman Canon; after in Eucharistic Prayers II–IV; before or after in EP III   | Solemnly after the institution narrative                                                                      |
| **Sign of Peace**            | Before the breaking of the bread                                                                                     | Exchanged earlier, at the Kiss of Peace after the Anaphora                                                    |
| **Lord's Prayer**            | Just before the breaking of the bread and the _Agnus Dei_                                                            | Just after the Anaphora, before Holy Communion                                                                |
| **Breaking of the Bread**    | Before Communion                                                                                                     | After the Lord's Prayer, before Communion (the _Artos_)                                                       |
| **Communion**                | Under one kind (Host) for laity in Latin Rite; under both kinds (Host and Chalice) by intinction or spoon in Eastern | Under both kinds (Body and Blood together) by intinction with a spoon for all the faithful, including infants |

For the doctrinal points shared by both rites — the Real Presence, transubstantiation (or the equivalent _metousiosis_), the sacrificial nature of the Eucharist, and the sacrificial priesthood of the New Covenant — see [sacraments/eucharist.md](../sacraments/eucharist.md).

### 5.3 Other Eastern Catholic Rites

Beyond the Byzantine liturgy, the Eastern Catholic Churches preserve other venerable liturgical traditions:

- **Antiochian / West Syriac** (Maronite, Syro-Malankara Catholic): the _Liturgy of the Twelve Apostles_ (Maronite _Sharrar_) and the _Anaphora of St. James_ (the Jerusalem origin of all anaphoral families).
- **Chaldean / East Syriac** (Chaldean Catholic, Syro-Malabar Catholic): the _Anaphora of Addai and Mari_, unique among Catholic anaphoras in having no explicit institution narrative yet recognized as a true eucharistic prayer by the Holy See.
- **Alexandrian / Coptic** (Coptic Catholic, Eritrean Catholic, Ethiopian Catholic): the Liturgy of St. Basil and the Liturgy of St. Mark.
- **Armenian** (Armenian Catholic): the _Liturgy of St. Gregory the Illuminator_, related to the Syriac and Byzantine traditions.

These rites are equal in dignity to the Latin Rite; no rite is "higher" or "lower" within the Catholic communion ([Code of Canons of the Eastern Churches, c. 28](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/4a72892b-0a56-4371-a138-ff4aea2b0093/ref/)).

## 6. The Matter and Form of the Eucharistic Celebration

### 6.1 The Minister

The **ordinary minister** of the Mass — that is, the minister who consecrates the bread and wine — is a **bishop or a priest** (in the Latin Rite) acting _in persona Christi Capitis_, "in the person of Christ the Head" ([CCC 1142](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/0583c069-d4bf-42dd-97de-c19f0b80150f/ref/1142); [Code of Canon Law, c. 900](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/4a72892b-0a56-4371-a138-ff4aea2b0093/ref/)). Deacons and lay faithful are not ordinary ministers of the consecration, but deacons (and, where pastoral necessity warrants, instituted acolytes or designated lay faithful) may distribute Holy Communion.

In the Latin Rite, the priest ordinarily celebrates alone, though the Mass may be **concelebrated** by several priests together with a single bishop or principal celebrant. In the Eastern Catholic Churches, concelebration in the strict sense is not used in the same way; each priest concelebrant normally receives Holy Communion from the principal celebrant at the moment of communion.

### 6.2 The Matter

The matter for the Eucharist is:

- **Wheaten bread** (in the Latin Rite, unleavened — Latin _azyma_; in the Eastern Catholic Churches, leavened — Greek _artos_).
- **Wine of the grape** (the natural fermented juice of the grape), with which the Latin Rite ordinarily mixes a small quantity of water, in memory of the water and blood that flowed from Christ's side (John 19:34).

The use of unleavened bread in the Latin Rite and leavened bread in the Eastern rites is a matter of **liturgical discipline, not dogma**; the substance of the sacrament is identical in both traditions ([Council of Trent, Session XIII](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/4a72892b-0a56-4371-a138-ff4aea2b0093/ref/)).

### 6.3 The Form

The **form** of the Eucharist is the prayer of thanksgiving (Eucharistic Prayer / Anaphora), including the **words of institution** ("This is my Body... This is my Blood...") over the bread and wine. The Latin Rite combines these within the Eucharistic Prayer, which always includes the epiclesis (invocation of the Holy Spirit), the institution narrative, and the anamnesis (memorial of the Passion and Resurrection). The Eastern anaphoras place the epiclesis more prominently after the institution narrative, in keeping with the Eastern tradition's emphasis on the work of the Spirit in the consecration.

## 7. The Mass as Act of the Whole Church

The Order of Mass is not an isolated clerical action but the prayer of the whole People of God, gathered and sent. The **Second Vatican Council** teaches that the Eucharist is "the source and summit of the whole Christian life" ([Lumen Gentium 11](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/4a72892b-0a56-4371-a138-ff4aea2b0093/ref/); [CCC 1324](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/0583c069-d4bf-42dd-97de-c19f0b80150f/ref/1324)) and that every liturgical celebration "is an action of Christ the Priest and of his Body, which is the Church" ([_Sacrosanctum Concilium_ §7](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/f3bd930d-74b4-46ee-86ed-06d79db364a9/ref/undefined)).

The faithful exercise their own proper participation through:

- **The responses** (e.g., "And with your spirit," "Amen," "Lord, hear our prayer") that pervade the liturgy.
- **The chants** — Entrance, Responsorial Psalm, Gospel Acclamation, Sanctus, Memorial Acclamation, Agnus Dei, Communion Antiphon — which belong to the whole assembly, not just to the choir.
- **The postures** — standing, sitting, kneeling — which bodily express the Church's interior posture of adoration.
- **The homily** — to which the faithful listen attentively and respond in faith.
- **Holy Communion** — the sacramental reception of Christ's Body and Blood, which renews the unity of the Mystical Body.

The priest acts in _persona Christi_, but he acts also _in nomine totius Ecclesiae_, "in the name of the whole Church." The Mass is the supreme _opus Dei_ and the supreme _opus populi Dei_ at once.
