# The Sacrament of the Anointing of the Sick

The second of the two sacraments of healing, in which the seriously ill are strengthened, comforted, and prepared for the journey to eternal life ([CCC 1499](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/0583c069-d4bf-42dd-97de-c19f0b80150f/ref/1499); [CCC 1532](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/0583c069-d4bf-42dd-97de-c19f0b80150f/ref/1532)). The sacrament was traditionally called **Extreme Unction** (Latin: _extrema unctio_, "final anointing") from the predominant medieval practice of conferring it near the moment of death; the Second Vatican Council (1962–1965) restored the more scriptural name **Anointing of the Sick**, recognizing that the sacrament is for any baptized person in danger of death from illness or old age, not only for those at the very last moment of life.

> "By the sacred anointing of the sick and the prayer of her priests, the whole Church commends the sick to the suffering and glorified Lord, asking that he may lighten their suffering and save them; she exhorts them, moreover, to associate themselves freely with the Passion and death of Christ, and thus contribute to the good of the People of God." (_Lumen Gentium_ 11)

---

## 1. Biblical Foundation

The sacrament is rooted in Christ's own ministry of healing and in the apostolic practice of the early Church:

- **James 5:14–15** — "Are any among you sick? They should call for the elders of the church and have them pray over them, anointing them with oil in the name of the Lord. The prayer of faith will save the sick, and the Lord will raise them up; and anyone who has committed sins will be forgiven."
- **Mark 6:13** — "[The disciples] cast out many demons, and anointed with oil many who were sick and cured them."
- **Mark 6:7** — Christ sends out the Twelve, giving them authority over unclean spirits.
- **Luke 9:1–2** — The Twelve are sent to "proclaim the kingdom of God and to heal."
- **Matthew 10:8** — "Cure the sick, raise the dead, cleanse the lepers, cast out demons."
- **Acts 9:32–35** — Peter's healing of Aeneas at Lydda.
- **Acts 28:8–9** — Paul's healing of Publius's father and others on Malta.

The Fathers of the Church (Origen, Chrysostom, Augustine, Leo the Great) all attest to the practice of anointing the sick with consecrated oil as a true sacrament.

---

## 2. Matter and Form

### 2.1 Matter

The matter of the sacrament is **olive oil** (or, where olives are not available, oil from other plants) that has been **duly blessed by the bishop** (Code of Canon Law, c. 847; [CCC 1530](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/0583c069-d4bf-42dd-97de-c19f0b80150f/ref/1530)). The bishop ordinarily blesses the **Oil of the Sick** (Latin: _oleum infirmorum_) during the **Chrism Mass** on Holy Thursday; if necessary, the celebrating priest may bless the oil himself.

### 2.2 Form

In the Latin Rite, the essential rite is the **anointing of the forehead and hands** of the sick person, accompanied by the prayer of the celebrant. The current Roman Ritual of 1972 (_Ordo Unctionis Infirmorum eorumque Pastoralis Curae_) provides the form:

> **"Through this holy anointing may the Lord in his love and mercy help you with the grace of the Holy Spirit. May the Lord who frees you from sin save you and raise you up."** ([CCC 1499](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/0583c069-d4bf-42dd-97de-c19f0b80150f/ref/1499); citing _Ordo Unctionis Infirmorum_)

The Eastern Catholic Churches use a similar prayer, frequently an epiclesis asking that the Holy Spirit descend upon the oil and the sick person.

---

## 3. Minister

The minister of the Anointing of the Sick is **only a bishop or a priest** (presbyter) ([CCC 1530](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/0583c069-d4bf-42dd-97de-c19f0b80150f/ref/1530); [Code of Canon Law, c. 1003](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/4a72892b-0a56-4371-a138-ff4aea2b0093/ref/)). Deacons and lay persons are not ministers of the sacrament, although in danger of death they may administer non-sacramental anointing with the appropriate prayers and oil of the sick, depending on local discipline.

The Council of Trent defined that the priest is the proper minister of this sacrament ([Council of Trent, Session XIV, c. 1](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/4a72892b-0a56-4371-a138-ff4aea2b0093/ref/)).

---

## 4. Recipient

The recipient of the sacrament is a **baptized person** who is **seriously ill**, in danger of death from illness or advanced old age ([CCC 1514](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/0583c069-d4bf-42dd-97de-c19f0b80150f/ref/1514); [CCC 1528–1530](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/0583c069-d4bf-42dd-97de-c19f0b80150f/ref/1528)). The sacrament may be repeated if the sick person recovers and again falls ill, or if the illness becomes more grave.

The faithful may receive the sacrament **before surgery** when the surgery is necessitated by a serious illness. The elderly who are visibly failing may receive it, even if no specific illness has been diagnosed.

> "The Anointing of the Sick is not a sacrament for those only who are at the point of death. Hence, as soon as anyone of the faithful begins to be in danger of death from sickness or old age, the fitting time for him to receive this sacrament has certainly already arrived." ([CCC 1514](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/0583c069-d4bf-42dd-97de-c19f0b80150f/ref/1514); citing _Sacram Unctionem Infirmorum_, Paul VI, 1972)

---

## 5. Effects

The Catechism lists five principal effects of the Anointing of the Sick:

1. **Union of the sick person with the Passion of Christ**, for their own good and that of the whole Church ([CCC 1532](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/0583c069-d4bf-42dd-97de-c19f0b80150f/ref/1532)).
2. **Strengthening, peace, and courage** to endure the illness or old age in a Christian manner ([CCC 1532](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/0583c069-d4bf-42dd-97de-c19f0b80150f/ref/1532)).
3. **Forgiveness of sins**, if the sick person was unable to obtain it through the sacrament of Penance ([CCC 1532](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/0583c069-d4bf-42dd-97de-c19f0b80150f/ref/1532)).
4. **Restoration of health**, if this is conducive to the salvation of the person's soul ([CCC 1532](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/0583c069-d4bf-42dd-97de-c19f0b80150f/ref/1532)).
5. **Preparation for passing over to eternal life** (Latin: _transitus ad Patrem_), the "passing over" to the Father ([CCC 1532](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/0583c069-d4bf-42dd-97de-c19f0b80150f/ref/1532)).

The Council of Trent taught the same effects, adding that the sacrament strengthens the soul, gives confidence in God's mercy, makes the bearing of illness easier, resists the temptations of the Evil One, and sometimes restores bodily health if this is salutary for the soul ([Council of Trent, Session XIV, c. 2](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/4a72892b-0a56-4371-a138-ff4aea2b0093/ref/)).

---

## 6. Viaticum: The Final Eucharist

The Anointing of the Sick is naturally connected to **Viaticum** (Latin: "food for the journey"), the final reception of Holy Communion by a person in danger of death. The Catechism teaches:

> "In addition to the Anointing of the Sick, the Church offers the Eucharist as a 'Viaticum' — food for the journey — to the dying, who, having received it, are strengthened against the terrors of death and given the pledge of their future resurrection." ([CCC 1525](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/0583c069-d4bf-42dd-97de-c19f0b80150f/ref/1525))

Together, the Anointing of the Sick and Viaticum complete the Christian's sacramental preparation for the passage from this life to eternal life.

---

## 7. Pastoral Context: The Pastoral Care of the Sick

The 1972 Apostolic Constitution _Sacram Unctionem Infirmorum_ reorganized the celebration of the sacrament, integrating it with the entire pastoral care of the sick. The Latin Rite now provides for:

- A **celebration for a single sick person**, ordinarily within Mass when possible, with the family and community participating in the prayers.
- A **celebration for several sick persons**, particularly in hospitals or parishes, at a suitable time.
- A **celebration in danger of death**, which may be brief and focused, with the priest alone or with a small group.

The Eastern Catholic Churches maintain the practice of administering the sacrament with great solemnity, frequently during the Divine Liturgy or in a dedicated service.

---

## 8. Latin Rite vs. Eastern Catholic Tradition

### 8.1 Shared Doctrine

The theology of the sacrament is identical in East and West:

- The same matter (olive oil blessed by the bishop).
- The same essential form (prayer and anointing).
- The same minister (bishop or priest).
- The same recipient (baptized person in danger of death from sickness or old age).
- The same five effects (union with the Passion, strength, forgiveness of sins, possible restoration of health, preparation for eternal life).

### 8.2 Differences in Liturgical Practice

| Aspect | Latin Rite | Eastern Catholic Churches |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Name** | Anointing of the Sick (formerly Extreme Unction) | Holy Unction of the Sick |
| **Celebration** | Ordinarily in a quiet, intimate setting, with the priest and possibly family | Often in a more liturgical setting, with a fuller ritual |
| **Number of anointings** | Forehead and hands (with optional anointing of other places) | In Byzantine tradition: forehead, eyes, nostrils, lips, ears, breast, hands, feet |
| **Link with Viaticum** | The final Eucharist is given immediately as Viaticum | The final Eucharist is also received as Viaticum |
| **Sacramental confession** | If possible, before the anointing | If possible, before the anointing |

Theological content is the same in both traditions; the differences are primarily liturgical and cultural.

---

## 9. Historical Note: From "Extreme Unction" to "Anointing of the Sick"

The medieval and early-modern Latin tradition concentrated the sacrament's celebration at the moment of death, when the sick person might not be fully conscious and could not easily cooperate. The Second Vatican Council's _Sacrosanctum Concilium_ (1963) and the Apostolic Constitution _Sacram Unctionem Infirmorum_ (1972) restored the more scriptural practice of celebrating the sacrament earlier in the illness, when the sick person can benefit fully from the grace and the spiritual consolations. The Council of Trent's doctrine was not changed; only the pastoral practice was renewed.
