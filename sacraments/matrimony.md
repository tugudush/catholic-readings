# The Sacrament of Matrimony

The sacrament of **Christian Matrimony**, also called the sacrament of Marriage, is the conjugal covenant between one man and one woman, raised by Christ to the dignity of a sacrament for the baptized. Matrimony is ordered to the **good of the spouses**, to the **procreation and education of children**, and to the **indissoluble unity of the spouses**, who mirror Christ's faithful and permanent love for his Church ([CCC 1601](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/0583c069-d4bf-42dd-97de-c19f0b80150f/ref/1601); [CCC 1647](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/0583c069-d4bf-42dd-97de-c19f0b80150f/ref/1647)).

> "The matrimonial covenant, by which a man and a woman establish between themselves a partnership of the whole of life, is by its nature ordered toward the good of the spouses and the procreation and education of offspring; this covenant between baptized persons has been raised by Christ the Lord to the dignity of a sacrament." ([CCC 1601](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/0583c069-d4bf-42dd-97de-c19f0b80150f/ref/1601); citing _Code of Canon Law_, c. 1055)

---

## 1. Biblical Foundation

The Catholic doctrine of marriage is rooted in the creation of man and woman in the Book of Genesis, restored and elevated by Christ, and revealed as a great mystery by Saint Paul.

- **Genesis 1:27–28** — "So God created humankind in his image, in the image of God he created them; male and female he created them. God blessed them, and God said to them, 'Be fruitful and multiply, and fill the earth and subdue it.'"
- **Genesis 2:18, 21–24** — "It is not good for the man to be alone; I will make him a helper as his partner." And: "Therefore a man leaves his father and his mother and clings to his wife, and they become one flesh."
- **Matthew 19:3–9** — Christ affirms the indissolubility of marriage: "Have you not read that the one who made them at the beginning 'made them male and female,' and said, 'For this reason a man shall leave his father and mother and be joined to his wife, and the two shall become one flesh'? So they are no longer two, but one flesh. Therefore what God has joined together, let no one separate."
- **Mark 10:6–9** — Parallel account.
- **Ephesians 5:21–33** — "Be subject to one another out of reverence for Christ. ... For this reason a man shall leave his father and mother and be joined to his wife, and the two shall become one flesh. This is a great mystery, and I am applying it to Christ and the church."
- **1 Corinthians 7:10–16** — Paul's teaching on marriage, divorce, and mixed marriages.
- **Tobit 8:7** — "Now, O Lord, you know that I take this kinswoman of mine, not out of lust, but with sincerity. Grant that she and I may find mercy and that we may grow old together."
- **John 2:1–11** — The Wedding at Cana, where Christ performs his first public miracle at a wedding, thereby showing his blessing on marriage.

The Council of Trent summarized the biblical and patristic tradition: Adam, the first man, spoke of marriage as "perpetual and indissoluble," and Jesus Christ confirmed the indissolubility of the marriage tie in the strongest terms ([Council of Trent, Session XXIV](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/4a72892b-0a56-4371-a138-ff4aea2b0093/ref/)).

---

## 2. The Conjugal Covenant

Catholic doctrine begins with the dignity of the **natural conjugal covenant** between man and woman, before raising it to the order of grace:

> "God himself is the author of matrimony. The vocation to marriage is written in the very nature of man and woman as they came from the hand of the Creator. Marriage is not a purely human institution despite the many changes it has undergone through the centuries in different cultures, social structures, and spiritual attitudes. These differences should not cause us to forget its common and permanent characteristics. Although the dignity of this institution is not transparent everywhere with the same clarity, some sense of the greatness of the matrimonial union exists in all cultures." ([CCC 1603](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/0583c069-d4bf-42dd-97de-c19f0b80150f/ref/1603); citing _Gaudium et Spes_ 48)

For the baptized, the natural institution is raised to the dignity of a sacrament, and the marriage becomes a sign of Christ's love for his Church ([CCC 1611–1617](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/0583c069-d4bf-42dd-97de-c19f0b80150f/ref/1611)).

---

## 3. Minister, Matter, and Form

### 3.1 Minister

The **ministers** of the sacrament of Matrimony are the **spouses themselves**, who mutually confer the sacrament upon each other by exchanging their consent before a properly authorized witness. The priest (or deacon, in some circumstances) is the **official witness of the Church** who blesses the marriage in the name of the Church and ensures the canonical validity of the rite.

> "In the Latin Church, the spouses are the ministers of the sacrament of Matrimony by exchanging their consent before a properly authorized witness (the priest or deacon who represents the Church)." ([CCC 1623](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/0583c069-d4bf-42dd-97de-c19f0b80150f/ref/1623))

In the Eastern Catholic Churches, the priest or bishop also acts in a ministerial liturgical role (e.g., the crowning), but the mutual consent of the spouses remains the constitutive element of the sacrament.

### 3.2 Matter

The matter of the sacrament is the **mutual consent of the spouses**, expressed by an outward sign and accepted by both parties. The consent must be:

- **Free** — without coercion or grave fear.
- **Internal** — proceeding from the will of the person.
- **External** — expressed by words (and sometimes by signs) in such a way that the Church can receive it as proof.
- **Canonically qualified** — by one man and one woman who are baptized and not impeded by canonical impediments.

### 3.3 Form

The form of the sacrament is the **outward expression of consent** by the two spouses in the presence of the authorized witness. In the Latin Rite, this ordinarily takes the form of the marriage vows exchanged during the nuptial Mass or the marriage ceremony; in the Eastern Catholic Churches, the crowning and the epiclesis follow the mutual consent.

---

## 4. Properties of Marriage

Catholic doctrine teaches that Christian marriage possesses two essential properties:

### 4.1 Unity (Monogamy)

Marriage is the union of **one man with one woman**. The marital bond cannot be replaced by polygamy, polyandry, or group marriage ([CCC 1605–1610](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/0583c069-d4bf-42dd-97de-c19f0b80150f/ref/1605); [CCC 1644](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/0583c069-d4bf-42dd-97de-c19f0b80150f/ref/1644)). Christ explicitly condemned polygamy and divorce in [Matthew 19:3–9](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/00a1de35-98f1-481a-956f-e98e2b52331d/ref/).

### 4.2 Indissolubility

The marriage bond is **indissoluble**: "What God has joined together, let no one separate" ([Matthew 19:6](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/00a1de35-98f1-481a-956f-e98e2b52331d/ref/); [CCC 1614–1620](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/0583c069-d4bf-42dd-97de-c19f0b80150f/ref/1614); [CCC 1647](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/0583c069-d4bf-42dd-97de-c19f0b80150f/ref/1647)). The Council of Trent defined the indissolubility of the marriage bond as a doctrine of the Catholic faith ([Council of Trent, Session XXIV, c. 5–9](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/4a72892b-0a56-4371-a138-ff4aea2b0093/ref/)).

In the Latin Rite, the only exception to the indissolubility of a consummated sacramental marriage is the **Pauline privilege** (rare, applicable to unbaptized parties) and the **Petrine privilege** (with papal intervention). These do not dissolve a consummated sacramental marriage of baptized persons; that marriage remains absolutely indissoluble.

> "Through the sacrament of Matrimony the spouses are enabled to represent this fidelity and witness to it. Through the sacrament, the indissolubility of marriage receives a new and deeper meaning." ([CCC 1647](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/0583c069-d4bf-42dd-97de-c19f0b80150f/ref/1647))

---

## 5. The Goods of Marriage

Catholic theology classically distinguishes three goods (Latin: _bona matrimonii_) that flow from the indissoluble conjugal covenant:

| Good | Latin | Meaning |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Offspring** | _bonum prolis_ | The procreation and education of children. |
| **Fidelity** | _bonum fidei_ | The mutual fidelity of the spouses, in which they pledge an unbreakable conjugal love. |
| **Sacrament** | _bonum sacramenti_ | The marriage as a sign of Christ's love for his Church, ordered to the salvation of the spouses and to the building up of the Mystical Body. |

Vatican II affirms:

> "The intimate partnership of married life and love has been established by the Creator and qualified by his laws. It is rooted in the conjugal covenant of irrevocable personal consent. Hence, by God's gift, a lasting bond arises between the spouses, a bond that God himself has established. The institution of marriage and conjugal love is by its very nature ordered toward the procreation and education of children and finds therein its ultimate crown." (_Gaudium et Spes_ 48)

---

## 6. Effects

The sacrament of Matrimony produces the following effects:

1. **Conjugal bond** — the permanent, exclusive bond of one man and one woman.
2. **Indissolubility** — the absolute durability of the bond, even in the face of grave difficulty ([CCC 1641](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/0583c069-d4bf-42dd-97de-c19f0b80150f/ref/1641)).
3. **Increase of sanctifying grace** through the sacrament, in proportion to the dispositions of the spouses.
4. **Sacramental grace** to live conjugal love in a manner consonant with the dignity of the sacrament: fidelity, indissolubility, openness to children, mutual support, and persevering prayer ([CCC 1642](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/0583c069-d4bf-42dd-97de-c19f0b80150f/ref/1642)).
5. **Incorporation into the Church's mission** through the formation of the "domestic church" (Latin: _ecclesiola_, "little church") and the Christian upbringing of children.

> "The Christian home is the place where the children of God are begotten not by fleshly birth but by the action of the Holy Spirit through the Sacrament of Matrimony." (_Lumen Gentium_ 11)

---

## 7. Marriage as Image of Christ's Union with the Church

The Second Vatican Council, following Saint Paul in Ephesians 5, teaches that Christian marriage is the **image and the sign of Christ's spousal union with the Church**:

> "Christ is the source of the whole Christian marriage, the source of its unity, the source of its sanctity. Christ 'loved his Church and gave himself up for her, that he might sanctify her' (Eph 5:25–26). The love of Christ is the foundation of the Christian family's vocation and its source of strength." (_Lumen Gentium_ 35)

This is the deepest theological meaning of the sacrament: the spouses are called to mirror Christ's faithful, permanent, and self-giving love for his Bride, the Church.

---

## 8. Preparation and Celebration

The Latin Rite ordinarily requires a period of preparation (engagement, marriage preparation course, premarital investigation), the exchange of consent in the presence of a priest or deacon and two witnesses, the celebration within the nuptial Mass (or, in the Eastern Catholic Churches, within the crowning rite), the blessing of the spouses, the Mass, and the registration of the marriage in the parish records.

The Eastern Catholic Churches have analogous requirements, with the addition of the crowning ceremony:

- The **Office of Crowning** (Greek: _Stephánoma_) in the Byzantine tradition.
- The crowning of the bride and groom by the priest after the exchange of consent.
- The common cup from which the spouses drink (in some traditions).
- The **Dance of Isaiah** (the procession around the altar) in the Byzantine tradition.

---

## 9. Mixed Marriages

Catholic marriages between a Catholic and a baptized non-Catholic (e.g., a Christian of another confession) are called **mixed marriages** (Latin: _matrimonia mixta_). They are valid but illicit without the express permission of the competent authority (Code of Canon Law, c. 1124). Catholic marriages between a Catholic and a non-baptized person (e.g., a Jewish or Muslim partner) are called **disparities of cult** (Latin: _disparitas cultus_); they are ordinarily not permitted without a dispensation (Code of Canon Law, c. 1086).

In all mixed marriages, the Catholic party undertakes to do everything possible to ensure that the children are baptized and raised in the Catholic faith.

## 10. Latin Rite vs. Eastern Catholic Tradition

### 10.1 Shared Doctrine

The theology of marriage is identical in East and West:

- The same matter (mutual consent of the spouses).
- The same form (outward expression of consent before a properly authorized witness).
- The same ministers (the spouses themselves).
- The same properties (unity, indissolubility).
- The same goods (offspring, fidelity, sacrament).
- The same theological foundation (Genesis, the teaching of Christ, Ephesians 5).

### 10.2 Differences in Liturgical Practice

| Aspect | Latin Rite | Eastern Catholic Churches |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Crowning** | The priest blesses the rings but does not crown the spouses | The priest (or bishop) crowns the bride and groom as a visible sign of the marriage covenant |
| **Liturgical context** | Within the nuptial Mass (when both parties are Catholic) or within the marriage ceremony | Within the crowning rite, frequently attached to the Divine Liturgy (Divine Liturgy of St. John Chrysostom in the Byzantine tradition) |
| **Common Cup** | Common cup is optional | Common cup is frequently used as a sign of the shared life of the spouses |
| **Procession** | The procession with the spouses and witnesses | The **Dance of Isaiah** (or "Great Entrance") in the Byzantine tradition |
| **Marriage indissolubility** | Absolutey indissoluble (with rare Pauline/Petrine exceptions) | Absolutey indissoluble (with rare Pauline/Petrine exceptions) |

Theological content is identical in East and West.

---

## 11. Annulment

A Catholic tribunal may declare that a particular marriage was **invalid** from the beginning (Latin: _matrimonium nullum_, "null marriage") if any essential element of consent was lacking, or if a canonical impediment existed. This is **not a divorce**, but a declaration of nullity (an "annulment") recognizing that what appeared to be a marriage was not, in fact, a valid sacramental marriage. The Code of Canon Law sets forth the grounds and the procedure for such declarations (c. 1671–1691).

The Eastern Catholic Churches have analogous canonical processes for declaring the nullity of marriage.

---

## 12. The Church's Teaching on Contraception

In the encyclical _Humanae Vitae_ (1968), Pope Paul VI taught that the marital act is ordained to the **procreation of human life** and to the **conjugal love** of the spouses, and that artificial contraception is **intrinsically disordered**:

> "In this matter, the teaching of the Church declares that artificial interference with the natural law of generation is intrinsically disordered, and therefore to be excluded." (_Humanae Vitae_, §13)

The spouses, however, may responsibly regulate the spacing of children through **natural family planning** (NFP), in conformity with the dignity of the marital act and the goods of marriage.

The Catechism affirms this teaching:

> "Periodic continence, that is, the methods of birth regulation based on self-observation and the use of infertile periods, is in conformity with the objective criteria of morality. These methods respect the bodies of the spouses, encourage tenderness between them, and favor the education of an authentic freedom." ([CCC 2370](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/0583c069-d4bf-42dd-97de-c19f0b80150f/ref/2370))

This teaching is part of the Catholic understanding of the goods of marriage, particularly the procreative and unitive goods, and of the dignity of the marital act.
