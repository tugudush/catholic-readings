# The Sacrament of Penance and Reconciliation

The first of the two sacraments of healing, in which the faithful who have sinned after Baptism obtain **pardon from God's mercy** and are **reconciled with the Church** they have wounded by their sins ([CCC 1422](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/0583c069-d4bf-42dd-97de-c19f0b80150f/ref/1422); [CCC 1423](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/0583c069-d4bf-42dd-97de-c19f0b80150f/ref/1423)). Also called the **sacrament of Confession**, the **sacrament of Forgiveness**, the **sacrament of Conversion**, and the **sacrament of Reconciliation**, this sacrament is the ordinary means by which the faithful who have committed **mortal sin** are restored to grace.

> "Those who approach the sacrament of Penance obtain pardon from God's mercy for the offense committed against him, and are, at the same time, reconciled with the Church which they have wounded by their sins and which by repentance, love, and humility seeks to make amends for the offenses they have committed." ([CCC 1422](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/0583c069-d4bf-42dd-97de-c19f0b80150f/ref/1422); citing _Paenitemini_ §V)

---

## 1. Biblical Foundation

The sacrament of Penance is rooted in the direct institution by Christ of the apostolic power to forgive sins:

- **John 20:21–23** — "Jesus said to them again, 'Peace be with you. As the Father has sent me, so I send you.' When he had said this, he breathed on them and said to them, 'Receive the Holy Spirit. If you forgive the sins of any, they are forgiven them; if you retain the sins of any, they are retained.'"
- **Matthew 18:18** — "Truly I tell you, whatever you bind on earth will be bound in heaven, and whatever you loose on earth will be loosed in heaven."
- **Matthew 16:19** — "I will give you the keys of the kingdom of heaven, and whatever you bind on earth will be bound in heaven, and whatever you loose on earth will be loosed in heaven."
- **Luke 15:11–32** — The parable of the **Prodigal Son**, illustrating the unfailing mercy of the Father and the joy of conversion.
- **Acts 2:38** — "Repent, and be baptized every one of you in the name of Jesus Christ so that your sins may be forgiven."
- **James 5:16** — "Confess your sins to one another, and pray for one another, so that you may be healed."

The constant practice of the early Church (Acts 19:18: "Many of those who believed now came forward openly and confessed their former practices") testifies to the apostolic origin of sacramental confession.

---

## 2. The Matter and Form of the Sacrament

The Council of Trent (Session XIV, 1551) defined the matter and form of the sacrament as the acts of the penitent and the sacramental absolution of the priest. The Catechism arranges these under three headings (the "acts of the penitent") and the priestly absolution:

### 2.1 The Acts of the Penitent (Matter)

The matter of the sacrament comprises the acts of the penitent:

#### a) Contrition (Interior Conversion)

Contrition is "sorrow of the soul and detestation for the sin committed, together with a resolution not to sin again" (Council of Trent, Session XIV, c. 4). The Catechism describes it as **"conversion of heart"** — a return to God with all one's heart, an end of sin, a turning away from evil, and a salutary _compunctio cordis_ ([CCC 1421](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/0583c069-d4bf-42dd-97de-c19f0b80150f/ref/1421); [CCC 1430](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/0583c069-d4bf-42dd-97de-c19f0b80150f/ref/1430)).

**Two kinds of contrition are distinguished:**

- **Perfect contrition** (Latin: _contritio cordis_) arises from a love of God above all things and effects the reconciliation of the sinner with God even before sacramental confession, provided there is a resolution to confess as soon as possible ([CCC 1452](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/0583c069-d4bf-42dd-97de-c19f0b80150f/ref/1452)).
- **Imperfect contrition** (Latin: _attritio_), also called **sorrow of the lower mind**, arises from the consideration of the ugliness of sin or the fear of God's punishment; it is sufficient for the validity of the sacrament when joined with the sacrament ([CCC 1453](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/0583c069-d4bf-42dd-97de-c19f0b80150f/ref/1453)).

##### Perfect Contrition When Sacramental Confession Is Impossible

The Church teaches that **perfect contrition obtains forgiveness of mortal sins even without the actual reception of the sacrament**, provided two conditions are met simultaneously:

1. **The contrition is truly perfect** — it arises from the love of God above all things (_amor Dei super omnia_), not merely from fear of hell or the ugliness of sin ([CCC 1452](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/0583c069-d4bf-42dd-97de-c19f0b80150f/ref/1452)).
2. **It includes the firm resolution (_votum_) to receive sacramental confession as soon as possible** — the desire for the sacrament is contained within perfect contrition itself and is inseparable from it ([Council of Trent, Session XIV, c. 4](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/4a72892b-0a56-4371-a138-ff4aea2b0093/ref/); Denzinger 1677).

This teaching applies precisely when sacramental confession is **morally or physically impossible** — for example, when no priest is available, in danger of sudden death, during persecution, or in any circumstance where the penitent _would_ confess if they could, but cannot. The Baltimore Catechism states the principle plainly: _"Perfect contrition obtains pardon for mortal sin when we cannot go to confession"_ (Baltimore Catechism No. 3, Q. 766).

**How does this not contradict the necessity of the sacrament?** The Council of Trent itself answered this question: the reconciliation effected by perfect contrition is _"not to be attributed to the contrition without the desire of the sacrament which is included in it"_ (Denzinger 1677). In other words, the extraordinary forgiveness does not bypass the sacrament — it already contains the sacrament by desire (_in voto_). The ordinary means established by Christ (sacramental confession to a priest) remains binding; the extraordinary means (perfect contrition with the _votum confessionis_) is the Church's teaching on how God, who is not bound by His sacraments, extends mercy when the ordinary means is inaccessible.

**A pastoral caution**: No one can infallibly self-diagnose perfect contrition. The subjective certainty that one has perfect contrition is not equivalent to the objective certainty conferred by sacramental absolution. This is precisely why the Church **obliges** the faithful to confess all mortal sins in kind and number to a priest whenever possible ([CCC 1456](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/0583c069-d4bf-42dd-97de-c19f0b80150f/ref/1456); [Code of Canon Law, c. 988](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/4a72892b-0a56-4371-a138-ff4aea2b0093/ref/)). The extraordinary means is a safety net of divine mercy, not a substitute for the ordinary means. One who deliberately avoids confession while claiming "perfect contrition" deceives himself; one who genuinely longs for the sacrament but cannot reach it entrusts himself to the God who reads hearts and will not be outdone in mercy.

> "God cannot be bound by the sacraments. Man is bound to the sacraments; God is not." (cf. St. Thomas Aquinas, _Summa Theologiae_ III, q. 64, a. 7; [CCC 1257](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/0583c069-d4bf-42dd-97de-c19f0b80150f/ref/1257) on the analogous principle for Baptism).

#### b) Confession (Disclosure of Sins)

The disclosure or confession of sins to a priest is an essential element of the sacrament ([CCC 1422](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/0583c069-d4bf-42dd-97de-c19f0b80150f/ref/1422)). The Council of Trent defined that, for those who have committed **mortal sin**, confession of each grave sin is required by divine law ([Council of Trent, Session XIV, c. 6](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/4a72892b-0a56-4371-a138-ff4aea2b0093/ref/); [CCC 1456](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/0583c069-d4bf-42dd-97de-c19f0b80150f/ref/1456)). Venial sins may be confessed profitably but are not required.

#### c) Satisfaction (Penance)

The penitent is to perform the satisfaction or penance assigned by the priest. This sacramental penance corresponds to the kind and gravity of the confessed sins and represents a participation in the work of Christ ([CCC 1459](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/0583c069-d4bf-42dd-97de-c19f0b80150f/ref/1459); Council of Trent, Session XIV, c. 8). The temporal punishment due to sin (distinct from the eternal punishment remitted by absolution) is partially remitted here and partially purified after death in Purgatory (the doctrine of the **temporal punishment of sin**, [CCC 1472–1473](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/0583c069-d4bf-42dd-97de-c19f0b80150f/ref/1472)).

### 2.2 The Sacramental Absolution (Form)

The form of the sacrament is the **words of absolution** spoken by the priest:

> **"God, the Father of mercies, through the death and the resurrection of his Son, has reconciled the world to himself and sent the Holy Spirit among us for the forgiveness of sins; through the ministry of the Church may God give you pardon and peace, and I absolve you from your sins in the name of the Father, and of the Son, and of the Holy Spirit."** (Latin Rite, _Order of Penance_, 1973)

In the Eastern Catholic Churches, the form is older and more liturgical, including the priest's prayer of supplication and the explicit invocation of the Holy Spirit; the essential words of absolution declare the forgiveness of sins.

## 3. Minister

The ordinary ministers of the sacrament of Penance are **bishops and priests**, who act _in persona Christi Capitis_ ([CCC 986](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/0583c069-d4bf-42dd-97de-c19f0b80150f/ref/986); [CCC 1461](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/0583c069-d4bf-42dd-97de-c19f0b80150f/ref/1461); [Code of Canon Law, c. 965–986](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/4a72892b-0a56-4371-a138-ff4aea2b0093/ref/)). The priest must have:

- **Ordination** to the priesthood (or episcopate).
- **Jurisdiction** (the lawful faculty to absolve) — ordinarily granted by the diocesan bishop or, for religious, by the religious superior. Without jurisdiction, absolution is ordinarily invalid (except in danger of death, when any priest may absolve from every sin).

> "By Christ's will, the Church possesses the power to forgive the sins of the baptized and exercises it through bishops and priests normally in the sacrament of Penance." ([CCC 986](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/0583c069-d4bf-42dd-97de-c19f0b80150f/ref/986); citing _Lumen Gentium_ 26)

The priest who hears confession is bound by the **seal of confession** (Latin: _sigillum confessionis_), which obliges him under the gravest penalty never to reveal, directly or indirectly, any sin learned in confession ([Code of Canon Law, c. 983–984](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/4a72892b-0a56-4371-a138-ff4aea2b0093/ref/); [Code of Canons of the Eastern Churches, c. 733](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/4a72892b-0a56-4371-a138-ff4aea2b0093/ref/)).

---

## 4. Effects

The sacrament of Penance produces the following effects when received with the proper dispositions:

1. **Reconciliation with God** through the forgiveness of sins, including the remission of eternal punishment due to mortal sin ([CCC 1468](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/0583c069-d4bf-42dd-97de-c19f0b80150f/ref/1468)).
2. **Reconciliation with the Church**: "The sinner wounds the communion of the Church by his sin; the sacrament of Penance heals and restores it" ([CCC 1449](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/0583c069-d4bf-42dd-97de-c19f0b80150f/ref/1449)).
3. **Remission of the eternal punishment** due to mortal sin, and partial remission of the temporal punishment due to sin (Council of Trent, Session XIV, c. 1–2).
4. **Restoration of sanctifying grace** lost by mortal sin, and an increase in grace for venial sins ([CCC 1468–1470](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/0583c069-d4bf-42dd-97de-c19f0b80150f/ref/1468)).
5. **Peace, serenity, and spiritual consolation** ([CCC 1468](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/0583c069-d4bf-42dd-97de-c19f0b80150f/ref/1468)).
6. **Help against future temptations** and the strengthening of the spiritual faculties weakened by sin ([CCC 1468](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/0583c069-d4bf-42dd-97de-c19f0b80150f/ref/1468)).

The Council of Trent defined that the confession of mortal sins is **necessary by divine law** (Latin: _ex iure divino_) for the forgiveness of post-baptismal grave sin, and that this obligation extends to every mortal sin that the penitent can recall after diligent examination of conscience ([Council of Trent, Session XIV, c. 5–7](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/4a72892b-0a56-4371-a138-ff4aea2b0093/ref/)).

## 5. Mortal and Venial Sin

Catholic moral theology distinguishes two kinds of sin:

- **Mortal sin** (Latin: _peccatum mortale_) is sin whose object is **grave matter** and which is committed with **full knowledge** and **deliberate consent**. Mortal sin destroys charity in the heart of man and turns him away from God ([CCC 1855](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/0583c069-d4bf-42dd-97de-c19f0b80150f/ref/1855)).
- **Venial sin** (Latin: _peccatum veniale_) is sin whose object is **less serious matter**, or which is committed without full knowledge or deliberate consent. Venial sin does not break the friendship with God, but it weakens charity and is properly confessed as part of ongoing conversion ([CCC 1855–1863](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/0583c069-d4bf-42dd-97de-c19f0b80150f/ref/1855)).

For those who have committed **mortal sin**, sacramental confession is required before the reception of Holy Communion (Code of Canon Law, c. 916; [CCC 1385](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/0583c069-d4bf-42dd-97de-c19f0b80150f/ref/1385)).

---

## 6. Necessity of the Sacrament

The Council of Trent defined that, although the faithful who have committed grave sin after Baptism can be reconciled by perfect contrition (which, however, implies the desire to confess — see [§2.1.a, Perfect Contrition When Sacramental Confession Is Impossible](#perfect-contrition-when-sacramental-confession-is-impossible)), the **ordinary means** by which the Christian who has fallen into mortal sin is restored to grace is the sacramental confession to a priest ([Council of Trent, Session XIV, c. 6](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/4a72892b-0a56-4371-a138-ff4aea2b0093/ref/)).

> "Without the sacrament of Penance, the sacrament of the Eucharist, the Body and Blood of Christ, would be received unworthily and would be a cause of condemnation. Those who have not confessed their sins in Penance cannot worthily receive the Holy Eucharist unless they have recourse to sacramental confession as soon as possible." (cf. [CCC 1385](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/0583c069-d4bf-42dd-97de-c19f0b80150f/ref/1385); [Council of Trent, Session XIII, c. 7](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/4a72892b-0a56-4371-a138-ff4aea2b0093/ref/))

The faithful are therefore **strongly urged** to confess their sins frequently, especially mortal sins as soon as possible after committing them.

## 7. Forms of Celebration

The Latin Rite recognizes three forms of celebrating the sacrament:

1. **The individual form** — the ordinary form, in which the penitent confesses to a single priest and receives absolution.
2. **The communal form with individual confession and absolution** — used especially during Lent and Advent, in which the community gathers for prayer, readings, and a homily, followed by individual confession and absolution.
3. **The general absolution** (with individual confession when possible) — permitted only in extraordinary circumstances (e.g., war, persecution, danger of death), when there is a grave necessity and no possibility of individual confession ([Code of Canon Law, c. 961](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/4a72892b-0a56-4371-a138-ff4aea2b0093/ref/)).

In the Eastern Catholic Churches, the celebration of the sacrament is frequently preceded by a long preparation (prayers of the _Tipikón_ or _Euchologion_), a meditation on the penitential psalms, and an act of humility before the priest. Absolution is then pronounced with a longer liturgical formula invoking the saints and the Holy Trinity.

---

## 8. Indulgences

A **plenary indulgence** is ordinarily attached to the devout reception of the sacrament of Penance (in addition to the requirements of Confession, Communion, prayer for the Pope's intentions, and freedom from attachment to sin). The indulgence extends also to the souls in Purgatory ([CCC 1471](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/0583c069-d4bf-42dd-97de-c19f0b80150f/ref/1471); see [miracles/README.md](../miracles/README.md) for context).

## 9. Latin Rite vs. Eastern Catholic Tradition

### 9.1 Shared Doctrine

The theology of the sacrament is identical in East and West:

- The same matter (contrition, confession, satisfaction).
- The same form (the priestly absolution in the name of the Holy Trinity).
- The same minister (a bishop or priest with jurisdiction).
- The same effects (forgiveness of mortal sin, reconciliation with God and the Church, restoration of grace).
- The same necessity for those in mortal sin.

### 9.2 Differences in Liturgical Practice

| Aspect                    | Latin Rite                                                                                                                                             | Eastern Catholic Churches                                                                              |
| ------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| **Celebration**           | Often individual and brief; sometimes in the confessional, sometimes face-to-face                                                                      | Often more public and liturgical, especially in monastic and cathedral traditions                      |
| **Formula of absolution** | "I absolve you from your sins in the name of the Father, and of the Son, and of the Holy Spirit." (Roman Missal of Paul VI, _Ordo Paenitentiae_, 1973) | Longer, deprecatory formula invoking the saints and the Holy Trinity; the priest prays and blesses     |
| **Preparation**           | Examination of conscience; Act of Contrition                                                                                                           | Reading of the _Prayer of St. Basil the Great_ and the _Prayer of St. Ephrem the Syrian_; prostrations |
| **Indulgences**           | Plenary indulgence attached to the devout reception of the sacrament                                                                                   | Plenary indulgence also attached                                                                       |

The Eastern traditions sometimes call the sacrament **"Holy Repentance"** (Greek: _Hagia Metanoia_) or **"Confession"** (Greek: _Exomologēsis_).

---

## 10. The Seal of Confession

The **seal of confession** (Latin: _sigillum confessionis_) is absolute: the confessor may never, for any reason, reveal any sin heard in confession, even indirectly. The priest who violates the seal incurs automatic excommunication reserved to the Holy See (Code of Canon Law, c. 1388; [Code of Canons of the Eastern Churches, c. 1458](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/4a72892b-0a56-4371-a138-ff4aea2b0093/ref/)). The seal is binding even after the death of the penitent.

---

## 11. The First Confession and First Communion

In the Latin Rite, children are ordinarily admitted to the sacrament of Penance for the first time before First Communion, around the age of seven. The Eastern Catholic Churches commonly admit children to Confession around the same age of reason.
