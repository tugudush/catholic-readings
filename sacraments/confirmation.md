# The Sacrament of Confirmation (Chrismation)

The second of the three sacraments of Christian initiation. By Confirmation (called **Chrismation** in the Eastern Catholic Churches), the already-baptized are more perfectly bound to the Church, enriched with the special strength of the Holy Spirit, and configured as witnesses of Christ. Like Baptism, it imprints an **indelible character** on the soul and can never be repeated ([CCC 1302–1305](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/0583c069-d4bf-42dd-97de-c19f0b80150f/ref/1302)).

> "Confirmation perfects Baptismal grace; it is the sacrament which gives the Holy Spirit in order to root us more deeply in the divine filiation, incorporate us more firmly into Christ, strengthen our bond with the Church, associate us more closely with her mission, and help us bear witness to the Christian faith in words accompanied by deeds." ([CCC 1316](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/0583c069-d4bf-42dd-97de-c19f0b80150f/ref/1316))

---

## 1. Biblical Foundation

The New Testament reports that the apostles communicated the Holy Spirit to the baptized by the **laying on of hands**, the gesture from which the sacramental sign of Confirmation / Chrismation is historically derived:

- **Acts 8:14–17** — "When the apostles at Jerusalem heard that Samaria had accepted the word of God, they sent Peter and John to them. The two went down and prayed for them that they might receive the Holy Spirit (for as yet the Spirit had not come upon any of them; they had only been baptized in the name of the Lord Jesus). Then Peter and John laid their hands on them, and they received the Holy Spirit."
- **Acts 19:5–6** — "When they heard this, they were baptized in the name of the Lord Jesus. When Paul had laid his hands on them, the Holy Spirit came upon them, and they spoke in tongues and prophesied."
- **Acts 10:44–48** — At the house of Cornelius, the Holy Spirit "fell upon all those who heard the word" before their Baptism, anticipating the sacramental logic of the rite.
- **Hebrews 6:2** — Among the elementary doctrines of Christ, the author lists "instruction about baptisms, the laying on of hands, the resurrection of the dead, and eternal judgment."
- **2 Corinthians 1:21–22** — "God ... has put his seal upon us and given us his Spirit in our hearts as a guarantee."

To make this outpouring of the Spirit more visibly and permanently present, the Church added the **anointing with chrism** to the apostolic laying on of hands. This ancient practice remains in use in both the East and the West (Pope Francis, _General Audience of 30 May 2018_).

---

## 2. Matter and Form

### 2.1 Matter

The matter is **chrism**, a perfumed oil consecrated by a bishop.

- Chrism is composed of **olive oil** and **balsam** (aromatic resin), blessed and consecrated by the bishop on **Holy Thursday** of Holy Week, traditionally in the **Chrism Mass** (Latin: _Missa Chrismatis_).
- The chrism used in the Latin Rite must be consecrated by a bishop, even when a priest administers the sacrament (Code of Canon Law, c. 880).
- In the Eastern Catholic Churches the consecrated oil is called **myron** (Greek: μύρον) or _chrismation_, with a richer ritual of preparation.

In the Latin Rite, Confirmation is conferred by **anointing the forehead** of the candidate with chrism, together with the **imposition of the hand** and the words from the approved liturgical books (Code of Canon Law, c. 880).

In the Eastern Catholic Churches, the **post-baptismal anointing with myron** of various parts of the body (forehead, eyes, nostrils, lips, ears, breast, hands, feet, in the Byzantine rite) constitutes the visible matter.

### 2.2 Form

- **Latin Rite**: The priest (or bishop) traces the sign of the cross with chrism on the forehead of the candidate and pronounces the words: **"Be sealed with the Gift of the Holy Spirit."** The bishop extends his hands over those to be confirmed and invokes the outpouring of the Spirit (the Prayer of Consecration / _Epiclesis_).
- **Eastern Catholic (Byzantine) Rite**: The priest anoints with myron and pronounces: **"The seal of the gift of the Holy Spirit."** The newly baptized (or their godparents in the case of infants) immediately respond: **"Amen."** ([_Catechism of the Ukrainian Greek Catholic Church_, §429](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/00a1de35-98f1-481a-956f-e98e2b52331d/ref/))

### 2.3 Intention

For Confirmation to be valid, the minister must intend to do what the Church does, must use chrism consecrated by a bishop, and must pronounce the essential sacramental words.

## 3. Minister

### 3.1 Latin Rite

In the **Latin Church**, the **ordinary minister** of Confirmation is the **bishop** (Code of Canon Law, c. 882; [CCC 1313](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/0583c069-d4bf-42dd-97de-c19f0b80150f/ref/1313)). This emphasizes the link between Confirmation and the apostolic mission entrusted to the college of bishops in communion with the Bishop of Rome. For grave reasons, the bishop may grant the faculty to priests, who in fact ordinarily confer Confirmation today in many parishes.

### 3.2 Eastern Catholic Churches

In the **Eastern Catholic Churches**, Confirmation (Chrismation) is ordinarily administered by the **presbyter (priest)** who celebrates the Baptism, using chrism consecrated by the patriarch or bishop (Code of Canons of the Eastern Churches, c. 694; [CCC 1313](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/0583c069-d4bf-42dd-97de-c19f0b80150f/ref/1313)). John Paul II explained the Eastern emphasis:

> "In the East, Chrismation is given by 'the same priest who administers Baptism,' though the anointing uses chrism consecrated by the Bishop." (Pope John Paul II, _General Audience of 30 September 1998_, §4)

The chrism is therefore always linked to the bishop, even when administered by a presbyter, preserving the ecclesial and apostolic nature of the sacrament.

---

## 4. Recipient

In the Latin Rite, Confirmation is ordinarily conferred on the faithful who have already received Baptism and, ordinarily, First Communion, and who are properly disposed and instructed (Code of Canon Law, c. 889–891). The Catholic Church recognizes the Confirmation of the Eastern Orthodox Churches as valid when conferred by a priest using chrism consecrated by a bishop with the proper matter and form; such Orthodox Christians may be received into the Catholic Church without re-Confirmation.

In the Eastern Catholic Churches, Chrismation is administered **immediately after Baptism**, even in infancy. The two sacraments together with first Holy Communion form a single rite of Christian initiation ([CCC 1242](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/0583c069-d4bf-42dd-97de-c19f0b80150f/ref/1242)).

---

## 5. Effects

Confirmation perfects baptismal grace. Its principal effects are:

1. **Deepening of baptismal grace and divine filiation**: Confirmation roots the confirmed more deeply in the divine filiation.
2. **Strengthening by the Holy Spirit** to be a courageous witness to Christ, both in word and in deed.
3. **More perfect incorporation into the Mystical Body of Christ**, namely the Church; more strictly obliged to spread and defend the faith by word and deed ([Order of Confirmation, Promontory Rite](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/00a1de35-98f1-481a-956f-e98e2b52331d/ref/)).
4. **Indelible character** — a permanent seal, like Baptism and Holy Orders, that configures the confirmed permanently to Christ and to his Church and that renders the sacrament unrepeatable ([Council of Trent, _De Confirm._](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/4a72892b-0a56-4371-a138-ff4aea2b0093/ref/); [CCC 1304](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/0583c069-d4bf-42dd-97de-c19f0b80150f/ref/1304)).
5. **Increase of the seven gifts of the Holy Spirit**: wisdom, understanding, counsel, fortitude, knowledge, piety, and fear of the Lord ([Isaiah 11:2–3](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/00a1de35-98f1-481a-956f-e98e2b52331d/ref/)).

> "It is evident from its celebration that the effect of the sacrament of Confirmation is the special outpouring of the Holy Spirit as was granted to the apostles on the day of Pentecost." (_Catechism of the Ukrainian Greek Catholic Church: Christ – Our Pascha_, §426)

Pope Francis describes Confirmation as the gift of an **"indelible spiritual imprint"** — the character that no sin can erase (Pope Francis, _General Audience of 30 May 2018_).

---

## 6. Sponsor

In the Latin Rite, the sponsor (godparent) for Confirmation should ordinarily be one of the baptismal godparents; the same conditions apply as for baptismal sponsors (Code of Canon Law, c. 892). In the Eastern tradition, the baptismal sponsor (Greek: _anadektēs_) typically also serves as the sponsor for Chrismation.

---

## 7. Latin Rite vs. Eastern Catholic Tradition

| Aspect | Latin Rite | Eastern Catholic Churches |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Name** | Confirmation | Chrismation (_Myron_) |
| **Ordinary Minister** | Bishop (priests may be delegated) | Priest (the one who baptizes) |
| **Matter** | Chrism on the forehead | Myron anointed on multiple parts of the body |
| **When administered** | Ordinarily at the age of discretion (13–16 in many dioceses) | Immediately after Baptism, including in infancy |
| **Initiation pattern** | Baptism, then later Confirmation and First Communion | Baptism, Chrismation, and First Communion as a single rite |
| **Form** | "Be sealed with the Gift of the Holy Spirit." | "The seal of the gift of the Holy Spirit." |
| **Consecration of chrism** | By the bishop at the Chrism Mass on Holy Thursday | By the patriarch or bishop in a more elaborate ritual |

The **theological content is identical**: the same sacrament, the same outpouring of the Holy Spirit, the same indelible character, the same mission of witness. The differences are in **discipline, liturgical form, and ministerial practice**, not in dogma.

---

## 8. Preparation

The Latin Rite traditionally requires the candidates to be in a state of grace, properly instructed in the mysteries of faith and the duties of Christian witness, and prepared through prayer, retreats, and the reception of the sacrament of Penance if necessary. The sacrament is ordinarily conferred during the Easter Season or, in some dioceses, at Pentecost, emphasizing the link to the outpouring of the Spirit at the first Pentecost.

---

## 9. Eastern Catholic Names and Concelebration

In the Byzantine tradition, the sacrament is called **Holy Chrismation** or simply **Chrismation**. The candidate (or the godparent for infants) recites the **Apostles' Creed** and the **Our Father** as part of the rite, and the priest binds the head of the newly sealed with a cord and a ribbon, reminiscent of the gifts of healing and protection.
