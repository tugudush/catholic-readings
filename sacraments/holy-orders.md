# The Sacrament of Holy Orders

The sacrament by which the mission entrusted by Christ to his apostles continues to be exercised in the Church until the end of time, in three degrees: the **episcopate** (bishop), the **presbyterate** (priest), and the **diaconate** (deacon) ([CCC 1535](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/0583c069-d4bf-42dd-97de-c19f0b80150f/ref/1535); [CCC 1576](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/0583c069-d4bf-42dd-97de-c19f0b80150f/ref/1576)).

> "Holy Orders is the sacrament through which the mission entrusted by Christ to his apostles continues to be exercised in the Church until the end of time. It is the sacrament of apostolic ministry." ([CCC 1535](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/0583c069-d4bf-42dd-97de-c19f0b80150f/ref/1535))

Holy Orders is the **third sacrament of the three that imprint an indelible character** on the soul (the other two being Baptism and Confirmation). For this reason, it can never be repeated.

---

## 1. Biblical Foundation

The sacrament is rooted in the institution by Christ of the apostolic ministry and in the practice of the early Church:

- **Matthew 16:18–19** — "And I tell you, you are Peter, and on this rock I will build my church, and the gates of Hades will not prevail against it. I will give you the keys of the kingdom of heaven, and whatever you bind on earth will be bound in heaven, and whatever you loose on earth will be loosed in heaven."
- **Luke 22:14–20** — Christ institutes the Eucharist at the Last Supper, ordaining the apostles as priests of the New Covenant.
- **John 20:21–23** — "As the Father has sent me, so I send you."
- **John 21:15–17** — Christ entrusts the care of his flock to Peter.
- **Acts 1:15–26** — The apostles fill the vacancy left by Judas to preserve the apostolic college.
- **Acts 6:1–6** — The institution of the first deacons.
- **Acts 14:23** — Paul and Barnabas "appointed elders for them in each church, with prayer and fasting, and entrusted them to the Lord in whom they had put their trust."
- **1 Timothy 4:14** — "Do not neglect the gift that is in you, which was given to you through prophecy with the laying on of hands by the council of elders."
- **2 Timothy 1:6** — "For this reason I remind you to rekindle the gift of God that is within you through the laying on of my hands."
- **Titus 1:5** — "I left you behind in Crete for this reason, that you should put in order what remained to be done, and should appoint elders in every town, as I directed you."
- **1 Clement (c. 96 AD)** — Pope St. Clement of Rome witnesses to the apostolic succession in the early Church: "Our apostles knew through our Lord Jesus Christ that there would be strife for the title of bishop. For this reason, having received perfect foreknowledge, they appointed those who have been mentioned, and afterwards added the codicil that, if they should die, other approved men should succeed to their ministry."

The apostolic succession is therefore understood as the continuity of episcopal ordination from the apostles to the bishops of every age.

---

## 2. The Three Degrees

The sacrament of Holy Orders is conferred in three degrees, all of which are conferred by the bishop acting as the ordinary minister:

### 2.1 Episcopate

The episcopate is the fullness of the sacrament of Holy Orders. A bishop is a successor of the apostles and a member of the **college of bishops**, which is the successor of the college of apostles (see [CCC 1557–1558](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/0583c069-d4bf-42dd-97de-c19f0b80150f/ref/1557); [Lumen Gentium 19–22](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/4a72892b-0a56-4371-a138-ff4aea2b0093/ref/)). The bishop possesses the **triple munera** of:

- **Teaching** (_munus docendi_): to preach the Gospel faithfully and to guard the deposit of faith.
- **Sanctifying** (_munus sanctificandi_): to administer the sacraments, especially Confirmation and Holy Orders.
- **Governing** (_munus regendi_): to govern the local church entrusted to him in communion with the Bishop of Rome.

The bishop is the visible principle and foundation of unity in his particular church (diocese), and a sign of the unity of all the faithful in the one Body of Christ.

### 2.2 Presbyterate

The priesthood (Latin: _presbyteratus_, from Greek: πρεσβυτέρα, "elder") is the second degree of Holy Orders, in which the priest is configured to Christ the High Priest and shares in the bishop's triple ministry in a subordinate role. The priest is an "alter Christus" (another Christ) who, in the person of Christ the Head, offers the Eucharistic sacrifice, forgives sins, and teaches the faithful ([CCC 1142](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/0583c069-d4bf-42dd-97de-c19f0b80150f/ref/1142); [CCC 1562](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/0583c069-d4bf-42dd-97de-c19f0b80150f/ref/1562)).

### 2.3 Diaconate

The diaconate is the first degree of Holy Orders. There are two kinds:

- **Transitional deacons** are ordained deacons in preparation for ordination to the priesthood.
- **Permanent deacons** are ordained deacons who do not intend to proceed to the priesthood. In the Latin Rite, permanent deacons may be married men (Latin: _diaconus permanens_, restored as a permanent state of life by Vatican II, see [Lumen Gentium 29](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/4a72892b-0a56-4371-a138-ff4aea2b0093/ref/)).

Deacons assist the bishop and priests in the liturgy (proclaiming the Gospel, preaching, distributing Holy Communion, assisting at the altar), in pastoral work, and in charity (e.g., ministry to the poor and the sick).

## 3. Matter and Form

The essential rite of Holy Orders is the **imposition of hands by the bishop** on the head of the ordinand, together with the **consecratory prayer** invoking the Holy Spirit and asking for the outpouring of the gifts proper to the ministry being conferred ([CCC 1573](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/0583c069-d4bf-42dd-97de-c19f0b80150f/ref/1573)).

In the Latin Rite, additional visible signs accompany the imposition of hands:

- For the **priesthood**: the imposition of hands, the prayer of consecration, the handing over of the **chalice and paten** with the bread and wine, the **anointing of the hands** with chrism, and the fraternal kiss of peace.
- For the **diaconate**: the imposition of hands, the prayer of consecration, and the handing over of the **Book of the Gospels**.
- For the **bishop**: the imposition of hands by the consecrating bishop (assisted by two other bishops), the prayer of consecration, the anointing of the head, the handing over of the **Book of the Gospels**, the ring, the mitre, and the crosier.

The Eastern Catholic Churches have analogous rites, frequently with their own liturgical customs (e.g., the presentation of the deacon with the epitrachelion, the stole).

---

## 4. Minister

The **ordinary minister** of episcopal ordination is a **bishop**. The Second Vatican Council (see [Lumen Gentium 21](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/4a72892b-0a56-4371-a138-ff4aea2b0093/ref/)) defined that episcopal consecration is reserved to bishops, who are the successors of the apostles, and that the consecration is performed by the **consecrating bishop** assisted by at least **two other bishops**.

The ordination of priests and deacons is reserved to the bishop, who ordains by virtue of his own episcopal consecration ([CCC 1600](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/0583c069-d4bf-42dd-97de-c19f0b80150f/ref/1600)). A priest cannot validly ordain another priest or a deacon.

---

## 5. Effects

The sacrament of Holy Orders produces the following effects:

1. **Indelible character** — A permanent seal that configures the ordained minister to Christ the High Priest, and that renders the sacrament unrepeatable ([CCC 1597](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/0583c069-d4bf-42dd-97de-c19f0b80150f/ref/1597)).
2. **Sacramental configuration** — The ordained minister is configured to act **in _persona Christi Capitis_** (in the person of Christ the Head), particularly in the celebration of the Eucharist, the forgiveness of sins, and the preaching of the Gospel ([CCC 1142](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/0583c069-d4bf-42dd-97de-c19f0b80150f/ref/1142); [CCC 1535](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/0583c069-d4bf-42dd-97de-c19f0b80150f/ref/1535)).
3. **Outpouring of the Holy Spirit** — The consecration asks for the special gifts of the Holy Spirit suited to the ministry.
4. **Mission** — The ordained receives the canonical mission from the competent authority (bishop, pope) to exercise the ministry in the Church ([CCC 1581](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/0583c069-d4bf-42dd-97de-c19f0b80150f/ref/1581)).
5. **Office** — The ordained is entrusted with a permanent office within the Church, which he is called to exercise in charity and holiness.

Lumen Gentium adds that episcopal consecration, more than the other degrees, confers the **fullness of the sacrament of Orders** and is the highest degree of participation in Christ's priestly office ([Lumen Gentium 21](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/4a72892b-0a56-4371-a138-ff4aea2b0093/ref/)).

## 6. Apostolic Succession

Apostolic succession is the theological principle by which the **gift of the Spirit** transmitted by the apostles through the imposition of hands is preserved and handed on in the Church through the **episcopal consecration** of bishops ([CCC 1576](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/0583c069-d4bf-42dd-97de-c19f0b80150f/ref/1576)). Lumen Gentium explains:

> "Christ, whom the Father sanctified and sent into the world to sanctify, has, through his apostles, made their successors, the bishops, partakers of his own consecration and mission; these bishops have in turn handed on to their assistants, the priests, what in varying degrees they themselves have received, so that they may be appointed in the Church and serve the People of God." ([Lumen Gentium 28](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/4a72892b-0a56-4371-a138-ff4aea2b0093/ref/))

The continuity of the apostolic ministry from the apostles to the bishops of every age is therefore a constitutive element of the Church.

## 7. Celibacy: Latin Rite and Eastern Catholic Tradition

### 7.1 Latin Rite Discipline

In the **Latin Church**, the ordinary discipline is that of **clerical celibacy**: candidates for the priesthood are admitted only if they are celibate and willing to live a life of perpetual continence for the Kingdom of heaven ([Code of Canon Law, c. 277](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/4a72892b-0a56-4371-a138-ff4aea2b0093/ref/); [CCC 1579](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/0583c069-d4bf-42dd-97de-c19f0b80150f/ref/1579)). Permanent deacons, however, may be married men (and are ordinarily ordained only with the consent of their wives).

The discipline of celibacy is not a dogma but a Latin Church discipline, intended to configure the ordained minister more fully to Christ and to free him for pastoral work. The Council of Trent defined that **marriage after ordination is invalid** for those ordained in the Latin Church ([Council of Trent, Session XXIV, c. 9](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/4a72892b-0a56-4371-a138-ff4aea2b0093/ref/)).

### 7.2 Eastern Catholic Tradition

In the **Eastern Catholic Churches**, the discipline is different:

- **Married men** may be ordained to the **diaconate** and **presbyterate**. Married candidates are admitted with the consent of their wives, and their wives participate in the ordination rite.
- **Bishops**, however, must be chosen **solely from among celibate men** (Latin Rite discipline is followed in this regard in the Eastern Catholic Churches, with very rare exceptions granted by the Holy See) ([CCC 1580](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/0583c069-d4bf-42dd-97de-c19f0b80150f/ref/1580); [Code of Canons of the Eastern Churches, c. 373](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/4a72892b-0a56-4371-a138-ff4aea2b0093/ref/)).

The two disciplines are not contradictory; they are alternative legitimate disciplines, each proceeding from ancient tradition and confirmed by the Holy See. A man who has received Holy Orders cannot subsequently marry ([CCC 1580](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/0583c069-d4bf-42dd-97de-c19f0b80150f/ref/1580)).

### 7.3 The Common Requirement of Bishops

In both traditions, **bishops are chosen solely from among celibate men**. This is a discipline that the Catholic Church, both East and West, has preserved for many centuries.

## 8. Formation

The Latin Rite ordinarily requires candidates for the priesthood to complete a period of formation that includes:

- A period of **human formation** (cultivating human and Christian virtues).
- A period of **spiritual formation** (cultivating the spiritual life, prayer, discernment).
- A period of **intellectual formation** in philosophy and theology (ordinarily at least six years in a seminary, leading to a graduate degree).
- A period of **pastoral formation** (practical ministry in parishes, hospitals, schools).

The Eastern Catholic Churches maintain their own formation programs, frequently in connection with monastic formation.

---

## 9. Latin Rite vs. Eastern Catholic Tradition: Summary

| Aspect | Latin Rite | Eastern Catholic Churches |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Name of the sacrament** | Holy Orders | Holy Orders (_Teletourgia_) |
| **Degrees** | Episcopate, Presbyterate, Diaconate (including permanent) | Episcopate, Presbyterate, Diaconate (including permanent) |
| **Celibacy** | Required for priests and bishops; permanent deacons may be married | Married men may be ordained to the diaconate and presbyterate; bishops chosen only from among celibate men |
| **Consecrating minister** | A bishop, assisted by two other bishops (for episcopal ordination) | A bishop, assisted by two other bishops (for episcopal ordination) |
| **Theological teaching** | Identical | Identical |
| **Apostolic succession** | Through the line of bishops | Through the line of bishops |

The theological content — apostolic succession, indelible character, _in persona Christi Capitis_ — is identical in East and West.

## 10. The Priesthood of the Faithful

It is important to distinguish the **common priesthood of the faithful** (Latin: _sacerdotium commune fidelium_), which all the baptized share by their incorporation into Christ, and the **ministerial priesthood** (Latin: _sacerdotium ministeriale_), which is conferred by Holy Orders.

> "The ministerial priesthood is at the service of the common priesthood. It is directed at the unfolding of the baptismal grace of all Christians. The ministerial priesthood is a means by which Christ unceasingly builds up and leads his Church. For this reason it is transmitted by its own sacrament, namely by Holy Orders." ([CCC 1547](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/0583c069-d4bf-42dd-97de-c19f0b80150f/ref/1547))

The priest is therefore called to act for the people, not in their place, but in the name of Christ the Head.
