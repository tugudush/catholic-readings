# The Sacrament of the Most Holy Eucharist

The Most Holy Eucharist is the third sacrament of Christian initiation, and the **"source and summit of the whole Christian life"** ([Lumen Gentium 11](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/4a72892b-0a56-4371-a138-ff4aea2b0093/ref/); [CCC 1324](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/0583c069-d4bf-42dd-97de-c19f0b80150f/ref/1324)). It is at once a **sacrament** (the Real Presence of Christ given to the faithful) and a **sacrifice** (the sacramental memorial of the sacrifice of the Cross, perpetuated on the altar). The other sacraments and all the works of the Church are bound up with, and oriented toward, the Eucharist.

> "The Eucharist is 'the source and summit of the Christian life.' The other sacraments, and indeed all ecclesiastical ministries and works of the apostolate, are bound up with the Eucharist and are oriented toward it. For in the blessed Eucharist is contained the whole spiritual good of the Church, namely Christ himself, our Pasch." ([CCC 1324](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/0583c069-d4bf-42dd-97de-c19f0b80150f/ref/1324); citing _Presbyterorum Ordinis_ 5)

---

## 1. Biblical Foundation

The Eucharist was instituted by Jesus Christ at the **Last Supper**, on the night before his Passion. Four New Testament accounts are normative:

- **Matthew 26:26–29** — "While they were eating, Jesus took a loaf of bread, and after blessing it he broke it, gave it to the disciples, and said, 'Take, eat; this is my body.' Then he took a cup, and after giving thanks he gave it to them, saying, 'Drink from it, all of you. For this is my blood of the covenant, which is poured out for many for the forgiveness of sins.'"
- **Mark 14:22–25** — Parallel account.
- **Luke 22:14–20** — Parallel account, including the command: "Do this in remembrance of me."
- **1 Corinthians 11:23–26** — St. Paul's account, received from the Lord: "For I received from the Lord what I also handed on to you, that the Lord Jesus on the night when he was betrayed took a loaf of bread, and when he had given thanks, he broke it and said, 'This is my body that is for you. Do this in remembrance of me.' In the same way he took the cup also, after supper, saying, 'This cup is the new covenant in my blood. Do this, as often as you drink it, in remembrance of me.' For as often as you eat this bread and drink this cup, you proclaim the Lord's death until he comes."

Additional New Testament texts illuminate the early Christian celebration: [Acts 2:42](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/00a1de35-98f1-481a-956f-e98e2b52331d/ref/) ("They devoted themselves to the apostles' teaching and fellowship, to the breaking of bread and the prayers"); [Acts 20:7](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/00a1de35-98f1-481a-956f-e98e2b52331d/ref/) ("On the first day of the week, when we met to break bread"); [John 6:51–58](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/00a1de35-98f1-481a-956f-e98e2b52331d/ref/) ("I am the living bread that came down from heaven. Whoever eats of this bread will live forever; and the bread that I will give for the life of the world is my flesh").

## 2. The Eucharist as a Sacrament: the Real Presence

The Catholic Church teaches that in the Most Holy Eucharist, the **Body and Blood, together with the Soul and Divinity, of our Lord Jesus Christ — that is, the whole Christ — are truly, really, and substantially contained**, under the appearances of bread and wine ([CCC 1374](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/0583c069-d4bf-42dd-97de-c19f0b80150f/ref/1374); citing [Council of Trent, Session XIII, c. 1, c. 3](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/4a72892b-0a56-4371-a138-ff4aea2b0093/ref/)).

This doctrine was solemnly defined at the **Fourth Lateran Council (1215)** and at the **Council of Trent (1551, Session XIII)**, against the errors of certain medieval reformers. Trent uses the classical term **transubstantiation** to name the change that takes place at the consecration: the whole substance of bread is converted into Christ's Body, and the whole substance of wine into his Blood, while the appearances (species) of bread and wine remain ([CCC 1376](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/0583c069-d4bf-42dd-97de-c19f0b80150f/ref/1376); [Council of Trent, Session XIII, c. 4](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/4a72892b-0a56-4371-a138-ff4aea2b0093/ref/)).

> "By the consecration of the bread and wine there takes place a change of the whole substance of the bread into the substance of the body of Christ our Lord, and of the whole substance of the wine into the substance of his blood. This change the holy Catholic Church has fittingly and properly called transubstantiation." (Council of Trent, Session XIII, c. 4)

The Real Presence is **substantial** rather than local or spatial, so that Christ is wholly and entirely present under each of the species, under every part of each species, and as long as the species remain ([CCC 1377](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/0583c069-d4bf-42dd-97de-c19f0b80150f/ref/1377); [Council of Trent, Session XIII, c. 3](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/4a72892b-0a56-4371-a138-ff4aea2b0093/ref/)).

### 2.1 The Words of Consecration

In the Latin Rite, the priest pronounces the words of institution over the bread and wine: **"Take this, all of you, and eat of it: this is my Body which will be given up for you"; "Take this, all of you, and drink from it: this is the cup of my Blood, the Blood of the New and Eternal Covenant, which will be poured out for you and for many for the forgiveness of sins. Do this in memory of me."** The Eastern liturgies of the Catholic communion preserve the anaphoral prayers of the early Church (the _Anaphora of St. John Chrysostom_, the _Anaphora of St. Basil the Great_, the _Liturgy of St. James_, and others), all of which include the epiclesis (invocation of the Holy Spirit) and the words of institution.

## 3. The Eucharist as a Sacrifice: the Mass

The Eucharist is a true sacrifice because it is the **sacramental memorial** of Christ's Passover, the same sacrifice of the Cross made present on the altar under the appearances of bread and wine ([CCC 1365](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/0583c069-d4bf-42dd-97de-c19f0b80150f/ref/1365)). The Mass is not a new sacrifice, nor a repetition of the Cross, but the **perpetuation** of the one sacrifice of Christ throughout the ages until he comes again ([Council of Trent, Session XXII](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/4a72892b-0a56-4371-a138-ff4aea2b0093/ref/)).

> "At the Last Supper Christ instituted the Eucharistic sacrifice of his Body and Blood. He did this in order to perpetuate the sacrifice of the Cross throughout the ages until he should come again, and so to entrust to his beloved spouse, the Church, a memorial of his death and resurrection: a sacrament of love, a sign of unity, a bond of charity, a Paschal banquet in which Christ is eaten, the mind is filled with grace, and a pledge of future glory is given to us." ([CCC 1323](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/0583c069-d4bf-42dd-97de-c19f0b80150f/ref/1323); _Sacramentum Concilium_ §47)

The Mass is "inseparably" both:

- the **sacrificial memorial** in which the sacrifice of the Cross is perpetuated; and
- the **sacred banquet** of communion with Christ's Body and Blood ([CCC 1382](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/0583c069-d4bf-42dd-97de-c19f0b80150f/ref/1382)).

### 3.1 The Offertory and the Consecration

In the Latin Rite, the Mass comprises:

1. The **Liturgy of the Word**, with readings from Sacred Scripture and the homily.
2. The **Liturgy of the Eucharist**, beginning with the presentation of the gifts (bread and wine), the Eucharistic Prayer (with the Preface, the Sanctus, the epiclesis, the institution narrative, the anamnesis, the offering, and the doxology), the breaking of the bread (_fractio panis_), and Holy Communion.

In the Eastern Divine Liturgy, the corresponding structure is the **Liturgy of the Catechumens** (Word) followed by the **Liturgy of the Faithful** (Eucharist), culminating in the epiclesis and the reception of Holy Communion.

---

## 4. Minister

The ordinary minister of the Eucharist, that is, the minister who **consecrates** the bread and wine, is a **bishop or a priest** acting _in persona Christi Capitis_, in the person of Christ the Head ([CCC 1142](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/0583c069-d4bf-42dd-97de-c19f0b80150f/ref/1142); [Code of Canon Law, c. 900](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/4a72892b-0a56-4371-a138-ff4aea2b0093/ref/)). Deacons and lay persons are not ordinary ministers of the consecration but may distribute Holy Communion, in the Latin Rite, as extraordinary ministers when warranted by necessity.

The celebration of the Eucharist is the principal act of the bishop's ministry, and priests share in it in communion with their bishop ([CCC 1142](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/0583c069-d4bf-42dd-97de-c19f0b80150f/ref/1142)).

## 5. Effects of Holy Communion

Holy Communion produces a manifold union of the communicant with Christ and with his Church:

1. **Intimate union with Christ**: "To receive Communion is to receive Christ himself who has offered himself for us" ([CCC 1382](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/0583c069-d4bf-42dd-97de-c19f0b80150f/ref/1382); [CCC 1391](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/0583c069-d4bf-42dd-97de-c19f0b80150f/ref/1391)).
2. **Increase of sanctifying grace** and the conferral of actual graces suited to the communicant's needs.
3. **Remission of venial sins** and preservation from grave sin (Council of Trent, Session XIII, c. 2).
4. **Strengthening of charity**, of unity with the Mystical Body, and of the Christian's mission in the world.
5. **Pledge of future glory** — the Eucharist is a foretaste of the heavenly banquet, an _anticipatio resurrectionis_ and an _anticipatio gloriae_ ([CCC 1419](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/0583c069-d4bf-42dd-97de-c19f0b80150f/ref/1419)).

> "Communion with the Body and Blood of Christ increases the communicant's union with the Lord, forgives his venial sins, and preserves him from grave sins. Since receiving this sacrament strengthens the bonds of charity between the communicant and Christ, it also reinforces the unity of the Church as the Mystical Body." ([CCC 1416](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/0583c069-d4bf-42dd-97de-c19f0b80150f/ref/1416))

## 6. The Eucharistic Presence Outside of Mass

The Catholic Church teaches that the worship of the Eucharist is offered not only during the celebration of Mass, but also outside of it. The faithful adore the reserved sacrament by:

- **Reservation of the consecrated hosts** in the **tabernacle**, ordinarily on the altar of repose in the church (Code of Canon Law, c. 938).
- **Exposition of the Blessed Sacrament** (solemn exposition, eucharistic adoration, Benediction of the Blessed Sacrament).
- **Eucharistic processions**, particularly on the Solemnity of Corpus Christi.
- **The Eucharistic Congresses** of the local Church.

> "The Catholic Church has always offered and still offers to the sacrament of the Eucharist the cult of adoration, not only during Mass, but also outside of it, by reserving the consecrated hosts with the utmost care, by exposing them to the solemn veneration of the faithful, and by carrying them in procession." ([CCC 1378](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/0583c069-d4bf-42dd-97de-c19f0b80150f/ref/1378); citing _Mysterium Fidei_)

This cult of adoration is founded on the doctrine of the Real Presence: Christ, whole and entire, is present under each consecrated host as long as the species remain.

## 7. Requirements for the Reception of Holy Communion

The Catholic Church teaches that to receive Holy Communion worthily, the communicant must be:

- **Baptized** in the name of the Father and of the Son and of the Holy Spirit.
- **In a state of grace** — that is, not conscious of mortal sin unconfessed. Anyone conscious of grave sin must first receive the sacrament of [Penance and Reconciliation](penance-reconciliation.md) before receiving Holy Communion ([CCC 1385](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/0583c069-d4bf-42dd-97de-c19f0b80150f/ref/1385); [Code of Canon Law, c. 916](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/4a72892b-0a56-4371-a138-ff4aea2b0093/ref/)).
- **Observing the Eucharistic fast** (in the Latin Rite, ordinarily one hour before receiving Holy Communion for food and drink, except water; the sick are dispensed — Code of Canon Law, c. 919).
- **In communion with the Catholic Church**. Catholics may not receive Communion in non-Catholic ecclesial communities; non-Catholics who wish to receive in a Catholic celebration ordinarily are not admitted, except for the Eastern Orthodox who are believed to possess true sacraments and the apostolic succession, and members of other churches whose eucharistic theology and practice is recognized as valid by competent authority.

## 8. Latin Rite vs. Eastern Catholic Tradition

### 8.1 Shared Doctrine

In both rites, the same Catholic doctrine of the Real Presence, transubstantiation, the sacrificial nature of the Mass, and the sacrificial priesthood of the New Covenant is professed in identical faith.

### 8.2 Differences in Liturgical Practice

| Aspect | Latin Rite | Eastern Catholic Churches |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Principal liturgy** | The Roman Missal (Novus Ordo of 1969/2002; the Tridentine Mass of 1962 as _Forma Extraordinaria_) | The Byzantine Divine Liturgy of St. John Chrysostom or of St. Basil the Great; other liturgies (Antiochian, Alexandrian, Armenian, Chaldean, Maronite) |
| **Leavened bread** | Unleavened bread (Latin: _azyma_) | Leavened bread (Greek: _artos_; the Eastern tradition holds that leaven signifies the divine nature assumed by Christ) |
| **Communion of the faithful** | Communion under one kind (Host) for the laity; under both kinds (Host and Chalice) ordinarily only for the celebrant, or for the faithful on certain occasions | Communion ordinarily under both kinds (Body and Blood together) even for the laity, administered by intinction (Host dipped in the Blood) or by a spoon |
| **Infant Communion** | First Communion ordinarily at the age of reason (~7 years) | First Communion in infancy, immediately after Chrismation |
| **The epiclesis** | Before the words of institution (in the Roman Canon) and after them (in the 1969 Missal); the precise theological debate is still open | Invoked after the words of institution, in the explicit invocation of the Holy Spirit to transform the gifts |
| **Communion fast** | One hour (food and drink); water does not break the fast | The Eastern discipline varies; in many traditions, no fasting before the Divine Liturgy is required for the faithful on ordinary days, although preparation includes prayer and fasting from the previous midnight in the strict observance |
| **Adoration / reservation** | Tabernacle, exposition, processions | Reservation in the artophorion (tabernacle); exposition occurs in some traditions, but the public adoration of the Blessed Sacrament is less prominent than in the Latin Rite |

The use of unleavened or leavened bread is a matter of **discipline, not dogma**; the substance of the sacrament is identical in both rites.

### 8.3 Communion under One Kind in the Latin Rite

The Latin Rite ordinarily distributes Holy Communion to the faithful **under one kind** (the consecrated Host), although Communion under both kinds (Host and Chalice) is permitted and encouraged on certain occasions (Code of Canon Law, c. 925). The Council of Trent defined that the whole Christ is received under either species alone, and that Communion under one kind is sufficient for the reception of the full sacramental grace ([Council of Trent, Session XXI, c. 1–3](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/4a72892b-0a56-4371-a138-ff4aea2b0093/ref/)).

## 9. The Eucharist and the Other Sacraments

The Catechism teaches that:

- All the other sacraments are ordered toward the Eucharist as their end ([CCC 1324](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/0583c069-d4bf-42dd-97de-c19f0b80150f/ref/1324)).
- The Eucharist is the perfect complement of Christian initiation; together with Baptism and Confirmation it constitutes the "sacraments of Christian initiation."
- Penance and Reconciliation prepares the way for the worthy reception of Holy Communion.
- The Anointing of the Sick is intimately connected with the final Eucharist, which is given to the dying as **Viaticum** (literally, "food for the journey").
- Holy Orders exists for the celebration of the Eucharist.
- Matrimony is ordered to the Christian upbringing of children, the domestic church, and the building up of the Mystical Body, of which the Eucharist is the supreme sacramental sign.

## 10. Approved Eucharistic Miracles

The Church has investigated and approved several historical miracles in which the accidents of bread and wine appear to have been supernaturally transformed into the accidents of flesh and blood. These miracles are not the basis of the doctrine of the Real Presence — which rests on the words of Christ, the Tradition of the apostles, and the constant faith of the Church — but they are held to confirm the faith already received. Two of the most extensively investigated are documented in this repository:

- [miracles/eucharistic-miracles/lanciano.md](../miracles/eucharistic-miracles/lanciano.md) — the 8th-century miracle at Lanciano, Italy, where the host became flesh and the wine became blood.
- [miracles/eucharistic-miracles/bolsena-orvieto.md](../miracles/eucharistic-miracles/bolsena-orvieto.md) — the 13th-century miracle at Bolsena, which prompted Pope Urban IV to establish the Solemnity of Corpus Christi.
