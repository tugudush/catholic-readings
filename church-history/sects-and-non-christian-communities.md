# Sects, Churches & Non-Christian Communities: The Catholic Church's Classification

This document presents the Catholic Church's theological and pastoral classification of all Christian and para-Christian communities outside the Catholic Church, arranged in **three tiers** descending from full ecclesial reality to non-Christian groups. The Church distinguishes between:

- **True particular Churches separated from Rome** — communities with valid apostolic succession, valid Eucharist, and valid Holy Orders, which are true Churches in the theological sense, though not in full communion with the Pope (e.g., the Eastern Orthodox Churches, the Oriental Orthodox Churches, the Assyrian Church of the East, the Polish National Catholic Church).
- **Ecclesial Communities** — post-Reformation communities that, while containing elements of sanctification and truth, cannot be called "Churches in the proper sense" due to the lack of apostolic succession in Holy Orders ([CDF, _Responses to Some Questions_](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/3b25a70e-73ed-44d0-85cd-a4b93e408f29/ref/35), 2007).
- **Non-Christian religious groups** — groups that lack essential elements of Christian faith (especially Trinitarian doctrine and valid baptism) and are often described by the Magisterium as sects or new religious movements.

---

## 1. Magisterial Vocabulary: Why Not "Cult"?

The Catholic Church does **not** use the English word "cult" in its official documents as a formal classification for religious groups. The preferred terminology, found in Vatican documents, is:

| Term                       | Magisterial Usage                                                                                                                                                            | Source                                                                                  |
| -------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Sect**                   | Groups that fall outside ecumenical dialogue, often hostile to the Catholic Church, and that "confuse the faithful regarding fundamental truths of the faith"                | Pope John Paul II, _Ad Limina Address to the Bishops of the Philippines_ (1990)         |
| **New Religious Movement** | A broader category that includes both Christian-derived and non-Christian groups; "the most noticeable development has been the growth of sects and new religious movements" | Pontifical Council for Promoting Christian Unity, _Directory for Ecumenism_ (1993), §35 |
| **Non-Christian**          | Groups whose baptism is invalid and whose doctrine departs from the essentials of Christian faith (Trinity, divinity of Christ)                                              | CDF, _Response on LDS Baptism_ (2001)                                                   |

The 1986 joint report of four Roman Curia dicasteries drew "the vital distinction that must be made between sects and new religious movements on the one hand and Churches and ecclesial Communities on the other" ([Directory for Ecumenism](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/3b25a70e-73ed-44d0-85cd-a4b93e408f29/ref/35), §35).

---

## 2. The Criteria for Ecclesial Recognition

A community is recognized as a **Church in the proper sense** when it possesses:

1. **Apostolic succession** in the sacrament of Holy Orders (valid episcopal ordination traceable to the Apostles).
2. **The genuine and integral substance of the Eucharistic Mystery** (a valid Eucharist celebrated by a validly ordained priest).
3. **Trinitarian faith** as expressed in valid baptism (water + Trinitarian formula + proper intention).

Communities that lack the first two elements are called **Ecclesial Communities** (the category applied to most Protestant denominations). Communities that lack the third — having invalid baptism — are **not** considered Christian communities in any proper sense for sacramental and juridical purposes.

## 3. Groups Whose Baptism Is Declared Invalid

The following groups' baptisms have been formally judged **invalid** by the Holy See or its designated authorities. For Catholic sacramental and canonical purposes, their members are treated as **unbaptized**.

### 3.1 The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints (Mormons / LDS)

**Status:** Baptism declared invalid (2001).

On June 5, 2001, the Congregation for the Doctrine of the Faith, under Prefect Cardinal Joseph Ratzinger, issued a formal response to a _dubium_ regarding the validity of LDS baptism:

> **Response:** Negative. The Supreme Pontiff John Paul II approved this response and ordered its publication. ([_Answer to the Doubt on the Validity of Baptism in the LDS Church_](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/b5b506c4-df4e-40e8-9cd1-b69d8f5806b9/ref/), AAS 93, 2001)

The accompanying theological explanation states that LDS baptism "differs essentially" from Catholic baptism both:

- "in the faith in the Father, Son, and Holy Spirit in whose name Baptism is conferred," and
- "in the relationship to Christ who instituted Baptism." ([CDF, _The Question of the Validity of Baptism in the LDS Church_](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/3004439d-2c7c-44ba-af61-1487f5fd9cf3/ref/))

**Pastoral note:** The CDF explicitly clarified that "the decision … obviously does not indicate a judgment on those who are members of" the LDS Church as persons. However, for Catholic sacramental and matrimonial purposes, LDS converts must receive baptism _in forma absoluta_ (i.e., unconditionally).

### 3.2 Jehovah's Witnesses

**Status:** Baptism treated as invalid in canonical jurisprudence.

The canonical norms for the dissolution of marriage _in favorem fidei_ (the Pauline Privilege and Petrine Privilege) list Jehovah's Witnesses among the communities for which decisions of baptismal invalidity exist ([Kowal, _The Norms for the Dissolution of Marriage in Favorem Fidei_, 2004](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/9da0cd77-4cc5-4df2-a88f-e9bd869a5d86/ref/10), p. 31).

The theological basis: Jehovah's Witnesses deny the doctrine of the Trinity and the divinity of Jesus Christ, holding that Christ is a created being (the Archangel Michael). Their baptismal formula and understanding therefore do not correspond to the Trinitarian faith required for valid Christian baptism (cf. CCC 1284; CCC 260).

### 3.3 Oneness Pentecostals (Jesus Only / Apostolic Pentecostals)

**Status:** Baptism invalid by extension of the CDF's Trinitarian-formula rulings.

While no group-specific CDF ruling on "Oneness Pentecostals" as a named category exists in published sources, the CDF has ruled that baptism conferred with formulas that substitute or depart from the Trinitarian naming of Father, Son, and Holy Spirit is **invalid** ([CDF, _Responses to Questions on the Validity of Baptism_](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/16df7bcf-0d3f-4ee4-8494-90d4570a1b76/ref/), 2008). Since Oneness Pentecostals baptize using the formula "in the name of Jesus" (Acts 2:38) and reject the doctrine of the Trinity, their baptism is generally regarded as invalid.

### 3.4 Iglesia ni Cristo (Church of Christ)

**Status:** Baptism generally regarded as invalid (non-Trinitarian).

The Iglesia ni Cristo (INC), founded in 1914 by Felix Manalo in the Philippines, denies the divinity of Jesus Christ and rejects the doctrine of the Trinity. It holds that Jesus is a man created by God, and that the Holy Spirit is not a divine Person but the power of God. Because INC's baptismal theology lacks the Trinitarian faith essential to valid Christian baptism (CCC 1284; CCC 260), the Catholic Church generally regards INC baptism as invalid.

Pope John Paul II, addressing the Bishops of the Philippines (1990), spoke of:

> "the effects of proselytizing efforts by numerous sects and fundamentalist religious groups [that] confuse the faithful regarding fundamental truths of the faith and present a false interpretation of Scripture." ([_Ad Limina Address_](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/7d6b7594-8b6e-4843-8a66-9e2a5a5e4ab9/ref/6), April 24, 1990)

While the Pope did not name INC specifically, the Philippine bishops have consistently treated INC in this category.

### 3.5 Unitarian and Universalist Unitarian Churches

**Status:** Baptism generally regarded as invalid due to denial of the Trinity.

The _Catholic Encyclopedia_ observes that Unitarians "deny the necessity of baptism," so "the presumption is that they do not administer it accurately" ([Catholic Encyclopedia, "Baptism"](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/f2c979a8-871d-4f46-a059-320d3b837a76/ref/Sect%20and%20Sects)). Since Unitarian communities reject the Trinity (holding that God is one Person, not three Persons) and deny the divinity of Christ, their baptismal formula and intention cannot correspond to what the Catholic Church requires for valid Christian baptism.

## 4. True Particular Churches Separated from Rome (Tier 1)

The CDF, in its 1992 letter _Communionis Notio_ (§17), teaches:

> "Among the non-Catholic Churches and Christian communities, there are indeed to be found many elements of the Church of Christ… This communion exists especially with the Eastern orthodox Churches, which, though separated from the See of Peter, remain united to the Catholic Church by means of very close bonds, such as the apostolic succession and a valid Eucharist, and therefore **merit the title of particular Churches**."

The CDF's _Responses to Some Questions_ (2007), ratified by Pope Benedict XVI, confirms the same criterion: communities that possess **apostolic succession** and a **valid Eucharist** are true particular Churches, even if separated. Those that lack apostolic succession "cannot, according to Catholic doctrine, be called 'Churches' in the proper sense."

### 4.1 The Eastern Orthodox Churches

The Eastern Orthodox communion consists of **14 autocephalous Churches** (plus several autonomous churches under them), all sharing the Byzantine liturgical tradition and mutual sacramental recognition:

| Church                                               | Ancient See         |
| ---------------------------------------------------- | ------------------- |
| Ecumenical Patriarchate of Constantinople            | Istanbul (New Rome) |
| Patriarchate of Alexandria                           | Alexandria          |
| Patriarchate of Antioch                              | Damascus            |
| Patriarchate of Jerusalem                            | Jerusalem           |
| Patriarchate of Moscow                               | Moscow              |
| Patriarchate of Serbia                               | Belgrade            |
| Patriarchate of Romania                              | Bucharest           |
| Patriarchate of Bulgaria                             | Sofia               |
| Patriarchate of Georgia                              | Tbilisi             |
| Autocephalous Church of Cyprus                       | Nicosia             |
| Autocephalous Church of Greece                       | Athens              |
| Autocephalous Church of Poland                       | Warsaw              |
| Autocephalous Church of Albania                      | Tirana              |
| Autocephalous Church of the Czech Lands and Slovakia | Prague              |

**Autonomous churches** (under various patriarchates) include the Church of Finland, the Church of Sinai, the Church of Ukraine, the Church of Mount Sinai, and others.

#### Similarities to the Catholic Church

Eastern Orthodox share with Catholics:

- **The seven sacraments** (called "mysteries"): Baptism, Chrismation (Confirmation), Eucharist, Penance, Holy Orders, Matrimony, Anointing of the Sick — all valid in Catholic eyes
- **Apostolic succession** — their bishops trace their ordination to the Apostles; the Catholic Church recognizes their orders as fully valid
- **Valid Eucharist** — the true Body and Blood of Christ, though celebrated with leavened bread and a different liturgical rite (the Divine Liturgy of St. John Chrysostom)
- **Veneration of the Blessed Virgin Mary as Theotokos** ("God-bearer"), her perpetual virginity, and her intercession
- **Veneration of saints and icons** (defined at the Seventh Ecumenical Council, Nicaea II, 787)
- **Prayers for the dead** and belief in an intermediate state after death
- **Monasticism**, the same ecumenical councils (the first seven), and the Niceno-Constantinopolitan Creed

#### Key Differences from the Catholic Church

| Doctrine                          | Catholic Position                                                                                  | Eastern Orthodox Position                                                                                                                        |
| --------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| **Papal primacy**                 | The Pope has supreme, universal, immediate jurisdiction over the whole Church (CCC 937; Vatican I) | The Pope is the "first among equals" — a primacy of honor, not of jurisdiction; each autocephalous Church is self-governing                      |
| **Papal infallibility**           | The Pope is infallible when defining dogma _ex cathedra_ (Vatican I)                               | Infallibility resides in the whole Church and ecumenical councils, not in one person                                                             |
| **Filioque**                      | The Holy Spirit proceeds "from the Father **and the Son**" (added to the Creed in the West)        | The Holy Spirit proceeds "from the Father **alone**" (or "from the Father through the Son") — the original Creed without _Filioque_              |
| **Immaculate Conception**         | Defined dogma (1854): Mary was conceived without original sin                                      | Reject the defined dogma, though they honor Mary as "all-holy" (_Panagia_) and cleansed at the Annunciation                                      |
| **Assumption / Dormition**        | Defined dogma (1950): Mary was assumed body and soul into heavenly glory                           | Celebrate the _Dormition_ ("falling asleep") — she died a natural death, was resurrected, and taken to heaven; they reject the term "Assumption" |
| **Purgatory**                     | Defined as a state of purification after death (Lyons II, Florence, Trent)                         | Reject the juridical term "purgatory" but pray for the dead and believe in an intermediate state of purification                                 |
| **Original sin**                  | Inherited guilt from Adam (Trent)                                                                  | "Ancestral sin" — we inherit the _consequences_ (mortality, inclination to sin), not the guilt                                                   |
| **Leavened vs. unleavened bread** | Unleavened bread (Latin Rite)                                                                      | Leavened bread (Byzantine Rite)                                                                                                                  |
| **Eucharistic consecration**      | Consecration occurs at the words of institution                                                    | Consecration also requires the _epiclesis_ (invocation of the Holy Spirit) after the words of institution                                        |

### 4.2 The Oriental Orthodox Churches

The Oriental Orthodox Churches (also called "non-Chalcedonian" or "miaphysite" Churches) accepted only the first three ecumenical councils (Nicaea I, Constantinople I, Ephesus) and rejected the Council of Chalcedon (451). They are **seven Churches**:

| Church                                                   | Tradition   | Members (approx.) |
| -------------------------------------------------------- | ----------- | ----------------- |
| Coptic Orthodox Church of Alexandria                     | Alexandrian | ~10 million       |
| Syrian Orthodox Patriarchate of Antioch                  | Antiochene  | ~1.5 million      |
| Armenian Apostolic Church (Catholicosate of Etchmiadzin) | Armenian    | ~6 million        |
| Armenian Apostolic Church (Catholicosate of Cilicia)     | Armenian    | ~1 million        |
| Malankar Orthodox Syrian Church                          | Indian      | ~2.5 million      |
| Ethiopian Orthodox Tewahedo Church                       | Alexandrian | ~36 million       |
| Eritrean Orthodox Tewahedo Church                        | Alexandrian | ~2 million        |

Like the Eastern Orthodox, they possess valid apostolic succession, valid sacraments, and are recognized as true particular Churches by the Catholic Church. Their Christology — once thought to be "monophysite" — has been largely reconciled through joint declarations (e.g., Pope Paul VI and Pope Shenouda III, 1973).

### 4.3 The Assyrian Church of the East

The Assyrian Church of the East (sometimes called "Nestorian") accepted only the first two ecumenical councils. It is recognized as a true particular Church with valid apostolic succession and a valid Eucharist. In 2001, the Pontifical Council for Promoting Christian Unity issued _Guidelines for Admission to the Eucharist Between the Chaldean Church and the Assyrian Church of the East_, recognizing the validity of the Assyrian Eucharist.

A related body, the **Ancient Church of the East** (separated from the Assyrian Church in 1964), is generally regarded as sharing the same sacramental reality.

### 4.4 The Polish National Catholic Church (PNCC)

The PNCC separated from the Roman Catholic Church in the early 20th century over issues of lay involvement and episcopal authority. It retains valid apostolic succession through Old Catholic lines. The Holy See has formally judged it to be a true particular Church under the _Dominus Iesus_ criterion.

### 4.5 The Old Catholic Churches of the Union of Utrecht

The Union of Utrecht comprises **six national Churches** that separated from Rome after the First Vatican Council (1870) over the definition of papal infallibility. They retained valid apostolic succession through the See of Utrecht. The six Churches are:

1. Old Catholic Church of the Netherlands
2. Catholic Diocese of the Old Catholics in Germany
3. Christian Catholic Church of Switzerland
4. Old Catholic Church of Austria
5. Old Catholic Church of the Czech Republic
6. Polish Catholic Church in Poland

**Note:** The Catholic Church recognizes their orders as valid, but their sacramental discipline has diverged in some areas (e.g., women's ordination in some provinces, same-sex blessings), which creates new obstacles to full communion.

## 5. Groups with Valid Baptism but Not "Churches in the Proper Sense" (Tier 2)

The Second Vatican Council and subsequent Magisterium teach that communities born from the Protestant Reformation:

> "cannot, according to Catholic doctrine, be called 'Churches' in the proper sense" ([CDF, _Responses to Some Questions_](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/3b25a70e-73ed-44d0-85cd-a4b93e408f29/ref/36), 2007, ratified by Pope Benedict XVI).

The reason: these communities "do not enjoy apostolic succession in the sacrament of Orders" and are therefore "deprived of a constitutive element of the Church." Consequently, they "have not preserved the genuine and integral substance of the Eucharistic Mystery."

**However**, the Church recognizes that valid baptism is conferred in most mainstream Protestant communities (Lutheran, Anglican, Presbyterian, Methodist, Baptist, etc.), since they use water and the Trinitarian formula with the requisite intention. Such persons are validly baptized Christians, even though their communities are not called "Churches" in the fullest theological sense.

The _Catechism of the Catholic Church_ (CCC 818–819) teaches that "many elements of sanctification and truth are found outside its visible confines," including the written Word of God, the life of grace, faith, hope, and charity, and other interior gifts of the Holy Spirit.

### 5.1 Seventh-day Adventists

**Status:** Christian ecclesial community. Baptism generally treated as valid.

The Seventh-day Adventist Church (formed from the Millerite movement of the 1840s, formally organized in 1863) is a Protestant Christian denomination that holds to Trinitarian orthodoxy. Unlike the groups treated in §3 above, Seventh-day Adventists:

- **Affirm the Trinity** (Father, Son, and Holy Spirit are the one God)
- **Affirm the divinity of Jesus Christ** (the eternal Son of God, true God and true man)
- **Baptize by immersion** using the Trinitarian formula: "in the name of the Father, and of the Son, and of the Holy Spirit"
- **Intend to do what the Church does** in baptism

Because the essential elements of valid baptism (water, Trinitarian form, and proper intention) are present, Adventist baptism is **generally treated as valid** in Catholic practice. Converts from Seventh-day Adventism are therefore **not** routinely rebaptized unless a specific doubt arises about the particular administration of the sacrament (cf. CDF, _The Question of the Validity of Baptism in the LDS Church_, for the general principle; CCC 818).

**However**, as with all Protestant ecclesial communities, the Seventh-day Adventist Church:

- Lacks **apostolic succession** in Holy Orders (its ministers are not ordained by bishops in apostolic succession)
- Therefore does **not** possess a valid Eucharist in the Catholic sense
- Is **not** called a "Church" in the proper theological sense

Adventists are **regarded as Christians** — members of a separated ecclesial community. The Second Vatican Council teaches that those who are "truly baptized" are "in communion with the Catholic Church even though this communion is imperfect" and are "correctly accepted as brothers" (_Unitatis Redintegratio_, 3).

**Distinctive doctrines that differ from Catholicism:**

- Observance of Saturday (the seventh-day Sabbath) as the day of worship
- Belief in the "investigative judgment" (begun in 1844) and a particular emphasis on the sanctuary doctrine
- Belief in the conditional immortality of the soul and annihilation of the wicked (rather than eternal torment)
- Rejection of the natural immortality of the soul
- Emphasis on the writings of Ellen G. White as a prophetic voice (though not considered canonical Scripture)

These doctrinal differences do **not** render Adventist baptism invalid, since the essential Trinitarian formula and intention remain intact. The _Catechism_ (CCC 818) recognizes that separated Christians "have preserved … faith in God the Father and in Jesus Christ" and "have received Baptism which unites them to Christ."

## 6. The Category of "Sects" in Magisterial Teaching

The Congregation for the Evangelization of Peoples, in its _Guide for Catechists_ (1993), provides a thorough pastoral analysis of sects:

> "The rapid spread of sects of both Christian and non-Christian origin presents a pastoral challenge for the Church throughout the world today. In mission territories they are a serious obstacle to the preaching of the gospel and the orderly growth of the young Churches, because they damage the integrity of faith and communion." ([_Guide for Catechists_](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/9a6a7b0b-bd40-4cd1-9ef8-d3238c3daf37/ref/16), §16)

The guide notes that sects typically:

- Present "apparently simple and immediate answers to the felt needs of the people"
- Are "intolerant and are particularly hostile to Catholicism"
- Often make "constructive dialogue … not possible"

The recommended pastoral response is **not** a "positive campaign against them" but rather "a renewal of mission itself," with catechists giving "positive instruction and encouraging the Christian community to greater fervour."

## 7. The Philippine Independent Church (Iglesia Filipina Independiente / Aglipayan)

The IFI, discussed fully in [philippine-independent-church-schism.md](philippine-independent-church-schism.md), occupies a unique position:

- **Baptism:** Valid (the IFI retained the Trinitarian formula and water).
- **Holy Orders:** Invalid (the first IFI bishops were consecrated by presbyters in 1903, lacking apostolic succession; subsequent attempts to remedy this through Old Catholic and Anglican lines remain theologically disputed from a Catholic perspective).
- **Eucharist:** Not a valid Eucharist, since valid Holy Orders are required.

Thus the IFI is not a "Church" in the Catholic sense, but it is **not** classed as a non-Christian sect either. Its members are validly baptized Christians, though their sacramental life lacks full ecclesial reality.

---

## 8. Freemasonry

Freemasonry is **not** a Christian denomination and is **not** classed alongside the groups above. The Dicastery for the Doctrine of the Faith (formerly CDF) reaffirmed in 2023 that the Catholic faithful **may not join** Masonic associations, citing the "irreconcilability between Catholic doctrine and Freemasonry" ([Dicastery for the Doctrine of the Faith, _Request of the Bishop of Dumaguete_](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/1ea71ba7-c06d-4fb4-9442-01f1165d8670/ref/), November 13, 2023). Masonic lodges are not religious communities in the Christian sense but initiatic societies with a deistic or indifferentist worldview.
