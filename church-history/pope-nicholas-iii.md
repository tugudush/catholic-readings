# Pope Nicholas III: Giovanni Gaetano Orsini (1277–1280)

> **Note:** This document draws on the Catholic Encyclopedia, Magisterium AI sources, and the text of the bull _Exiit qui seminat_. Citations are provided where available; some historical details rest on the received scholarly tradition.

---

## 1. Introduction

**Pope Nicholas III** (Latin: _Nicolaus III_; born **Giovanni Gaetano Orsini**; c. 1216 – 22 August 1280) was the **188th Bishop of Rome** and sovereign of the Papal States, serving from **25 November 1277** until his death on **22 August 1280**. Despite a pontificate of less than three years, Nicholas III left a lasting mark on the institutional Church: he issued the landmark Franciscan constitution _Exiit qui seminat_ (1279), established the papal residence permanently at the Vatican Palace, asserted papal authority against imperial and Angevin influence in central Italy, and advanced the Orsini family through extensive nepotism — a policy that earned him a place among the simoniacs in Dante's _Inferno_.

The table below provides a quick reference for his pontificate:

## 2. Early Life: Giovanni Gaetano Orsini

### Birth and Family

Giovanni Gaetano Orsini was born in **Rome** around **c. 1216** into the illustrious **Orsini** family, one of the most powerful noble houses of medieval Rome. His father was **Matteo Rosso** (also known as Matteo Rosso Orsini), a Roman senator who defended the city against the forces of Emperor **Frederick II** and preserved Rome for the papacy during the turbulent struggles between the Guelphs and Ghibellines. His mother was **Perna Gaetana**, from the noble **Gaetani** (Caetani) family, which would later produce Pope Boniface VIII (1294–1303). [^2]

Matteo Rosso Orsini had a personal friendship with **St. Francis of Assisi** and is said to have been a member of the saint's "Third Order" (the Secular Franciscans). This familial connection to the Poverello predisposed Giovanni — both as cardinal and as pope — toward a special solicitude for the Franciscan Order. [^2]

### Education and Early Career

The **Catholic Encyclopedia** candidly records that there is **"no knowledge of his education and early life"** beyond his noble lineage. [^2] Given his family's station, he would have received the standard clerical formation available to Roman noble youth in the thirteenth century, but no specific teachers, schools, or writings from his youth survive.

## 3. Cardinalate (1244–1277)

### Creation as Cardinal-Deacon

On **28 May 1244**, Pope **Innocent IV** created Giovanni Gaetano Orsini a **cardinal-deacon** with the titular church of **St. Nicholas in Carcere Tulliano**. He also received benefices at **York**, **Laon**, and **Soissons** — a common practice for curial cardinals of the period. [^2]

### Exile at Lyons

In the summer of 1244, Innocent IV was forced to flee Rome before the advancing forces of Emperor Frederick II. Cardinal Orsini was among the party that departed from **Cività Vecchia**, traveled to **Genoa**, and then proceeded to **Lyons**, where the Pope convoked the **First Council of Lyons (1245)**. This experience of papal dependence on French soil likely shaped Orsini's later determination to strengthen papal independence in Italy. [^2]

### Diplomatic Missions

The Catholic Encyclopedia records several missions that demonstrate Orsini's standing:

- **1252**: He was sent on an **unsuccessful peace mission** to the warring **Guelphs and Ghibellines of Florence** — a conflict that had torn the city apart since the 1216 murder of Buondelmonte dei Buondelmonti. [^2]
- **1258**: King **Louis IX of France** (St. Louis) is said to have praised Orsini's **impartiality** so highly that he proposed him as an equally acceptable choice for the solemn ratification of peace between **England and France**. [^2]
- His integrity as a cardinal is underscored by the note that he **"never accepted gifts for his services"** — a detail that makes his later nepotism as pope all the more striking. [^2]

### Influence in Papal Elections

Cardinal Orsini's influence in the Sacred College was considerable:

- His intervention was mainly responsible for the election of **Pope Urban IV (1261)**. [^2]
- Under Urban IV, he was appointed **general inquisitor (1262)** and **protector of the Franciscans (1263)** — the latter a role that foreshadowed his deep engagement with Franciscan theology as pope. [^2]
- Under **Pope Clement IV (1265–1268)**, he was part of a delegation of four cardinals who, on **28 June 1265**, invested **Charles of Anjou** with the **Kingdom of Naples** — a decision whose consequences he would later try to limit as pope. [^2]
- He played a prominent part at the elections of **Blessed Gregory X** (receiving the tiara "at his hands") and **John XXI**; he then served as John XXI's **counsellor** and was named **archpriest of St. Peter's Basilica**. [^2]

## 4. Papal Election (1277)

Nicholas III was elected pope after the death of **John XXI**, who had died on 20 May 1277 when the ceiling of his study at Viterbo collapsed upon him. The ensuing vacancy lasted **six months** — a period of intense political maneuvering among the cardinals, who were divided between French and Italian factions, and between Roman noble families. [^2]

The conclave met at **Viterbo**, and on **25 November 1277**, Cardinal Giovanni Gaetano Orsini was elected. He took the name **Nicholas III** — the third pope to bear that name after Nicholas I (858–867) and Nicholas II (1058–1061). His election was widely seen as a victory for the Roman noble faction (the Orsini) over the Angevin influence of Charles of Naples. [^2] [^1]

## 5. Pontificate (25 November 1277 – 22 August 1280)

### 5.1 Strengthening Papal Authority in Rome and Central Italy

Nicholas III's central political goal was to **free Rome and the Papal States from foreign domination** — particularly from the influence of **Charles of Anjou**, King of Sicily and Naples, who had become a dominant force in Italian politics after the defeat of the Hohenstaufen.

#### The Constitution of 18 July 1278

In a decisive assertion of papal independence, Nicholas III issued a constitution on **18 July 1278** that ordained:

> "…that thenceforward the **senatorial power and all municipal offices were to be reserved to Roman citizens** to the exclusion of **emperor, king, or other potentate**…" [^2]

This measure effectively barred Charles of Anjou (who had held the office of Roman Senator) and any other foreign ruler from holding civic power in Rome, restoring control of the city's governance to its native nobility — and, through them, to the papacy. [^2]

#### The Romagna and Rudolf of Habsburg

Nicholas III also moved to secure papal control over the **Romagna** (the northeastern region of the Papal States). In **1278**, he obtained from **Rudolf of Habsburg**, King of the Romans (the first Habsburg to hold that title), a formal **renunciation of imperial rights in the Romagna**. This renunciation was later confirmed by the imperial princes. [^2]

The Pope then took possession of the province through his nephew **Latino Malabranca Orsini**, whom he had raised to the cardinalate on **12 March 1278**. A second nephew, **Berthold Orsini**, was created **Count of the Romagna**. [^2]

#### Limiting Charles of Anjou

Nicholas III compelled Charles of Anjou (1278) to resign his roles as **regency of Tuscany** and the dignity of **Roman Senator**. This rollback of Angevin power was one of the chief diplomatic achievements of the pontificate, though it earned the Pope the lasting enmity of Charles and his faction. [^2]

### 5.2 The Bull _Exiit qui seminat_ (14 August 1279)

The most theologically significant act of Nicholas III's pontificate was the issuance of the apostolic constitution **_Exiit qui seminat_** ("He who went out to sow") on **14 August 1279**. This document remains **"still fundamental for the interpretation of the Rule of St. Francis"** and approving a "stricter observance of poverty." [^2] [^11]

#### Historical Context: The Franciscan Poverty Controversy

After the death of St. Francis of Assisi (1226), the Franciscan Order became divided over the interpretation of the vow of poverty. One party — the **Spiritual Franciscans** — insisted on the strictest possible observance, holding that Christ and the Apostles owned no property either individually or in common. A second party — the **Conventuals** — argued for a more moderate interpretation, allowing the corporate ownership of goods necessary for the Order's ministry. The controversy had already produced papal interventions: Gregory IX's _Quo elongati_ (1230) and Innocent IV's _Ordinem vestrum_ (1245), but the underlying tensions remained unresolved. [^11]

#### Content of the Bull

_Exiit qui seminat_ took its title from the opening words of the Gospel parable of the Sower (Matthew 13:3). The bull addressed the question of **dominion (ownership) versus use** of goods by the friars. Its key provisions included:

1. **Distinction between Dominion and Use**: The bull taught that the friars could have the **lawful use** of goods (food, clothing, shelter, books, churches) without claiming **dominion (property ownership)** over them. Dominion was retained by the **Roman Church** (the papacy) as the owner, while the friars were mere users. [^11]

2. **Moderate Use**: A "moderate use" of temporal goods was "conceded to the friars" according to their rule, as necessary for life and ministry. [^11]

3. **Temporary Usufruct**: Because civil and political law shows that "use or usufruct cannot be separated from perpetual dominion," the constitution framed the friars' use as **temporary**, with the owner (the Holy See) retaining full dominion "for eternity" — a structure that allowed the friars to live in poverty while having lawful access to necessities. [^11]

4. **Rejection of Absolute Poverty**: The bull rejected the extreme Spiritual Franciscan position that the friars could own nothing at all, even in usufruct, and that absolute poverty in all things was the only authentic Franciscan life. [^11]

#### Aftermath and Historical Significance

_Exiit qui seminat_ was the most authoritative papal pronouncement on Franciscan poverty until the early fourteenth century, when Pope John XXII's _Quia nonnumquam_ (1322) and _Cum inter nonnullos_ (1323) would revisit — and in significant ways reverse — its framework. Dante's placement of Nicholas III among the simoniacs in _Inferno_ must be understood alongside the fact that Dante himself took a position in the Franciscan poverty controversy that aligned more closely with the Spiritual Franciscans than with the papal constitution. [^3]

### 5.3 The Vatican Palace: Establishing the Papal Residence

A major institutional change traditionally attributed to Nicholas III is the establishment of the **papal residence at the Vatican Palace**. While earlier popes had occasionally occupied buildings near Old St. Peter's Basilica, the principal papal residence had been the **Lateran Palace** since the time of Constantine the Great.

Nicholas III **remodelled and enlarged the existing structures** on the Vatican Hill and **secured landed property in the neighborhood**, which later became the **Vatican Gardens**. The Catholic Encyclopedia records that he "established the papal residence there" — a move that shifted the center of papal governance from the Lateran to the Vatican, a process that would culminate in the construction of the current Apostolic Palace and the Vatican's definitive role as the seat of the papacy after the Avignon period. [^2]

Nicholas III also built the **Chapel of St. Nicholas** in St. Peter's Basilica, where he would later be buried. [^2]

### 5.4 Missionary Work and Relations with the East

In **1278**, at the request of **Abaga, Khan of the Tatars** (the Mongol Ilkhan of Persia), Nicholas III dispatched **five Franciscan missionaries** intended first for **Persia** and then for **China**. This missionary outreach to the Mongol court was part of a broader papal strategy to explore the possibility of a Mongol alliance against the Muslims in the Holy Land — and, secondarily, to evangelize the Mongol rulers. The long-term results of this mission would become more evident under his successor, Nicholas IV, who sent the Franciscan **John of Montecorvino** to China. [^2]

Nicholas III also pursued the organization of a **Crusade** to recover the Holy Land, but European political conflicts — particularly the rivalry between Charles of Anjou and the Byzantine Empire — frustrated these plans. [^2]

### 5.5 Heresy and the Cathars

The "Brief History of the Popes" summary records that Nicholas III "addressed the issue of heresy" and took steps to combat the **Cathar** movement in southern France. The Cathars (also known as Albigensians) were a dualist heretical sect that rejected the material world as evil, denied the Incarnation, and repudiated the sacraments and the hierarchy of the Church. The papal inquisition against the Cathars, initiated in the reign of Gregory IX, continued under Nicholas III's oversight. [^1]

### 5.6 Orsini Nepotism

The most controversial aspect of Nicholas III's pontificate — and the one for which Dante condemned him — was his **nepotism**. The Catholic Encyclopedia, while otherwise praising his character as "unblemished," records specific instances: [^2]

1. When Nicholas took possession of the **Romagna** (after Rudolf of Habsburg's renunciation), he did so "through his nephew, **Latino**," whom he had "shortly before (12 March 1278) raised to the cardinalate." Latino Malabranca Orsini became one of the most influential cardinals of the period. [^2]

2. He created another nephew, **Berthold Orsini**, as **Count of the Romagna** — a secular title that gave the Orsini family direct territorial control over a key papal province. [^2]

3. "On other occasions" he "remembered his relatives in the distribution of honourable and lucrative places." [^2]

This pattern of advancing family interests was not unique among medieval popes — the earlier Innocent III and the later Sixtus IV and Alexander VI were far more egregious in this regard — but Nicholas III's nepotism was notable for its concentration on territorial and political power, not merely on benefices. The Orsini family emerged from his pontificate as the dominant Roman noble house, a position they would retain for centuries. [^2] [^7]

## 6. Dante's Condemnation: _Inferno_, Canto XIX

### The Simoniacs in Hell

In **Canto XIX** of the _Inferno_, Dante Alighieri places Pope Nicholas III among the **simoniacs** — those who bought or sold spiritual things (simony) — in the **Third Bolgia (Ditch) of the Eighth Circle of Hell**, reserved for those who committed fraud. The punishment of the simoniacs is a ghastly inversion of their earthly sin: they are buried head-first in holes, with flames burning the soles of their feet, much like the punishment of an inverted baptism. [^3]

Dante encounters Nicholas III in **Inferno XIX, lines 31–87**. The Pope mistakes Dante at first for **Pope Boniface VIII**, whose arrival (in 1303) he has been expecting — a powerful literary device that allows Dante to condemn two popes at once. When Dante corrects him, Nicholas III identifies himself: [^3]

> "I was the **son of the She-Bear** [Orsini], so eager to advance the cubs [nephews] that I put riches up above the purse above."  
> — _Inferno_ XIX, lines 70–72 (paraphrased)

### Context of Dante's Accusation

Dante's condemnation of Nicholas III must be understood within the broader polemical purpose of the _Divine Comedy_. The **Catholic Encyclopedia** notes that Dante's work includes a "vehement denunciation of the ecclesiastical corruption of his times" and that he "condemns most of the contemporary popes … to hell." [^3]

The specific accusation against Nicholas III is **simony** — the abuse of ecclesiastical office for material gain, particularly through the sale of offices and the advancement of unworthy relatives to positions of spiritual authority. For Dante, the nepotism of Nicholas III was not merely a political fault but a **spiritual crime**: by treating the goods of the Church as instruments of family power, Orsini had inverted the proper order of grace and turned the papacy into a tool of worldly ambition. [^3]

### Historical Judgment

Modern historians tend to be more balanced than Dante. While Nicholas III's nepotism is undeniable, his pontificate also included genuine achievements: the assertion of papal independence from imperial and Angevin domination, the careful theological framing of Franciscan poverty, and the institutional development of the Vatican as the papal seat. The Catholic Encyclopedia's judgment that his character was "unblemished" except for nepotism suggests that, in the context of thirteenth-century papal politics, his faults were seen as conventional rather than uniquely corrupt. [^2]

## 7. Death and Burial

Nicholas III died at **Soriano**, near **Viterbo**, on **22 August 1280**. The cause of death is recorded as a sudden illness (possibly a stroke or heart attack) — he was apparently in good health until shortly before his death. [^2] [^1]

He was buried in the **Chapel of St. Nicholas** in **Old St. Peter's Basilica**, a chapel that he himself had built. His tomb did not survive the demolition of Old St. Peter's in the sixteenth century for the construction of the new basilica; the precise location of his remains within the modern St. Peter's is unknown. [^2]

## 8. Legacy

Pope Nicholas III's historical legacy is a study in contrasts:

| Positive Contributions                                                                     | Negative Aspects                                                                                                              |
| ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ | ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Issued _Exiit qui seminat_, a landmark in Franciscan theology                              | Extensive nepotism that advanced Orsini family power                                                                          |
| Established the Vatican Palace as papal residence, shaping the future center of the Church | Dante's condemnation in _Inferno_ has permanently associated him with simony                                                  |
| Checked Angevin domination in Italy, preserving papal independence                         | His family-based governing style set a troubling precedent for later Renaissance popes                                        |
| Asserted papal authority over imperial claims in the Romagna                               | The Franciscan poverty question remained unresolved, leading to the more severe controversies of the early fourteenth century |
| Supported missionary outreach to the Mongol Empire                                         |                                                                                                                               |

Nicholas III remains an important figure in the transition from the **thirteenth-century papal monarchy** (which reached its apex under Innocent III) to the more **politically entangled papacy of the late Middle Ages**. His pontificate was too short to achieve lasting institutional reform, but his decisions — particularly _Exiit qui seminat_ and the Vatican residence — had enduring consequences for the life of the Church.
