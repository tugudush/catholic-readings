# Traditionalist Catholic Societies: SSPX, FSSPX, and FSSP

This document explains the origins, canonical status, and mutual relationship of three abbreviations frequently encountered in discussions of Catholic traditionalism: **SSPX**, **FSSPX**, and **FSSP**. The first two denote the same society; the third denotes a distinct but related one.

---

## 1. SSPX and FSSPX: The Same Society

**SSPX** and **FSSPX** are abbreviations for the **identical entity** — the **Society of St. Pius X**. The difference is purely linguistic:

| Abbreviation | Language | Full Name                            |
| ------------ | -------- | ------------------------------------ |
| **SSPX**     | Latin    | _Societas Sacerdotalis Sancti Pii X_ |
| **FSSPX**    | French   | _Fraternité Sacerdotale Saint-Pie-X_ |

SSPX is the standard abbreviation in English-speaking contexts; FSSPX is common in French-speaking countries. Both refer to the same priestly society.

### 1.1 Foundation and Purpose

The Society of St. Pius X was founded in **1970** at the International Seminary of St. Pius X in Fribourg, Switzerland, by **Archbishop Marcel Lefebvre** (1905–1991), then the retired Archbishop of Tulle and former Superior General of the Congregation of the Holy Ghost. Archbishop Lefebvre obtained permission from the local Ordinary of Fribourg, Bishop François Charrière, to establish the Society as a _pia unio_ (pious union) of priests dedicated to the formation of seminarians and the preservation of the traditional Latin liturgy.

The founding purpose was:

- To form priests according to the traditional disciplines and doctrine of the Catholic Church, particularly as expressed in the **Tridentine Mass** (the 1962 Roman Missal of Pope St. John XXIII).
- To oppose what Lefebvre and his followers identified as errors introduced by the **Second Vatican Council (1962–1965)** and its post-conciliar reforms, especially in the areas of liturgy, ecumenism, and religious liberty.

### 1.2 The 1988 Episcopal Consecrations and the Schism

The decisive rupture occurred on **June 30, 1988**, when Archbishop Lefebvre — in defiance of a express prohibition by Pope John Paul II — consecrated **four bishops** (Bernard Fellay, Bernard Tissier de Mallerais, Richard Williamson, and Alfonso de Galarreta) without a papal mandate. The consecration took place at the Society's seminary in Écône, Switzerland.

On **July 1, 1988**, the Congregation for Bishops, with the approval of the Supreme Pontiff, issued a decree declaring that the four bishops-elect and Archbishop Lefebvre had incurred **automatic excommunication** (_latae sententiae_) by virtue of canons 751 and 1382 of the 1983 Code of Canon Law, since the consecration constituted an act of **schism** (the refusal of submission to the Supreme Pontiff and the rejection of communion with the members of the Church subject to him).

Pope John Paul II addressed the situation in the apostolic letter **[_Ecclesia Dei_](https://www.vatican.va/content/john-paul-ii/en/motu_proprio/documents/hf_jp-ii_motu-proprio_02071988_ecclesia-dei.html)** (July 2, 1988), in which he:

- Declared the consecration an act of **disobedience** to the Roman Pontiff and a **repudiation of the unity of the Church**.
- Confirmed that Archbishop Lefebvre and the four newly consecrated bishops had incurred **excommunication**.
- Established the **Pontifical Commission _Ecclesia Dei_** to facilitate the return of traditionalist groups to full communion.

### 1.3 Canonical Status

The canonical status of the SSPX is best described as **irregular**:

- The Society has **no canonical mission** in the Catholic Church. It is not recognized as a society of apostolic life or any other canonical institute.
- SSPX priests are **suspended** from the exercise of their orders (_suspens a divinis_) — meaning that while their ordinations remain **valid** (by virtue of valid episcopal succession), they are **illicit** (celebrated without the Church's authorization).
- The Masses and sacraments celebrated by SSPX priests are **valid** but **not licit**. The faithful may attend SSPX Masses for the purpose of fulfilling the Sunday obligation under certain restricted conditions (see below), but such attendance is not encouraged as the ordinary means of participation in the Eucharist.
- The sacraments of Penance and Matrimony celebrated by SSPX priests are of doubtful validity in many cases due to jurisdictional questions, particularly for marriage.

The **Pontifical Commission _Ecclesia Dei_** — originally established to oversee dialogue with traditionalist groups — was merged into the **Congregation for the Doctrine of the Faith** (now the **Dicastery for the Doctrine of the Faith**) by Pope Francis in 2019.

### 1.4 Developments Since 1988

- **2009**: Pope Benedict XVI lifted the excommunications of the four SSPX bishops. The gesture was aimed at fostering reconciliation, but the SSPX's canonical irregularity remained.
- **2015–2016**: The Jubilee Year of Mercy extended faculties for the reception of sacramental Confession by the faithful from SSPX priests, a provision later extended indefinitely by Pope Francis (2016).
- **2017**: Pope Francis granted SSPX priests the faculty to **validly and licitly** absolve penitents and to assist at marriages, under certain conditions.
- **2021**: Pope Francis issued the motu proprio **[_Traditionis Custodes_](https://www.vatican.va/content/francesco/en/motu_proprio/documents/20210716-motu-proprio-traditionis-custodes.html)**, restricting the use of the 1962 Roman Missal in the ordinary life of the Latin Church. The SSPX, being outside canonical structures, was not directly bound by this legislation and continues to celebrate exclusively according to the 1962 Missal.

Despite these gestures, the SSPX remains without full canonical regularization as of 2026. Doctrinal discussions continue between the Holy See and the Society, particularly on the interpretation of the Second Vatican Council.

### 1.5 When May the Faithful Attend SSPX Masses?

The Pontifical Commission _Ecclesia Dei_ (and subsequently the Congregation for the Doctrine of the Faith) has clarified:

- SSPX Masses are **not** the ordinary means of fulfilling the Sunday obligation.
- The faithful who, **with full knowledge of the illicit nature** of SSPX celebrations and without any intent to adhere to the schism, attend SSPX Masses due to genuine **impossibility** (e.g., no other Mass available for a prolonged period) do not necessarily sin gravely.
- However, deliberately preferring SSPX Masses to those celebrated by priests in full communion as a matter of principle — or holding that the SSPX represents the "true Church" while the post-conciliar Church does not — may constitute adherence to the schism.

## 2. FSSP: The Priestly Fraternity of St. Peter

**FSSP** stands for _Fraternitas Sacerdotalis Sancti Petri_ — the **Priestly Fraternity of St. Peter**. It is a **distinct society** from the SSPX, founded in direct response to the 1988 consecrations by those who chose to remain in full communion with Rome.

### 2.1 Foundation

The Fraternity was founded on **July 18, 1988** — just 18 days after the Écône consecrations — by **12 former members of the SSPX** who rejected Archbishop Lefebvre's schismatic act. They were joined by several seminarians who likewise chose fidelity to the Holy See.

On **October 18, 1988**, Pope John Paul II, through the Pontifical Commission _Ecclesia Dei_, granted the FSSP the status of a **Clerical Society of Apostolic Life of Pontifical Right** — a fully approved canonical institute within the Catholic Church.

### 2.2 Charism and Mission

The FSSP's founding charism is identical in outward focus to that of the SSPX: the **preservation and promotion of the traditional Latin liturgy** (the 1962 Roman Missal) and the formation of priests in the traditional disciplines. The crucial difference is that the FSSP pursues this mission **in full communion with the Holy See and under the authority of local bishops**.

Key characteristics:

- The FSSP celebrates **exclusively** the **Traditional Latin Mass** (the 1962 Missal) in all its apostolates. It does not celebrate the Mass of Pope St. Paul VI (the Novus Ordo).
- FSSP priests are **fully incardinated** into the Fraternity and exercise their ministry **licitly**, with the permission of the local diocesan bishop where they serve.
- The FSSP is a **Society of Apostolic Life** — its members live in community and follow a rule of life, but they are not religious in the strict canonical sense (they take no public vows).

### 2.3 The 2023 Dicastery Confirmation

Following the 2021 motu proprio _Traditionis Custodes_, which restricted the use of the 1962 Missal for diocesan priests and religious institutes founded after 1970, the status of the FSSP raised questions. In **February 2023**, the **Dicastery for Divine Worship and the Discipline of the Sacraments** issued a clarification confirming that the FSSP — by virtue of its founding pontifical recognition and its specific charism — **continues to enjoy the right to celebrate the traditional Latin Mass** without restriction. This confirmation has been reaffirmed in subsequent correspondence.

Thus, every Mass celebrated by an FSSP priest is a Traditional Latin Mass, and all such celebrations are **valid and licit**.

### 2.4 The FSSP Today

As of 2026, the FSSP operates in dozens of dioceses worldwide, with:

- Over **300 priests** and seminarians in formation.
- Parishes, apostolates, and oratories across North America, Europe, South America, Africa, and Asia.
- Its **International Seminary of St. Peter** in Wigratzbad, Germany (founded 1988), and a second seminary in Denton, Nebraska, USA (founded 1999).
- A growing number of younger vocations, making it one of the fastest-growing priestly societies in the Latin Church.

## 4. Key Distinctions Summary

1. **SSPX = FSSPX**: The same society. Use SSPX in English, FSSPX in French.
2. **SSPX vs. FSSP**: Two entirely different societies with different canonical statuses.
3. **Both celebrate the TLM exclusively**: Both societies use only the 1962 Roman Missal as their liturgical form.
4. **Canonical status is the decisive difference**: The SSPX is irregular (valid but illicit sacraments); the FSSP is regular (valid and licit sacraments).
5. **The 1988 consecrations are the dividing line**: Those who accepted Lefebvre's consecrations remained in or joined the SSPX; those who rejected them and sought full reconciliation founded the FSSP.
6. **The FSSP was founded to fill the same niche licitly**: It offers traditionalist Catholics a way to attend the traditional Latin Mass while remaining in full communion with the Holy See.
