## 1. Introduction

The **Apostolic Fathers** are the earliest Christian writers who were traditionally believed to have been direct disciples of the Apostles themselves or to have been formed under their immediate authority. They bridge the period between the apostolic age (c. 30–100 A.D.) and the later patristic era, providing an indispensable witness to the life, liturgy, governance, and doctrine of the early Church in the generations immediately following the deaths of the Apostles.

The term _Apostolic Father_ (_Patres Apostolici_) was first used in the seventeenth century by the patrologist Jean-Baptiste Cotelier (Cotelerius) and was popularized by the Anglican scholar William Wake in his 1693 English translation of their writings. The corpus traditionally includes:

| Apostolic Father                                  | Approximate Dates | Likely Apostolic Connection                      | Key Writings                                        |
| ------------------------------------------------- | ----------------- | ------------------------------------------------ | --------------------------------------------------- |
| **St. Clement of Rome**                           | c. 35–99 A.D.     | Disciple of St. Peter and/or St. Paul            | _1 Clement_                                         |
| **St. Ignatius of Antioch**                       | c. 35–108 A.D.    | Disciple of St. John the Evangelist              | Seven letters                                       |
| **St. Polycarp of Smyrna**                        | c. 69–155 A.D.    | Disciple of St. John                             | _Letter to the Philippians_                         |
| **St. Papias of Hierapolis**                      | c. 60–130 A.D.    | Disciple of St. John                             | _Exposition of the Oracles of the Lord_ (fragments) |
| **The Didache (Teaching of the Twelve Apostles)** | c. 65–100 A.D.    | Anonymous, reflecting apostolic tradition        | _The Didache_                                       |
| **The Epistle of Barnabas**                       | c. 70–130 A.D.    | Anonymous (traditionally linked to St. Barnabas) | _Epistle of Barnabas_                               |
| **The Shepherd of Hermas**                        | c. 100–150 A.D.   | Anonymous (Hermas, brother of Pope St. Pius I)   | _The Shepherd_                                      |
| **The Epistle to Diognetus**                      | c. 130–200 A.D.   | Anonymous                                        | _Epistle to Diognetus_                              |

These writings were not included in the canon of Sacred Scripture, but they were held in great esteem in the early Church and were sometimes read liturgically alongside the apostolic writings. They are of immense historical and theological value because they:

1. **Confirm the apostolic origin of Catholic doctrine** — the Real Presence, the Eucharist as Sacrifice, episcopal hierarchy, baptismal regeneration, and the primacy of the Roman See.
2. **Provide the earliest extra-biblical witness to the New Testament canon** — demonstrating which books were already regarded as Scripture in the late first and early second centuries.
3. **Reveal the structure of early Christian worship** — including the shape of the Eucharistic liturgy, the role of bishops, presbyters, and deacons, and the practice of Sunday observance.

> "The Apostolic Fathers are the link between the Apostles and the subsequent Church. They show us that the Catholicism of the fourth century did not appear suddenly, but was already present in seed form in the first and second generations after Christ."
> — Adapted from Johannes Quasten, _Patrology_

## 2. Historical Context: Why the Apostolic Fathers Matter

The period from **c. 70 A.D. to 150 A.D.** was one of the most critical in Church history:

- **The Destruction of Jerusalem (70 A.D.):** The temple was destroyed, fulfilling Christ's prophecy (Matthew 24:1–2). The Jerusalem Church, led by St. James the Just and later St. Simeon, ceased to be the geographic center of Christianity.
- **The Death of the Apostles:** By the end of the first century, all of the original Apostles had died as martyrs. The question of who would preserve and transmit their teaching became urgent.
- **The Rise of Heresy:** Gnosticism, Marcionism, and Docetism began to emerge, claiming secret apostolic traditions that contradicted the public teaching of the Church.
- **Persecution:** The Church faced intermittent but severe persecution under Emperors Nero, Domitian, Trajan, and Marcus Aurelius.

It was in this crucible that the Apostolic Fathers wrote, demonstrating that the Catholic faith—with its bishops, sacraments, canon of Scripture, and Roman primacy—was already a fully formed reality, not a later invention.

## 3. St. Clement of Rome: The Apostolic Succession and Roman Primacy

### Life and Identity

**St. Clement of Rome** (sometimes called **Clemens Romanus** to distinguish him from Clement of Alexandria) was the **third Bishop of Rome** (c. 88–99 A.D.) following St. Peter:

$$ \text{St. Peter} \longrightarrow \text{St. Linus} \longrightarrow \text{St. Anacletus (Cletus)} \longrightarrow \text{St. Clement I} $$

For dedicated biographies of the early Roman succession and other Apostolic Fathers, see:

- [st-linus-pope.md](st-linus-pope.md) — St. Linus, the immediate successor of St. Peter and second Bishop of Rome.
- [st-cletus-anacletus.md](st-cletus-anacletus.md) — St. Cletus (Anacletus), the second successor of St. Peter and third Bishop of Rome.
- [st-ignatius-antioch.md](st-ignatius-antioch.md) — St. Ignatius of Antioch, third Bishop of Antioch, disciple of St. John, and author of seven letters on the monarchial episcopate and the Real Presence.

According to St. Irenaeus of Lyons (_Against Heresies_, III, 3, 3), Clement had seen the Apostles and conferred with them; he still had "the preaching of the Apostles in his ears and their tradition before his eyes." The early Church historian Eusebius of Caesarea identifies him as the companion of St. Paul mentioned in **Philippians 4:3** ("whose names are in the book of life"), though this identification is not certain.

Tertullian and St. Jerome record that Clement was consecrated by St. Peter himself. He was martyred under the Emperor Trajan, traditionally by being tied to an anchor and thrown into the Black Sea. He is commemorated in the Roman Canon (Eucharistic Prayer I) and his feast day is **23 November**.

### The _First Epistle of Clement_ (1 Clement)

**Date:** c. 96 A.D.
**Occasion:** A faction in the Church of Corinth had unlawfully deposed several presbyters (elders/bishops). Hearing of this, the Church of Rome intervened. Clement wrote a lengthy letter in the name of the Roman Church to restore order.

**Key Doctrinal Contributions:**

1. **Apostolic Succession (Chapter 42–44):** Clement provides the earliest explicit witness to the doctrine of apostolic succession. He explains that the Apostles, knowing that conflict would arise over the episcopal office, appointed their first converts as bishops and deacons, and then "gave further instructions that when these should die, other approved men should succeed them in their ministry." Therefore, to remove those who have "blamelessly and holily" offered the gifts (the Eucharistic sacrifice) is to sin against God.

2. **The Monarchial Episcopate:** Clement assumes a threefold hierarchy (bishop, presbyters, deacons) and treats the presbyters of Corinth as the legitimate local clergy. He does not use the term "bishop" in the singular for Corinth, suggesting that at this date the Church of Corinth was still governed by a college of presbyter-bishops (as in Philippians 1:1 and Acts 20:17, 28).

3. **Roman Primacy:** The tone of the letter is remarkable. Clement writes not as an equal, but with authoritative instruction—commanding the Corinthians to submit, to cease their sedition, and to restore the deposed clergy. While the letter is sent in the name of "the Church of God which sojourns in Rome," it carries clear jurisdictional authority.

4. **The Resurrection and the Phoenix (Chapter 24–26):** Clement uses the pagan myth of the phoenix as a natural analogy for the resurrection of the dead, demonstrating how early Christians engaged with pagan culture to argue for Christian truth.

5. **The Eucharist as Sacrifice:** Clement uses sacrificial language (_prosphora_ and _leitourgia_) to describe the Eucharistic offering, confirming the early Church's understanding of the Mass as a true sacrifice.

### The So-Called _Second Epistle of Clement_ (2 Clement)

This is not by Clement but is likely the transcript of a homily by an unknown Corinthian bishop (c. 140–150 A.D.). It is the oldest surviving Christian sermon outside the New Testament.

## 4. St. Ignatius of Antioch: The Monarchial Episcopate and the Real Presence

For a dedicated biography, see [st-ignatius-antioch.md](st-ignatius-antioch.md).

### Life and Identity

**St. Ignatius of Antioch** (also known as **Ignatius Theophorus** — "God-bearer") was the **third Bishop of Antioch**, succeeding St. Peter (who founded the See) and St. Evodius. Antioch was one of the three great centers of early Christianity (alongside Rome and Alexandria) and was where "the disciples were first called Christians" (Acts 11:26).

According to tradition, Ignatius was a disciple of **St. John the Evangelist**, the beloved disciple who wrote the Fourth Gospel, the three Epistles of John, and the Book of Revelation. St. John Chrysostom states that Ignatius received the laying on of hands from the Apostles themselves.

### Arrest and Journey to Rome (c. 107–110 A.D.)

During the reign of Emperor Trajan (98–117 A.D.), Ignatius was arrested, convicted, and sentenced to death by wild beasts in the Roman arena. He was escorted from Antioch to Rome by a squad of ten soldiers (whom he calls "ten leopards") under guard. The journey took many months, winding through Asia Minor and Greece.

Along the way, he wrote **seven letters** that have survived as some of the most precious documents of Christian antiquity:

| Letter                  | Written At | Recipient                      | Purpose                                           |
| ----------------------- | ---------- | ------------------------------ | ------------------------------------------------- |
| _To the Ephesians_      | Smyrna     | Church of Ephesus              | Praise and warning against false teachers         |
| _To the Magnesians_     | Smyrna     | Church of Magnesia             | On obedience to the bishop                        |
| _To the Trallians_      | Smyrna     | Church of Tralles              | Against Docetism (Christ only "seemed" to suffer) |
| _To the Romans_         | Smyrna     | Church of Rome                 | Plea not to prevent his martyrdom                 |
| _To the Philadelphians_ | Troas      | Church of Philadelphia         | On unity with the bishop                          |
| _To the Smyrnaeans_     | Troas      | Church of Smyrna               | Against Docetism; first use of "Catholic Church"  |
| _To Polycarp_           | Troas      | St. Polycarp, Bishop of Smyrna | Personal advice on episcopal duties               |

### Key Doctrinal Contributions

1. **The Monarchial Episcopate (One Bishop):** Ignatius is the first writer to insist emphatically on the single bishop as the center of unity in each local church. He distinguishes clearly between the bishop (_episcopos_), the presbyters (_presbyteroi_), and the deacons (_diakonoi_). He writes:

   > "Be obedient to the bishop as to Jesus Christ... It is fitting that you should live in harmony with the will of the bishop, as indeed you do. For your justly renowned presbytery, worthy of God, is attuned to the bishop as the strings to a harp."
   > — _To the Ephesians_, 4

   > "Let no one do anything that pertains to the Church without the bishop. Let that be considered a valid Eucharist over which the bishop presides, or one to whom he entrusts it. Wherever the bishop appears, there let the people be, just as wherever Jesus Christ is, there is the Catholic Church."
   > — _To the Smyrnaeans_, 8

2. **The First Use of "Catholic Church":** In the same passage, Ignatius coins the term **"Catholic Church"** (Greek: _ἡ καθολικὴ ἐκκλησία_) to distinguish the universal Church from heretical conventicles. The word _katholikē_ means "according to the whole" or "universal." This usage is cited in the Catechism of the Catholic Church:

   > "Wherever the bishop shall appear, there let the multitude also be; even as, wherever Jesus Christ is, there is the Catholic Church."
   > — _Letter to the Smyrnaeans_, 8:2 (cited in _CCC_, 830)

3. **The Real Presence of Christ in the Eucharist:** Ignatius combats the Docetists who denied the reality of Christ's flesh and, consequently, the reality of the Eucharist. He writes:

   > "They abstain from the Eucharist and from prayer, because they do not confess that the Eucharist is the flesh of our Saviour Jesus Christ, which suffered for our sins and which the Father in His goodness raised up."
   > — _To the Smyrnaeans_, 7

4. **The Eucharistic Sacrifice:** He describes the altar (_thysiastērion_) and the offering, indicating that the Eucharist is understood as a sacrifice.

5. **The Virgin Birth and the Incarnation:** Ignatius provides a clear confession:

   > "There is one Physician, both fleshly and spiritual, begotten and unbegotten, God in man, true life in death, both of Mary and of God, first passible and then impassible—Jesus Christ our Lord."
   > — _To the Ephesians_, 7

6. **The Primacy of the Roman Church:** In his letter to the Romans, Ignatius writes with extraordinary deference:

   > "You [the Roman Church] have taught others. What I command, I desire that what you have enjoined in your teaching may stand firm."
   > — _To the Romans_, 3

   He addresses the Roman Church as "presiding in the region of the Romans" (_προκάθηται ἐν τόπῳ χωρίου Ῥωμαίων_), a phrase that has been understood by Catholic scholars as acknowledging Rome's presidency over the universal Church.

### Martyrdom

Ignatius arrived in Rome and was thrown to the wild beasts in the Flavian Amphitheater (the Colosseum) c. **108 A.D.** , during the reign of Trajan. His relics were later translated to Antioch and eventually to the Church of San Clemente in Rome.

> "I am the wheat of God, and I am ground by the teeth of the wild beasts, that I may be found the pure bread of Christ."
> — _To the Romans_, 4

## 5. St. Polycarp of Smyrna: The Johannine Tradition and Apostolic Martyrdom

### Life and Identity

**St. Polycarp of Smyrna** (Greek: _Πολύκαρπος_, "much fruit") was the Bishop of Smyrna (modern-day Izmir, Turkey) and, along with St. Ignatius of Antioch, a direct disciple of **St. John the Evangelist**. St. Irenaeus, who was a disciple of Polycarp, testifies:

> "I can even describe the place where the blessed Polycarp used to sit and talk—his goings out and his comings in, the character of his life, his personal appearance, his discourses to the people, how he would relate his intercourse with John and with the others who had seen the Lord, how he would recount their words. And whatsoever things he had heard from them about the Lord, about His miracles and His teaching, Polycarp would relate them in harmony with the Scriptures."
> — St. Irenaeus, _Letter to Florinus_ (in Eusebius, _Church History_, V, 20)

Polycarp was ordained bishop of Smyrna by the Apostles themselves and served for many decades.

### Encounter with St. Ignatius of Antioch

When St. Ignatius passed through Smyrna on his way to Rome, he wrote his letter to Polycarp, urging him to "stand firm as an anvil under the hammer" and to "care for unity, than which there is nothing better."

### The _Letter to the Philippians_

**Date:** c. 110–140 A.D.
**Purpose:** The Church of Philippi had asked Polycarp for a copy of St. Ignatius's letters. Polycarp sent them along with this covering letter of his own.

**Key Contributions:**

1. **Witness to the Pauline Corpus:** Polycarp cites from St. Paul's letters, including Romans, 1–2 Corinthians, Galatians, Ephesians, Philippians, 1–2 Timothy, and 1 Peter, demonstrating that these works were already recognized as authoritative Scripture.
2. **Justification by Faith and Works:** Polycarp seamlessly combines Paul's teaching on justification by faith with the necessity of good works, prefiguring the Catholic synthesis of the Council of Trent: "He who raised Him from the dead will raise us also, if we do His will and walk in His commandments and love what He loved."
3. **Opposition to Docetism and Marcionism:** He warns against anyone who "does not confess that Jesus Christ has come in the flesh" (echoing 1 John 4:2–3).

### The _Martyrdom of Polycarp_

The account of Polycarp's martyrdom is the earliest detailed record of a Christian martyrdom outside of the New Testament (c. 155–160 A.D.) and established the literary genre of the _acta martyrum_ ("acts of the martyrs").

**Key Elements:**

- **The Date:** Polycarp was arrested during the proconsulship of Statius Quadratus, on a Great Sabbath (Saturday). He was brought before the Roman proconsul in the stadium of Smyrna.
- **The Demand:** The proconsul urged Polycarp to "Swear by the fortune of Caesar... Repent. Say: 'Away with the atheists!'" (the "atheists" being the Christians).
- **The Confession:** Polycarp replied: "Away with the atheists!" (meaning the pagans). Pressed to curse Christ, he gave his famous response:

  > "Eighty-six years I have served Him, and He never did me any wrong. How can I blaspheme my King and my Savior?"

- **The Threat:** When threatened with wild beasts, fire, and the sword, Polycarp remained unmoved.
- **The Martyrdom:** He was burned at the stake. The flames, according to the account, did not touch his body but arched around him like a sail, and the smell was of "frankincense and myrrh." The executioner finally stabbed him with a dagger.
- **The Veneration of Relics:** The faithful gathered his charred bones, "more valuable than precious stones and finer than refined gold," and laid them in a fitting place where they would gather annually to celebrate the "birthday" (heavenly birthday) of his martyrdom. This is the earliest witness to the Catholic practices of **relic veneration** and the **celebration of saints' feast days**.

## 6. St. Papias of Hierapolis: The Oral Tradition of the Apostles

### Life and Identity

**St. Papias of Hierapolis** (c. 60–130 A.D.) was the Bishop of Hierapolis in Phrygia (modern-day Pamukkale, Turkey). He was, like Polycarp, a hearer of **St. John the Evangelist** and a companion of St. Polycarp. His work survives only in fragments quoted by Eusebius of Caesarea and other early writers.

### _Exposition of the Oracles of the Lord_ (_Logiōn Kyriakōn Exēgēsis_)

This five-volume work (now lost) was a collection and explanation of sayings of Jesus that Papias had gathered from those who had known the Apostles.

**Key Contributions:**

1. **On the Gospel of Mark:** Papias preserves the earliest explicit tradition about the origin of the Gospel of Mark:

   > "Mark, having become the interpreter of Peter, wrote down accurately everything that he remembered of the things said and done by the Lord, but not in order. For he neither heard the Lord nor followed him, but later, as I said, he followed Peter, who adapted his teaching as needed, with no intention of giving an ordered account of the sayings of the Lord."
   > — Papias, fragment (cited in Eusebius, _Church History_, III, 39)

2. **On the Gospel of Matthew:** Papias also notes that "Matthew compiled the sayings [of the Lord] in the Hebrew language, and everyone translated them as best they could."

3. **Oral Tradition over Written Books:** Papias famously preferred the living voice of tradition to written documents:

   > "For I did not suppose that information from books would help me so much as the word of a living and surviving voice."
   > — Papias, fragment

4. **Chiliasm (Millenarianism):** Papias appears to have held a form of millenarianism—the belief in a literal thousand-year earthly reign of Christ before the Last Judgment—based on a tradition he attributed to the Apostles. This belief was later abandoned by the mainstream Church tradition in favor of the amillennial interpretation.

## 7. The Didache: The Teaching of the Twelve Apostles

### Identity and Rediscovery

The **_Didache_ (_Διδαχή_, "Teaching")**, also called _The Teaching of the Twelve Apostles_, is an anonymous early Christian manual dating from **c. 65–100 A.D.** , making it contemporary with the later New Testament writings. It was lost for centuries and rediscovered in **1873** by Philotheos Bryennios, the Metropolitan of Nicomedia, in a manuscript in the Library of the Holy Sepulchre in Constantinople (Codex Hierosolymitanus 105).

### Structure and Content

The Didache is divided into three main sections:

| Section                       | Chapters | Content                                                             |
| ----------------------------- | -------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **The Two Ways**              | 1–6      | A moral catechesis contrasting the Way of Life and the Way of Death |
| **Liturgical Instructions**   | 7–15     | Baptism, fasting, the Eucharist, and Church order                   |
| **Eschatological Conclusion** | 16       | Warning of the coming of the Antichrist and the Second Coming       |

**Key Contributions:**

1. **Baptism by Immersion or Pouring (Chapter 7):** The Didache prescribes baptism in "living water" (running water) by triple immersion in the name of the Father, Son, and Holy Spirit. If no running water is available, it permits pouring water on the head three times. This is the earliest extra-biblical directive for the form of baptism.

2. **The Eucharistic Prayers (Chapters 9–10):** The Didache contains two beautiful Eucharistic prayers:

   > "We thank you, our Father, for the holy vine of David your servant, which you have made known to us through Jesus your servant. To you be the glory forever."

   > "We thank you, holy Father, for your holy name which you have made to dwell in our hearts, and for the knowledge and faith and immortality which you have made known to us through Jesus your servant. To you be the glory forever."

   These prayers are more "Jewish" in structure than the later anaphoras, but they already show a clear distinction between the Eucharist and ordinary meals.

3. **Sunday Worship (Chapter 14):** The Didache commands weekly gathering on the "Lord's Day" for the "breaking of bread and giving of thanks" and connects the Eucharistic sacrifice to the prophecy of Malachi 1:11 ("in every place incense is offered to my name, and a pure offering").

4. **Confession and Reconciliation (Chapter 4, 14):** It instructs: "In the assembly you shall confess your transgressions," and before the Eucharist: "First make peace with your brother, so that your sacrifice may not be defiled."

5. **Church Order (Chapters 11–15):** The Didache shows a fluid period in Church governance where itinerant apostles and prophets still operated alongside resident bishops and deacons. It provides rules for discerning true from false prophets, indicating how the charismata were being regularized into the episcopal structure that Ignatius would fully articulate.

## 8. The Epistle of Barnabas

### Identity

The **_Epistle of Barnabas_** (c. 70–130 A.D.) is an anonymous treatise traditionally attributed to **St. Barnabas**, the Levite from Cyprus who accompanied St. Paul on his first missionary journey (Acts 4:36; 13–14). Most modern scholars doubt this attribution, but the work was highly esteemed in antiquity and is included in the fourth-century Codex Sinaiticus (one of the earliest complete Bibles) immediately after the New Testament.

### Key Contributions

1. **Typological Interpretation of Scripture:** The author reads the Old Testament as a prefiguration of Christ and the Church. For example, the 318 servants of Abraham (Genesis 14:14) are interpreted typologically: the Greek letters ΙΗ (Iota-Eta = Iēsous, Jesus) and Τ (Tau = Cross).

2. **Anti-Judaizing Polemic:** The letter argues forcefully that the Old Covenant was never intended to be observed literally—that the Jews lost their covenant at Sinai when they turned to idolatry with the golden calf, and that the Christian Church is the true inheritor of the promises.

3. **The Two Ways:** Like the Didache, Barnabas includes a "Two Ways" moral catechesis (of light and darkness), suggesting a common source or catechetical tradition.

## 9. The Shepherd of Hermas

### Identity

**_The Shepherd of Hermas_** (c. 100–150 A.D.) is a lengthy apocalyptic and moral work written in Rome by a man named **Hermas**. According to the _Muratorian Fragment_ (c. 170 A.D.), Hermas was the brother of **Pope St. Pius I** (c. 140–155 A.D.). The work is divided into three parts: _Visions_, _Mandates_ (Commandments), and _Similitudes_ (Parables).

It was immensely popular in the early Church. St. Irenaeus, St. Clement of Alexandria, and Origen all cited it as Scripture, and it appears in the Codex Sinaiticus.

### Key Contributions

1. **Post-Baptismal Penance:** The Shepherd addresses the controversial question of whether Christians who fall into serious sin after baptism can be forgiven. Hermas teaches that there is **one** opportunity for repentance after baptism—a "second repentance" (_secunda paenitentia_)—granted through the ministry of the Church. This is an early witness to the sacrament of Penance and the distinction between the once-for-all remission of sins in baptism and the ongoing forgiveness available through confession.

2. **The Allegory of the Tower:** The Church is depicted as a tower being built with stones—the Apostles and bishops are the foundation stones; the faithful are the building stones; and those who fall away are rejected stones.

3. **Angelic Theology:** The Shepherd develops the doctrine of guardian angels and the two spirits (good and evil) that accompany every person.

4. **The "Woman" (the Church):** The Church appears as an aged woman (because she was created before the world) who grows younger as the faithful are purified.

## 10. The Epistle to Diognetus

### Identity

The **_Epistle to Diognetus_** (c. 130–200 A.D.) is an anonymous apology for Christianity addressed to a certain "Diognetus," possibly the tutor of Emperor Marcus Aurelius. It is one of the most beautiful literary works of the early Church, noted for its elegant Greek prose and its vivid description of early Christian life.

### Key Contributions

1. **The Distinctiveness of Christians (Chapter 5–6):** The author provides a famous description of Christians living in the world but not of it:

   > "Christians are not distinguished from the rest of humanity by country, language, or customs... They live in their own countries, but as aliens; they participate in everything as citizens, and endure everything as foreigners... They live in the flesh, but not according to the flesh. They pass their days on earth, but are citizens of heaven."

2. **The Incomprehensibility of God and the Mission of the Son:** The letter presents a sophisticated theology of God's transcendence and His revelation through the Son.

3. **Critique of Paganism and Judaism:** The author systematically refutes both pagan idolatry and Jewish ceremonial observances.

## 11. The Relationship Between the Apostolic Fathers and the Canon of Scripture

The writings of the Apostolic Fathers are not part of the inspired canon of Scripture, but they provide invaluable evidence for the formation of the canon:

- **Use of the Old Testament:** They cite the Septuagint (Greek Old Testament) extensively, including the deuterocanonical books (Wisdom, Sirach, Tobit, etc.), confirming the Church's acceptance of the longer canon of the Old Testament.
- **Use of the New Testament:** By the time of St. Polycarp and St. Clement, the four Gospels, the Pauline corpus, 1 Peter, and 1 John were already treated as authoritative Scripture.
- **The Muratorian Fragment (c. 170 A.D.):** While not by an Apostolic Father, this Roman document lists the canonical books and is a direct descendant of the tradition the Apostolic Fathers preserved.

## 13. The Apostolic Fathers and the Eastern Catholic Tradition

The Apostolic Fathers wrote in Greek (the lingua franca of the Eastern Mediterranean), and their theological and liturgical sensibilities are deeply rooted in the Semitic and Hellenistic world of the early Church. As such, they are especially revered in the **Eastern Catholic Churches** and the **Eastern Orthodox tradition**.

- St. Ignatius of Antioch is a patron saint of the **Syriac Catholic Church** and the **Syriac Orthodox Church**.
- The Didache's Eucharistic prayers bear close resemblance to the anaphoras of the **Addai and Mari** tradition used in the **Chaldean Catholic Church** and the **Assyrian Church of the East**.
- St. Polycarp's martyrdom account established the template for the veneration of martyrs that is shared by all 24 churches of the Catholic communion.
