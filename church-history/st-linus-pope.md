# St. Linus: Second Successor of St. Peter and Second Bishop of Rome

> **Note:** This document was composed without Magisterium AI retrieval. Citations should be verified against primary sources where possible.

---

## 1. Introduction

**St. Linus** (Latin: _Linus_; Greek: _Λῖνος_; c. 10–76/79 A.D.) was the **second Bishop of Rome** after St. Peter, serving as the visible head of the Catholic Church from approximately **c. 64 to 76/79 A.D.** He stands as the immediate successor of the Prince of the Apostles and is the first Roman-born pope, bridging the apostolic age and the early sub-apostolic period.

St. Linus is mentioned in the New Testament—**2 Timothy 4:21**—where St. Paul, near the end of his life, sends greetings from Linus and his companions. This makes Linus one of the very few early popes whose name appears in Sacred Scripture itself. He is commemorated in the **Roman Canon** (Eucharistic Prayer I) of the Latin Mass alongside St. Cletus and St. Clement I, and his feast day is celebrated on **23 September** in the Latin Rite and on **7 June** in the Byzantine Rite.

## 2. Life and Identity

### Dates and Origins

The exact dates of Linus's birth and death are uncertain. He was born in **Tuscia** (the region of modern-day **Tuscany**, Italy) around **c. 10 A.D.** , making him a Roman citizen of Italic (Latin) ethnicity. According to the _Liber Pontificalis_, his father was named **Herculanus**.

### The Succession of the Roman See

The earliest and most authoritative testimony to Linus's place in the Roman succession comes from **St. Irenaeus of Lyons** (c. 180 A.D.), who writes:

> "The blessed Apostles [Peter and Paul], then, having founded and built up the Church [of Rome], committed the ministry of the episcopate to Linus. This Linus is mentioned by Paul in his Epistle to Timothy."
> — St. Irenaeus, _Against Heresies_, III, 3, 3

The early episcopal succession in Rome, as preserved by St. Irenaeus and other ancient witnesses, is as follows:

$$ \text{St. Peter (Prince of the Apostles, c. 30–64/67)} \longrightarrow \text{St. Linus (c. 64–76/79)} \longrightarrow \text{St. Anacletus / Cletus (c. 76/79–88)} \longrightarrow \text{St. Clement I (c. 88–99)} $$

St. Linus thus stands as the **first successor of St. Peter** in the See of Rome, the immediate link between the Prince of the Apostles and the subsequent line of Roman pontiffs.

### The New Testament Witness (2 Timothy 4)

St. Linus is one of only two popes (alongside St. Peter himself) whose name appears in the New Testament. His name occurs in the closing verses of St. Paul's **Second Epistle to Timothy**, written from Rome during the Apostle's final imprisonment (c. 64–67 A.D.), just before his martyrdom. The full concluding passage (2 Timothy 4:9–22) reads:

> "Do your best to come to me soon. For Demas, in love with this present world, has deserted me and gone to Thessalonica; Crescens has gone to Galatia, Titus to Dalmatia. Luke alone is with me. Get Mark and bring him with you; for he is very useful in serving me. Tychicus I have sent to Ephesus. When you come, bring the cloak that I left with Carpus at Troas, also the books, and above all the parchments. Alexander the coppersmith did me great harm; the Lord will repay him according to his deeds. Beware of him yourself, for he strongly opposed our message. At my first defense no one took my part; all deserted me. May it not be charged against them! But the Lord stood by me and gave me strength to proclaim the message fully, that all the Gentiles might hear it. So I was rescued from the lion's mouth. The Lord will rescue me from every evil and save me for his heavenly kingdom. To him be the glory for ever and ever. Amen.
>
> **Eubulus sends greetings to you, as do Pudens and Linus and Claudia** and all the brethren.
>
> The Lord be with your spirit. Grace be with you all."
> — 2 Timothy 4:9–22 (RSV-CE)

The specific reference to Linus appears in **verse 21**:

> "Eubulus sends greetings to you, as do **Pudens and Linus and Claudia** and all the brethren."
> — 2 Timothy 4:21 (RSV-CE)

This passage places Linus in Rome during the lifetime of St. Paul and in the closest possible proximity to the apostolic circle. The mention of **Pudens** and **Claudia** alongside Linus suggests a Christian household or community in Rome with which Linus was associated. Ancient tradition identifies Pudens as a Roman senator and Claudia as a British woman whom he married, though these identifications remain speculative.

The surrounding verses provide the poignant context: Paul is alone except for Luke, abandoned by many of his companions, facing imminent execution. The fact that Linus is named among the few who remain with the Apostle until the end speaks volumes about his fidelity and his standing in the Roman Church.

### Other Successors and Direct Disciples in the New Testament

St. Linus is one of several apostolic successors and direct disciples of the Apostles whose names are preserved in the New Testament. These figures represent the first generation of Church leaders after the Apostles themselves and form the earliest links in the chain of apostolic succession. The following table lists those named in Scripture, with the relevant verses:

Of these, only **St. Linus** and **St. Clement** (possibly) are early Bishops of Rome mentioned in the New Testament, making Linus a uniquely important scriptural witness to the first-generation Roman succession. The identification of the "Clement" of Philippians 4:3 with Pope St. Clement I is an ancient tradition (attested by Origen, Eusebius, and St. Jerome) but remains uncertain, as the name Clement was common in the Roman world. Linus is the only certain successor of St. Peter whose name is recorded in Sacred Scripture.

### Relationship with St. Peter and St. Paul

St. Linus was consecrated as Bishop of Rome by **St. Peter the Apostle** himself, or immediately after Peter's departure from Rome for his missionary journeys. The fact that St. Paul includes Linus in his final greetings indicates that Linus was already a prominent figure in the Roman Church during the lifetime of the two chief Apostles.

According to the tradition preserved in the _Liber Pontificalis_ and supported by the testimony of St. Irenaeus, St. Peter appointed Linus to assist him in the governance of the Roman Church and, upon Peter's martyrdom (c. 64–67 A.D. under Emperor Nero), Linus succeeded him as Bishop of Rome.

### Martyrdom

Ancient tradition holds that St. Linus died a **martyr's death**, though the precise circumstances are not recorded with certainty. The _Liber Pontificalis_ states that he was martyred and buried on the **Vatican Hill** next to the tomb of St. Peter. The reign of Emperor **Nero** (54–68 A.D.) and the subsequent persecution under Emperor **Domitian** (81–96 A.D.) frame the likely period of his martyrdom, though some scholars suggest he may have died a natural death during the relative peace between persecutions.

His tomb on the Vatican Hill, in close proximity to St. Peter's burial place, is a powerful symbol of his direct succession from the Prince of the Apostles and of the continuity of the Roman See.

## 3. Pontificate

### Duration

The chronology of St. Linus's pontificate is uncertain. Ancient sources assign him a reign of approximately **twelve years**, though the exact dates vary:

| Source               | Duration of Pontificate     | Approximate Dates   |
| -------------------- | --------------------------- | ------------------- |
| St. Irenaeus         | Not specified               | After Peter's death |
| Eusebius of Caesarea | 12 years                    | c. 64–76 A.D.       |
| _Liber Pontificalis_ | 12 years, 3 months, 12 days | c. 64–76/79 A.D.    |
| Later traditions     | 11–12 years                 | c. 67–79 A.D.       |

The most commonly accepted chronology places his pontificate from **c. 64 A.D.** (following Peter's martyrdom in the Neronian persecution) to **c. 76/79 A.D.**

### Historical Context: The Neronian Persecution

St. Linus assumed the governance of the Roman Church at a time of extreme peril. The Great Fire of Rome (64 A.D.) had been blamed by Emperor Nero on the Christians, unleashing the first state-sponsored persecution of the Church. Sts. Peter and Paul were both martyred in this persecution—Peter by crucifixion (upside down) and Paul by beheading.

The Roman Church, now deprived of its two apostolic founders, looked to Linus as its shepherd. His task was to:

1. **Consolidate** the Christian community after the decimation of the persecution.
2. **Preserve** the apostolic teaching and tradition intact.
3. **Organize** the Church's worship, governance, and charitable works in the imperial capital.
4. **Defend** the flock against both external persecution and internal threats of heresy.

### Disciplinary Traditions

The _Liber Pontificalis_ attributes to St. Linus a disciplinary decree: that **women should cover their heads in church**. This regulation, based on St. Paul's instruction in 1 Corinthians 11:5–6 ("any woman who prays or prophesies with her head unveiled dishonors her head"), reflects the early Church's concern for liturgical propriety and the preservation of apostolic tradition.

While this decree cannot be authenticated with certainty (the _Liber Pontificalis_ was compiled in the sixth century, centuries after Linus's death), it testifies to the early Church's understanding of Linus as an authoritative legislator for the Roman Church.

### Opposition to Heresy

The sub-apostolic age saw the early stirrings of **Gnosticism** and **Docetism**—heresies that denied the goodness of creation, the reality of the Incarnation, and the physical resurrection of Christ. As Bishop of Rome, St. Linus would have been called upon to uphold the apostolic faith against these emerging errors, though no writings from his hand survive to detail his specific responses.

The testimony of St. Irenaeus, who emphasizes that the Roman Church preserved the apostolic tradition "through the succession of her bishops," implicitly includes St. Linus in this chain of orthodox transmission:

> "For it is a matter of necessity that every Church should agree with this Church [of Rome], on account of her preeminent authority, in whom the apostolic tradition has always been preserved by those who are everywhere."
> — St. Irenaeus, _Against Heresies_, III, 3, 2

## 4. Patristic Testimony to St. Linus

> "The blessed Apostles, then, having founded and built up the Church, committed the ministry of the episcopate to Linus. This Linus is mentioned by Paul in his Epistle to Timothy."
> — **St. Irenaeus of Lyons**, _Against Heresies_, III, 3, 3 (c. 180 A.D.)

> "Linus, the first after Peter, was bishop of the Church of Rome for twelve years."
> — **Eusebius of Caesarea**, _Church History_, III, 4 (c. 325 A.D.)

> "Linus, the first after the blessed Peter, is recorded to have been bishop of the Roman Church... Paul mentions him in his Epistle to Timothy."
> — **Eusebius of Caesarea**, _Church History_, III, 13

> "Peter the Apostle... first established the see at Antioch, and then at Rome, where he presided as bishop for twenty-five years. He appointed Linus as his successor."
> — **St. Jerome**, _Illustrious Men_, 1 (c. 392 A.D.)

> "Linus, son of Herculanus, from the region of Tuscia, sat as bishop for twelve years, three months, and twelve days. He was bishop in the time of Nero... He decreed that women should cover their heads in church. He was buried on the Vatican Hill next to the body of the blessed Peter. He held the see for twelve years, three months, and twelve days, and was bishop in the time of the consuls Saturninus and Scipio."
> — _Liber Pontificalis_ (sixth century compilation; some details are of uncertain historicity)

## 5. Legacy

St. Linus, though shrouded in historical obscurity compared to his successors Clement I and Ignatius of Antioch, is of enduring importance for the following reasons:

1. **First Successor of St. Peter:** As the immediate successor of the Prince of the Apostles, Linus is the foundational link in the unbroken chain of Roman pontiffs that continues to the present day. The principle of **apostolic succession**—that the authority of the Apostles has been transmitted through an unbroken line of bishops—rests in part on the recognition of Linus as the legitimate second Bishop of Rome.

2. **New Testament Witness:** His mention in 2 Timothy 4:21 gives him a unique place among the early popes, placing him within the pages of Sacred Scripture itself and confirming his existence and his connection to the apostolic circle.

3. **Roman Canon Commemoration:** His name is solemnly invoked in the **Roman Canon** (Eucharistic Prayer I) of the Latin Mass—the most ancient and venerable Eucharistic Prayer of the Roman Rite—alongside St. Cletus and St. Clement:

   > "Linus, Cletus, Clement, Sixtus, Cornelius..." — Roman Canon

   This liturgical commemoration ensures that St. Linus is remembered at every celebration of the Mass according to the Roman Rite, a privilege shared by only the earliest popes and martyrs.

4. **Bridge Figure:** He connects the apostolic age (represented by Sts. Peter and Paul) to the early sub-apostolic period (represented by Sts. Clement, Ignatius, and Polycarp). Through his faithful governance of the Roman Church during the Neronian persecution, he ensured the preservation and transmission of the apostolic deposit of faith.

5. **Patronage:** St. Linus is venerated as a saint in both the Latin and Eastern Catholic Churches, as well as in Eastern Orthodoxy. In art, he is sometimes depicted with **papal vestments** and the **Book of the Gospels**, or with the **palm of martyrdom**.
