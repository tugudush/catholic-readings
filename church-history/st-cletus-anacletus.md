# St. Cletus (Anacletus): Third Successor of St. Peter and Third Bishop of Rome

> **Note:** This document was composed without Magisterium AI retrieval. Citations should be verified against primary sources where possible.

---

## 1. Introduction

**St. Cletus** (Latin: _Cletus_ or _Anacletus_; Greek: _Ἀνάκλητος_, _Anaklētos_, meaning "the one who is called back" or "invoked"; c. 25–88 A.D.), also known as **Anacletus** and **Pope St. Cletus**, was the **third Bishop of Rome** after St. Peter, serving as the visible head of the Catholic Church from approximately **c. 76 to 88 A.D.** He stands as the immediate successor of St. Linus and the second link in the unbroken chain of Roman pontiffs after the Prince of the Apostles.

The name **Cletus** (from Greek _Κλητός_, _Klētos_, meaning "called" or "chosen") and **Anacletus** (from Greek _Ἀνάκλητος_, _Anaklētos_) appear in ancient sources as referring to the same person, though the duplication of names led some later writers to mistakenly divide him into two distinct popes. Modern scholarship recognizes that **Cletus and Anacletus are the same figure**, with the _Liber Pontificalis_ and the Roman Canon using "Cletus" while Eusebius of Caesarea and St. Irenaeus use "Anacletus."

St. Cletus is commemorated in the **Roman Canon** (Eucharistic Prayer I) of the Latin Mass alongside St. Linus and St. Clement I, and his feast day is celebrated on **26 April** in both the Latin Rite and the Byzantine Rite.

## 2. Life and Identity

### Dates and Origins

The exact dates of Cletus's birth and death are uncertain. He was likely born around **c. 25 A.D.** , possibly in **Athens** or the broader Greek world, making him the **first Greek pope** (or more precisely, of Greek descent). According to the _Liber Pontificalis_, he was a **Roman citizen of Greek ethnicity** (_natione Graecus_), born in the region of Rome but of Hellenic heritage.

### The Name: Cletus or Anacletus?

The duplication of names has caused considerable confusion in the historical record:

| Name Form     | Meaning                    | Used By                                                         |
| ------------- | -------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Cletus**    | "Called" or "Chosen"       | _Liber Pontificalis_, Roman Canon, Latin liturgical tradition   |
| **Anacletus** | "Called Back" or "Invoked" | St. Irenaeus, Eusebius of Caesarea, St. Jerome, Greek tradition |

The fourth-century _Liberian Catalogue_ lists "Cletus" as the second bishop of Rome after Peter, but with a note identifying him as "Anacletus." The _Martyrologium Hieronymianum_ also treats them as the same person. By the sixth century, the _Liber Pontificalis_ definitively merged the two names, listing "Cletus" with the alternate name "Anacletus."

Modern historical scholarship unanimously affirms that **Cletus and Anacletus are one and the same person**, the third Bishop of Rome. The erroneous division into two separate popes (Cletus and Anacletus) that appeared in some medieval lists has been corrected in the modern _Annuario Pontificio_, which lists only one pontiff under the name **Cletus** (also called **Anacletus**).

### The Succession of the Roman See

The earliest and most authoritative testimony to Cletus's place in the Roman succession comes from **St. Irenaeus of Lyons** (c. 180 A.D.), who writes:

> "After [Peter and Paul] had founded and built up the Church [of Rome], they committed the ministry of the episcopate to Linus. This Linus is mentioned by Paul in his Epistle to Timothy. **Anacletus succeeded him.** After him, in the third place from the Apostles, Clement was allotted the episcopate."
> — St. Irenaeus, _Against Heresies_, III, 3, 3

The early episcopal succession in Rome, as preserved by St. Irenaeus and other ancient witnesses, is as follows:

$$ \text{St. Peter (Prince of the Apostles, c. 30–64/67)} \longrightarrow \text{St. Linus (c. 64–76/79)} \longrightarrow \text{St. Cletus / Anacletus (c. 76/79–88)} \longrightarrow \text{St. Clement I (c. 88–99)} $$

St. Cletus thus stands as the **second successor of St. Peter** in the See of Rome, the middle link between St. Linus and St. Clement I.

### The "Cletus vs. Anacletus" Error in Later Lists

The confusion arose in the early medieval period when Greek and Latin lists of popes were harmonized without recognizing that "Cletus" and "Anacletus" referred to the same person. Some lists therefore erroneously showed:

1. St. Linus (2nd pope)
2. St. Cletus (3rd pope)
3. St. Anacletus (4th pope)
4. St. Clement I (5th pope)

This error persisted in some reference works until the twentieth century but has since been corrected. The true succession is:

1. St. Peter (1st)
2. St. Linus (2nd)
3. St. Cletus / Anacletus (3rd)
4. St. Clement I (4th)

### Martyrdom

Ancient tradition holds that St. Cletus died a **martyr's death**, though the precise circumstances are not recorded with certainty. The _Liber Pontificalis_ records that he was martyred under the Emperor **Domitian** (81–96 A.D.), though the exact date of his death is traditionally placed at c. 88 A.D., earlier than the main Domitianic persecution.

According to the _Liber Pontificalis_, he was buried on the **Vatican Hill** next to the tomb of St. Peter, in close proximity to St. Linus. His tomb on the Vatican Hill, like that of his predecessor, is a powerful symbol of the continuity of the Roman See and the unbroken chain of apostolic succession.

## 3. Pontificate

### Duration

The chronology of St. Cletus's pontificate is uncertain. Ancient sources assign him a reign of approximately **twelve years**, though the exact dates vary:

| Source               | Duration of Pontificate | Approximate Dates         |
| -------------------- | ----------------------- | ------------------------- |
| St. Irenaeus         | Not specified           | Between Linus and Clement |
| Eusebius of Caesarea | 12 years                | c. 76–88 A.D.             |
| _Liber Pontificalis_ | 12 years                | c. 76–88 A.D.             |
| Later traditions     | 11–12 years             | c. 79–91 A.D.             |

The most commonly accepted chronology places his pontificate from approximately **c. 76/79 A.D.** to **c. 88 A.D.**

### Historical Context: The Post-Apostolic Consolidation

St. Cletus assumed the governance of the Roman Church during the period of consolidation following the Neronian persecution (64 A.D.) and the martyrdom of Sts. Peter and Paul. The Church in Rome, though still a small and persecuted minority, was growing in numbers and organization.

His pontificate falls between two great persecutions:

- The **Neronian Persecution** (64–68 A.D.) had already claimed Sts. Peter and Paul.
- The **Domitianic Persecution** (81–96 A.D.) would later claim St. Clement I and many others.

Cletus thus governed during a period of relative but fragile peace, when the Church could strengthen its internal structures while remaining cautious of imperial hostility.

### Disciplinary and Organizational Traditions

The _Liber Pontificalis_ attributes several important initiatives to St. Cletus:

1. **Ordination of Priests:** He is said to have ordained **twenty-five priests** for the Roman Church, a sign of the growing Christian community in the imperial capital and the need for an organized presbyterate to serve the faithful.

2. **Construction of a Memorial to St. Peter:** According to tradition, Cletus built or completed an **oratory (memorial shrine) over the tomb of St. Peter** on the Vatican Hill. This structure, though modest, was a precursor to the Old St. Peter's Basilica built by Emperor Constantine in the fourth century and the present Renaissance basilica.

3. **Liturgical Prescriptions:** The _Liber Pontificalis_ records that Cletus established certain liturgical norms, including regulations for the vestments of the clergy and the conduct of divine worship, though the specific details are uncertain.

While these traditions from the _Liber Pontificalis_ (compiled in the sixth century) cannot be authenticated with certainty, they testify to the early Church's understanding of Cletus as an organizing and stabilizing figure in the post-apostolic Roman Church.

### Preservation of the Apostolic Deposit

The primary task of the early popes was the faithful transmission of the apostolic deposit of faith. St. Cletus, as the third Bishop of Rome, was a crucial link in this chain of transmission. The testimony of St. Irenaeus, who emphasizes that the Roman Church preserved the apostolic tradition "through the succession of her bishops" (_Against Heresies_, III, 3, 2), implicitly includes Cletus in this chain of orthodox transmission alongside Linus and Clement.

## 4. Patristic Testimony to St. Cletus

> "After [Peter and Paul] had founded and built up the Church, they committed the ministry of the episcopate to Linus. This Linus is mentioned by Paul in his Epistle to Timothy. **Anacletus succeeded him.** After him, in the third place from the Apostles, Clement was allotted the episcopate."
> — **St. Irenaeus of Lyons**, _Against Heresies_, III, 3, 3 (c. 180 A.D.)

> "After Linus, Anacletus held the office for twelve years."
> — **Eusebius of Caesarea**, _Church History_, III, 13 (c. 325 A.D.)

> "Cletus, the third bishop of the Roman Church, was the successor of Linus. He held the office for twelve years."
> — **Eusebius of Caesarea**, _Church History_, III, 4

> "Peter the Apostle... appointed Linus as his successor. After Linus, Cletus held the bishopric for twelve years."
> — **St. Jerome**, _Illustrious Men_, 1 (c. 392 A.D.)

> "Cletus, a Greek by birth, from the region of the Athenians, sat as bishop for twelve years. He ordained twenty-five priests. He built a memorial to the blessed Peter on the Vatican. He was buried on the Vatican Hill next to the body of the blessed Peter. He held the see for twelve years, in the time of the Emperor Domitian."
> — _Liber Pontificalis_ (sixth century compilation; some details are of uncertain historicity)

## 5. Legacy

St. Cletus (Anacletus), though one of the most historically obscure of the early popes, is of enduring importance for the following reasons:

1. **Second Successor of St. Peter:** As the immediate successor of St. Linus, Cletus is the second link in the unbroken chain of Roman pontiffs. The principle of **apostolic succession**—that the authority of the Apostles has been transmitted through an unbroken line of bishops—depends on the recognition of all the early popes, including Cletus, as legitimate successors of St. Peter.

2. **Witness to the Early Roman Succession:** The unity of the names Cletus and Anacletus, and their consistent inclusion in the earliest episcopal lists (St. Irenaeus, Hegesippus, Eusebius), demonstrates that the Roman Church preserved careful records of its succession from the apostolic age. This documentation is itself a powerful witness to the Church's historical continuity.

3. **Roman Canon Commemoration:** His name (as "Cletus") is solemnly invoked in the **Roman Canon** (Eucharistic Prayer I) of the Latin Mass—the most ancient and venerable Eucharistic Prayer of the Roman Rite—alongside St. Linus and St. Clement:

   > "Linus, **Cletus**, Clement, Sixtus, Cornelius..." — Roman Canon

   This liturgical commemoration ensures that St. Cletus is remembered at every celebration of the Mass according to the Roman Rite.

4. **The "Cletus-Anacletus" Clarification:** The historical resolution of the Cletus-Anacletus confusion is a valuable case study in historical theology, demonstrating how modern scholarship, building on the testimony of the earliest sources (particularly St. Irenaeus), corrected a medieval textual error and restored the accurate understanding of the early Roman succession.

5. **Patronage:** St. Cletus is venerated as a saint in both the Latin and Eastern Catholic Churches, as well as in Eastern Orthodoxy. In art, he is sometimes depicted with **papal vestments** and the **palm of martyrdom**, or with a **scroll** symbolizing his ordination of priests.
