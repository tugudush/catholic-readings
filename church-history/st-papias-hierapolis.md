# St. Papias of Hierapolis: Presbyter of the Johannine Tradition

> **Note:** This document was composed without Magisterium AI retrieval. Citations should be verified against primary sources where possible.

---

## 1. Introduction

**St. Papias of Hierapolis** (Greek: _Παπίας_; c. 60–130 A.D.) was the **Bishop of Hierapolis** in Phrygia (modern-day Pamukkale, Turkey) and, like St. Polycarp of Smyrna, a direct **hearer of St. John the Evangelist**. He is the author of a lost five-volume work titled the _Exposition of the Oracles of the Lord_ (_Logiōn Kyriakōn Exēgēsis_), which survives only in fragments quoted by later writers—principally Eusebius of Caesarea.

Despite the fragmentary preservation of his work, St. Papias is of extraordinary importance for two reasons:

1. He preserves the **earliest patristic testimony to the origins of the Gospels of Mark and Matthew**, providing critical evidence for the apostolic authorship and historical reliability of the New Testament.
2. He embodies the early Church's preference for the **living voice of apostolic tradition** over written records, a principle that would become foundational for Catholic teaching on the relationship between Sacred Scripture and Sacred Tradition (cf. _Dei Verbum_, 7–10).

His feast day is celebrated on **22 February** in the Latin Rite and on **22 February** in the Byzantine Rite.

---

## 2. Life and Identity

### Dates and Origins

Papias was likely born around **60 A.D.** in the region of Phrygia, in the Roman province of Asia (western Asia Minor). The exact date of his birth is uncertain, but his mature literary activity is placed in the first half of the second century (c. 100–130 A.D.).

### Bishop of Hierapolis

Papias served as the **Bishop of Hierapolis**, a city in the Lycus River valley in Phrygia, near Laodicea and Colossae (both of which are mentioned in the New Testament). Hierapolis was a notable center of early Christianity and the site of a church that may have been founded by the Apostle **St. Philip**, whose daughters were known as prophetesses (cf. Acts 21:8–9; Eusebius, _Church History_, III, 31).

The city is also famous in secular history for its hot springs and the **Plutonium** (a cave believed to be a gateway to the underworld), which St. Paul may have referenced in Colossians 2:8 and 2:18 when warning against "philosophy and empty deceit."

### Hearer of St. John the Evangelist

The most important fact about Papias is preserved in his own words. In the preface to his work, he writes:

> "I will not hesitate to set down for you, along with my interpretations, everything I carefully learned from the presbyters and carefully remembered, guaranteeing its truth. For I did not take pleasure, as most do, in those who speak many things, but in those who teach the truth; not in those who recall foreign commandments, but in those who recall the commandments given by the Lord to the faith and proceeding from the Truth itself. And also, if ever a person came who had followed the presbyters, I inquired about the words of the presbyters: what Andrew or Peter said, or Philip or Thomas or James or John or Matthew, or any other of the Lord's disciples; and what Aristion and the Presbyter John, disciples of the Lord, say. For I did not suppose that information from books would help me so much as the word of a living and surviving voice."
> — Papias, fragment (cited in Eusebius, _Church History_, III, 39)

This passage reveals several crucial facts:

1. **Papias distinguished between two Johns**: the **Apostle John** (son of Zebedee) and the **Presbyter John** (another disciple of the Lord). This distinction, first noted by Eusebius, has been the subject of much scholarly debate regarding the authorship of the Gospel and Epistles of John.
2. **He preferred oral tradition** to written documents, a sentiment that reflects the early Church's understanding that apostolic teaching was first transmitted orally before being committed to writing.
3. **He gathered information** from those who had known the Apostles personally, making him a crucial link to the apostolic generation.

### The Connection to St. Polycarp

Eusebius notes that Papias was a contemporary of **St. Polycarp of Smyrna**, and that both were hearers of St. John. St. Irenaeus, in _Against Heresies_ (V, 33, 4), describes Papias as a "hearer of John and a companion of Polycarp," placing him firmly within the Johannine circle.

## 3. The _Exposition of the Oracles of the Lord_

### The Lost Work

The _Exposition of the Oracles of the Lord_ was a five-volume work, now lost, that combined:
- A collection of the sayings of Jesus (the "oracles" or _logia_)
- Papias's own interpretations and commentary
- Traditions about the origins of the Gospels
- Apocalyptic material about the future kingdom

The work was still extant in the Middle Ages and was consulted by later writers such as **Maximus the Confessor** and perhaps **Andrew of Caesarea**, but it subsequently disappeared. It is known today only through:
- Quotations in **Eusebius of Caesarea**, _Church History_ (c. 324 A.D.)
- A reference in **St. Irenaeus of Lyons**, _Against Heresies_ (c. 180 A.D.)
- A citation in **St. Apollinaris of Laodicea** (fourth century)
- A possible reference in **Andrew of Caesarea** (sixth century)

### Key Contributions

#### 1. On the Origin of the Gospel of Mark

The most precious fragment preserved from Papias concerns the origins of the Gospel of Mark:

> "Mark, having become the interpreter of Peter, wrote down accurately everything that he remembered of the things said and done by the Lord, but not in order. For he neither heard the Lord nor followed him, but later, as I said, he followed Peter, who adapted his teaching as needed, with no intention of giving an ordered account of the sayings of the Lord. So Mark made no mistake in writing down some things as he remembered them, for he made it his one concern not to omit anything he had heard or to falsify anything."
> — Papias, fragment (cited in Eusebius, _Church History_, III, 39, 15)

This testimony is invaluable for several reasons:

- **It confirms Petrine authorship**: The Gospel of Mark is directly linked to the preaching of St. Peter, the Prince of the Apostles.
- **It explains Mark's style**: Peter's preaching was adapted to his audience, not a strict chronological narrative, which accounts for Mark's distinctive "immediate" style and non-chronological ordering.
- **It affirms historical reliability**: Papias insists that Mark wrote accurately and without falsification.
- **It is early**: This tradition dates to within living memory of the Apostles themselves, making it far more reliable than later speculation.

#### 2. On the Origin of the Gospel of Matthew

Papias also preserves a tradition about the Gospel of Matthew:

> "Matthew compiled the sayings [of the Lord] in the Hebrew language, and everyone translated them as best they could."
> — Papias, fragment (cited in Eusebius, _Church History_, III, 39, 16)

This tradition—that Matthew first wrote his Gospel in Hebrew or Aramaic—was widely accepted in the early Church (Irenaeus, Origen, Eusebius, Jerome all repeat it). It explains certain features of Matthew's Gospel and confirms its apostolic authorship. Modern scholarship, however, generally holds that the canonical Gospel of Matthew was originally composed in Greek, though it may have drawn on earlier Semitic source material (the so-called "Q" source and/or an earlier Aramaic Matthew).

#### 3. The Preference for Oral Tradition

Papias's famous declaration about the living voice is a crucial patristic witness to the Catholic understanding of **Tradition**:

> "For I did not suppose that information from books would help me so much as the word of a living and surviving voice."

This does not mean that Papias disparaged Scripture—he was, after all, composing a written commentary on the Lord's sayings. Rather, he recognized that the **living teaching of the apostolic Church** is the authentic interpreter of the written Word, a principle later articulated by the Council of Trent and Vatican II:

> "Sacred Tradition and Sacred Scripture form one sacred deposit of the word of God, committed to the Church."
> — Vatican II, _Dei Verbum_, 10

#### 4. Chiliasm (Millenarianism)

Papias appears to have held a form of **chiliasm** (millenarianism)—the belief in a literal **thousand-year earthly reign of Christ** before the Last Judgment, based on Revelation 20:1–6. Eusebius, who was himself suspicious of millenarianism, criticizes Papias for this:

> "He [Papias] says that after the resurrection of the dead there will be a period of a thousand years, during which the kingdom of Christ will be set up in material form on this earth."
> — Eusebius, _Church History_, III, 39

Papias claimed to have received this teaching from the Apostles themselves, specifically citing a tradition about the supernatural fertility of the millennial kingdom that is also found in the Epistle of Barnabas and the apocryphal Apocalypse of Peter.

It is important to note:
- **Millenarianism was not a defined dogma** and has never been the official teaching of the Catholic Church.
- The Church's mainstream tradition has followed **St. Augustine** in interpreting the "thousand years" of Revelation 20 as a symbolic reference to the present age of the Church (amillennialism).
- Papias's chiliasm does not affect the reliability of his other traditions, and he held it in good faith, believing it to be apostolic in origin.
- The subject remains a matter of **theological opinion**, not defined doctrine (cf. [eschatology/doctrinal-foundations.md](../eschatology/doctrinal-foundations.md)).

#### 5. The Miraculous Grapevine

St. Irenaeus preserves from Papias a tradition about the supernatural fertility of the millennial kingdom:

> "The days will come when vines will grow, each having ten thousand branches, and on each branch ten thousand twigs, and on each twig ten thousand clusters, and on each cluster ten thousand grapes, and each grape, when pressed, will yield twenty-five measures of wine."
> — Papias, fragment (cited in Irenaeus, _Against Heresies_, V, 33, 3–4)

This tradition, which Papias attributed to the Apostles, illustrates the concrete, material character of his millenarian expectation.

#### 6. The Fate of Judas Iscariot

Papias preserves a vivid tradition about the death of Judas Iscariot that differs from the account in Acts 1:18:

> "Judas walked about in this world as a great example of impiety. He became so swollen in the flesh that he could not pass through a place where a wagon could easily pass... After many torments and punishments, he died, and his own body was so loathsome that the ground around it was foul for many generations."
> — Papias, fragment (cited in Apollinaris of Laodicea)

While not canonical, this tradition was widely repeated in the early Church.

## 4. Papias and the Canon of the New Testament

Papias's testimony is critical for establishing the early Church's recognition of the canonical Gospels:

| Gospel | Papias's Testimony | Implication |
|--------|-------------------|-------------|
| **Mark** | Written by Mark, interpreter of Peter, based on Peter's preaching | Petrine authority; Mark as the earliest Gospel? |
| **Matthew** | Compiled the sayings of the Lord in Hebrew | Apostolic authorship; priority of Semitic tradition |
| **John** | (Indirectly) Papias moves in Johannine circles | The Johannine corpus emerges from the circle of the Beloved Disciple |
| **Luke** | (Not directly mentioned by Papias) | - |

Papias does not mention the Gospel of Luke or the Acts of the Apostles by name. However, his detailed interest in the sayings of Jesus and the ordering of the Gospel tradition places him at the center of the development of the canonical Gospel tradition.

## 5. Patristic Testimony to St. Papias

> "Papias, who was a hearer of John and a companion of Polycarp, an ancient man, testifies in the fourth of his books... He is also a witness of ancient tradition."
> — **St. Irenaeus of Lyons**, _Against Heresies_, V, 33, 4

> "There are extant five books of Papias, entitled _Exposition of the Oracles of the Lord_... He says that after the resurrection of the dead there will be a period of a thousand years."
> — **Eusebius of Caesarea**, _Church History_, III, 39

> "He [Papias] was a man of very small intelligence, to judge from his books."
> — **Eusebius of Caesarea**, _Church History_, III, 39 (a famously harsh judgment, likely motivated by Eusebius's opposition to millenarianism)

> "Papias, the disciple of John and bishop of Hierapolis in Asia, wrote five books entitled _Exposition of the Sayings of the Lord_."
> — **St. Jerome**, _Illustrious Men_, 18 (who softens Eusebius's criticism)

## 6. The Distinction Between the Apostle John and the Presbyter John

One of the most debated questions arising from Papias's fragments concerns his distinction between the **Apostle John** (son of Zebedee) and the **Presbyter John** (an otherwise unknown disciple of the Lord).

Papias writes: "What Andrew or Peter said... and what **Aristion** and the **Presbyter John**, disciples of the Lord, say." The distinction suggests:

- The **Apostle John** was one of the Twelve and the author of the Fourth Gospel, the Epistles, and Revelation (though some scholars assign Revelation to the Presbyter).
- The **Presbyter John** was a separate figure—a disciple of the Lord but not one of the Twelve—who may have been an eyewitness of Christ and a teacher in the early Church.

This distinction has led some scholars to propose that the Gospel of John and the Book of Revelation were written by different authors (the "Johannine community" hypothesis). The Catholic Church, however, has consistently affirmed the apostolic authorship of the Fourth Gospel and the unity of the Johannine corpus, a position supported by the unanimous testimony of the early Church (Irenaeus, Clement of Alexandria, Tertullian, Origen, Eusebius himself).

---

## 7. The Question of Papias's Orthodoxy

Eusebius of Caesarea—the principal source for Papias's fragments—judged Papias to be a "man of very small intelligence," largely because of his millenarianism. Modern scholarship has generally been more charitable, recognizing that:

1. **Millenarianism was not heretical** in the second century; many orthodox writers held forms of it (Justin Martyr, Irenaeus, Tertullian).
2. **Papias was primarily a collector** of traditions, not a systematic theologian. His value lies in what he preserves, not in his own interpretations.
3. **His testimony to the Gospels** is independent of his millenarianism and remains historically valuable.

The Catholic Church has not pronounced any judgment on Papias's orthodoxy, but his millenarian views are classified as **theological opinion** that did not enter the mainstream of Catholic teaching.

---

## 8. Legacy

Despite the fragmentary survival of his work, St. Papias of Hierapolis remains a figure of singular importance:

1. **Witness to Gospel Origins:** He provides the earliest and most reliable patristic testimony to the authorship of the Gospels of Mark and Matthew, confirming their apostolic foundation.
2. **Bridge to the Apostolic Age:** As a hearer of St. John, he represents the last living connection to the eyewitness generation, actively collecting their testimony.
3. **Witness to Oral Tradition:** His preference for the "living voice" over written documents is an important patristic precedent for the Catholic doctrine of Sacred Tradition as the Church's living interpretation of Scripture.
4. **Preserver of Johannine Tradition:** Alongside St. Polycarp, he ensures the transmission of the Johannine tradition to subsequent generations, particularly through St. Irenaeus.
