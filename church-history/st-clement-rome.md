# St. Clement of Rome: Third Successor of St. Peter and Witness to Apostolic Succession

> **Note:** This document was composed without Magisterium AI retrieval. Citations should be verified against primary sources where possible.

---

## 1. Introduction

**St. Clement of Rome** (Latin: _Clemens Romanus_; Greek: _Κλήμης_; c. 35–99 A.D.), also known as **Clement I** and **Pope St. Clement I**, was the **third Bishop of Rome** after St. Peter, serving as the visible head of the Catholic Church from approximately **c. 88 to 99 A.D.** He is the first Apostolic Father for whom we have extant writings, and his _First Epistle to the Corinthians_ (_1 Clement_) is one of the most precious documents of Christian antiquity—a letter that provides the earliest explicit witness to the doctrines of **apostolic succession** and **Roman primacy** outside of the New Testament.

St. Clement is commemorated in the **Roman Canon** (Eucharistic Prayer I) of the Latin Mass alongside St. Linus and St. Cletus (Anacletus), and his feast day is celebrated on **23 November** in the Latin Rite and on **25 November** in the Byzantine Rite.

---

## 2. Life and Identity

### Dates and Lineage

The exact dates of Clement's birth and death are uncertain. He was likely born in **Rome** around **35 A.D.** , possibly to a Jewish or Greco-Roman family of some standing. His death is traditionally dated to **99 A.D.** , during the reign of Emperor Trajan.

### Apostolic Connection

The early Church unanimously testifies to Clement's close connection with the Apostles:

- **St. Irenaeus of Lyons** (c. 180 A.D.) writes that Clement "had seen the blessed Apostles and conferred with them, and still had their preaching in his ears and their tradition before his eyes" (_Against Heresies_, III, 3, 3).
- **Eusebius of Caesarea** (_Church History_, III, 15) identifies Clement as the companion of St. Paul mentioned in **Philippians 4:3** ("whose names are in the book of life"). While this identification is not certain, it was widely accepted in antiquity.
- **Tertullian** and **St. Jerome** record that Clement was consecrated as bishop by **St. Peter the Apostle** himself.
- **Origen** and **Epiphanius** also affirm Clement's connection to the Apostles.

### The Succession of the Roman See

The early episcopal succession in Rome is preserved by St. Irenaeus and others:

$$ \text{St. Peter (Prince of the Apostles)} \longrightarrow \text{St. Linus} \longrightarrow \text{St. Anacletus (Cletus)} \longrightarrow \text{St. Clement I} $$

St. Linus is mentioned in 2 Timothy 4:21. St. Clement thus stands as the **third successor of St. Peter**, occupying the See of Rome in the generation immediately after the Apostles.

### Martyrdom

According to tradition, Clement was martyred under the Emperor **Trajan** (c. 99 A.D.) by being **tied to an iron anchor** and thrown into the Black Sea near the Greek city of Chersonesus (in modern-day Crimea). His body was later recovered and enshrined:

- The **anchor** is his most common iconographic symbol and is a permanent witness to the manner of his death.
- Legend holds that the sea receded three miles to reveal his body in a stone-built shrine. This miracle was a source of popular devotion throughout the Middle Ages.
- His relics were translated to Rome by **St. Cyril and St. Methodius** (the Apostles to the Slavs) in the ninth century and are now enshrined in the **Basilica of San Clemente al Laterano** in Rome, one of the most ancient and beautiful of the Roman titular churches.

## 3. The _First Epistle of Clement_ (1 Clement)

### Date and Occasion

**Date:** c. 96 A.D. (during the reign of Emperor Domitian, who conducted a persecution of the Church).

**Occasion:** A serious disturbance had arisen in the Church of **Corinth** (the same city to which St. Paul had written his two canonical letters). A faction within the Church had unlawfully deposed several presbyters (elders/bishops) from their office, apparently driven by jealousy and ambition. Hearing of this, the Church of Rome—through its bishop Clement—intervened with an authoritative letter of correction.

The letter is written in the name of **"the Church of God which sojourns in Rome to the Church of God which sojourns in Corinth"**, but its authoritative tone and the identity of its author were never in doubt.

### Content and Structure

_1 Clement_ is a lengthy letter of 65 chapters. Its structure follows a deliberate rhetorical pattern:

| Section | Chapters | Content |
|---------|----------|---------|
| **Introduction** | 1–2 | Praise for the Corinthian Church's former virtue |
| **The Corinthian Schism** | 3–6 | Jealousy as the root of the conflict; examples from the Old Testament |
| **Call to Penitence** | 7–8 | Exhortation to repentance |
| **Unity and Order** | 9–39 | A grand theological exposition on God's created order, the harmony of the universe, the resurrection, and the need for hierarchical order in the Church |
| **Apostolic Succession** | 40–44 | The heart of the letter: the divine institution of Church offices |
| **Exhortations to Virtue** | 45–57 | Examples of faith, humility, and obedience from Scripture |
| **Liturgical Prayers** | 58–61 | A beautiful closing prayer for rulers and the world |
| **Conclusion** | 62–65 | Final appeal and the dispatch of bearers |

### Key Doctrinal Contributions

#### 1. Apostolic Succession (Chapters 40–44)

This is the most important passage in all the Apostolic Fathers for the doctrine of apostolic succession. Clement writes:

> "The Apostles have preached the Gospel to us from the Lord Jesus Christ; Jesus Christ was sent from God. Christ therefore is from God, and the Apostles are from Christ... They appointed their first converts, after testing them by the Spirit, as bishops and deacons of the future believers."

Clement then explains that the Apostles, foreseeing that conflict would arise over the office of the bishop, "gave further instructions that when these should fall asleep, other approved men should succeed them in their ministry."

Therefore, to remove from office those who have "blamelessly and holily" offered the gifts (the Eucharistic sacrifice) is to commit a grave sin against God's established order. The deposed clergy must be restored.

#### 2. Roman Primacy

The tone and substance of _1 Clement_ are remarkable evidence of the early Church's recognition of Roman primacy:

- **Clement intervenes** in the affairs of a distant Church (Corinth is about 800 km from Rome by sea) without any apology for doing so.
- **He writes with authority**, commanding obedience: "You will give us joy and gladness if you obey what we have written through the Holy Spirit" (Chapter 63).
- **The Corinthian Church receives** the letter without any objection to Rome's authority—there is no hint that Clement was acting beyond his competence.
- **The letter was so revered** that it was read liturgically in many churches (including Corinth) for centuries, and it is included in the **Codex Alexandrinus** (fifth century), one of the great early manuscripts of the Greek Bible.

#### 3. The Eucharist as Sacrifice

Clement uses explicitly sacrificial language to describe the Eucharistic liturgy. He speaks of the presbyters who have "offered the gifts" (Chapter 44) and connects the Church's liturgy to the Old Testament sacrificial system (Chapters 40–41), where each person—high priest, priest, and Levite—had their proper office in the liturgy.

This confirms that the early Church understood the Eucharist as a **true sacrifice**, not merely a memorial meal.

#### 4. The Resurrection of the Body (Chapters 24–26)

Clement provides an extended argument for the resurrection, drawing analogies from nature: the cycle of day and night, the germination of seeds, and the mythical phoenix (which was said to rise from its own ashes after 500 years). He writes:

> "Let us consider, beloved, how the Lord continually proves to us that there will be a resurrection of the dead... Day and night show us a resurrection: the night sleeps, the day arises."

#### 5. The Unity of the Old and New Testaments

Clement cites extensively from the Old Testament (especially Isaiah, the Psalms, and the wisdom literature), treating it as the inspired Word of God. He also cites from the New Testament (including the words of Jesus, Paul's letters, and Hebrews), demonstrating that the Old and New Testaments were already being read together as the single, unified Word of God.

#### 6. The Prayer for Civil Authorities (Chapter 60–61)

Clement offers a beautiful prayer for rulers and civil authorities, prefiguring the prayer of the Church in 1 Timothy 2:1–2:

> "Grant to them, Lord, health, peace, concord, and stability, that they may administer the government which You have given them without offense."

## 4. The So-Called _Second Epistle of Clement_ (2 Clement)

The document known as _2 Clement_ is **not by Clement of Rome**. It is the transcript of a homily delivered by an unknown Corinthian bishop or presbyter, dating from **c. 140–150 A.D.** It is nonetheless the **oldest surviving Christian sermon** outside of the New Testament.

Its themes include:

- **Repentance and conversion** in the present life
- **The incarnation as a revelation** of the Father's love
- **The necessity of good works** flowing from faith
- **The resurrection of the flesh** (against Gnostic denials)

---

## 5. Other Writings Falsely Attributed to Clement

Several other works were later attributed to Clement, forming part of the pseudo-Clementine literature:

- **The _Clementine Homilies_ and _Recognitions_** (fourth century): An elaborate Jewish-Christian romance novel that uses Clement as its protagonist.
- **The _Apostolic Constitutions_** (fourth century): A collection of ecclesiastical law and liturgy, later recognized as not authentic.
- **The _Clementine Liturgy_**: A liturgy attributed to Clement but of later composition.

None of these works are genuine, but their attribution demonstrates the immense authority Clement enjoyed in the early Church.

## 6. Patristic Testimony to St. Clement

> "This Clement... had seen the blessed Apostles and conferred with them, and still had their preaching in his ears and their tradition before his eyes."
> — **St. Irenaeus of Lyons**, _Against Heresies_, III, 3, 3

> "Clement, the third bishop of Rome after Peter, if indeed the second was Linus, and the third was Clement... wrote an excellent letter to the Corinthians."
> — **Eusebius of Caesarea**, _Church History_, III, 4

> "Clement, of whom the Apostle Paul writes in Philippians 4:3, was the fourth bishop of Rome (counting Peter as first)."
> — **St. Jerome**, _Illustrious Men_, 15

> "The letter to the Corinthians... is read publicly in many churches."
> — **Dionysius of Corinth** (c. 170 A.D.), cited in Eusebius, _Church History_, IV, 23

## 7. Legacy

St. Clement of Rome is a monumental figure in the history of the Church for the following reasons:

1. **Apostolic Succession:** He provides the earliest extra-biblical evidence for the apostolic succession of bishops—the foundational Catholic principle that the authority of the Apostles has been handed down through an unbroken line of episcopal ordination.

2. **Roman Primacy:** He exercised jurisdictional authority over a distant Church, demonstrating that the Church of Rome already claimed and exercised a primacy of governance in the late first century.

3. **Bridge Figure:** He connects the apostolic age (Sts. Peter and Paul) to the later patristic age (Sts. Ignatius, Polycarp, Irenaeus).

4. **Patronage:** He is the patron saint of **mariners, stone-cutters, and blacksmiths** (because of the anchor of his martyrdom). He is also a patron saint of the **Slavic peoples** (because Sts. Cyril and Methodius translated his relics and his writings).

5. **Liturgical Commemoration:** He is one of the few early popes commemorated by name in the Roman Canon of the Mass.
