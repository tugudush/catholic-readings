# Hierarchy and Choirs of Angels in Catholic Tradition

In Catholic theology, angels are purely spiritual, personal, and immortal creatures possessing intelligence and free will (Catechism of the Catholic Church [CCC 330]). While the existence of angels is a dogma of faith defined by the Church, the specific classification and hierarchy of angels are regarded as highly respected theological opinions and traditional doctrine rather than strictly binding dogmatic definitions.

---

## The Classification of the Angelic Hierarchy: The Nine Choirs

The traditional schema of the angelic hierarchy—grouping angels into **nine "choirs"** (or orders)—was popularized primarily by the sixth-century Christian Neoplatonist writer **Pseudo-Dionysius the Areopagite** in his treatise _De Coelesti Hierarchia_ (_On the Heavenly Hierarchy_). This structure was later synthesized and deeply integrated into Western theology by **St. Gregory the Great** and **St. Thomas Aquinas** (the _Doctor Angelicus_).

The nine choirs are systematically ordered into **three distinct triads** (or hierarchies), descending in terms of their closeness to God and their specific ministries:

### 1. The First Triad: Dedicated to the Direct Contemplation of God

These angels are closest to the throne of God, entirely consumed by love, contemplation, and praise of the Divine Majesty.

- **Seraphim (The "Burning Ones"):** Mentioned in Isaiah 6:2-6, they are the highest choir. They burn with the ultimate fire of divine love and charity, standing closest to God's presence, continuously chanting the _Sanctus_ (_Holy, Holy, Holy_).
- **Cherubim (The "Fullness of Wisdom"):** Mentioned in Genesis 3:24 and Ezekiel 10, they are associated with the profound contemplation of God’s wisdom and divine plan. They are messengers of God’s power and are often depicted on the Ark of the Covenant.
- **Thrones:** Mentioned in Colossians 1:16, these angels represent God's majesty, divine justice, and stability. They serve as "living thrones" reflecting the sovereign judgment of the Almighty.

### 2. The Second Triad: Ordering and Directing the Universe

These choirs are charged with the ordering, governance, and preservation of the created universe.

- **Dominations (or Dominions):** Mentioned in Colossians 1:16 and Ephesians 1:21, they direct the offices and ministries of lower angelic choirs. They represent the sovereignty, majesty, and ordering power of God over all of creation.
- **Virtues:** Mentioned in Ephesians 1:21 and 1 Peter 3:22, they are associated with the movement of heavenly bodies, the implementation of miracles, and imparting strength (virtue) and courage to human beings during great tasks or trials.
- **Powers:** Mentioned in Colossians 1:16 and Ephesians 3:10, they have the special duty of keeping the physical world aligned with cosmic order and protecting humanity by keeping demonic/fallen powers in check.

### 3. The Third Triad: Direct Ministry to Humanity and Earthly Affairs

These angels are the primary executors of God’s direct orders and interact most intimately with human affairs.

- **Principalities:** Mentioned in Colossians 1:16 and Ephesians 3:10, they are the guardians of nations, empires, cities, dioceses, and religious institutions. They oversee and guide human leaders to govern according to God’s laws.
- **Archangels:** Classed both as a specific choir in the hierarchy (just above common Angels) and as a general term for great messengers. They are given major divine tasks that affect the salvation of humanity.
- **Angels (or Ordinary Angels):** The lowest choir, yet the closest to human individuals on a daily basis. They act as messengers of lesser divine truths and serve as **Guardian Angels** assigned to protect and guide individual human beings.

## Popular and Famous Angels in Catholic Piety and Scripture

The Catholic Church exercises great sobriety and prudence regarding angels. The **Directory on Popular Piety and the Liturgy (No. 217)** explicitly cautions:

> _"The practice of assigning names to the Holy Angels should be discouraged, except in the cases of Gabriel, Raphael and Michael whose names are contained in Holy Scripture."_

Therefore, only these three named Archangels are liturgically venerated by name in the Catholic Church:

### 1. Saint Michael the Archangel ("Who is like God?")

- **Scriptural Basis:** Daniel 10:13, Daniel 12:1, Jude 1:9, Revelation 12:7-9.
- **Role & Tradition:** St. Michael is the Prince of the Heavenly Host, the defender of the Church against the onslaughts of Satan and the fallen angels. He is traditionally depicted as a warrior clad in armor casting Satan out of Heaven.
- **Patronage:** Soldiers, police officers, EMTs, mariners, and the dying. Special prayer is often offered to him after Mass (the St. Michael Prayer).

### 2. Saint Gabriel the Archangel ("God is my strength" or "Hero of God")

- **Scriptural Basis:** Daniel 8:16, Daniel 9:21, Luke 1:11-20 (to Zechariah), Luke 1:26-38 (The Annunciation to Mary).
- **Role & Tradition:** St. Gabriel is the supreme messenger of the Incarnation. He announced the birth of St. John the Baptist and, most famously, delivered the Annunciation to the Blessed Virgin Mary, prompting her _Fiat_.
- **Patronage:** Communications workers, postal workers, ambassadors, and broadcasters.

### 3. Saint Raphael the Archangel ("God heals")

- **Scriptural Basis:** The Book of Tobit (specifically Tobit 5-12).
- **Role & Tradition:** St. Raphael is the angel of healing and companionship. In the Book of Tobit, he disguises himself as Azarias to guide the young Tobias on his long journey, heals Tobit’s blindness, and expels the demon Asmodeus.
- **Patronage:** Travelers, the sick, physicians, blind people, matched couples, and Christian youth.

## Key Catholic Theological Distinctions

- **Dogma:** That angels exist as created, non-corporeal, spiritual beings is a solemnly defined dogma/matter of faith (cf. Fourth Lateran Council and Pius XII's _Humani Generis_).
- **Doctrine vs. Theological Opinion:** The classic Neoplatonic scheme of the nine choirs is a deeply respected _theological opinion_ that has attained the weight of traditional _doctrine_ due to its widespread and consistent acceptance by the Fathers, Doctors (especially Aquinas), and its integration into the liturgy. However, the precise ordering of the triads is not a dogma of faith.
- **Guardian Angels:** The belief that every human being has a personal Guardian Angel is a deeply rooted doctrine of the Church, celebrated with its own liturgical feast day on October 2nd (CCC 336).

## The Fall of Lucifer and the Question of His Original Angelic Choir

A longstanding theological question concerns the original angelic rank of **Satan (Lucifer)** before his fall — specifically, whether he belonged to the **Cherubim**, the **Seraphim**, or some other choir. The Church has never defined this as a matter of faith, and the tradition records a range of opinions.

### Scriptural Foundations

Two Old Testament passages, traditionally interpreted by the Fathers and Doctors as typologically referring to the fall of the devil, provide the primary scriptural data:

- **Ezekiel 28:12–17** — The lamentation over the king of Tyre describes a being who was "an **anointed cherub**" (v. 14, Hebrew: _kerub_), covered with precious stones, walking in Eden, the garden of God, until iniquity was found in him and he was cast down. This is the **closest biblical association** of Satan with a specific angelic order. The passage describes the cherub as "full of wisdom and perfect in beauty," dwelling "upon the holy mountain of God," and walking "among the stones of fire."
- **Isaiah 14:12–15** — The taunt against the king of Babylon addresses "**Lucifer**" (Latin Vulgate: _lucifer_, "light-bearer"; Hebrew: _helel_, "shining one"), the son of the dawn, who boasts "I will ascend to heaven; I will exalt my throne above the stars of God… I will be like the Most High" and is cast down to Sheol. This passage does not name a specific choir but implies an exalted rank.
- **Revelation 12:7–9** — St. John describes a war in heaven: "Michael and his angels fought against the dragon, and the dragon and his angels fought back, but they were defeated, and there was no longer any place for them in heaven." The passage confirms Satan's leadership over a multitude of fallen angels but gives no information about his original order.

### Patristic and Theological Tradition

The Church Fathers and later theologians are divided on the question:

| ---------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Cherub (Ezekiel's "anointed cherub")** | The plain sense of Ezekiel 28:14, 16 calls the prince of Tyre (understood typologically) a "cherub." ### St. Thomas Aquinas on Satan's Original State

St. Thomas Aquinas (_Summa Theologiae_ I, q. 63, a. 7) holds that **the highest angel among those who fell was of the lower order of the highest hierarchy** — that is, Satan belonged to the **Cherubim** (third choir of the first triad) rather than the Seraphim (first choir). Thomas reasons that the Seraphim, whose very name means "the burning ones," are so inflamed with the fire of divine charity that they could not have sinned. Since Satan's sin was one of pride rather than malice (_ex superbia, non ex malitia_), he must have been of a high but not the highest order — specifically, a Cherub, the order most associated with wisdom, whose sin would be an intellectual pride rather than a defect of love.

### Pope St. Gregory the Great's Position

Pope St. Gregory the Great (_Moralia in Job_ XXXIV) explicitly identifies the "anointed cherub" of Ezekiel 28 as the devil before his fall, noting that he was "set above the other choirs of angels" and "adorned with every beauty," but that his beauty led to pride and his pride to ruin.

### The _Catholic Encyclopedia_ on the Question

> _"Many theologians hold that Lucifer was the foremost of the angels before his fall; others maintain that he was not of the highest hierarchy (Seraphim, Cherubim, and Thrones), but of a lower order."_
> — _Catholic Encyclopedia_, s.v. "Devil"

The _Encyclopedia_ further notes that the Church has never pronounced on the matter, and both opinions remain permissible.

### Summary

| Position                               | Certainty                    | Basis                                                                                                                              |
| -------------------------------------- | ---------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Satan was originally a **cherub**      | Most common traditional view | Ezekiel 28:14, 16 (explicit "cherub" language); supported by St. Gregory the Great and St. Thomas Aquinas.                         |
| Satan was originally a **seraph**      | Theological opinion          | The magnitude of his fall suggests the highest possible origin; supported by some Fathers and medieval commentators (St. Bernard). |
| Satan was not among the highest choirs | Permissible minority opinion | The perfect charity of the Seraphim and Cherubim would have prevented any fall.                                                    |

**Conclusion:** The Church **does not definitively teach** whether Satan/Lucifer was a cherub, a seraph, or of some other order. The **most common and best-supported traditional view**, grounded in Ezekiel 28 and endorsed by St. Gregory the Great and St. Thomas Aquinas, is that he was an **anointed cherub** — a member of the second-highest choir of the first triad. This is a respected theological conclusion, but not a dogma of faith, and the faithful are free to hold other permissible opinions on the matter.

## Does Satan Retain His Rank and Power After the Fall?

A closely related question follows from the discussion above: now that Satan has fallen, **does he retain the natural superiority of his original high rank, or does the fall strip him of his angelic power?** The answer involves a key distinction between natural gifts and supernatural grace.

### The Fall Impairs Grace, Not Nature

Catholic doctrine distinguishes between an angel's **natural perfection** (its created spiritual intelligence, strength, and mode of acting) and its **supernatural life** (grace, friendship with God, and beatitude). The fall severs the latter but leaves the former intact.

The *Catholic Encyclopedia* (s.v. "Devil") states this explicitly:

> *"The fall of the angels did not impair their natural powers... Lucifer still retained the gifts that enabled him to influence his brethren before their fall."*

The Catechism (CCC 391–395) confirms that the fallen angels were **created good by nature** and **became evil by their own doing** — their nature is not destroyed, only their orientation to God.

### The Natural Hierarchy Persists After the Fall

St. Thomas Aquinas (*Summa Theologiae* I, q. 109, a. 1) teaches that the natural inequality among angels persists even after the fall. Since sin does not destroy nature, the differences in natural capacity between a high-ranking angel (such as a Cherub) and a lower-ranking one (such as an Archangel or Guardian Angel) remain. On this view:

- A **fallen Cherub** (Satan, according to the most common traditional view) retains the **native intellectual power, spiritual strength, and capacity** of the Cherubic order.
- This means he remains **naturally superior** in these respects to an **unfallen Archangel** (e.g., St. Michael, St. Gabriel, St. Raphael) and even more so to a **common Guardian Angel** (the lowest choir).

St. Thomas further notes that God's providence permits an ordering of authority among the wicked for the "training of men and the punishment of the damned" — so the natural rank among fallen angels has consequences even after the fall.

### Three Critical Qualifications

While Satan retains his natural superiority, Catholic tradition introduces three qualifications that prevent a simplistic "power ranking":

**1. Victory comes through God, not natural rank**

In Revelation 12:7–9, it is **St. Michael and his angels** — St. Michael is an **Archangel**, of the second-lowest choir — who defeat Satan and cast him out of heaven. This is not because Archangels naturally outrank Cherubim, but because they fight **under God's authority**. The victory is God's, not a matter of angelic power tiers. As St. Paul writes, *"The God of peace will soon crush Satan under your feet"* (Rom 16:20).

**2. The unfallen angels are confirmed in grace**

While Satan lost grace irrevocably, the good angels who remained faithful were **confirmed in grace** by God after the trial of the faithful angels (Augustine, *Enchiridion* 28; cf. CCC 393). This means the good angels possess something Satan cannot have: **union with God**, which elevates and perfects their natural operations. An unfallen Guardian Angel, though lower by nature, acts as an instrument of divine omnipotence.

**3. Satan acts only by divine permission**

The Catechism (CCC 395) teaches that Satan's power is **not infinite** — he cannot prevent the building of God's Kingdom. He *"acts only by God's permission"* and is ultimately subordinate to divine Providence. A Guardian Angel, by contrast, acts in **direct obedience to God's will** for the salvation of the soul entrusted to its care.

### Summary Comparison

| Dimension | Satan (Fallen Cherub) | St. Michael (Unfallen Archangel) | Guardian Angel (Unfallen, lowest choir) |
|-----------|----------------------|----------------------------------|----------------------------------------|
| **Natural rank** | Higher (Cherub, first triad) | Lower (Archangel, third triad) | Lowest (Angel, third triad) |
| **Natural power** | Retains full native capacity | Full native capacity | Full native capacity |
| **Supernatural grace** | Irreparably lost | Confirmed in grace | Confirmed in grace |
| **Source of victory** | Defeated by God's power | Triumphs by God's authority, not own rank | Protects the soul by God's authority |
| **Scope of action** | Acts only by divine permission | Acts in direct obedience to God | Acts in direct obedience to God |

**Bottom line:** Yes, Satan retains the **natural superiority** of his original high rank — he is naturally more powerful than an Archangel or a Guardian Angel. But that does not mean he can overcome them. The good angels, though lower in natural order, are **confirmed in grace**, act under **God's direct authority**, and ultimately prevail because their power is not their own — it is God's. As the *Catholic Encyclopedia* summarizes: *"The devil's power is great, but it is not equal to God's; it is limited and can be overcome by the grace of Christ."*
