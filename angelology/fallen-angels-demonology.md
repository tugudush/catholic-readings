# Catholic View of Fallen Angels and the Nephilim

## Short Answer

Catholic teaching is clear about fallen angels, but not dogmatic about the Nephilim.

- Fallen angels are real.
- Satan and the demons are fallen angels.
- Angels are purely spiritual creatures, not bodily beings.
- The Nephilim in Genesis 6 are mentioned in Scripture, but the Church has not defined a single binding interpretation of who they were.

## What the Church Clearly Teaches

The Catholic Church teaches that angels are real, personal, immortal spiritual creatures.

> "As purely spiritual creatures angels have intelligence and will: they are personal and immortal creatures..."
>
> Catechism of the Catholic Church 330

The Church also teaches that some angels freely rebelled against God and became demons.

> "Satan or the devil and the other demons are fallen angels who have freely refused to serve God and his plan. Their choice against God is definitive."
>
> Catechism of the Catholic Church 414

The Catechism also says:

> "The devil and the other demons were indeed created naturally good by God, but they became evil by their own doing."
>
> Catechism of the Catholic Church 391, citing Lateran IV

So, in Catholic doctrine:

- demons are not symbols or myths;
- they are fallen angels;
- they were created good by God;
- they became evil by their own free choice.

## What the Church Does Not Define About the Nephilim

Genesis 6:1-4 speaks of the "sons of God," the "daughters of men," and the Nephilim. Catholics accept this passage as inspired Scripture, but the Church has not issued a dogmatic definition settling exactly who the "sons of God" were or exactly how the Nephilim should be identified.

That means Catholics should distinguish between:

- doctrine, which is binding;
- theological interpretation, which can vary within the bounds of the faith.

## Common Catholic Approach to Genesis 6

Because the Church teaches that angels are purely spiritual beings, Catholics are not required to believe that fallen angels physically married women and produced hybrid offspring.

This caution also fits Christ's teaching that angels do not marry.

- Matthew 22:30
- Luke 20:35-36

For that reason, many Catholic commentators have preferred human-centered readings of Genesis 6, such as:

- the "sons of God" were the descendants of Seth intermarrying with the ungodly line of Cain;
- the Nephilim were giants, warriors, tyrants, or men of great violence and renown.

These are interpretations, not defined dogmas.

## What Catholics May Safely Say

Catholics may say the following with confidence:

- Fallen angels are real and are called demons.
- Satan is a fallen angel.
- Angels are spiritual beings, not biological creatures.
- Genesis 6 is true Scripture and should be read within the whole of Scripture and the Church's Tradition.
- The exact identity of the Nephilim is not defined by the Magisterium.

## What Catholics Should Avoid

Catholics should avoid presenting speculation as doctrine, especially claims such as:

- "The Church teaches Nephilim were angel-human hybrids."
- "Catholics must believe Genesis 6 proves fallen angels had bodies and children."
- "All ancient giant lore is established Catholic doctrine."

Those claims go beyond what the Church has actually taught.

## Practical Summary

The Catholic position is straightforward:

- Fallen angels are a matter of Catholic doctrine.
- The Nephilim are a matter of biblical interpretation.
- Any interpretation of Genesis 6 must remain consistent with Catholic teaching on the nature of angels.

## Demon Hierarchy in Catholic Theology

### What the Church Affirms

The Catholic Church does **not** teach a detailed, binding taxonomy of demonic ranks. The Church's authoritative teaching affirms the reality of fallen angels without speculating on an elaborate "bureaucracy" of hell. What it does confirm is:

- **Satan (the Devil)** is the chief of the demons (CCC 414).
- **Other demons** are subordinate fallen angels who, like Satan, definitively rejected God.
- The biblical category **"Legion"** (Mark 5:9, Luke 8:30) indicates that multiple demons can operate collectively.
- **Fallen angels retain their natural angelic powers** — the fall impairs grace, not nature (see "Retained Natural Powers After the Fall" below).

The Church's official exorcism rite, _De Exorcismis et Supplicationibus Quibusdam_ (1999), invokes authority over "every unclean spirit, every power of darkness, every infernal assault of the adversary, every legion, company and diabolical faction" — comprehensive biblical language rather than a numbered rank chart.

### The Theological Opinion of St. Thomas Aquinas

While not a defined dogma, the theological reasoning of St. Thomas Aquinas (the _Doctor Angelicus_) offers a substantial traditional reflection on order among demons:

- **By nature**: Some fallen angels were created higher than others in the angelic hierarchy. Since sin does not destroy nature, the natural inequality among angels persists even after the fall.
- **By divine permission**: God's providence permits an ordering of authority among the wicked for the "training of men and the punishment of the damned."
- **By confederation**: Demons share a common enmity against God and humanity, and thus may be said to form a confederation to attack more effectively.

Aquinas is careful to note, however, that the order directed toward beatitude (holiness and grace) is **irreparably lost** by the fallen angels. Whatever authority they retain does not mitigate their punishment — "the higher their office, the more severe the torment."

### Retained Natural Powers After the Fall

A key principle in Catholic angelology is that **the fall of the angels did not impair their natural powers** — only their supernatural relationship with God was lost.

#### The Distinction Between Nature and Grace

Catholic doctrine draws a clear line between:

- **Natural perfection**: what an angel is and can do by its created nature — its spiritual intelligence, strength, mode of acting, and capacity for knowledge.
- **Supernatural life**: grace, friendship with God, and the capacity for beatitude — a gratuitous gift above and beyond created nature.

The fall severs the latter definitively but leaves the former intact. The *Catholic Encyclopedia* (s.v. "Devil") states:

> *"The fall of the angels did not impair their natural powers... Lucifer still retained the gifts that enabled him to influence his brethren before their fall."*

The Catechism (CCC 391, citing the Fourth Lateran Council) affirms that the devil and the other demons *"were indeed created naturally good by God, but they became evil by their own doing."* Their nature, being created good, remains even after their sin.

#### Implications for Satan's Power Relative to Unfallen Angels

Since the natural hierarchy among angels persists after the fall (per St. Thomas Aquinas, *ST* I, q. 109, a. 1), a fallen angel of a higher original choir retains a **greater native capacity** than an unfallen angel of a lower choir:

- **Satan** (likely a Cherub or Seraph before his fall, see [angelic-hierarchy-choirs.md](angelic-hierarchy-choirs.md)) retains the natural capacities of that high order — immense intelligence, spiritual strength, and influence.
- An **unfallen Guardian Angel** (the lowest choir) has less natural capacity by virtue of its lower place in the angelic hierarchy.
- An **unfallen Archangel** such as St. Michael has greater natural capacity than a Guardian Angel but still less than a fallen Cherub.

However, this natural superiority is **not the decisive factor** in spiritual combat, for three reasons:

1. **The good angels are confirmed in grace** (Augustine, *Enchiridion* 28; CCC 393). They possess something Satan irrevocably lacks: union with God, which elevates and perfects their natural operations.

2. **Satan acts only by divine permission** (CCC 395). His power is not infinite; he cannot prevent the building of God's Kingdom. The good angels, by contrast, act in direct obedience to God's will.

3. **Victory belongs to God, not to natural rank**. In Revelation 12:7–9, St. Michael the Archangel (of the second-lowest choir) defeats Satan and casts him out of heaven — not because Archangels naturally outrank Cherubim, but because Michael fights under God's authority.

#### Summary

| Aspect | Effect of the Fall |
|--------|-------------------|
| **Natural powers** (intelligence, strength, abilities) | Retained — not diminished |
| **Supernatural grace** (friendship with God, beatitude) | Irreparably lost |
| **Natural hierarchy among fallen angels** | Persists — some demons remain naturally higher than others |
| **Power relative to unfallen angels** | Naturally superior if originally higher-ranked, but constrained by divine Providence |
| **Ultimate authority** | Subject to God's permission; good angels act by God's command |

### Important Distinctions

- **Dogma**: That angels (including fallen angels) exist as created, non-corporeal, spiritual beings is a defined truth of faith (Fourth Lateran Council, _Humani Generis_).
- **Theological opinion**: The precise ordering, ranking, and classification of demons — including any mapping to the nine choirs of angels or the seven deadly sins — is speculative theology, not binding doctrine.
- **Pastoral caution**: The Church warns against treating demonology as a "knowledge project." The Congregation for the Doctrine of the Faith (1985) instructed bishops to prevent assemblies where "demons are questioned directly and their identity sought to be known."

## Named Demons in Scripture and Catholic Tradition

The Catholic Church exercises sobriety in identifying demons by name. Only a few appear in Sacred Scripture, and the Church does not add to this list through private revelation. Below are the principal named figures in order of their scriptural prominence.

### 1. Satan / the Devil

- **Scriptural Basis**: Genesis 3:1-15, Job 1-2, 1 Chronicles 21:1, Zechariah 3:1-2, Matthew 4:1-11, Luke 4:1-13, Luke 10:18, John 8:44, Revelation 12:7-9, Revelation 20:1-10.
- **Meaning of the Name**: "Satan" (Hebrew: _ha-satan_, "the adversary"); "Devil" (Greek: _diabolos_, "slanderer" or "accuser").
- **Role in Salvation History**: Satan is the chief of the fallen angels, the original tempter of Adam and Eve, the accuser of Job, the tempter of Christ in the wilderness, and the "great dragon" cast out of heaven in the Apocalypse. He is described by Christ as "a murderer from the beginning" and "the father of lies" (John 8:44).
- **The "Strong Man"**: Jesus speaks of Satan as a "strong man" whose domain is opposed and plundered by the coming of the Kingdom of God (Matthew 12:29, Luke 11:21-22).
- **Definitive Defeat**: While permitted to act in the world, Satan's ultimate defeat is assured by Christ's Paschal Mystery. The Book of Revelation depicts his final condemnation to the lake of fire (Revelation 20:10).

### 2. Beelzebub / Beelzebul

- **Scriptural Basis**: Matthew 10:25, Matthew 12:24-27, Mark 3:22, Luke 11:15-19.
- **Meaning of the Name**: "Beelzebul" likely means "lord of the dwelling" or "prince" (a Hebrew pun on the Philistine god Baal-Zebub, "lord of the flies," found in 2 Kings 1:2-3).
- **Role in Scripture**: The Pharisees accuse Jesus of casting out demons "by Beelzebul, the ruler of the demons" (Matthew 12:24). St. Thomas Aquinas, in his _Commentary on Matthew_, identifies Beelzebub as "the prince of demons." The name functions in the Gospels as a title for the chief of the demons, closely associated with Satan himself.

### 3. Legion

- **Scriptural Basis**: Mark 5:1-20, Luke 8:26-39.
- **Meaning of the Name**: From the Latin _legio_, a Roman military unit of several thousand soldiers. It is not a personal name but a designation of multitude.
- **Role in Scripture**: The Gerasene demoniac, when asked his name by Jesus, replies "Legion, for we are many" (Mark 5:9). The episode demonstrates the reality of multiple demons possessing a single individual and the absolute authority of Christ over even a vast multitude of unclean spirits.
- **Catholic Interpretation**: The name indicates that demons can act collectively. Jesus expels them at His command, and they enter a herd of swine, which rushes into the sea.

### 4. Asmodeus

- **Scriptural Basis**: Tobit 3:7-8, Tobit 6:13-17, Tobit 8:1-3.
- **Meaning of the Name**: Most probably derived from a Hebrew root meaning "to destroy," relating Asmodeus to the broader biblical theme of destruction (cf. Abaddon, the "Destroyer").
- **Role in Scripture**: In the Book of Tobit (a deuterocanonical book of the Old Testament), Asmodeus is a "wicked demon" who kills seven successive husbands of Sarah before the marriage can be consummated. The Archangel Raphael is sent by God to deliver Sarah: he instructs Tobias to burn the heart and liver of a fish on embers, the odor of which repels the demon. Raphael then pursues Asmodeus and binds him "hand and foot" (Tobit 8:3).
- **Catholic Interpretation**: The Catholic Encyclopedia emphasizes that Tobit does **not** present Asmodeus as a jealous lover, but rather associates the demon's oppression with moral disorder. Deliverance comes through **prayer, chastity, and the ministry of the Archangel Raphael** — not through superstition or magical incantation.

### 5. Abaddon / Apollyon

- **Scriptural Basis**: Revelation 9:11.
- **Meaning of the Name**: "Abaddon" (Hebrew) means "ruin" or "destruction"; "Apollyon" (Greek) means "Destroyer."
- **Role in Scripture**: In the Apocalypse, Abaddon/Apollyon is identified as "the angel of the bottomless pit" and the king of the plague of demonic locusts released during the fifth trumpet.
- **Catholic Interpretation**: The Catholic Encyclopedia notes that some have asserted an identity between Abaddon and Asmodeus, but this remains unproven. Catholic tradition treats the name primarily as a description of the figure's destructive function within the apocalyptic vision.

### 6. "Lucifer" — The Traditional Latin Name

- **Scriptural Basis**: Isaiah 14:12-15 (the "Day Star, son of Dawn"), Ezekiel 28:12-19 (the "anointed cherub" cast out of Eden).
- **Meaning of the Name**: "Lucifer" (Latin: _lux_, "light" + _ferre_, "to bear" = "light-bearer") is the Vulgate's rendering of the Hebrew _helel_ ("Day Star" or "morning star") in Isaiah 14:12.
- **Patristic and Traditional Usage**: The Latin Church Fathers and subsequent tradition identified the "Day Star" of Isaiah 14 — who boasts "I will ascend to heaven; I will make myself like the Most High" — as the prideful rebellion of Satan. The name "Lucifer" thus became a traditional designation for Satan **before his fall**, emphasizing his original glory as a radiant cherub and the depth of his downfall through pride.
- **Theological Note**: This interpretation is a **typological** reading: Isaiah 14 primarily refers to the King of Babylon historically, but the Church has long read the passage as prefiguring the primordial rebellion and fall of the devil. Ezekiel 28, describing the King of Tyre as a once-blameless "anointed cherub" cast down for pride, is similarly applied to Satan's fall.

### Summary Table of Named Demons

| Name                   | Scripture                         | Meaning                                     | Role                                             |
| ---------------------- | --------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------ |
| **Satan / the Devil**  | Genesis, Job, Gospels, Revelation | "Adversary," "Slanderer"                    | Chief of the fallen angels; tempter; accuser     |
| **Beelzebub**          | Matthew, Mark, Luke               | "Lord of the dwelling" / "Prince of demons" | Ruler of demons (title used by Pharisees)        |
| **Legion**             | Mark, Luke                        | "Many" (Latin military unit)                | Collective of many demons                        |

## A Caution on Speculative Demonology

The Church consistently warns against excessive speculation about demonic affairs:

1. **No binding "rank chart"**: The Church has not dogmatically defined a demonic hierarchy mapped to the nine angelic choirs, the seven deadly sins, or any other scheme. Such classifications belong to the realm of theological opinion or popular piety, not to the deposit of faith.

2. **No mandatory belief in private revelations**: Although some private revelations (e.g., the reported messages of Fatima) mention demonic activity, belief in such details is not required for salvation. Private revelations never add to or correct the definitive public Revelation of Jesus Christ.

3. **Avoidance of superstition**: Catholic tradition rejects treating demons as beings to be manipulated through charms, incantations, or magical arts. The early Christian writer Origen, in _Contra Celsum_, warns that calling on demons by charms is incompatible with worship of the Most High God.

4. **Pastoral focus**: The Church's exorcism rite focuses on **deliverance and the power of Christ**, not on interrogating demons or cataloguing their identities. The 1985 CDF letter on norms for exorcism expressly discourages assemblies where "demons are questioned directly and their identity sought to be known."

> _"The devil and the other demons were indeed created naturally good by God, but they became evil by their own doing."_ — CCC 391, citing the Fourth Lateran Council
