# The Eucharistic Miracle of Bolsena-Orvieto (1263)

## Theological Introduction
Within Catholic Eucharistic theology, Eucharistic miracles are viewed as exceptional, visible signs designed to reinforce the central dogma of the **Real Presence** and the conversion of **Transubstantiation**:
- At the consecration of the Mass, the *substance* of bread and wine is converted into the *substance* of the Body and Blood of Christ, while the *accidents* (physical appearances like shape, taste, color, and texture) remain unchanged (Council of Trent, Session XIII, Decree on the Eucharist).
- This separation of accidents and substance persists through the supernatural, sustaining power of God.
- In a Eucharistic miracle, God allows the physical accidents themselves to temporarily change into the appearance of human flesh and blood, making the sacramental reality visible to strengthen human faith.

## The Event in Bolsena
In 1263, a German priest named Father Peter of Prague was traveling on a pilgrimage to Rome. During his journey, he stopped to celebrate Mass at the Basilica of Saint Christina in Bolsena, Italy.

### Key Historical Details:
1. **The Priest's Doubt**: Father Peter was an otherwise pious priest, but he struggled with a recurring and painful intellectual doubt regarding the truth of the Real Presence of Christ in the Eucharist after the consecration.
2. **The Bleeding Host**: During the celebration of the Mass, immediately after Father Peter pronounced the words of consecration, the Host began to bleed real blood.
3. **The Stained Corporal**: Drops of blood dripped from the Host onto his hands, the altar, and the linen **corporal** (the cloth spread on the altar to receive the Eucharistic vessels).
4. **The Interruption**: Terrified and deeply moved, Father Peter interrupted the Mass, wrapped the Host in the corporal, and immediately traveled to the nearby city of Orvieto, where Pope Urban IV was residing with his court.

## Papal Investigation and Universal Impact
- **The Investigation**: Pope Urban IV commissioned a thorough theological and canonical investigation. He sent the Bishop of Orvieto, along with a team of theologians, to Bolsena to inspect the Host, the corporal, and the testimonies of those present.
- **The Relics to Orvieto**: The miraculous corporal was brought to Orvieto. On inspection, the Pope declared the miracle authentic and knelt in reverence. He ordered the relics to be permanently preserved.
- **Institution of Corpus Christi (1264)**: Prompted by the miracle of Bolsena-Orvieto and the efforts of Saint Juliana of Cornillon, Pope Urban IV promulgated the Papal Bull *Transiturus de hoc mundo* on August 11, 1264. This papal act instituted the Solemnity of **Corpus Christi** (The Body and Blood of Christ) as a feast for the universal Church.
- **St. Thomas Aquinas's Office**: Pope Urban IV commissioned the Dominican theologian **Saint Thomas Aquinas** to write the liturgical Office and Mass for the new feast of Corpus Christi. This produced some of the Church's most beautiful hymns, including the *Adoro Te Devote*, *Pange Lingua* (including the *Tantum Ergo*), and *Panis Angelicus*.

## Devotion and Preservation
The stained linen corporal is still preserved and venerated today in the magnificent Gothic Cathedral of Orvieto (the Duomo), where it is housed in a golden reliquary. Multiple popes, including Saint John Paul II (in his homily at Orvieto on June 17, 1990) and Pope Benedict XVI, have made reference to the enduring spiritual power of the relic, which continues to invite the faithful to contemplate the self-giving love of Christ in the Sacrament of the Altar.
