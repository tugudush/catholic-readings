# Our Lady of La Salette (1846)

## Theological Introduction to Private Revelation
The apparition of Our Lady of La Salette has been declared worthy of belief by the competent ecclesiastical authority, placing it under the Catholic theology of **private revelation**:
- As stated in the Catechism of the Catholic Church (CCC 67), private revelations do not form part of the deposit of faith. They are meant to guide Christian living in specific historical epochs.
- The Church permits public devotion but requires prudent discernment to distinguish the central message from any subsequent devotional exaggerations or unauthorized publications.

## The Apparition at La Salette
On September 19, 1846, at approximately 3:00 p.m., the Blessed Virgin Mary appeared on a high mountain pasture near the village of La Salette-Fallavaux, France.

### Key Historical Details:
1. **The Witnesses**: Two young peasant shepherds, Mélanie Calvat (age 14) and Maximin Giraud (age 11), witnessed the apparition.
2. **The Phenomenon**: They saw a "beautiful lady" sitting on a stone, weeping intensely with her face in her hands, surrounded by a brilliant light. She wore a crucifix featuring a hammer and pincers (representing the tools of the Passion and the choices of mankind) and a heavy chain.
3. **The Message**: Mary spoke to the children in French and then in their local patois (Occitan dialect). Her message was one of profound sorrow over the widespread impiety, despoiling of the Sabbath (working on Sundays), blasphemy, and disregard for the Lenten fast.
4. **The Warning and Promises**: She warned of a coming famine, crop failures, and deep suffering if people did not amend their lives, but promised abundance and divine mercy if they returned to prayer and conversion.
5. **The Secrets**: She entrusted a secret message to each child individually, which they was not to disclose until a later time.

## Ecclesiastical Investigation and Controversy
Due to the dramatic warnings in the message and the subsequent behaviors of the seers in their adult lives, La Salette attracted significant controversy. However, the Church carefully separated the core apparition from later disputes:
- **Diocesan Approval**: After a rigorous investigation by a canonical commission, Bishop Philibert de Bruillard of Grenoble published a pastoral letter on November 16, 1851, declaring:
  > "We judge that the apparition of the Blessed Virgin to two shepherds... on September 19, 1846... carries in itself all the characteristics of truth, and that the faithful are justified in believing it certain and doubtless."
- **Inception of the Shrine**: On May 25, 1852, Bishop de Bruillard laid the foundation stone for a major church at the site, which was later elevated to the status of a basilica. The congregation of the Missionaries of Our Lady of La Salette was founded to serve the shrine and spread the message of reconciliation.
- **Vatican Disciplinary Actions**: Due to unauthorized and sensationalist booklets being published about the "Secret of La Salette," the Holy Office issued a decree on December 21, 1915, ordering the faithful to cease publishing or discussing these speculative interpretations, while explicitly confirming that this disciplinary measure did not diminish the approved devotion to "Our Lady, Reconciler of Sinners."
