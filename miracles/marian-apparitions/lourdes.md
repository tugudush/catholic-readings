# Our Lady of Lourdes (1858)

## Theological Introduction to Private Revelation
The apparitions at Lourdes are recognized by the Catholic Church as a legitimate **private revelation**:
- As taught in the Catechism of the Catholic Church (CCC 67), private revelations do not belong to the deposit of faith. Their proper purpose is to assist the faithful in living more deeply by Christ's definitive truth.
- Although beliefs in approved apparitions are not binding on Catholic dogma, they provide a powerful "motive of credibility" and a pastoral aid for the spiritual life of believers.
- The hallmark of Lourdes is its absolute conformity to the public Faith of the Church—especially concerning the dogma of the Immaculate Conception.

## The Apparitions at Massabielle
Between February 11 and July 16, 1858, a fourteen-year-old peasant girl named Saint Bernadette Soubirous witnessed 18 apparitions of a silent, beautiful lady at the grotto of Massabielle, near Lourdes, France.

### Key Historical Details:
1. **The Vision**: Bernadette described seeing a young, extraordinarily beautiful woman dressed in white, with a blue sash, holding a rosary, and with gold roses on her feet.
2. **The Call to Penance**: The Lady called for prayer and conversion, asking Bernadette to repeat the word: "Penance, penance, penance!" She requested that the priests build a chapel at the grotto and that people come in procession.
3. **The Miraculous Spring (February 25)**: During the ninth apparition, the Lady told Bernadette, "Go, drink at the spring and wash yourself there." Confused because there was no visible water, Bernadette began scraping the muddy ground with her hands. As she did, a small puddle formed. Over the following days, this trickle became an abundant grotto spring flowing with pure water. To this day, the spring produces thousands of gallons daily and is linked to numerous medically certified, scientifically inexplicable cures.
4. **The Declaration of Name (March 25)**: On the Feast of the Annunciation, Bernadette repeatedly asked the Lady for her name. The Lady finally joined her hands, looked up to heaven, and declared in the local Gascon Occitan dialect:
   > "Que soy era Immaculada Councepciou" ("I am the Immaculate Conception").
   Bernadette, who was uneducated and did not understand the theological meaning of this phrase, immediately ran to her parish priest, Father Dominique Peyramale, repeating the words so she would not forget them.

## Relationship to Church Dogma
Only four years prior, on December 8, 1854, Pope Pius IX had solemnly defined the dogma of the **Immaculate Conception** in his apostolic constitution *Ineffabilis Deus*. The apparition at Lourdes, occurring so shortly after, was viewed by the Church and successive popes (such as Pope Pius XII in *Le Pèlerinage de Lourdes*) as a providential, heavenly confirmation of the solemn definition.

## Ecclesiastical Investigation and Approval
- **Episcopal Approval**: On January 18, 1862, after a rigorous four-year canonical commission investigation, Bishop Bertrand-Sévère Laurence of Tarbes published a pastoral letter declaring:
  > "We judge that the Immaculate Mary, Mother of God, did really appear to Bernadette Soubirous... Our belief is based on the testimony of Bernadette, but above all on the things which have happened, things which can be explained only by divine intervention."
- **Saints' Cause**: Saint Bernadette Soubirous lived out her days as a humble Sister of Charity of Nevers. Her body remains incorrupt in Nevers. She was beatified in 1925 and canonized by Pope Pius XI on December 8, 1933.
- **Universal Liturgical Feast**: The Holy See authorized the liturgical Office and Mass for the feast "In Apparitione Beatae Mariae Virginis Immaculatae." In 1907, Pope Pius X extended the feast of Our Lady of Lourdes (February 11) to the universal Church.
