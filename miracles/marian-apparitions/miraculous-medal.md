# The Miraculous Medal (Our Lady of the Miraculous Medal - 1830)

## Theological Introduction
The manifestations connected to the Miraculous Medal represent a cornerstone of modern Marian devotion and are classified as an approved **private revelation**:
- Consistent with Catholic teaching, special practices and medals associated with private revelations are not objects of adoration or direct objects of divine faith. They serve as "sacramentals"—sacred signs instituted by the Church to prepare the faithful to receive grace and cooperate with the Holy Spirit (CCC 1667-1670).
- The symbols inscribed on the medal are deeply rooted in Catholic dogma, particularly the Immaculate Conception and Mary's role in the economy of salvation under her Son, Jesus Christ.

## The Apparitions in Paris
In 1830, the Blessed Virgin Mary appeared three times to Saint Catherine Labouré, a novice (seminarian) of the Daughters of Charity of Saint Vincent de Paul, in the mother-house chapel on the Rue du Bac in Paris, France.

### Key Historical Details:
1. **First Apparition (July 18, 1830)**: Catherine was awakened in the night by a child shining with light (whom she believed to be her guardian angel) and led to the chapel. There, the Blessed Virgin Mary appeared and sat in the spiritual director's chair. Catherine knelt beside her, placing her hands on Mary's knees, and Mary gave her instructions on how to handle upcoming trials in France.
2. **Second Apparition (November 27, 1830)**: During evening prayer, Catherine saw Mary standing on a globe, holding a golden ball representing the world, which she offered to God. Radiant beams of light streamed from rings on Mary's fingers, representing graces she pours out on those who ask for them.
3. **The Design of the Medal**: An oval frame formed around Mary, and Catherine saw an inscription in gold letters:
   > "O Mary, conceived without sin, pray for us who have recourse to thee."
   The frame turned, showing the reverse side of the medal design:
   - The letter **M** surmounted by a **cross** with a crossbar below.
   - The **Sacred Heart of Jesus** crowned with thorns and the **Immaculate Heart of Mary** pierced with a sword.
   - Twelve stars encircling the entire design (recalling Revelation 12:1).
4. **The Command**: Catherine heard a voice instructing her: "Have a medal struck after this model. All who wear it will receive great graces... abundant graces will be bestowed upon those who wear it with confidence."

## Canonical Inquiry and Global Devotion
- **The First Striking (1832)**: Saint Catherine confided these visions only to her spiritual director, Father Jean-Marie Aladel. After initial hesitation, Father Aladel presented the case to the Archbishop of Paris, Mgr Hyacinthe-Louis de Quélen. Given the spiritual fruits and lack of conflict with Church teachings, the Archbishop authorized the first striking of 20,000 medals on June 30, 1832.
- **The Canonical Inquiry (1836)**: A formal canonical inquiry was initiated by the Archbishop of Paris in 1836. The tribunal officially declared the visions authentic, praising Catherine's integrity, sanity, and the high spiritual fruits of the devotion.
- **Conversion of Alphonse Ratisbonne (1842)**: An aristocratic French Jew and staunch critic of Catholicism, Alphonse Ratisbonne, reluctantly wore the medal as a dare while visiting Rome. On January 20, 1842, he had a sudden, silent vision of the Virgin Mary of the Miraculous Medal in the church of Sant'Andrea delle Fratte. He converted instantly to the Catholic faith and later founded the Congregation of Our Lady of Sion. This miracle received formal ecclesiastical sanction and became globally famous.
- **Saint Catherine's Hidden Life**: Catherine lived for 46 years after the apparitions as an ordinary, silent sister caring for the elderly, keeping her identity as the visionary secret until shortly before her death in 1876. Her body remains incorrupt in the chapel of Rue du Bac. She was canonized on November 27, 1947, by Pope Pius XII.
- **Liturgical Recognition**: Pope Leo XIII authorized a proper liturgical Mass and Office for the "Manifestation of the Immaculate Virgin of the Miraculous Medal," assigned to November 27.
