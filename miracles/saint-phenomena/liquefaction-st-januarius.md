# The Liquefaction of the Blood of Saint Januarius (San Gennaro)

## Theological Introduction
In Catholic theology and popular devotion, the mystical liquefaction of the dried blood of Saint Januarius is treated as a **prodigy**—an ongoing physical sign connected to the relics of the saints:
- Relics of the saints are not worshiped (which is due to God alone, *latria*), but are highly venerated (*dulia*) because the bodies of the saints were temples of the Holy Spirit and will be raised in glory (CCC 1674, Council of Trent).
- Ongoing physical signs associated with relics serve as popular testimonies of the reality of the Communion of Saints and the resurrection of the body.

## The Martyrdom and the Relic
Saint Januarius (San Gennaro) was the Bishop of Benevento who was martyred by beheading around 305 AD at Pozzuoli during the fierce Diocletianic Persecution.

### Key Historical Details:
1. **The Relic**: Following his martyrdom, a Christian woman named Eusebia gathered a small amount of his spilled blood and preserved it in two small glass vials.
2. **The Cathedral of Naples**: Since 1497, these vials have been kept in the Cathedral of Naples, Italy, inside a special silver reliquary.
3. **The Phenomenon of Liquefaction**: For over six centuries (with the first recorded instance dating back to 1389), the dark, dried-crust substance in the sealed glass vials has periodically changed its physical state.
4. **The Annual Dates**: The liquefaction occurs publicly on specific days of the liturgical and local calendar:
   - **September 19**: His principal liturgical feast day, commemorating his martyrdom.
   - **December 16**: Commemorating the miraculous preservation of the city of Naples from the catastrophic eruption of Mount Vesuvius in 1631.
   - **The Saturday before the first Sunday of May**: Marking the historic translation of his relics to Naples.
5. **The Miracle Action**: During the public ceremony, the Archbishop of Naples takes the silver reliquary containing the vials and turns it in full view of the assembly. As prayers of the faithful are recited, the solid, dark red crust in the vial liquefies—often rapidly—turning into a bubbling, flowing liquid and changing its volume and weight.

## Scientific Investigations and Explanations
The phenomena has been studied by various secular and religious scientists over many decades:
- **Spectrophotometric Tests**: In 1902 and again in subsequent years, scientists analyzed the contents using spectrophotometric analysis. The absorption spectrum of the light passing through the liquefied substance matched that of **human hemoglobin** (the oxygen-carrying protein in red blood cells), verifying that the vial contains genuine blood.
- **Doubt of Mechanical Causes**: Skeptics have proposed theories like thixotropy (gels that liquefy when shaken), but observers note that the liquefaction often occurs before the reliquary is even handled, and it does not liquefy on other random days when shaken for maintenance. The temperature, density, and color changes remain outside of a definitive natural physical explanation.

## Ecclesiastical Status and Cultural Impact
The liquefaction is treated as a highly cherished, venerable tradition in southern Italy. Popes visiting Naples—including Saint John Paul II, Pope Benedict XVI, and Pope Francis—have venerated the relic, and on several of these papal visits, the blood has been observed to liquefy in their presence. The Church continues to sponsor and oversee the public display of the relics, treating it as an auxiliary sign of faith and hope in Christ's victory over death.
