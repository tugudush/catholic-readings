# Divine Mercy and Saint Faustina (1930s)

## Theological Introduction
The Divine Mercy devotion, revealed to Saint Maria Faustina Kowalska, is one of the most widely practiced **private revelations** in the modern Catholic Church:
- As clarified in the Catechism (CCC 67), private revelations do not add to or surpass the public Revelation of Christ.
- The theological brilliance of the Divine Mercy revelations is that they point directly back to the biblical truth of God's abundant, unconditional mercy and love, as expressed in the sacraments of Confession and the Holy Eucharist.

## The Revelations to Saint Faustina
Sister Maria Faustina Kowalska was a humble, uneducated Polish sister of the Congregation of the Sisters of Our Lady of Mercy. In the 1930s, she experienced numerous interior locutions and visible apparitions of Jesus Christ, which she faithfully recorded in a 600-page notebook titled *Diary: Divine Mercy in My Soul*.

### Key Historical and Mystical Details:
1. **The Divine Mercy Image (February 22, 1931)**: In her cell at Płock, Saint Faustina saw Jesus dressed in a white garment. One hand was raised in blessing, and the other was touching his chest, from which two large rays shone forth—one red (symbolizing the Blood which is the life of souls) and one pale (symbolizing the Water which makes souls righteous, i.e., Baptism and Confession). Jesus instructed her:
   > "Paint an image according to the pattern you see, with the signature: 'Jesus, I trust in You.' I desire that this image be venerated, first in your chapel, and then throughout the world."
2. **The Chaplet of Divine Mercy (September 13-14, 1935)**: Faustina received the words of a powerful new prayer to be recited on standard Rosary beads, offering the "Body and Blood, Soul and Divinity" of Jesus to God the Father in atonement for the world's sins.
3. **The Hour of Mercy (3:00 p.m.)**: Jesus asked Faustina to pray the stations of the cross or meditate on his Passion at the hour of his death (3 p.m.), promising that "in this hour, you can obtain everything for yourself and for others for the asking; it was the hour of grace for the whole world."
4. **The Feast of Mercy**: Jesus requested that the first Sunday after Easter be solemnly dedicated as the Feast of Mercy, promising that any soul who goes to Confession and receives Holy Communion on this day shall obtain complete forgiveness of sins and punishment.

## Ecclesiastical Investigation and Official Approvals
The journey to formal Church approval was long and filled with trials, as St. Faustina had predicted:
- **Temporary Prohibition (1959-1978)**: Due to faulty and inaccurate translations of Faustina's diary into Italian and French, the Holy Office (now the DDF) issued a notification in 1959 that banned the distribution of the writings and the specific devotions.
- **The Removal of Impediments**: The Archbishop of Kraków, Karol Wojtyła (the future Saint John Paul II), initiated a formal historical and theological review of Faustina's actual Polish texts. Finding no theological errors, the path was cleared. On April 15, 1978, the Congregation for the Doctrine of the Faith issued a new notification rescinding the 1959 ban and authorizing the devotion in its authentic forms.
- **Beatification and Canonization**: Cardinal Karol Wojtyła, as Pope John Paul II, beatified Faustina in 1993 and solemnly canonized her on April 30, 2000, presenting her as "the great apostle of Divine Mercy."
- **Universal Feast of Divine Mercy**: During her canonization, Saint John Paul II officially declared that the Second Sunday of Easter would henceforth be celebrated throughout the universal Church as **Divine Mercy Sunday**.
