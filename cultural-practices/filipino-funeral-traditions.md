# Filipino Funeral Traditions and the 40-Day Mourning Period

A comprehensive guide to the cultural and religious practices surrounding death in the Philippines, documenting the distinctive Filipino customs that have developed over centuries of Catholic faith and pre-colonial tradition. This document clearly distinguishes between **Filipino cultural practices** and **universal Catholic liturgical practice**, providing clarity for the faithful navigating bereavement.

## 1. Historical Context

The Philippines is one of the largest Catholic nations in the world, with approximately 86% of its population identifying as Roman Catholic. The Catholic faith arrived with Spanish colonization in 1565, but the Filipino people already possessed deeply rooted beliefs about death and the afterlife stemming from pre-colonial ancestral veneration (_anito_ worship).

Over more than four centuries, these indigenous beliefs merged with Spanish Catholic teaching to produce a unique synthesis of faith and culture. Filipino funeral practices reflect this blending:

- **Catholic elements**: The Rosary, Requiem Mass, novenas, prayers for the dead, and the Christian hope of Resurrection
- **Indigenous elements**: Extended wakes, communal feasting, specific taboos, and beliefs about the soul's journey
- **Spanish colonial influences**: The structure of the wake, the use of mourning colors, and the cemetery-centered observances

Understanding this history is essential for distinguishing between what the Church requires and what Filipino culture has developed as devotional custom.

## 2. The Wake (_Burol / Lamay_)

The Filipino wake — known as _burol_ (Tagalog), _lamay_ (general), or _paglalamay_ — is the most visible and communal aspect of Filipino mourning. It typically lasts **3 to 7 nights**, though it may be extended if family members are traveling from afar.

### 2.1 The Setting

| Aspect             | Filipino Cultural Practice                                                                             | Catholic Practice                                                                                             |
| :----------------- | :----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | :------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| **Location**       | Traditionally held at the family home; increasingly at funeral homes                                   | The Church supports wakes at either location, provided the body is treated with respect                       |
| **Duration**       | 3–7 nights (or longer)                                                                                 | No fixed liturgical requirement for wake length                                                               |
| **Casket Display** | Open casket is standard; the body is embalmed and displayed                                            | The Church permits both open and closed caskets; the _Rite of Reception_ sprinkles the casket with holy water |
| **Candles**        | Funeral lights surround the casket; candles must remain lit (a lit candle symbolizes the soul's light) | The paschal candle or a blessed candle is lit beside the coffin, recalling Baptism and the light of Christ    |

### 2.2 Activities at the Wake

**Filipino Cultural Practices:**

- **Communal Feasting**: The bereaved family serves food and drinks to all visitors throughout the wake. This reflects the Filipino value of _pakikisama_ (communal solidarity).
- **Socializing**: Guests engage in conversation, card games (_tong-its_), singing, or videoke to pass the time and keep mourners awake.
- **Financial Support (_Abuloy_)**: Visitors offer cash donations to help defray funeral and burial expenses. This is a deeply ingrained cultural expectation.
- **Mourning Pins**: Immediate family members wear black armbands or mourning pins, often kept until the 40th day after death.
- **Dirges and Laments**: In some regions (particularly Ilocano communities), professional mourners or family members sing _dung-áw_ (dirges) to honor the deceased.

**Catholic Liturgical Practices:**

- **Praying the Rosary**: The Rosary is prayed nightly at the wake, typically led by family members or a designated prayer leader. The Glorious Mysteries are most commonly offered for the deceased.
- **Rite of Vigil**: The Church provides the _Rite of Vigil for the Deceased_, which includes Scripture readings (especially Psalms and Gospel accounts of the Resurrection), prayers of intercession, and the _Requiem Aeternam_.
- **Office of the Dead**: The Liturgy of the Hours includes specific prayers for the deceased, which may be recited during the wake.
- **Scripture Readings**: Passages such as John 11:25–26 (_"I am the resurrection and the life"_), Romans 8:38–39, and Revelation 21:1–7 are appropriate.

### 2.3 The Funeral Day

On the day of the funeral, the following sequence typically occurs:

1. **Final Rosary and Prayers** at the wake location
2. **Funeral Procession** to the church, with the hearse followed by mourners on foot
3. **Funeral Mass** (the central act of Catholic funeral worship)
4. **Rite of Committal** at the cemetery or place of cremation

> **Note**: In many Filipino communities, it is customary for the entire community to participate in the funeral procession, walking alongside the hearse from the home to the church and then to the cemetery.

## 3. The 9-Day Period: _Pasiyam_ / _Pagsisiyam_

The _Pasiyam_ (literally, "that which is done for nine days") is a period of communal prayer lasting nine consecutive days after the burial. This is one of the most distinctly Filipino Catholic devotions.

### 3.1 Filipino Cultural Practice

- Families gather every evening for nine nights to pray together
- The Rosary, novenas, and other prayers are offered
- Food is served to all who attend
- On the **ninth night**, a feast is held to mark the completion of the _Pasiyam_
- There is a strong folk belief that the soul enters the spirit world specifically on the ninth day

### 3.2 Catholic Theological Basis

The _Pasiyam_ corresponds to the **Novena for the Dead**, a traditional Catholic practice rooted in:

- The **nine days of prayer** by the Apostles between the Ascension and Pentecost (Acts 1:14)
- The Church's teaching that **prayers for the dead are beneficial** to the souls in Purgatory (CCC 1032)
- The tradition that the soul undergoes **particular judgment** shortly after death

**Catholic Practice:**

- The _Directory on Popular Piety and the Liturgy_ (Congregation for Divine Worship, 2002) encourages prayers for the dead, especially during the first days after death (n. 255)
- The novena may include the _Requiem Aeternam_, _De Profundis_ (Psalm 130/129), Litany of the Saints, and the Rosary
- The Church teaches that these prayers assist the soul in Purgatory but does not specify that the soul must wait exactly nine days

### 3.3 Theological Distinction

| Element                | Filipino Cultural Interpretation    | Catholic Teaching                                                     |
| :--------------------- | :---------------------------------- | :-------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Duration of Novena** | Exactly 9 days, ending with a feast | Nine days is a devotional tradition, not a dogmatic requirement       |
| **Soul's Journey**     | Soul enters spirit world on 9th day | Particular judgment occurs at death; the soul's state is known to God |
| **Purpose of Prayers** | To guide and protect the soul       | To assist the soul in Purgatory and commend it to God's mercy         |

## 4. The 40-Day Period: _Waksi_ / _Ika-apatnapung Araw_

The 40th day after death is the most significant milestone in Filipino mourning. Known as _Waksi_ or simply "the 40th day," it marks the culmination of the mourning period.

### 4.1 Filipino Cultural Practice

- A **Requiem Mass** is offered on the 40th day
- A **feast** is held for family and friends
- The mourning period formally ends (though some families extend mourning to the first anniversary)
- The 40th day is believed to be when the soul **finishes its earthly wandering** and ascends to its final destination
- Some families observe the **Babang-luksa** ("descent from mourning") on the first anniversary, marking the formal end of bereavement

### 4.2 Catholic Theological Basis

The 40th day holds deep significance in Catholic tradition, recalling:

- **Christ's Ascension**: 40 days after His Resurrection (Acts 1:3)
- **Moses on Mount Sinai**: 40 days receiving the Law (Exodus 24:18)
- **The Israelites in the Desert**: 40 years of wandering (Numbers 14:33)
- **Christ's Fasting in the Wilderness**: 40 days of temptation (Matthew 4:2)

**Catholic Practice:**

- The Church offers the **Requiem Mass** on the 40th day as a pastoral custom, not a dogmatic requirement
- The _Directory on Popular Piety and the Liturgy_ (n. 256) notes that the fortieth day is a traditional time for special commemoration
- Prayers include the _Requiem Aeternam_, _De Profundis_, Rosary (especially Glorious Mysteries), and Litany of the Saints
- The Church teaches that the soul's particular judgment and initial purification may be understood in light of these 40 days, but this is **theological opinion**, not dogma

### 4.3 Theological Distinction

| Element                     | Filipino Cultural Interpretation  | Catholic Teaching                                                      |
| :-------------------------- | :-------------------------------- | :--------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Significance of 40 Days** | Soul completes earthly wandering  | Commemorates Christ's Ascension; traditional time of special prayer    |
| **Mourning Period**         | Formally ends on 40th day         | The Church encourages ongoing prayer for the dead                      |
| **Soul's State**            | Soul ascends to final destination | The soul's state is known to God; prayers assist the soul in Purgatory |

## 5. All Souls' Day: _Undas_

The observance of All Saints' Day (November 1) and All Souls' Day (November 2) is one of the most significant events in the Filipino Catholic calendar. Collectively known as _Undas_ or _Araw ng mga Yumao_, these days draw millions of Filipinos to cemeteries across the country.

### 5.1 Filipino Cultural Practice

- Families **clean and repair** the tombs of their loved ones in the weeks before November 1
- On November 1–2, families gather at the cemetery for **extended visits**, often staying overnight
- **Feasting** is common; families bring food, drinks, and even entertainment (music, games)
- **Candles and flowers** are placed on graves
- Chinese-Filipino families may burn **joss sticks and joss paper** (_kim_) alongside Catholic prayers
- The atmosphere is often described as a "reunion" rather than a somber occasion

### 5.2 Catholic Liturgical Practice

- **November 1 — All Saints' Day** (_Todos los Santos_): A Holy Day of Obligation honoring all the Saints, both canonized and those known only to God
- **November 2 — All Souls' Day** (_Commemoratio Omnium Fidelium Defunctorum_): A day of prayer for all the faithful departed, especially those in Purgatory

**Catholic Practice on All Souls' Day:**

- **Plenary Indulgence**: A plenary indulgence may be gained for the souls in Purgatory by visiting a church or oratory, praying a _Pater_ and _Credo_, and fulfilling the usual conditions (sacramental confession, Eucharistic communion, prayer for the intentions of the Holy Father, and detachment from all sin) — [Enchiridion Indulgentiarum, §29](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/b4e30e90-7ed9-4cc5-be4b-305ed07d425b/ref/Concessiones.%2029)
- **Partial Indulgence**: Gained by visiting a cemetery and praying for the dead
- **Plenary Indulgence (Nov 1–8)**: The faithful may gain a plenary indulgence for the souls in Purgatory each day from November 1 to November 8 by visiting a cemetery and praying for the dead
- **Masses**: Special Requiem Masses are offered for the deceased

### 5.3 Theological Distinction

| Element             | Filipino Cultural Practice                    | Catholic Liturgical Practice                 |
| :------------------ | :-------------------------------------------- | :------------------------------------------- |
| **Duration**        | Extended visits lasting days/nights           | November 1–2 are the liturgical days         |
| **Activities**      | Feasting, socializing, entertainment          | Prayer, Mass, gaining indulgences            |
| **Cemetery Visits** | Cleaning, decorating, and "camping" at graves | Visiting graves to pray and gain indulgences |
| **Atmosphere**      | Celebratory reunion                           | Solemn remembrance and prayer                |

## 7. Superstitions and Folk Beliefs

Filipino mourning culture is rich with superstitions that, while deeply held by many families, have **no basis in Catholic doctrine**. The Church encourages the faithful to distinguish between legitimate cultural expression and superstitious practice.

### Common Folk Beliefs (Not Catholic)

| Belief                                                                                   | Origin                      | Catholic Assessment                |
| :--------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | :-------------------------- | :--------------------------------- |
| **Black butterflies** signify death                                                      | Pre-colonial _anito_ belief | Superstition; no theological basis |
| **Owls near a sick person's home** signal imminent death                                 | Folk belief                 | Superstition                       |
| **Sudden scent of burning candles** (without a lit candle) indicates a relative has died | Folk belief                 | Superstition                       |
| **Losing a tooth in a dream** is an omen of a relative's death                           | Folk belief                 | Superstition                       |
| **Three persons photographed together** may lead to the death of the one in the middle   | Folk belief                 | Superstition                       |
| **Preventing all family members from viewing the face** of the dead                      | Folk belief                 | Superstition                       |
| **Lifting children over the coffin** before entombment                                   | Folk belief                 | Superstition                       |
| **_Pagpag_** — stopping at a store before going home                                     | Folk belief                 | Superstition                       |
| **Not sweeping the floor** during the wake                                               | Folk taboo                  | Superstition                       |
| **Beans or seeded fruits** cause skin diseases if eaten during the wake                  | Folk taboo                  | Superstition                       |

**Catholic Position**: The _Catechism of the Catholic Church_ (CCC 2110–2111) teaches that superstition is a vice that "attributes the efficacy of prayers or sacramental signs to their mere external performance, apart from the interior dispositions that they demand." While Filipino folk customs may carry cultural meaning, the faithful should not attribute salvific power to them.

## 8. Theological Foundations

### 8.1 The Communion of Saints

Catholic teaching on death is grounded in the **Communion of Saints** — the spiritual bond between the faithful on earth (the Church Militant), the souls in Purgatory (the Church Suffering), and the blessed in Heaven (the Church Triumphant).

> _"The bond which creates communion of saints does not disappear after death. The dead remain closely linked to the living members of Christ's Body."_ — CCC 1689

### 8.2 Particular Judgment and the Soul's Journey After Death

One of the most common questions families ask when a loved one dies is: **does the soul immediately go before God, or does it linger for a period of time?** Catholic teaching and Filipino folk beliefs offer very different answers to this question.

#### 8.2.1 Catholic Teaching: Immediate Particular Judgment

The Catholic Church teaches with **certainty of faith** that **Particular Judgment** occurs **at the very moment of death**. There is no waiting period, no interval of uncertainty, no "lingering" between the death of the body and the judgment of the soul.

The _Catechism of the Catholic Church_ is explicit:

> _"Each man receives his eternal retribution in his immortal soul **at the very moment of his death**, in a particular judgment by Christ, the judge of the living and the dead."_ — [CCC 1022](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/0583c069-d4bf-42dd-97de-c19f0b80150f/ref/1022)

And again:

> _"Every man receives his eternal recompense in his immortal soul **from the moment of his death** in a particular judgment by Christ, the judge of the living and the dead."_ — [CCC 1051](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/0583c069-d4bf-42dd-97de-c19f0b80150f/ref/1051)

**The three possible outcomes** of Particular Judgment are:

1. **Immediate entry into Heaven** — for those who died in a state of perfect grace and charity, free from all attachment to sin.
2. **Entry into Purgatory** — for those who died in God's grace but are not yet fully purified. Purgatory is a state of **assured salvation** — the soul is certain of heaven but must undergo purification before entering the beatific vision ([CCC 1030](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/0583c069-d4bf-42dd-97de-c19f0b80150f/ref/1030)).
3. **Immediate and everlasting damnation** — for those who died in a state of mortal sin without repentance ([CCC 1033–1037](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/0583c069-d4bf-42dd-97de-c19f0b80150f/ref/1033)).

This doctrine was solemnly affirmed by the **Council of Florence** (1439), **Benedict XII** in _Benedictus Deus_ (1336), and the **Council of Trent** (1563). The _Compendium of the Catechism_ summarizes it plainly:

> _"What happens at the moment of death? — The soul is immediately judged by Christ. The particular judgment determines the destiny of each soul in relation to its eternal life."_

**Key distinction**: Particular Judgment (at death) is **not** the same as the **Last Judgment** (at the end of time). The Last Judgment concerns all humanity together, when Christ returns in glory and all the dead rise in their bodies ([CCC 1038–1041](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/0583c069-d4bf-42dd-97de-c19f0b80150f/ref/1038)). Particular Judgment is personal, immediate, and concerns the soul alone.

#### 8.2.2 Filipino Folk Beliefs: The Soul Lingers

In contrast to Catholic teaching, many Filipino families hold deeply rooted folk beliefs that the soul **does not immediately depart** after death but instead lingers for a period of time, undergoing a gradual transition to the afterlife. These beliefs have pre-colonial origins rooted in _anito_ (ancestral spirit) veneration and have been woven into the fabric of Filipino Catholic practice over centuries.

**The belief in the 9-day journey:**

- Many Filipinos believe that the soul remains in a **transitional state** for nine days after death
- It is believed that the soul "wanders" or "travels" during this period, gradually detaching from earthly life
- The _Pasiyam_ (nine-day novena) is thought to **guide and protect** the soul during this vulnerable journey
- On the **ninth night**, it is believed that the soul departs to the spirit world or encounters God
- The feast held on the ninth night is sometimes interpreted as a "send-off" for the departing soul

**The belief in the 40-day journey:**

- An extended version holds that the soul's complete detachment from the earthly realm takes **40 days**
- The 40th day is believed to be when the soul **finishes its earthly wandering** and ascends to its final destination
- The _Waksi_ (40th-day Mass and feast) marks the soul's "arrival" in the afterlife
- This belief parallels Christ's 40-day ascent to heaven after His Resurrection (Acts 1:3), though the Filipino interpretation is more literal than the scriptural parallel

**Other common folk beliefs about the soul's state:**

| Belief                                         | Description                                                                                                                                     |
| :--------------------------------------------- | :---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **The soul needs guidance**                    | The soul may become "lost" or confused after death; prayers, candles, and the Rosary serve as a spiritual compass                               |
| **The soul visits the living**                 | Some believe the soul may return to visit family members, especially during the wake period; this is why candles are kept burning               |
| **The soul can be "comforted" or "disturbed"** | The behavior of the living during the wake can affect the soul's journey; wailing, fighting, or neglecting prayers may cause the soul suffering |
| **The soul lingers at the home**               | The _burol_ (wake) at the family home is sometimes understood as providing the soul a familiar place to rest before departing                   |
| **The soul requires sustenance**               | Placing the deceased's favorite food on the grave or at home is understood as offering nourishment for the soul's journey                       |

#### 8.2.3 Theological Assessment

| Aspect                         | Catholic Teaching                                                          | Filipino Folk Belief                                                      |
| :----------------------------- | :------------------------------------------------------------------------- | :------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| **Timing of judgment**         | Immediate, at the moment of death (CCC 1022, 1051)                         | Delayed — the soul transitions over 9 or 40 days                          |
| **Nature of the soul's state** | The soul's eternal destiny is determined instantly                         | The soul is in a transitional or wandering state                          |
| **Role of the living**         | Prayers assist the soul in Purgatory (CCC 1032)                            | Prayers guide, protect, and "comfort" the soul on its journey             |
| **Duration of transition**     | The soul enters its eternal state immediately (heaven, purgatory, or hell) | The soul takes 9 days or 40 days to reach its final destination           |
| **Theological status**         | **Dogma** — defined by ecumenical councils and the Magisterium             | **Folk belief** — culturally widespread but not doctrinally authoritative |

**The Church's pastoral response** is one of both clarity and charity. The _Directory on Popular Piety and the Liturgy_ (2002) acknowledges that many popular devotions surrounding death — including extended periods of prayer — reflect genuine faith and filial piety, even when they include elements that go beyond strict doctrine:

> _"Popular piety is a locus wherein the faithful, spontaneously and freely, express their relationship with the supernatural world and with the world beyond death."_ — [Directory on Popular Piety and the Liturgy, n. 249](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/4fba701f-fda0-47a5-a2de-2e210d81f770/ref/249)

The faithful are encouraged to **offer prayers for the dead** — not because the soul is "lost" or "wandering," but because prayer is an act of charity that benefits the soul, especially those in Purgatory, regardless of the precise timing of their purification.

#### 8.2.4 Why the Distinction Matters

Understanding the distinction between Catholic doctrine and Filipino folk belief is important for several reasons:

1. **Doctrinal integrity**: The Church's teaching on Particular Judgment is clear and definitive. The faithful should not believe that the soul's eternal destiny hangs in the balance for 9 or 40 days.

2. **Pastoral sensitivity**: Filipino folk beliefs about the soul's journey reflect a deep love for the deceased and a genuine desire to assist them. This is laudable and should be honored, even as the faithful are gently formed in the fullness of Catholic teaching.

3. **The power of prayer**: Regardless of whether one understands the soul's journey as immediate or gradual, **prayers for the dead are always beneficial**. The Mass, the Rosary, indulgences, and acts of charity offered for the deceased assist souls in Purgatory and are among the most powerful works of mercy the Church can offer.

4. **The Communion of Saints**: The bond between the living and the dead is real and enduring. Whether one imagines the soul as having already arrived in heaven or still journeying toward it, the Christian response is the same: **pray, offer Masses, and trust in God's mercy**.

### 8.4 Purgatory

The Church teaches that those who die in God's grace but who are not yet purified undergo a process of purification called **Purgatory** (CCC 1030–1032). This doctrine provides the theological basis for prayers for the dead:

> _"The Church encourages prayers for the dead, particularly the offering of the Eucharistic sacrifice, almsgiving, indulgences, and works of penance."_ — CCC 1032

### 8.5 Indulgences for the Dead

The Church grants indulgences that can be applied to the souls in Purgatory:

- **Plenary Indulgence**: Available on All Souls' Day (and during the octave from November 1–8) for visiting a church or cemetery and praying for the dead — [Enchiridion Indulgentiarum, §29](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/b4e30e90-7ed9-4cc5-be4b-305ed07d425b/ref/Concessiones.%2029)
- **Partial Indulgence**: Available each time one visits a cemetery and prays for the dead

### 8.6 The Mass as the Perfect Prayer

The **Funeral Mass** is the central act of Catholic funeral worship. The Mass is the perfect prayer because it unites the sacrifice of Christ on the Cross with the prayers of the faithful:

> _"In the liturgy of the dead, the Church offers the sacrifice of the Cross to God, and the dead share in its saving effects."_ — [Order of Christian Funerals, General Introduction, §5](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/3d220e66-7234-440a-966d-169484c63632/ref/5)

| **Participate in the Rite of Committal** | **Catholic**                     | Final prayers at the cemetery or place of cremation                                               |
| **Join the funeral procession**          | **Both**                         | **Catholic**: Liturgical procession to church. **Filipino**: Community walks alongside the hearse |
| **Pass children under the coffin**       | **Filipino (Folk Superstition)** | Based on the belief it protects children from the deceased's spirit; **not** Catholic             |

### Days 1–9: The _Pasiyam_

| Action                                       | Classification             | Notes                                                                             |
| :------------------------------------------- | :------------------------- | :-------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Pray the novena for the dead**             | **Catholic**               | Nine days of prayer rooted in apostolic tradition                                 |
| **Gather nightly for communal prayer**       | **Filipino (Cultural)**    | The Filipino expression of the novena tradition                                   |
| **Hold a feast on the 9th night**            | **Filipino (Cultural)**    | Marks the completion of the _Pasiyam_                                             |
| **Believe the soul enters the spirit world** | **Filipino (Folk Belief)** | **Not** Catholic doctrine; the Church teaches particular judgment occurs at death |

### Days 10–39: The Continuing Mourning

| Action                            | Classification                   | Notes                                                       |
| :-------------------------------- | :------------------------------- | :---------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Offer Masses for the deceased** | **Catholic**                     | The most important ongoing practice                         |
| **Continue to pray for the dead** | **Catholic**                     | The Church encourages perpetual remembrance of the deceased |
| **Refrain from cutting hair**     | **Filipino (Folk Superstition)** | **Not** Catholic; a cultural expression of mourning         |
| **Avoid sweeping the floor**      | **Filipino (Folk Superstition)** | **Not** Catholic; a folk taboo                              |

### Day 40: _Waksi_ / The 40th Day

| Action                                             | Classification             | Notes                                                |
| :------------------------------------------------- | :------------------------- | :--------------------------------------------------- |
| **Attend the Requiem Mass**                        | **Catholic**               | Traditional day for special commemoration            |
| **Hold a feast for family and friends**            | **Filipino (Cultural)**    | Marks the end of the formal mourning period          |
| **Believe the soul completes its earthly journey** | **Filipino (Folk Belief)** | **Not** Catholic doctrine; a cultural interpretation |
| **Remove mourning pins/armbands**                  | **Filipino (Cultural)**    | Some families remove mourning attire on this day     |

### Beyond the 40 Days

| Action                                         | Classification          | Notes                                                                                                 |
| :--------------------------------------------- | :---------------------- | :---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Observe _Babang-luksa_** (first anniversary) | **Filipino (Cultural)** | Formal end of mourning period                                                                         |
| **Continue to offer Masses**                   | **Catholic**            | The Church encourages perpetual prayer for the dead                                                   |
| **Visit the grave on All Souls' Day**          | **Both**                | **Catholic**: Gain indulgences for the deceased. **Filipino**: Extended cemetery visits with feasting |
| **Remember the deceased at annual Masses**     | **Catholic**            | The anniversary of death is a traditional time for a Requiem Mass                                     |
