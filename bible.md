# Bible History and the Douay-Rheims Bible

## Short answer

No. The **Douay-Rheims** is not the single official Bible for all Catholics.

It is a historic and approved **English Catholic translation**, but Catholics do not have one single universal vernacular Bible that every Catholic everywhere must use. Historically, the **Latin Vulgate** held special official standing in the Latin Church, while in practice Catholics today use different approved translations depending on language, country, and liturgical norms. So if the question is, "Is Douay-Rheims the official Bible of Catholics?" the best short answer is **no, not as the sole official Bible for the whole Church**.

## How the Bible was created and compiled

### 1. The Bible was written over a long period, not all at once

The Bible was not produced as a single book in one moment. It is a library of books written across many centuries.

- The **Old Testament** grew within the life of Israel through the Law, historical books, wisdom literature, and the prophets.
- The **New Testament** was written in the first century, as the apostles and their close associates recorded the life of Christ, apostolic preaching, and letters to the churches.

From a Catholic perspective, these books are Scripture because they were written under the inspiration of the Holy Spirit. The Church teaches that the books of Scripture are "sacred and canonical" because they are inspired by God and teach saving truth faithfully. See [Dei Verbum 11](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/4a72892b-0a56-4371-a138-ff4aea2b0093/ref/11).

### 2. Scripture and Tradition belong together

Catholics do not view the Bible as existing apart from the life of the Church. Sacred Scripture and Sacred Tradition together hand on the Word of God entrusted to the Church.

- See [Dei Verbum 9](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/4a72892b-0a56-4371-a138-ff4aea2b0093/ref/9)
- See [CCC 80](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/0583c069-d4bf-42dd-97de-c19f0b80150f/ref/para-80), [CCC 81](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/0583c069-d4bf-42dd-97de-c19f0b80150f/ref/para-81), and [CCC 82](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/0583c069-d4bf-42dd-97de-c19f0b80150f/ref/para-82)

That matters for the history of the canon: the Church did not invent Scripture, but she did receive, preserve, read, and recognize which books belonged to the canon.

### 3. The Old Testament came down through Jewish and early Christian use

Before Christ, the Scriptures of Israel were already in use. A major milestone was the **Septuagint**, the ancient Greek translation of the Old Testament. It became very important for Greek-speaking Jews and for early Christians, and it strongly shaped the Old Testament used in the early Church.

The formation of the Old Testament canon was not identical in every place from the beginning. The Pontifical Biblical Commission notes that the Church gradually came to a settled Old Testament canon through long usage and reception. See [The Jewish People and Their Sacred Scriptures in the Christian Bible, 18](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/00a1de35-98f1-481a-956f-e98e2b52331d/ref/18).

### 4. The New Testament books were recognized through apostolic and ecclesial use

The New Testament books were written in the apostolic age and circulated among the churches. Over time, the Church recognized which writings were truly apostolic and belonged to the rule of faith.

In practice, this recognition happened through:

- use in the Church's worship and preaching
- widespread acceptance among the bishops and churches
- consistency with apostolic teaching already received in Tradition

Catholic teaching also holds that the authentic interpretation of the Word of God is entrusted to the Church's Magisterium. See [CCC 100](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/0583c069-d4bf-42dd-97de-c19f0b80150f/ref/100) and [Dei Verbum 12](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/4a72892b-0a56-4371-a138-ff4aea2b0093/ref/12).

### 5. The canon was clarified over time and definitively reaffirmed for Catholics

Historically, Christians used the biblical books before there was a single universally defined list in the later technical sense. Several milestones are commonly cited:

- the widespread use of the **Septuagint** in the early Church
- the writing and circulation of the New Testament in the first century
- the witness of the Church Fathers
- regional councils such as **Hippo (393)** and **Carthage (397, 419)**, which reflected Western consensus on the canon
- the **Council of Trent (1546)**, which definitively reaffirmed the Catholic canon in response to Reformation disputes

For Catholics, the key point is that the Bible's canon was recognized within the life of the Church, not as a private or purely individual decision.

### 6. The Catholic Bible contains 73 books

The Catholic canon contains **73 books**:

- **46 books** in the Old Testament
- **27 books** in the New Testament

That includes the deuterocanonical books received in Catholic tradition, such as Tobit, Judith, Wisdom, Sirach, Baruch, 1 and 2 Maccabees, along with the Catholic forms of Esther and Daniel. See [CCC 120](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/0583c069-d4bf-42dd-97de-c19f0b80150f/ref/120).

## The Douay-Rheims Bible

### What it is

The **Douay-Rheims Bible** is the classic English Catholic Bible translated primarily from the **Latin Vulgate**. It was produced for English-speaking Catholics during the Reformation era.

### When it was published

- The **New Testament** was published at **Rheims in 1582**.
- The **Old Testament** was published at **Douay in 1609-1610**.

These dates are standard in Catholic reference works. See the [Catholic Encyclopedia, "Douay Bible"](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/f2c979a8-871d-4f46-a059-320d3b837a76/ref/Douay%20Bible).

### Why it was made

The Douay-Rheims was made because English Catholics wanted a translation they could rely on during a period of intense religious controversy in England. Protestant English Bibles were already circulating widely, so Catholic scholars produced an English Bible grounded in the Latin Vulgate and Catholic tradition.

### Its connection to the Vulgate

Unlike many modern translations, the Douay-Rheims was translated chiefly from the **Latin Vulgate**, not directly from Hebrew and Greek. That reflects the importance the Vulgate had in the Western Church.

### Was it important for English-speaking Catholics?

Yes. For a long time it was the standard English Catholic Bible tradition. In practice, many editions later used by Catholics were based on **Bishop Richard Challoner's revisions** in the eighteenth century, so many copies called "Douay-Rheims" today are actually Challoner-style revisions of the older text.

### Its relationship to the King James Version

The Douay-Rheims New Testament came out before the **King James Version**. Historians often note that it likely influenced later English biblical style, even though the traditions are distinct. See the [Catholic Encyclopedia, "The Authorized Version"](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/f2c979a8-871d-4f46-a059-320d3b837a76/ref/The%20Authorized%20Version).

## Is the Douay-Rheims the official Bible of Catholics?

Not in the sense of being the one exclusive Bible for all Catholics.

- It is a **historic English Catholic translation**.
- It has been widely used and respected.
- It is not the only Bible approved for Catholic use.
- Catholics today use different approved translations in different places.

So the most accurate statement is: **Douay-Rheims is an important traditional Catholic English Bible, but not the sole official Bible of the Catholic Church.**

## Modern vernacular translations approved by Catholics

According to **Canon 825 §1** of the 1983 Code of Canon Law, vernacular Bible translations cannot be published unless they are approved by the **Apostolic See or the conference of bishops**, and they must include **necessary and sufficient annotations** (explanatory notes).

Consequently, rather than having a single "official" Bible translation, the Catholic Church recognizes multiple translations that carry an official **imprimatur** (Latin for "let it be printed") and/or **approbation** by a bishops' conference.

Several prominent modern English translations are widely approved and used for study, devotion, and liturgy:

### 1. NABRE (New American Bible Revised Edition)

- **Usage:** Primarily used in the United States. The New American Bible (NAB) serves as the basis for the Liturgy of the Word (readings at Mass) in the USA.
- **Character:** It is a formal yet pastoral translation translated from the original languages, containing extensive explanatory notes, introductions, and cross-references.

### 2. RSV-CE and RSV-2CE (Revised Standard Version Catholic Edition)

- **Usage:** Widely used globally for academic study and personal devotion. The RSV-2CE (Second Catholic Edition) is used in the Anglican Use ordinariates and features updated liturgical English.
- **Character:** A highly literal (word-for-word) translation renowned for its accuracy, literary beauty, and fidelity to original Greek and Hebrew manuscripts.

### 3. NRSV-CE (New Revised Standard Version Catholic Edition)

- **Usage:** Approved for personal study and devotional use, and used liturgically in some English-speaking countries.
- **Character:** A popular modern translation that balance readability with literal precision, using gender-inclusive language where the translators deemed the original context referred to both men and women.

### 4. ESV-CE (English Standard Version Catholic Edition)

- **Usage:** Approved by various bishops' conferences and used for the liturgy in places such as Great Britain, India, and parts of the English-speaking world.
- **Character:** A highly precise, formal-equivalence (word-for-word) translation that maintains classical dignity while incorporating contemporary biblical scholarship.

### 5. The Jerusalem Bible (JB) and New Jerusalem Bible (NJB)

- **Usage:** Widely used throughout the British Commonwealth and Europe. The Jerusalem Bible is the source for Mass readings in several English-speaking countries (such as the UK, Ireland, and Australia).
- **Character:** A more dynamic-equivalence (sense-for-sense) translation based on French-led biblical scholarship (_La Bible de Jérusalem_). It is celebrated for its poetic, literary style and deep theological footnotes.

## Do these modern Bibles contain the deuterocanonical books?

Yes. **Catholic editions of all these modern Bibles contain the deuterocanonical books** (along with the deuterocanonical additions to Esther and Daniel).

In Catholic theology, these books are not considered optional or of a "lesser" status; they are fully inspired and canonical parts of the Old Testament.

Here is how they are treated and organized in modern Catholic editions:

### 1. Integrated into the Old Testament (not separated)

In standard Catholic Bibles, the deuterocanonical books are **interspersed naturally throughout the Old Testament** according to traditional historical and literary categories (reflecting the arrangement of the ancient Greek Septuagint and the Council of Florence in 1442). For example:

- **Tobit** and **Judith** are placed among the historical books (after Nehemiah).
- **Wisdom** and **Sirach** are placed among the wisdom books (after the Song of Songs).
- **Baruch** is placed among the prophets (after Jeremiah).
- **1 and 2 Maccabees** are placed at the end of the historical books.

They are not grouped together in a separate "Apocrypha" section at the end of the Old Testament, as is common in Protestant Bibles that include them.

### 2. Difference between "Catholic Editions" and "Standard Editions"

Several modern translations (such as the **RSV**, **NRSV**, and **ESV**) were originally translated by ecumenical committees and published in standard editions that omit these books or place them in an appendix labeled "Apocrypha."

When choosing a Bible for Catholic use, it is important to look specifically for the **"Catholic Edition"** (like the _RSV-CE_, _NRSV-CE_, or _ESV-CE_). These editions:

- Include the deuterocanonical books integrated into their proper historical places in the Old Testament.
- Secure the official ecclesiastical approval (**imprimatur** and **approbation**) required under canon law.
- Often make minor adjustments to the translation notes to align with Catholic theological and liturgical traditions.
- Translations such as _NABRE_, _JB_, and _NJB_ were created by Catholic scholars from the outset and always include these books as standard.

### 3. The distinction between "Protocanonical" and "Deuterocanonical"

For Catholics, the distinction between "protocanonical" (books whose canonization was never widely disputed in the early Church) and "deuterocanonical" (books whose canonical status was clarified later) is purely historical. It does **not** imply a difference in authority or inspiration. Both categories are equally the inspired Word of God.

## Guide to the Deuterocanonical Books

The seven deuterocanonical books (and the Greek additions to Esther and Daniel) provide a key theological bridge connecting the Old Testament prophets to the coming of Christ in the New Testament. They deepen major biblical themes: covenant fidelity under suffering, God’s wisdom and justice, prayer that trusts providence, and the purification of Israel’s hope.

Here is a concise overview of each book's historical context, main themes, and spiritual value:

### 1. Tobit (Tobias)

- **Historical Context:** Set among the northern kingdom Israelites exiled to Nineveh (Assyria) in the 8th century BC.
- **Main Themes:** Family faithfulness in exile, the spiritual power of almsgiving and works of mercy, and divine providence. Guided by the Archangel Raphael, young Tobias completes a journey that brings physical and spiritual healing to his family.
- **Spiritual Value:** Teaches that God hears the prayers of the righteous who are tested, and that those who serve others will experience God's goodness in the end. It also contains beautiful teachings on the holiness of marriage.

### 2. Judith

- **Historical Context:** Portrays a crisis when a massive foreign army threatens to destroy Jerusalem and force Israel to abandon God.
- **Main Themes:** Deliverance through faith-filled prayer, the role of courage, and the efficacy of trust in God. Judith—a righteous, prayerful widow—acts with great courage to defeat the opposing army.
- **Spiritual Value:** Highlights that God uses the "weak things of the world" to confound the strong. In Catholic tradition, Judith is often viewed as a prefiguration of the Virgin Mary, whose humble "yes" cooperated in defeating the power of sin.

### 3. Wisdom (of Solomon)

- **Historical Context:** Written in the Hellenistic Greek world (likely Alexandria) to defend Jewish faith against Greek pagan philosophy and cultural assimilation.
- **Main Themes:** The search for righteousness and justice, the immortality of the soul, and Wisdom as a gift of the Holy Spirit.
- **Spiritual Value:** Contains profound teachings on the afterlife (proclaiming that "the souls of the just are in the hand of God"). It provides a crucial foundation for NT theology, as New Testament writers use some of Wisdom’s terminology to describe Jesus Christ as the "radiance of God's glory."

### 4. Sirach (Ecclesiasticus)

- **Historical Context:** Written in Hebrew in Jerusalem by a wise teacher, Jesus son of Sirach, around 180 BC to guide young Jews under the cultural pressures of Hellenism.
- **Main Themes:** True wisdom identified as the "fear of the Lord" and direct obedience to His commandments. It details practical guidance on friendship, speech, family, work, and moral character.
- **Spiritual Value:** Historically used as a "church reading book" (_Ecclesiasticus_) for instructing catechumens in Christian ethics. It helps believers shape daily relationships and moral habits to conform to God's law.

### 5. Baruch

- **Historical Context:** Attributed to Baruch, the scribe and secretary of the prophet Jeremiah, writing as a message from the Babylonian exiles in the 6th century BC.
- **Main Themes:** Public confession of sins, deep repentance, turning back to God's Law as the source of wisdom, and comfort for those who are suffering.
- **Spiritual Value:** Establishes exile as a school of conversion. It models how liturgies of communal confession and repentance can restore trust in God's mercy and promise of restoration.

### 6. 1 Maccabees

- **Historical Context:** Written around 100 BC to record the Maccabean Revolt (167–134 BC), in which orthodox Jews rose up against the Seleucid King Antiochus IV Epiphanes, who attempted to outlaw Jewish worship and desecrate the Temple.
- **Main Themes:** Zealous defense of the Law, the preservation of Israel's covenant identity, and military campaigns of Mattathias and his sons.
- **Spiritual Value:** Inspires heroic courage under political and religious persecution. It explains the origin of the Feast of Hanukkah (the rededication of the Temple) and shows the costly fidelity of the Maccabean martyrs.

### 7. 2 Maccabees

- **Historical Context:** Covers the same historical era as 1 Maccabees but is written as a theological interpretation of the events, emphasizing the direct spiritual meaning of the Maccabean struggles.
- **Main Themes:** The reward of martyrs, the reality of the afterlife, and prayers for the dead. It features famous accounts of the martyrdom of Eleazar and the mother and her seven sons.
- **Spiritual Value:** Contains crucial explicit scriptural witness for Catholic doctrines regarding the resurrection of the body, the reward of martyrs, the intercession of saints (such as Jeremiah praying for Israel), and **prayer and sacrifice for the deceased** (specifically in [2 Maccabees 12:44-46](https://www.magisterium.com/docs/4a72892b-0a56-4371-a138-ff4aea2b0093/ref/12)).

### Greek Additions to Esther and Daniel

Catholic Bibles also contain important sections of Daniel and Esther translated from Greek (not found in the Hebrew Masoretic texts):

- **Additions to Esther:** Adds Mordecai’s dream, prayers of Esther and Mordecai, and documents detailing the royal decrees. They intensify the book's theological focus on prayer and God's providence.
- **Additions to Daniel:** Includes the **Prayer of Azariah and the Song of the Three Holy Children** (the hymn sung inside the fiery furnace), **Susanna** (where Daniel's wisdom exposes false witness), and **Bel and the Dragon** (two satirical narratives exposing pagan idolatry).

## King James Version (KJV) and the Protestant Canon

### 1. Is the King James Bible a Protestant Bible?

Yes. The **King James Version (KJV)**, also known as the **Authorized Version (AV)**, is a Protestant (Anglican) Bible translation.

It was commissioned in 1604 by King James I of England for the Church of England and published in **1611**. The translation was completed by 47 Anglican scholars who worked from the Hebrew and Aramaic texts for the Old Testament, and the Greek texts for the New Testament. Crucially, its translation choices and canon reflect the Protestant theological perspective of the Church of England during the Reformation era.

### 2. Does the KJV include the deuterocanonical books?

The answer is historically **yes**, but practically today **no**:

- **Historically (The 1611 Edition):** When the King James Bible was first published in 1611, it **did contain the deuterocanonical books** (which Protestants refer to as the "Apocrypha"). Under the laws of the Church of England at the time, Bibles were required to include these books. However, they were not mixed inside the Old Testament as they are in Catholic Bibles. Instead, the KJV translators placed them in a **separate section sandwiched between the Old Testament and the New Testament**, titled _"Mystery of the Apocrypha"_.
- **The Shift (17th–19th Century):** Because Protestant theologians did not accept these books as canonical Scripture, pressure grew to print Bibles without them to save on printing costs and align with Puritans who strongly opposed their inclusion. By 1666, some KJV printings began omitting them.
- **The Decisive Change (1826):** In **1826**, the _British and Foreign Bible Society_ (a major distributor of Protestant Bibles) decided to completely stop funding or distributing any Bible that contained the Apocrypha. After this decision, publishers almost entirely ceased printing the KJV with the Apocrypha. Today, the vast majority of King James Bibles printed and sold do not contain these books, leaving them with only 66 books (39 OT and 27 NT) instead of the complete 73-book Catholic canon.

### 3. Catholic Perspective on the KJV

From a Catholic standpoint, standard Protestant editions of the King James Bible are considered incomplete because they omit the 7 deuterocanonical books (and portions of Esther and Daniel) defined as sacred and canonical by the Councils of Florence and Trent. Additionally, under **Canon 825** of the Code of Canon Law, Catholics are only permitted to read and study translations that have been formally approved (with an imprimatur) and feature sufficient explanatory annotations to protect readers from doctrinal error.

## Simple summary

- The Bible was written over centuries and recognized as one canon within the life of the Church.
- Catholics hold that Scripture is inspired by God and must be read with Sacred Tradition and the Church's authentic teaching office.
- The Catholic canon contains 73 books.
- The Douay-Rheims is a major historic English Catholic translation from the Latin Vulgate.
- It is not the one universal official Bible for all Catholics.
