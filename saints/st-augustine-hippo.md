## 1. Introduction

**St. Augustine of Hippo** (Latin: *Aurelius Augustinus Hipponensis*; 13 November 354 – 28 August 430 A.D.) was a Latin Roman African philosopher, theologian, and Bishop of Hippo Regius (modern-day Annaba, Algeria). He is one of the most important figures in the development of Western Christianity and philosophy, honoured as a **Doctor of the Church** (the "Doctor of Grace") and a **Father of the Church**. His feast day is **28 August**.

> "You have made us for yourself, O Lord, and our heart is restless until it rests in you."
> — St. Augustine, *Confessions*, I.1

---

## 2. Early Life

### Birth and Family

Augustine was born on **13 November 354** in **Thagaste** (modern-day Souk Ahras, Algeria), a small Roman municipal town in the Roman province of Numidia. His father, **Patricius**, was a pagan Roman official; his mother, **St. Monica**, was a devout Christian whose prayers and tears for his conversion are legendary.

### Education

Augustine received a Latin education in Thagaste and **Madaura**, and at age sixteen went to the University of **Carthage** to study rhetoric. There he excelled in his studies but also fell into a life of worldly pleasure. He took a concubine with whom he fathered a son, **Adeodatus** ("gift of God"), in 372.

### The Search for Wisdom

Augustine's restless intelligence drove him through a series of philosophical and religious systems:

1. **Manichaeism** (c. 373–383): A dualistic religion that explained evil as a co-eternal principle. Augustine was a "hearer" for nine years but grew disillusioned with its inability to answer his questions.
2. **Skepticism** (c. 383): After abandoning Manichaeism, Augustine briefly embraced the Academic skepticism of Cicero.
3. **Neoplatonism** (c. 386): Reading the works of Plotinus in Latin translation, Augustine encountered a philosophy of spiritual ascent toward the One—a crucial step on his path to Christianity.

## 3. Conversion

### Milan and St. Ambrose

In **384**, Augustine moved to **Milan** to take up a prestigious position as professor of rhetoric. There he heard the sermons of **St. Ambrose**, the bishop of Milan, whose allegorical interpretation of Scripture overcame Augustine's objections to the Old Testament.

### The Garden Conversion

The decisive moment came in August **386**. In a garden in Milan, Augustine heard a child's voice chanting: **"Tolle, lege! Tolle, lege!"** ("Take up and read! Take up and read!"). He opened the Scriptures at random and read Romans 13:13–14:

> "Let us live honourably as in the day, not in revelry and drunkenness, not in debauchery and licentiousness, not in quarreling and jealousy. Instead, put on the Lord Jesus Christ, and make no provision for the flesh, to gratify its desires."

The reading pierced his heart. His long struggle ended. He later wrote: "No further would I read, nor needed I; for instantly at the end of this sentence, a light as it were of serenity infused into my heart, all the darkness of doubt vanished away" (*Confessions*, VIII.12).

### Baptism

Augustine was **baptized by St. Ambrose** at the Easter Vigil on **24–25 April 387** in Milan, along with his son Adeodatus and his friend Alypius.

## 4. Bishop of Hippo

### Return to Africa

After his mother's death at Ostia (387), Augustine returned to Africa, where he founded a small monastic community at Thagaste. In **391**, he was ordained a priest by Bishop Valerius of Hippo Regius, and in **395** he was consecrated as **coadjutor bishop** of Hippo, becoming its bishop upon Valerius's death in 396.

### The Shepherd of Hippo

Augustine served as Bishop of Hippo for **thirty-four years** (396–430). He preached daily, wrote extensively, and engaged in fierce controversies against:

- **Manichaeism**: From which he had converted, writing numerous works against Manichaean dualism.
- **Donatism**: A schismatic movement in North Africa that held that sacraments administered by priests who had lapsed during persecution were invalid. Augustine developed the doctrine that the efficacy of the sacraments depends on Christ, not the worthiness of the minister (*ex opere operato*).
- **Pelagianism**: The heresy that denied original sin and claimed humans could achieve salvation by their own efforts. Augustine developed the doctrines of original sin, grace, and predestination in response.

## 5. Major Works

### The Confessions (c. 397–401)

The ***Confessions*** (*Confessiones*) is Augustine's spiritual autobiography, addressed directly to God. It is the first Western autobiography and a masterpiece of world literature. In thirteen books, it recounts his life from infancy to his conversion, culminating in a profound meditation on the book of Genesis and the nature of time.

### The City of God (c. 413–426)

The ***City of God*** (*De Civitate Dei*) is Augustine's monumental apologetic work, written in response to the **Sack of Rome** by the Visigoths in 410. When pagans blamed Christianity for the fall of Rome, Augustine responded by contrasting the **Earthly City** (founded on self-love) with the **Heavenly City** (founded on love of God). This work shaped Western political thought for a millennium.

### On the Trinity (c. 400–416)

One of the most profound theological treatments of the Trinity, in which Augustine explores psychological analogies (memory, understanding, will) for the triune God.

### On Christian Doctrine (c. 396–426)

A foundational work of biblical hermeneutics, laying out rules for interpreting Scripture, including the famous principle: "Whoever thinks that he understands the divine Scriptures in such a way that he does not build up that double love of God and neighbor, does not yet understand them."

## 6. Death and Legacy

### The Siege of Hippo

In **430**, the **Vandals**, an Arian Germanic tribe under King Genseric, besieged Hippo Regius. During the siege, Augustine fell ill.

### Death

Augustine died on **28 August 430** at the age of seventy-five. He had ordered that the **Penitential Psalms** be inscribed on the walls of his sickroom so he could read them during his final days. He died as the Vandals surrounded the city.

### Relics

His relics were translated by the Church to **Pavia, Italy**, where they rest in the Basilica of San Pietro in Ciel d'Oro.
