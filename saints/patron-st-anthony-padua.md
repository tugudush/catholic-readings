# St. Anthony of Padua: Evangelical Doctor and Patron of Lost Things

> **Note:** This document was composed with the assistance of Magisterium AI. Citations should be verified against primary sources where possible.

---

## 1. Introduction

**St. Anthony of Padua** (Portuguese: _Santo António de Lisboa_; Italian: _Sant'Antonio di Padova_; 1195 – 13 June 1231), born **Fernando Martins de Bulhões**, was a Portuguese Catholic priest, a member of the **Order of Friars Minor**, and a **Doctor of the Church** (proclaimed by Pope Pius XII in 1946 with the title "Evangelical Doctor"). He is one of the most popular and widely invoked saints in the Catholic world, celebrated especially as the **patron of lost things**. His feast day is **13 June**.

> "The man who is filled with the Holy Spirit speaks in all languages. The Apostles were filled with the Holy Spirit and spoke in all tongues."
> — St. Anthony of Padua, _Sermons_

---

## 2. Early Life

### Birth and Family

Fernando Martins de Bulhões was born in **Lisbon, Portugal**, in **1195** to a wealthy and noble family. His father, Martinho de Bulhões, was a knight of the court of King Alfonso II, and his mother, Maria Teresa Pais Taveira, was a devout Christian. They ensured their son received an excellent education at the **Cathedral School of Lisbon**.

### The Augustinian Canons

At age fifteen, Fernando entered the **Augustinian Canons Regular** at the Monastery of St. Vincent on the outskirts of Lisbon, feeling called to religious life. He spent two years there before transferring to the **Monastery of the Holy Cross** in Coimbra, then the most important cultural centre of Portugal. During his nine years there, he studied Scripture, theology, and patristics, developing a profound knowledge that would later earn him the title of Doctor of the Church.

---

## 3. The Franciscan Vocation

### The Martyrs of Morocco

In **1220**, the relics of five Franciscan missionaries—Berard, Peter, Otto, Accursius, and Adjutus—who had been martyred in **Morocco** were brought to Coimbra. Deeply moved by their sacrifice, Fernando felt a powerful call to join the Friars Minor and to seek martyrdom himself.

### Joining the Friars Minor

Fernando obtained permission to leave the Augustinians and entered the **Franciscan Order** at the friary of St. Anthony of Oliphant in Coimbra, taking the religious name **Anthony** in honour of St. Anthony the Great, the father of monasticism. He immediately set out for Morocco to preach and seek martyrdom.

### Illness and Shipwreck

Shortly after arriving in Morocco, Anthony fell gravely ill and was forced to return to Portugal. His ship was caught in a storm and wrecked off the coast of **Sicily**, where he was taken in by the Franciscan friars. He eventually made his way to Assisi for the **Pentecost Chapter of Mats** (1221), where he encountered St. Francis himself.

---

## 4. The Great Preacher

### Discovery of His Gift

At the Chapter, the humble Anthony was planning to serve in a remote hermitage in northern Italy. He was assigned to the friary at **Montepaolo** near Forlì, where he lived in obscurity, performing menial tasks. His hidden gift for preaching was discovered by accident when he gave a spontaneous sermon at an ordination ceremony—the scheduled preacher failed to appear, and Anthony's profound knowledge of Scripture and compelling delivery astonished all present.

### Preaching Against Heresy

St. Anthony preached throughout northern Italy and southern France, then troubled by the **Cathar** and **Albigensian** heresies. His combination of deep learning, scriptural mastery, and personal holiness made him extraordinarily effective. He was known as the **"Hammer of the Heretics"** (_Malleus Hereticorum_).

### The First Franciscan Theology Teacher

St. Francis himself recognized Anthony's gifts and appointed him as the **first professor of theology** for the Franciscan Order, writing: "To Brother Anthony, my bishop, greetings. It is pleasing to me that you teach sacred theology to the brothers, provided that in such study they do not destroy the spirit of holy prayer and devotion" (Letter of St. Francis, c. 1223).

## 5. Miracles

St. Anthony's reputation for miracles was extraordinary, both during his life and after his death:

### The Miracle of the Eucharist

A heretic who doubted the Real Presence challenged Anthony to a test. The heretic's mule, given blessed hay to eat, knelt before the consecrated Host raised by Anthony, demonstrating the truth of the Eucharist.

### The Sermon to the Fish

When heretics in Rimini refused to listen to his preaching, Anthony went to the seashore and preached to the fish, who gathered in great numbers, raising their heads above water as if to listen. The people of the city, amazed, came running and were converted.

### The Doctorate of the Church

Pope **Pius XII** proclaimed Anthony a **Doctor of the Church** on **16 January 1946**, giving him the title **"Doctor Evangelicus"** (Evangelical Doctor), in recognition of his profound scriptural knowledge and preaching.

---

## 6. Death at Padua

In the spring of **1231**, exhausted by his labours, Anthony retired to a cell in the wood at **Arcella** near Padua. He fell ill with dropsy and, feeling his end approaching, received the last sacraments. After reciting the hymn _O Gloriosa Domina_, he died peacefully on **13 June 1231** at the age of thirty-six.

### Canonization

St. Anthony was **canonized by Pope Gregory IX** less than a year after his death, on **30 May 1232**—one of the fastest canonizations in Church history. The Pope, who had known Anthony personally, declared him a saint in a ceremony at Spoleto.

### The Basilica of St. Anthony

Anthony's relics rest in the magnificent **Basilica of St. Anthony of Padua**, one of the most visited pilgrimage sites in Italy. The basilica, begun in 1232 and completed in the 14th century, is an architectural masterpiece blending Romanesque, Gothic, and Byzantine elements.

## 9. The "Tonsure of St. Anthony" and Popular Devotion

St. Anthony is one of the most invoked saints in Catholic popular piety. The **"Bread of St. Anthony"** tradition—alms given in thanksgiving for favours received—continues in many parishes. His **Sermons** (_Sermones Dominicales et Festivi_) remain a treasure of scriptural exegesis.

The **Lily of St. Anthony** and the **Infant Jesus** (whom he once held in a vision) are his traditional iconographic attributes.
