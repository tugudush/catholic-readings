# St. George: The Great Martyr and Patron of England

## 1. Introduction

**St. George** (Greek: ***Γεώργιος***, *Georgios*; Latin: *Georgius*; c. 275/280 – 23 April 303 A.D.) was a Roman soldier and Christian martyr who has been venerated as one of the **Fourteen Holy Helpers** and one of the most prominent military saints in the Catholic and Orthodox traditions. His legendary victory over a dragon has made him a symbol of the triumph of good over evil. He is the **patron saint of England, Georgia, Portugal, and Catalonia**. His feast day is **23 April**.

> "I am a servant of Christ. I have come to do the will of God."
> — St. George (from the traditional account of his trial)

---

## 2. Historical Background

### Life and Military Service

George was born to a Christian family in **Cappadocia** (modern-day Turkey) around **275–280 A.D.** His father was a Roman army officer, and his mother was from Lydda (modern-day Lod, Israel). Following his father's example, George joined the Roman army and rose to the rank of **Tribune** (senior officer) in the Imperial Guard under Emperor **Diocletian**.

### The Diocletianic Persecution

In **303 A.D.**, Diocletian issued an edict ordering the persecution of all Christians. George, as a high-ranking officer, was ordered to enforce this edict. Instead, he **publicly declared his Christian faith** before the Emperor, resigning his commission and denouncing the persecution.

## 3. Martyrdom

### The Trial

According to the traditional account (including the *Acta Sanctorum*), George was subjected to a series of tortures over **seven years**:

1. **Laceration on a wheel of swords** (the "wheel of martyrdom").
2. **Burning with torches**.
3. **Thrown into a pit of quicklime**.
4. **Forced to run in red-hot iron boots**.
5. **Beaten with iron rods**.
6. **Given poison** (which did not harm him).
7. **Resurrection of the dead** (a miracle attributed to him during his trial).

After each torture, George was miraculously healed, leading to the conversion of thousands—including the Empress **Alexandra**, who was martyred with him.

### Execution

On **23 April 303 A.D.**, after confessing Christ one final time, George was **beheaded** at **Nicomedia** (modern-day İzmit, Turkey).

## 4. The Dragon Legend

The most famous legend associated with St. George—the **dragon**—first appears in Western sources in the 11th–12th centuries (the *Golden Legend* of Jacobus de Voragine, c. 1260). According to the story:

- A dragon terrorized the city of **Silene** in Libya, demanding human sacrifices.
- The lot fell upon the **king's daughter** to be sacrificed.
- St. George, riding by, made the **sign of the cross**, wounded the dragon with his lance, and subdued it.
- He led the dragon into the city, telling the people: "Believe in Christ and be baptized, and I will slay this dragon."
- The king and all his people were baptized, and George killed the dragon.

This legend is understood allegorically as representing the victory of Christ (through His soldier) over Satan, the ancient serpent.

## 7. Veneration

### England

St. George became the patron saint of England after the Crusades. His red cross on a white field became the **national flag of England**. The **Order of the Garter** (founded by King Edward III in 1348) is under his patronage. St. George's Chapel at Windsor Castle is the spiritual home of the Order.

### The Fourteen Holy Helpers

St. George is numbered among the **Fourteen Holy Helpers**, a group of saints invoked for their powerful intercession, particularly against diseases and dangers.

---

## 8. Iconography

St. George is traditionally depicted as a **Roman soldier on horseback**, slaying a dragon with a lance. His iconographic attributes include:

- **White horse** (purity and victory)
- **Dragon or serpent** (Satan)
- **Lance or sword** (the Word of God and martyrdom)
- **Red cross on white shield** (his emblem)
- **Palm of martyrdom**
