# St. Francis of Assisi: Il Poverello, Founder of the Friars Minor

> **Note:** This document was composed with the assistance of Magisterium AI. Citations should be verified against primary sources where possible.

## 1. Introduction

**St. Francis of Assisi** (Italian: *Francesco d'Assisi*; Latin: *Franciscus Assisiensis*; 1181/1182 – 3 October 1226), born **Giovanni di Pietro di Bernardone**, was an Italian Catholic deacon, mystic, and the **founder of the Order of Friars Minor** (the Franciscans). Affectionately known as **Il Poverello** ("the Little Poor One"), he is one of the most beloved and widely venerated saints in all of Christendom. His feast day is celebrated on **4 October**.

> "Preach the Gospel at all times. When necessary, use words."
> — Attributed to St. Francis of Assisi

---

## 2. Early Life

### Birth and Family

Francis was born in **Assisi, Umbria** (central Italy) in **1181 or 1182**, the son of **Pietro Bernardone**, a wealthy cloth merchant, and **Madonna Pica**, who may have been of Provençal origin. His father was away on business in France at the time of his birth, and his mother had him baptized as **Giovanni** (John). When Pietro returned, he renamed his son **Francesco** ("the Frenchman"), perhaps in honour of his trading connections or his love for French culture.

### Youth as a Troubadour

Francis grew up in relative prosperity and was drawn to the ideals of **chivalry and courtly love** propagated by the troubadours. He was known for his generous, carefree spirit, spending money lavishly on fine clothes and entertainment. He dreamed of becoming a knight and winning glory on the battlefield.

### The Failed Knight

In **1202**, Francis fought in the war between Assisi and Perugia. He was taken prisoner, spent a year in captivity, and fell seriously ill. After his release and recovery, he attempted to join the papal forces under **Walter of Brienne** but turned back after a mysterious dream in which God asked him: "Who can do you more good, the master or the servant?" When Francis answered "The master," the voice replied: "Why then are you leaving the master for the servant?"

## 3. Conversion

### The Encounter with the Leper

The turning point of Francis's conversion was his meeting with a **leper** on the road. Initially repulsed, Francis overcame his disgust, embraced the leper, and gave him alms. He later wrote: "When I was in sin, it seemed very bitter to me to see lepers, and the Lord Himself led me among them and I showed mercy to them" (*Testament*). This act of compassionate self-conquest marked his break with his former life.

### The Call at San Damiano

In **1205**, while praying before the crucifix in the dilapidated chapel of **San Damiano**, Francis heard a voice from the crucifix: **"Francis, go and repair my house, which, as you see, is falling into ruin."** He took this literally and sold cloth from his father's shop to raise money for the chapel's repair.

### The Break with His Father

Pietro Bernardone, enraged by his son's behaviour, brought Francis before the **Bishop of Assisi** to recover the money. In a dramatic public gesture, Francis **stripped off his clothes**, returned them to his father, and declared: "Until now I have called you my father on earth. From now on I can truly say: 'Our Father who art in heaven.'" The bishop, moved, covered Francis with his own cloak.

## 4. The Franciscan Brotherhood

### The First Followers

On **24 February 1208**, Francis heard the Gospel of the commissioning of the disciples read at Mass: "Take no gold, nor silver, nor copper in your belts, no bag for your journey, nor two tunics, nor sandals, nor a staff" (Matthew 10:9–10). He understood this as his rule of life. Soon **St. Bernard of Quintavalle**, **St. Peter Catanii**, and **Bl. Giles** joined him. The brotherhood grew rapidly.

### Papal Approval (1209–1210)

Francis and his brothers traveled to **Rome** to seek approval from **Pope Innocent III**. The Pope, initially hesitant, was persuaded by a dream in which he saw Francis supporting the collapsing Lateran Basilica. He gave oral approval to Francis's **Primitive Rule**, a simple Gospel-based way of life.

### The Rule of 1223

The definitive **Rule of the Order of Friars Minor** was approved by **Pope Honorius III** in the bull *Solet Annuere* on **29 November 1223**. It is based on the observance of the holy Gospel, with poverty, chastity, and obedience as its foundation.

## 5. The Stigmata (1224)

In **September 1224**, two years before his death, Francis retreated to the mountain hermitage of **La Verna** for a forty-day fast in preparation for the Feast of St. Michael the Archangel. On or about **14 September** (the Feast of the Exaltation of the Holy Cross), while in ecstatic prayer, Francis received the **stigmata** — the visible marks of Christ's five wounds on his own body: nail wounds in his hands and feet, and a spear wound in his side.

Francis became the **first recorded stigmatist** in Christian history. He bore these wounds in excruciating pain for the remaining two years of his life, concealing them from others as much as possible.

> "I have done what is mine to do; may Christ now teach you what is yours to do."
> — St. Francis of Assisi, on his deathbed

## 6. The Canticle of the Sun

In the **spring of 1225**, while suffering from severe illness and near-blindness, Francis composed the **Canticle of the Sun** (*Cantico delle Creature*), one of the earliest and most beautiful works of Italian vernacular literature. In it, he praises God through all creation:

> "Praised be You, my Lord, with all Your creatures,
> especially Sir Brother Sun,
> who is the day and through whom You give us light…
> Praised be You, my Lord, through Sister Mother Earth,
> who sustains and governs us."

This canticle is the foundation of Francis's reputation as the **patron of ecology and animals**.

## 7. Death and Canonization

### Death at the Portiuncula

As death approached in the autumn of **1226**, Francis asked to be taken to the **Portiuncula**, the little chapel near Assisi that he loved so dearly. He was laid on the bare ground, clothed in a borrowed habit, and asked his brothers to read from the Gospel of John. On the evening of **3 October 1226**, he died, having just completed his final composition, the *Canticle of the Sun*'s final verse welcoming "Sister Death."

### Canonization

Less than two years after his death, on **16 July 1228**, Pope **Gregory IX** canonized Francis in Assisi. The following day, the Pope laid the foundation stone for the **Basilica of St. Francis** in Assisi, where his relics rest.

## 10. The Franciscan Family

St. Francis founded **three orders**:

1. **First Order**: The Order of Friars Minor (O.F.M.), to which belong the **Friars Minor** (O.F.M.), the **Friars Minor Conventual** (O.F.M. Conv.), and the **Friars Minor Capuchin** (O.F.M. Cap.).
2. **Second Order**: The **Poor Clares** (O.S.C.), founded by St. Francis with **St. Clare of Assisi** in 1212.
3. **Third Order**: The **Secular Franciscan Order** (O.F.S.), for laypeople and clergy living the Franciscan spirit in the world.
