# St. Michael the Archangel: Prince of the Heavenly Host

> **Note:** This document was composed with the assistance of Magisterium AI. Citations should be verified against primary sources where possible.

---

## 1. Introduction

**St. Michael the Archangel** (Hebrew: ***מִיכָאֵל***, *Mikha'el*, "Who is like God?"; Latin: *Michael*; Arabic: ***ميكائيل***, *Mika'il*) is one of the three archangels named in Sacred Scripture (along with Gabriel and Raphael) and the **leader of the heavenly host** in the war against Satan. He is revered as the **Prince of the Angels**, the **guardian of the Church**, and the **patron of soldiers, police, and the sick**. His feast is celebrated on **29 September** (Michaelmas), with a separate commemoration of the Apparition of St. Michael on **8 May**.

> "There was war in heaven. Michael and his angels fought against the dragon."
> — Revelation 12:7

---

## 2. Biblical References

### The Book of Daniel

Michael appears in the Old Testament Book of Daniel as the **"great prince, the protector of your people"** (Daniel 12:1) and as one of the **"chief princes"** who defends Israel (Daniel 10:13, 21).

### The Epistle of Jude

St. Jude recalls that **Michael the archangel**, when disputing with the devil over the body of Moses, "did not dare to bring a condemnation of blasphemy, but said, 'The Lord rebuke you!'" (Jude 1:9).

### The Book of Revelation

The most dramatic depiction of Michael is in the Apocalypse:

> "And war broke out in heaven; Michael and his angels fought against the dragon. The dragon and his angels fought back, but they were defeated, and there was no longer any place for them in heaven. The great dragon was thrown down, that ancient serpent, who is called the Devil and Satan, the deceiver of the whole world."
> — Revelation 12:7–9

## 3. Theological Significance

### Name and Meaning

The name **Michael** is a battle cry: "Who is like God?" (*Mikha'el*). His name itself is a declaration of God's incomparable majesty and a challenge to the pride of Lucifer.

### Prince of the Heavenly Host

Michael is recognized in Catholic tradition as:

- **The leader of the angels** who remained faithful to God during Lucifer's rebellion.
- **The protector of the Church** against the forces of evil.
- **The angel of the Presence** who stands before God.
- **The angel of death** who receives the souls of the departed.
- **The guardian of the Blessed Sacrament** (according to revelation to St. Margaret Mary Alacoque).

### The Four Offices of St. Michael

1. **To fight against Satan and the fallen angels.**
2. **To rescue the souls of the faithful from the power of the enemy, especially at the hour of death.**
3. **To be the champion of God's people** (the Church).
4. **To call away souls from the body and present them at judgment.**

## 4. Liturgical Veneration

### The Feast of St. Michael (29 September)

Known as **Michaelmas**, this feast celebrates all three archangels (Michael, Gabriel, Raphael). In the traditional Roman calendar, it was a Holy Day of Obligation.

### The Apparition of St. Michael (8 May)

Commemorates the appearance of St. Michael in the fifth century at **Monte Sant'Angelo** in Apulia, Italy—one of the oldest and most important shrines to St. Michael in Europe.

### The Prayer to St. Michael

Composed by Pope Leo XIII in **1886** after a vision of the battle against the forces of evil, this prayer was traditionally recited after Low Mass:

> "St. Michael the Archangel, defend us in battle. Be our protection against the wickedness and snares of the devil. May God rebuke him, we humbly pray, and do thou, O Prince of the heavenly host, by the power of God, cast into hell Satan and all the evil spirits who prowl about the world seeking the ruin of souls. Amen."

## 7. Iconography

St. Michael is traditionally depicted as a **winged warrior** in Roman or Byzantine armour, holding:

- **A sword** (representing his battle against evil)
- **A shield** (the protection of the Church)
- **A spear** (piercing Satan)
- **Scales** (representing his role at the judgment of souls)
- **A banner or standard** (the victory of Christ)
- **A dragon or serpent under his feet** (trampling Satan)

In Eastern iconography, he is often shown in the robes of a courtier rather than a warrior, reflecting his role as a prince of the heavenly court.
