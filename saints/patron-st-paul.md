# St. Paul the Apostle: Apostle to the Gentiles

> **Note:** This document was composed with the assistance of Magisterium AI. Citations should be verified against primary sources where possible.

## 1. Introduction

**St. Paul the Apostle** (Hebrew: ***שָׁאוּל***, *Sha'ul*; Greek: ***Παῦλος***, *Paulos*; Latin: *Paulus*; c. 5 – c. 64–67 A.D.), originally named **Saul of Tarsus**, was a Pharisaic Jew who, after a dramatic conversion on the road to Damascus, became the **greatest missionary of the early Church** and the **author of fourteen New Testament epistles**. He is venerated as the **Apostle to the Gentiles**, a Doctor of the Church by virtue of his inspired writings, and one of the two great patron saints of Rome alongside St. Peter. His feast day is celebrated jointly with St. Peter on **29 June**, and his conversion is commemorated on **25 January**.

> "I have been crucified with Christ; it is no longer I who live, but Christ who lives in me."
> — St. Paul, *Galatians 2:20*

---

## 2. Early Life and Formation

### Birth and Lineage

Saul was born in **Tarsus**, the capital of the Roman province of Cilicia (modern-day Turkey), around the same time as Jesus Christ. Tarsus was a major centre of Hellenistic culture and learning, and Saul inherited Roman citizenship from his family—a privilege that would serve him well in his missionary journeys.

He was born a **Pharisee** of the tribe of Benjamin (Philippians 3:5), and his family was devoutly Jewish. He received the name **Saul** after the first king of Israel, also of the tribe of Benjamin.

### Education Under Gamaliel

Saul was sent to **Jerusalem** to study under **Rabban Gamaliel the Elder**, one of the most revered Pharisaic teachers of the first century (Acts 22:3). Under Gamaliel, Saul received a rigorous education in the Hebrew Scriptures, Pharisaic tradition, and rabbinic interpretation. He advanced in Judaism beyond many of his contemporaries, being "extremely zealous for the traditions of my ancestors" (Galatians 1:14).

### The Persecutor of the Church

Before his conversion, Saul was a **zealous persecutor of the early Christian movement**. He approved of the stoning of St. Stephen, the first martyr (Acts 7:58–8:1), and actively "ravaged the church by entering house after house; dragging off both men and women, he committed them to prison" (Acts 8:3). He obtained letters from the high priest authorizing him to arrest Christians in Damascus and bring them bound to Jerusalem (Acts 9:1–2).

## 3. The Conversion on the Road to Damascus

### The Damascus Road Experience

As Saul journeyed to Damascus around **34–36 A.D.**, suddenly a light from heaven flashed around him. He fell to the ground and heard a voice saying: **"Saul, Saul, why do you persecute me?"** He answered: "Who are you, Lord?" The voice replied: **"I am Jesus, whom you are persecuting"** (Acts 9:3–5).

Blinded by the experience, Saul was led by the hand into Damascus, where for three days he neither ate nor drank. The Lord appeared in a vision to a disciple named **Ananias**, instructing him to lay hands on Saul. Ananias, initially fearful, obeyed and addressed Saul as **"Brother Saul"**, and immediately scales fell from Saul's eyes and he was filled with the Holy Spirit. He was baptized forthwith (Acts 9:10–19).

### Retreat in Arabia

After his conversion, Paul retreated to **Arabia** (likely the Nabatean kingdom) for a period of about three years (Galatians 1:17). This period of solitude, prayer, and reflection was his preparation for the apostolic mission. He then returned to Damascus before making his first visit to Jerusalem as a Christian.

## 4. The Missionary Journeys

Paul undertook **three great missionary journeys** that took him across Asia Minor, Macedonia, Greece, and ultimately to Rome, establishing Christian communities in major urban centres.

### First Missionary Journey (c. 46–48 A.D.)

Accompanied by **Barnabas** and **John Mark**, Paul set out from Antioch. They:

- Evangelized **Cyprus** (where the proconsul Sergius Paulus converted).
- Preached in **Pisidian Antioch**, where Paul delivered his first recorded sermon (Acts 13:16–41).
- Founded churches in **Iconium, Lystra, and Derbe**.
- Returned to Antioch, where Paul and Barnabas reported how God "had opened a door of faith to the Gentiles" (Acts 14:27).

### The Council of Jerusalem (c. 49 A.D.)

Paul traveled to Jerusalem to address the question of whether Gentile converts must observe the Mosaic Law. At the **Council of Jerusalem**, Paul and Peter both defended the freedom of Gentiles from circumcision and the ceremonial law. The council's decree, issued by James, recognized that salvation is through the grace of the Lord Jesus for both Jews and Gentiles (Acts 15:1–35).

### Second Missionary Journey (c. 49–52 A.D.)

Paul, now accompanied by **Silas**, revisited the churches of Asia Minor. At **Troas**, Paul received the vision of a Macedonian man pleading: "Come over to Macedonia and help us" (Acts 16:9). This vision redirected the Gospel to Europe. Key events:

- **Philippi**: Conversion of Lydia; Paul and Silas imprisoned and miraculously freed by an earthquake (Acts 16).
- **Thessalonica**: Establishment of a church that would receive two of Paul's epistles.
- **Athens**: Paul's address at the **Areopagus**, engaging Greek philosophy with the proclamation of the "Unknown God" (Acts 17:22–31).
- **Corinth**: Eighteen-month ministry, where Paul wrote 1 and 2 Thessalonians.

### Third Missionary Journey (c. 53–57 A.D.)

Based in **Ephesus** for nearly three years, Paul conducted an extensive ministry that resulted in the conversion of many and the spread of the Gospel throughout the province of Asia. He wrote 1 Corinthians, Galatians, and Romans during this period. The silversmiths' riot in Ephesus (Acts 19) eventually forced his departure.

### Journey to Rome (c. 60–61 A.D.)

Arrested in Jerusalem after a riot, Paul appealed to **Caesar** as a Roman citizen. He was transported to Rome, suffering shipwreck on Malta (where he was unharmed by a viper and healed the sick). He arrived in Rome under house arrest, where he continued to preach and write (the "Captivity Epistles": Ephesians, Philippians, Colossians, Philemon).

## 5. The Pauline Epistles

St. Paul's letters constitute the earliest Christian writings in the New Testament and the foundation of Christian theology. They are traditionally divided into four groups:

| Group | Epistles | Date |
|-------|----------|------|
| **Eschatological** | 1 & 2 Thessalonians | c. 50–52 A.D. |
| **Major Epistles** | Romans, 1 & 2 Corinthians, Galatians | c. 53–57 A.D. |
| **Captivity Epistles** | Ephesians, Philippians, Colossians, Philemon | c. 60–63 A.D. |
| **Pastoral Epistles** | 1 & 2 Timothy, Titus | c. 63–65 A.D. |

### Key Doctrinal Contributions

- **Justification by Faith**: Paul teaches that "a person is justified by faith apart from works of the law" (Romans 3:28), while affirming that faith must be active in love (Galatians 5:6).
- **The Divinity of Christ**: Paul proclaims Christ as "the image of the invisible God" in whom "the whole fullness of deity dwells bodily" (Colossians 1:15; 2:9).
- **The Church as the Body of Christ**: Paul develops the organic metaphor of the Church as the Body of Christ, with Christ as the head and all believers as members (1 Corinthians 12; Ephesians 4).
- **The Eucharist**: Paul provides the earliest written account of the institution of the Eucharist (1 Corinthians 11:23–26).
- **The Resurrection**: 1 Corinthians 15 is the most extensive treatment of the Resurrection in the New Testament.
- **Charity as the Greatest Virtue**: The "Hymn to Charity" (1 Corinthians 13) is Paul's exposition of love as the greatest theological virtue.

## 6. Martyrdom in Rome

After his release from house arrest (c. 63 A.D.), Paul likely traveled to **Spain** (as he had intended in Romans 15:24) and the **East**, revisiting his churches. He was rearrested during the **Neronian persecution** and brought to Rome for a second imprisonment.

According to the consistent testimony of early Christian writers (Eusebius, St. Jerome, Tertullian, Gaius of Rome), Paul was **beheaded** around **64–67 A.D.** at the **Aquae Salviae** (Tre Fontane) on the Ostian Way. As a Roman citizen, he was exempted from crucifixion and executed by the sword.

> "I have fought the good fight, I have finished the race, I have kept the faith. From now on there is reserved for me the crown of righteousness, which the Lord, the righteous judge, will give me on that day."
> — 2 Timothy 4:7–8

He was buried on the Via Ostiense, where the **Basilica of St. Paul Outside the Walls** now stands.

## 7. Patronage

St. Paul is the patron saint of:

| Patronage | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Missionaries and Evangelists** | As the Apostle to the Gentiles |
| **Theologians and Scripture Scholars** | As the author of fourteen inspired epistles |
| **Roman Catholic Church** | Co-patron of the Diocese of Rome with St. Peter |
| **Journalists, Writers, and Publishers** | As a prolific writer of Scripture |
| **Rope Makers and Basket Weavers** | Traditional patronage (his trade was tentmaking) |
| **Against Snake Bite** | From his survival of the viper on Malta |
| **Tent Makers** | His trade mentioned in Acts 18:3 |

He is also the patron of numerous cities, dioceses, and nations, including **Malta**, **Greece**, and **Rome**.

## 9. Iconography

St. Paul is traditionally depicted with:

- **Sword** (symbol of his martyrdom and the "sword of the Spirit" which is the Word of God)
- **Book or Scroll** (representing his epistles)
- **Three Springs of Water** (legend says his severed head bounced three times, producing springs, at Tre Fontane)
- **High Forehead and Receding Hairline** (from the earliest iconographic tradition)
- **Long Pointed Beard** (contrasting with St. Peter's round beard)

## 10. Legacy

### The Apostle to the Gentiles

Paul's missionary strategy—preaching first in synagogues, then turning to the Gentiles—shaped the expansion of Christianity from a Jewish sect to a world religion. His principle that "there is no longer Jew or Greek, there is no longer slave or free, there is no longer male and female; for all of you are one in Christ Jesus" (Galatians 3:28) remains a foundation of Christian unity.

### Influence on Christian Theology

St. Paul has shaped Christian theology more than any other human author. His epistles have been the source of major theological developments, including the doctrines of grace, justification, the Church, the sacraments, and eschatology. St. Augustine, St. Thomas Aquinas, Martin Luther, and countless others have drawn their deepest insights from the Pauline corpus.

### In Eastern Catholic Tradition

The Eastern Catholic Churches honour Paul as the **Apostle to the Nations** and the **First of the Apostles after Peter**. The Byzantine Rite commemorates him with an entire season of preparation (the Apostles' Fast) leading to the feast of 29 June.
