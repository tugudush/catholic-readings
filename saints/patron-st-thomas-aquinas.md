# St. Thomas Aquinas: The Angelic Doctor

## 1. Introduction

**St. Thomas Aquinas** (Italian: *Tommaso d'Aquino*; Latin: *Thomas de Aquino*; 1225 – 7 March 1274) was an Italian Dominican friar, priest, and the most influential theologian of the Catholic Church. He is honoured as the **Angelic Doctor** (*Doctor Angelicus*), the **Universal Doctor** (*Doctor Communis*), and a **Doctor of the Church**. His masterwork, the ***Summa Theologica***, remains the definitive synthesis of Catholic theology. His feast day is **28 January**.

> "To one who has faith, no explanation is necessary. To one without faith, no explanation is possible."
> — Attributed to St. Thomas Aquinas

---

## 2. Early Life

### Birth and Family

Thomas was born in **1225** at the Castle of **Roccasecca**, near **Aquino** in the Kingdom of Sicily (modern-day Lazio, Italy). He was the youngest son of **Landolfo d'Aquino**, a nobleman, and **Teodora Caracciolo**. The family was well-connected; Thomas was a cousin of Emperor Frederick II.

### Education at Monte Cassino

At age five, Thomas was sent as an **oblate** to the **Benedictine Abbey of Monte Cassino**, where he received his early education. He studied the liberal arts and Scripture, developing a reputation for quiet intensity and a penetrating intellect.

### Studies at Naples

In **1239**, due to political conflicts, Thomas was forced to leave Monte Cassino and continued his studies at the **University of Naples**, then a centre of Aristotelian learning. It was there that he first encountered the works of **Aristotle**, the philosophy of which would profoundly shape his theological method.

---

## 3. The Dominican Vocation

### Joining the Friars Preachers

Despite fierce opposition from his family—who had expected him to become a Benedictine abbot—Thomas was drawn to the newly founded **Order of Preachers** (the Dominicans), who embraced poverty, preaching, and intellectual life. He received the Dominican habit in **1244**.

### The Imprisonment

Thomas's mother, Teodora, was so opposed to his joining the Dominicans that she had him **kidnapped** and imprisoned in the family castle for over a year. During this time, his brothers tried to tempt him into abandoning his vocation by sending a prostitute to his room. Thomas drove her out with a burning brand from the fire, and thereafter, according to tradition, he was granted the grace of perfect chastity, confirmed by angels who girded him with a mystical belt.

### Release and Recovery

Eventually, Thomas's family relented, and he returned to the Dominicans in **1245**. He completed his novitiate and was sent to study in **Paris** and **Cologne**.

---

## 4. Student and Teacher

### Study Under Albert the Great

In **Cologne**, Thomas studied under **St. Albert the Great** (*Albertus Magnus*), the greatest scholar of the age. Albert recognized Thomas's extraordinary intellect despite his silent, reserved manner. When a fellow student nicknamed Thomas the **"Dumb Ox"** (*Bos mutus*), Albert famously declared: "You call him a dumb ox, but I tell you that the whole world will hear his bellowing."

### Teaching at the University of Paris

Thomas received his doctorate in theology in **1256** and began teaching at the **University of Paris**, the intellectual capital of Christendom. He held one of the Dominican chairs of theology, engaging in fierce academic controversies with secular masters and defending the mendicant orders.

## 5. Major Works

### The Summa Contra Gentiles (c. 1259–1265)

Written as a manual for missionaries, this work presents Catholic doctrine in dialogue with non-Christian philosophies, especially Islam and Aristotelianism. Its structure follows the order of: (1) truths about God known by reason, (2) truths about God revealed in Scripture, and (3) the way to union with God.

### The Summa Theologica (c. 1265–1273)

The ***Summa Theologica*** is Thomas's magnum opus and the most comprehensive synthesis of Catholic theology ever written. It is structured in three parts:

| Part | Subject | Content |
|------|---------|---------|
| **Prima Pars (I)** | God and Creation | The one God, the Trinity, creation, angels, the six days |
| **Prima Secundae (I–II)** | The Moral Life | The ultimate end, human acts, grace, law, virtues |
| **Secunda Secundae (II–II)** | The Virtues | Faith, hope, charity, prudence, justice, fortitude, temperance |
| **Tertia Pars (III)** | Christ and the Sacraments | The Incarnation, the life of Christ, the seven sacraments |

The *Summa* employs Thomas's distinctive **scholastic method**: the presentation of objections (*videtur quod*), the citation of authority (*sed contra*), the reasoned argument (*respondeo*), and the replies to objections.

### Eucharistic Hymns

Commissioned by Pope Urban IV for the new feast of **Corpus Christi** (1264), Thomas composed some of the most beautiful Latin hymns of the liturgy, including:

- ***Pange Lingua Gloriosi*** (the final verses of which form the *Tantum Ergo*)
- ***Lauda Sion Salvatorem*** (the sequence for Corpus Christi)
- ***Adoro Te Devote***
- ***Verbum Supernum Prodiens*** (the final verses of which form the *O Salutaris Hostia*)

## 6. The Eucharistic Miracle and the End of His Life

### The Vision at Naples

On **6 December 1273**, while celebrating Mass at the Chapel of St. Nicholas in Naples, Thomas experienced a profound vision. After this, he **stopped writing altogether**. When his secretary, Reginald of Piperno, urged him to continue the *Summa*, Thomas replied:

> "I cannot. Everything I have written seems like straw compared to what I have seen."

### Death

Pope Gregory X summoned Thomas to the **Second Council of Lyons** in 1274. While traveling, Thomas fell ill and was taken to the **Cistercian Abbey of Fossanova**, where he died on **7 March 1274** at the age of forty-eight. His last act was to receive the Eucharist.

### Canonization

- **Canonized**: **18 July 1323** by Pope John XXII.
- **Declared Doctor of the Church**: **1567** by Pope St. Pius V.
- **Proclaimed patron of all Catholic schools and universities**: **1880** by Pope Leo XIII.

## 9. The Five Ways

In the *Summa Theologica* (I, q. 2, a. 3), Thomas presents **five proofs for the existence of God**, known as the **Five Ways** (*Quinque Viae*):

1. **Argument from Motion** (*Ex Motu*): There must be a First Mover.
2. **Argument from Efficient Causation** (*Ex Causa*): There must be a First Cause.
3. **Argument from Contingency** (*Ex Possibili et Necessario*): There must be a Necessary Being.
4. **Argument from Degrees of Perfection** (*Ex Gradibus*): There must be a Perfect Being.
5. **Argument from Design** (*Ex Fine*): There must be an Intelligent Designer.
