# St. Athanasius of Alexandria: Champion of Nicene Orthodoxy

## 1. Introduction

**St. Athanasius of Alexandria** (Greek: ***Ἀθανάσιος Ἀλεξανδρείας***, _Athanasios Alexandreias_; c. 296 – 2 May 373 A.D.) was the **twentieth Bishop (Patriarch) of Alexandria**, a **Father and Doctor of the Church**, and the most formidable defender of Nicene orthodoxy against the Arian heresy in the fourth century. For his unwavering confession of the divinity of Jesus Christ—that the Son is *homoousios* (consubstantial, "of one substance") with the Father—he earned the title **"Athanasius contra mundum"** ("Athanasius against the world").

He is one of the **four Great Doctors of the Eastern Church** (along with St. Basil the Great, St. Gregory the Theologian/Nazianzen, and St. John Chrysostom) and is venerated in both the Latin and Eastern Catholic Churches, the Eastern Orthodox Church, the Oriental Orthodox Churches, and most Protestant communions that honour the ancient creeds. His feast day is celebrated on **2 May** in the Latin Rite and on **18 January** (and 2 May) in the Byzantine Rite.

> "Athanasius was the greatest champion of Catholic truth on the Incarnation and the Consubstantiality of the Son that the Church has ever known. He was, next to St. Paul, the clearest and fullest exponent of the mystery of Christ."
> — Adapted from Cardinal John Henry Newman

## 2. Historical Context: The Church in the Early Fourth Century

To understand St. Athanasius, one must grasp the theological and political crisis into which he was born.

### The Persecution That Had Just Ended

Athanasius was born around **296 A.D.**, only a decade before the Great Persecution of Diocletian (303–311), the most severe and systematic persecution the Church had ever faced. Many of his clergy and older contemporaries had been confessors—Christians who had suffered torture or imprisonment for the faith. The Church emerged from this persecution under the Emperor Constantine I, who issued the **Edict of Milan (313)** granting legal toleration to Christianity.

### The Outbreak of Arianism

No sooner had external peace been secured than an internal crisis arose that threatened to tear the Church apart. In **c. 318 A.D.**, a popular Alexandrian presbyter named **Arius** (c. 256–336) began publicly teaching that the Son of God was not eternal but was a *creature*—the first and greatest of creatures, through whom God made the universe, but still a being who had a beginning. Arius's core slogan was:

> "There was a time when He was not" (Greek: ***ἦν ποτε ὅτε οὐκ ἦν***).

Arius insisted that the Son was *of a different substance* (***heteroousios***) from the Father, a perfect creature but not God by nature. This teaching struck at the very heart of Christian faith: if the Son is not truly God, then he cannot save us, for only God can redeem mankind (cf. CCC 456–460).

The controversy spread rapidly through Alexandria and beyond, dividing clergy, monks, and laity.

## 3. Early Life and Formation (c. 296–318)

### Birth and Childhood

Athanasius was born in **Alexandria, Egypt** (or possibly the Nile Delta region) around **296 A.D.**, to a Christian family. Details of his early life are sparse, but his formation was unmistakably Alexandrian—immersed in the catechetical tradition of that great see.

According to a well-known tradition recorded by the fifth-century historian Socrates Scholasticus and by St. Gregory of Nazianzus, the young Athanasius was playing on the seashore one day when Bishop Alexander of Alexandria noticed him re-enacting baptismal rites with other children. The bishop, discerning a vocation, took the boy under his protection and ensured his education.

### Education and Formation

Athanasius received a thorough education in:
- **Sacred Scripture**: He knew the biblical text intimately and would later quote it with precision and force in all his theological writings.
- **Classical Greek Literature**: He was well-versed in Greek philosophy, rhetoric, and Homeric poetry, which he deployed deftly in his polemical works.
- **Alexandrian Theology**: He was formed in the tradition of Origen (though he would later distance himself from some of Origen's subordinationist tendencies) and of St. Peter of Alexandria, a martyr-bishop who had resisted Arianizing influences.

He also spent time with the monks of the Egyptian desert, particularly **St. Antony the Great** (c. 251–356), whose life he would later immortalize in his most widely read work.

### Secretary to Bishop Alexander

By **318 A.D.**, Athanasius had been ordained a deacon and was serving as **secretary to Bishop Alexander of Alexandria** (bishop 313–328). In this capacity, he attended the early synods that condemned Arius and began drafting the theological arguments that would carry the day.

## 4. The Council of Nicaea (325)

### The Gathering of the First Ecumenical Council

The Arian controversy had become so disruptive that the Emperor Constantine I, recently the sole ruler of the Roman Empire, convened the **First Ecumenical Council of Nicaea** in **May–July 325 A.D.** Approximately 300–318 bishops gathered (traditionally 318, a number laden with symbolism from Abraham's 318 servants in Genesis 14:14), mostly from the Eastern half of the Empire, along with a handful of Western delegates.

Bishop Alexander led the Alexandrian delegation, and **Athanasius accompanied him as his deacon-secretary**. Though not a bishop himself and therefore not a voting participant, Athanasius was already recognized as a formidable intellect.

### The Council's Deliberations

Two major theological parties emerged:

| Party | Position | Key Leaders |
|-------|----------|-------------|
| **Nicene Orthodox** | The Son is *homoousios* (consubstantial, of one substance) with the Father, true God from true God, eternally begotten, not made. | Alexander of Alexandria, Hosius of Córdoba, the young deacon Athanasius |
| **Arians** | The Son is a creature (*ktisma*), not eternal, of a different substance (*heteroousios*) from the Father. | Arius, Eusebius of Nicomedia, Theognis of Nicaea |
| **Origenist Moderates** | The Son is "like" (*homoios*) the Father, but resisted *homoousios* as imprecise. | Eusebius of Caesarea (the historian) |

The Council decisively rejected Arianism and promulgated the **Nicene Creed**, which declared that the Son is:

> "God from God, Light from Light, true God from true God, begotten, not made, **consubstantial** (***homoousios***) with the Father."

The term *homoousios*—a non-biblical word—was deliberately chosen to exclude any Arian interpretation. The Council also fixed the date of Easter and issued twenty canons on ecclesiastical discipline.

### Athanasius's Role

Though a deacon, Athanasius's role was so significant that later tradition exaggerated it (some legends claimed he presided or was the Council's chief theologian). What is historically certain is that he was present, supported Bishop Alexander, and emerged from the Council with an unshakeable conviction that *homoousios* was the indispensable bulwark of Christian faith.

> "The Word of God became man so that you might learn from a man how man can become God."
> — St. Athanasius, *On the Incarnation*, 54

## 5. Bishop of Alexandria (328–373)

### Election as Patriarch

Upon the death of Bishop Alexander in **328 A.D.**, Athanasius—then about thirty-two years old—was elected the **twentieth Bishop (Patriarch) of Alexandria**. He would occupy this see for the next forty-five years, though more than seventeen of those years were spent in exile.

### The Five Exiles

Athanasius's episcopate was a drama of resistance, flight, and return. The Arians—or more precisely, the **Eusebian party** (named after Eusebius of Nicomedia, not the historian)—controlled the imperial court for decades and repeatedly secured Athanasius's condemnation on trumped-up charges. His life can be understood through the rhythm of **five exiles**:

| Exile | Duration | Emperor | Cause | Destination |
|-------|----------|---------|-------|-------------|
| **First** | 336–337 | Constantine I | Convicted at the Synod of Tyre (335) on fabricated charges (breaking a chalice, supporting schismatics); refused to readmit Arius to communion. | Trier (Gaul) |
| **Second** | 339–346 | Constantius II | The Arian Gregory of Cappadocia installed as rival bishop of Alexandria; Athanasius fled and appealed to Pope Julius I, who convened the Synod of Rome (340) and vindicated him. | Rome |
| **Third** | 356–361 | Constantius II | Condemned by the Synod of Milan (355) for refusing to condemn St. Athanasius (!); imperial troops stormed the Church of Theonas in Alexandria (8–9 February 356). | Egyptian Desert (among the monks) |
| **Fourth** | 362–364 | Julian the Apostate | Returned under Julian's general amnesty for exiled bishops (361), but was soon re-exiled by Julian as a "troublemaker" for re-converting pagans. | Egyptian Desert |
| **Fifth** | 365–366 | Valens | Julian's successor Jovian had restored Athanasius (363), but the Arian-leaning Emperor Valens renewed the persecution. A brief exile of four months. | Outside Alexandria (brief) |

### The First Exile (336–337)

The **Synod of Tyre** (335) was a carefully stage-managed affair. Athanasius's enemies accused him of:
- Murdering a Meletian bishop named Arsenius and using his severed hand for magic.
- Breaking a consecrated chalice.
- Extortion and violence against opponents.

When Athanasius proved Arsenius alive (the hand was a fabrication), the accusers switched to charges of political sedition. Constantine, persuaded by the Eusebian party, exiled Athanasius to **Trier** in Gaul (modern Germany). Upon Constantine's death in 337, his son Constantine II restored Athanasius.

### The Second Exile (339–346)

Constantius II, who ruled the East, favoured the Arians. He had **Gregory of Cappadocia** installed as a rival bishop of Alexandria. Athanasius fled to **Rome**, where **Pope Julius I** (337–352) convened a synod (340) that cleared Athanasius of all charges and affirmed the principle that the Bishop of Alexandria could not be deposed without the consent of the Bishop of Rome.

> "The case of Athanasius, beloved of God, ought to have been judged by our [Roman] church, and not in the manner in which it was handled."
> — Pope Julius I, *Letter to the Eusebians* (c. 341)

This episode is an important early landmark in the development of papal primacy. Athanasius spent these years in Rome, where he deepened his knowledge of Western theology and Latin Christianity. He returned to Alexandria in 346 after Constantius II temporarily relented, greeted as a hero.

### The Third Exile (356–361): "Athanasius contra Mundum"

The most famous of his exiles began when Constantius II, now sole emperor, determined to crush Nicene resistance. At the **Synod of Milan** (355), the emperor demanded that all Western bishops condemn Athanasius. Those who refused—including **Pope Liberius**, **St. Eusebius of Vercelli**, and **St. Hilary of Poitiers**—were themselves exiled.

On the night of **8–9 February 356**, imperial troops surrounded the **Church of Theonas** (the Cathedral of Alexandria) to arrest Athanasius during a Vigil service. As the soldiers burst in, Athanasius remained calm, ordered the deacon to begin the recitation of Psalm 136 ("For His mercy endures forever"), and slipped through the soldiers in the confusion. He escaped into the **Egyptian desert**, where he was sheltered by the monks.

For the next **six years (356–361)**, Athanasius lived as a fugitive among the Desert Fathers, moving constantly from monastery to monastery. Far from silencing him, this period was one of his most productive. He composed his greatest theological works:

- ***Three Orations against the Arians*** (c. 356–360)
- ***On the Incarnation of the Word*** (already written c. 318, but revised and circulated)
- ***The Life of St. Antony*** (c. 360)
- ***Letters to Serapion*** (on the divinity of the Holy Spirit)
- ***Four Letters to the Monks*** (including the famous *39th Festal Letter* on the canon of Scripture)

### The Fourth Exile (362–364)

The Emperor **Julian the Apostate** (r. 361–363), who had abandoned Christianity for paganism, issued a general amnesty for exiled bishops—not out of sympathy but to foment discord among Christians. Athanasius returned to Alexandria in 362 and immediately convened the **Synod of Alexandria** (362), a crucial council that:

- Reaffirmed the Nicene Creed and *homoousios*.
- Condemned Macedonianism (which denied the divinity of the Holy Spirit).
- Clarified that the Nicene term *hypostasis* could be used of the three Divine Persons (a concession to the Antiochenes).
- Extended reconciliation to former Arians who repented.

Julian, furious at Athanasius's continued influence, ordered his re-arrest. Athanasius fled once more to the desert, but he remained in communication with his flock. Julian's death in 363 allowed his return.

### The Fifth Exile (365–366)

The Emperor **Valens** (r. 364–378), a baptized Arian, renewed the persecution of Nicene bishops. Athanasius was forced to flee yet again, but this exile was brief—only **four months**—as popular outcry and the fear of civil unrest compelled Valens to allow him to return.

Athanasius spent his final years (366–373) in peace, governing the Church of Alexandria, consolidating Nicene orthodoxy, and preparing his successor, **St. Peter II of Alexandria**.

### Death

St. Athanasius died peacefully on **2 May 373 A.D.**, after forty-five years as bishop, having outlasted emperors, exiles, and enemies. He was buried in Alexandria, and his relics are venerated to this day.

## 6. Theological Contributions

St. Athanasius's theology is centred on the **Incarnation of the Word** and the **divinity of the Son**. His major contributions include:

### The Doctrine of the Incarnation

For Athanasius, the Incarnation was not an afterthought but the central act of divine economy. His classic work ***On the Incarnation of the Word*** (***De Incarnatione Verbi Dei***) is structured around a simple, powerful argument:

1. God created human beings in His image (*imago Dei*), giving them a share in the divine Logos.
2. Through sin, humanity fell into corruption and death.
3. Only the Creator Himself could re-create what had been corrupted.
4. Therefore, the eternal Word of God became man, died on the cross to destroy death, and rose again to restore incorruption to human nature.
5. This is proved by the transformation of the martyrs, the abandonment of pagan worship, and the moral renewal of Christians.

> "He became man that we might become divine." (***Αὐτὸς γὰρ ἐνηνθρώπησεν, ἵνα ἡμεῖς θεοποιηθῶμεν***)
> — St. Athanasius, *On the Incarnation*, 54

This celebrated formulation—often called the "exchange formula"—became a cornerstone of Eastern Catholic and Orthodox soteriology.

### The Full Divinity of the Son

Athanasius's entire career was devoted to defending the full divinity of the Son. He argued that:
- If the Son is a creature, we are still in our sins.
- Only God can save; therefore, the Son must be God.
- The liturgy of the Church—Baptism in the name of the Father, Son, and Holy Spirit—implies the co-equality of the Three.
- The scriptural titles of the Son (Word, Wisdom, Radiance, Image) imply eternal generation, not creation.

### The Divinity of the Holy Spirit

In his ***Letters to Serapion*** (c. 359–360), Athanasius was the first theologian to articulate a clear doctrine of the divinity of the Holy Spirit. He argued that since the Holy Spirit is given in Baptism alongside the Father and the Son, He must share the same divine nature. This prepared the ground for the **First Council of Constantinople** (381), which defined the divinity of the Holy Spirit and expanded the Nicene Creed.

### The Canon of Scripture

In his **39th Festal Letter** (367 A.D.), Athanasius provided the earliest surviving list of the **27 books of the New Testament** exactly as the Church recognizes them today. This letter is a landmark in the history of the biblical canon. He also listed the **Old Testament books** (including the deuterocanonicals), appending the books of Wisdom, Sirach, Judith, Tobit, Baruch, and 1–2 Maccabees as "approved for reading by catechumens."

### The Life of St. Antony

Written around **360 A.D.**, the ***Life of St. Antony*** (*Vita Antonii*) is arguably the most influential hagiography ever composed. It recounts the life of St. Antony the Great (c. 251–356), the father of monasticism. The work:

- Was quickly translated into Latin (by Evagrius of Antioch) and spread throughout the West.
- Deeply influenced **St. Augustine of Hippo**, whose own conversion (Confessions, VIII) was sparked by reading the *Life of St. Antony*.
- Inspired countless men and women to embrace the monastic life.
- Established the literary genre of hagiography for centuries to come.

## 7. Key Writings

St. Athanasius was a prolific writer. The following are his principal surviving works:

| Work | Date | Content |
|------|------|---------|
| ***On the Incarnation of the Word*** (*De Incarnatione Verbi Dei*) | c. 318 | A two-part treatise on the creation and fall of humanity and the divine remedy of the Incarnation. |
| ***Against the Heathen*** (*Contra Gentes*) | c. 318 | Apologetic work demonstrating the folly of pagan idolatry and the reasonableness of Christian faith. |
| ***Three Orations against the Arians*** (*Orationes contra Arianos*) | c. 356–360 | The most comprehensive patristic defence of the divinity of the Son against Arian exegesis. |
| ***Letters to Serapion*** (*Ad Serapionem*) | c. 359–360 | Four letters defending the divinity of the Holy Spirit against the Tropici (Macedonians). |
| ***The Life of St. Antony*** (*Vita Antonii*) | c. 360 | Hagiography of the Father of Monasticism. |
| ***History of the Arians*** (*Historia Arianorum*) | c. 358 | Polemical history of the Arian persecution of Nicene Christians. |
| ***Defence of the Nicene Definition*** (*De Decretis Nicanae Synodi*) | c. 350–351 | Explanation and defence of the Council of Nicaea's use of *homoousios*. |
| ***Defence of His Flight*** (*Apologia de Fuga*) | c. 357 | Defence of his decision to flee persecution, arguing that Christ Himself fled. |
| ***Defence Before Constantius*** (*Apologia ad Constantium*) | c. 357 | Legal defence addressed to the emperor. |
| ***Festal Letters*** (*Epistulae Festales*) | 329–373 | Annual pastoral letters announcing the date of Easter, containing theological reflections. The **39th Festal Letter (367)** contains the canon of Scripture. |
| ***Life of St. Syncletica*** (attributed) | Uncertain | Sayings and life of a female Desert Mother. |

## 8. Legacy and Veneration

### Athanasius Contra Mundum

The phrase **"Athanasius contra mundum"** ("Athanasius against the world") encapsulates his legacy. For decades, he stood alone—or nearly so—against an imperial church that had largely abandoned the Nicene faith. His steadfastness preserved orthodoxy until the next generation of Nicene champions—St. Basil the Great, St. Gregory of Nazianzus, and St. Gregory of Nyssa (the Cappadocian Fathers)—could bring the Church back to the Nicene confession.

### The Final Triumph of Nicaea

At his death in 373, Arianism was still politically dominant. But within a decade, the tide had turned. The **First Council of Constantinople (381)** reaffirmed the Nicene Creed and condemned all Arianizing positions. By the end of the fourth century, Nicene orthodoxy was the faith of the Empire.

### Doctor of the Church

St. Athanasius was declared a **Doctor of the Church** (one of the four Great Doctors of the Eastern Church) by Pope St. Pius V in 1568. He is also one of the four Great Fathers of the Eastern Church (along with St. Basil, St. Gregory Nazianzen, and St. John Chrysostom).

### Patronage

St. Athanasius is the patron saint of:
- Theologians and apologists
- The Coptic Catholic Church and the Coptic Orthodox Church
- The city of Alexandria
- Those persecuted for the faith

### Feast Days

- **2 May** — Latin Rite (principal feast); Byzantine Rite (commemoration of his death)
- **18 January** — Byzantine Rite (principal feast, shared with St. Cyril of Alexandria)
- **15 May** — Coptic Catholic and Coptic Orthodox
