# St. Ignatius of Loyola: Founder of the Society of Jesus

> **Note:** This document was composed without Magisterium AI retrieval. Citations should be verified against primary sources where possible.

---

## 1. Introduction

**St. Ignatius of Loyola** (Basque: _Iñigo Loiolakoa_; Spanish: _Ignacio de Loyola_; 1491 – 31 July 1556) was a Spanish Basque knight, hermit, priest, and theologian who founded the **Society of Jesus** (the Jesuits) in 1540. He is one of the most influential figures of the Catholic Reformation (the Counter-Reformation), and his _Spiritual Exercises_ remain a cornerstone of Catholic spirituality. His feast day is celebrated on **31 July**.

Pope Pius XI declared him the patron saint of spiritual exercises, retreats, and soldiers. He is also the patron of the Basque Country, of Guipúzcoa, and of the Society of Jesus.

---

## 2. Early Life and Secular Career (1491–1521)

### Birth and Lineage

Íñigo López de Loyola was born in **1491** (the precise date is not recorded) at the Castle of Loyola in the municipality of Azpeitia, in the Basque province of Guipúzcoa, Crown of Castile (modern-day Spain). He was the youngest of thirteen children born to Don Beltrán Ibáñez de Oñez y Loyola and Doña María Sáenz de Licona y Balda, both of noble Basque lineage.

The Loyola family belonged to the lesser nobility (the _hidalguía_), and the young Íñigo was raised with the expectations of a knightly class: arms, honor, and courtly service.

### Page and Courtier

At approximately **fifteen years of age** (c. 1506), Íñigo was sent to serve as a page in the household of Don Juan Velázquez de Cuéllar, the chief treasurer (_contador mayor_) of the Kingdom of Castile. He was thus formed at the court of King Ferdinand II of Aragon, where he absorbed the chivalric ideals of the age—prowess in arms, gallantry, and loyalty to one's lord.

During this period, he read popular romances of chivalry such as _Amadís de Gaula_ and took part in the social and military life of the court.

### Military Career

In **1517**, Don Juan Velázquez de Cuéllar fell from favor and died, leaving Íñigo without a patron. He entered the service of Antonio Manrique de Lara, the Duke of Nájera and Viceroy of Navarre, serving as a gentleman-in-waiting and, when required, as a soldier.

Íñigo fought in several minor military engagements and developed a reputation for pride, vanity, and skill with the sword. He confessed later in life to having been "a man given to the vanities of the world" and took particular delight in the exercise of arms and the desire for fame.

### The Battle of Pamplona (1521)

The turning point of St. Ignatius's life came on **20 May 1521**, during the defense of the fortress of Pamplona against a French army (which included the Navarrese and French forces under André de Foix). Though severely outnumbered, Íñigo urged his commanders to resist and, when the fortress was stormed, he fought bravely until a cannonball struck his legs, shattering his right leg and seriously wounding his left.

The French, admiring his courage, treated his wounds and returned him to the Loyola castle for convalescence.

## 3. The Conversion at Loyola (1521–1522)

### The Long Convalescence

At the Castle of Loyola, the wounded Íñigo underwent excruciating surgery to reset his shattered leg. The bones had been poorly set, so the surgeons broke and re-set them—a procedure endured without anesthetic. His convalescence stretched for many months, leaving him bedridden.

### Reading the Lives of the Saints

Desiring to pass the time with his accustomed chivalric romances, Íñigo asked for books of knightly tales. None were available in the castle. Instead, he was given two books:

1. **The _Life of Christ_ (_Vita Christi_) by Ludolph of Saxony** — a Carthusian meditation on the Gospels.
2. **The _Flos Sanctorum_ (_Golden Legend_) by Jacobus de Voragine** — a collection of hagiographies of the saints.

As he read, Íñigo began to experience a profound interior transformation. He reflected on the heroic deeds of saints such as St. Francis of Assisi and St. Dominic de Guzmán and began to ask himself: "What if I should do what St. Francis did, what St. Dominic did?" These thoughts alternated with his habitual fantasies of winning honor and the favor of a great lady.

### The First Discernment of Spirits

St. Ignatius later described how he began to notice a crucial interior difference:

- The chivalric fantasies left him dry, dissatisfied, and restless afterward.
- The thoughts of imitating the saints left him with lasting peace, joy, and consolation.

This experience—which he later codified in his **Rules for the Discernment of Spirits**—became the bedrock of his spiritual method. He came to recognize that the characteristic of the good spirit is to give "consolation, courage, and peace" while the evil spirit leaves "sadness, disturbance, and restlessness."

### The Vision at Loyola

One night, as he lay awake, he saw a vision of the **Blessed Virgin Mary** holding the Child Jesus. This vision, he recounted, left him filled with such intense disgust for his past sins and vanities that he resolved to dedicate himself entirely to God.

Upon his recovery in **February 1522**, Íñigo left the Castle of Loyola, determined to embark on a pilgrimage to Jerusalem and to live a life of penance and service to Christ.

---

## 4. The Pilgrim Years (1522–1524)

### Montserrat (March 1522)

Íñigo's first destination was the Benedictine Abbey of **Montserrat** in Catalonia, a renowned Marian shrine. On the night of **24–25 March 1522** (the eve of the Feast of the Annunciation), he made a vigil before the statue of the **Blessed Virgin of Montserrat**—the _Moreneta_. He hung his sword and dagger on the altar rail, renouncing his knightly arms, and donned a pilgrim's garb of coarse sackcloth.

He then spent three days making a general confession of his whole life, writing it out with great care.

### Manresa (March 1522 – February 1523)

After Montserrat, Íñigo retired to the town of **Manresa**, near Barcelona, where he lived as a beggar and penitent in a Dominican convent. He intended to stay a few days but remained for **eleven months**, from March 1522 to February 1523.

This period was one of intense spiritual turmoil and growth:

- **Trials and Scruples:** He suffered severe scruples and temptations to despair over his past sins, at one point contemplating suicide.
- **Illuminations:** He received extraordinary consolations and profound insights into the mysteries of the faith. The most famous occurred at the **Chapel of St. Paul** on the banks of the Cardener River, where a single illumination of the intellect gave him a deeper understanding of the Holy Trinity, the creation of the world, and the mystery of the Eucharist than all his later studies could match.

> "It was not so much that he learned new truths, but rather that his soul was enlightened to see the truths of faith with a clarity and certitude that he had never before possessed."
> — St. Ignatius, _Autobiography_

### The First Draft of the Spiritual Exercises

During his time at Manresa, St. Ignatius began to compose the core of his **_Spiritual Exercises_** — a structured program of meditations, prayers, and contemplations designed to help the exercitant discern the will of God and order one's life accordingly. The Exercises would be refined over the following decades but the foundational material dates from this period.

### Pilgrimage to Jerusalem (1523–1524)

From Manresa, Íñigo traveled by foot to **Barcelona**, then by sea to **Rome** (where he obtained the blessing of Pope Adrian VI), and finally to **Venice**, where he embarked for the Holy Land. He arrived in Jerusalem in **September 1523**.

His intention was to remain in the Holy Land permanently, evangelizing the Muslims. However, the Franciscan Custodian of the Holy Land, fearing for his safety, commanded him to leave, and he was forced to return to Europe by January 1524.

## 5. Studies for the Priesthood (1524–1537)

### Barcelona (1524–1526)

Recognizing that he needed education to be useful to souls, the now thirty-three-year-old Íñigo began studying Latin grammar alongside young boys in Barcelona. His zeal was such that he would discipline his restless mind by tying a rope around his thigh to stay awake at his desk.

### Alcalá de Henares (1526–1527)

He moved to the University of Alcalá de Henares, where he studied philosophy and theology, while simultaneously giving the Spiritual Exercises to others. His charismatic style of spiritual direction drew large crowds, but also attracted the suspicion of the Spanish Inquisition.

The Inquisition investigated him three times for possible heterodoxy (particularly because he was an uneducated layman giving spiritual direction). Each time he was cleared, but he was ordered to stop teaching others about the discernment of spirits until he had completed four more years of study.

### Salamanca (1527)

Frustrated with the restrictions, he moved to the University of Salamanca, but the Dominicans there also examined his teachings. Though again acquitted of doctrinal error, he was forbidden to teach about the difference between mortal and venial sin until further study.

### Paris (1528–1535)

Determined to study with greater freedom, Íñigo crossed the Pyrenees on foot and traveled to the **University of Paris**, then the intellectual capital of Europe.

- **1528–1529:** Studied the humanities at the Collège de Montaigu.
- **1529–1534:** Studied philosophy and theology at the Collège Sainte-Barbe, earning the Master of Arts degree.

It was at Paris that Íñigo adopted the Latin name **Ignatius** (after St. Ignatius of Antioch, the Apostolic Father and disciple of St. John).

## 6. The Foundation of the Society of Jesus (1534–1540)

### The First Companions (1534)

At Paris, Ignatius gathered a small group of fellow students who were drawn to his spirituality and apostolic vision. These **First Companions** were:

| Name | Nationality | Later Role |
|------|-------------|------------|
| **St. Francis Xavier** | Basque (Navarre) | Apostle of the Indies, missionary to Japan |
| **Bl. Peter Faber** | Savoyard | Theologian, diplomat, first Jesuit priest |
| **Diego Laínez** | Spanish | Second Superior General of the Jesuits |
| **Alfonso Salmerón** | Spanish | Theologian at the Council of Trent |
| **Simón Rodrigues** | Portuguese | Founder of the Jesuit province in Portugal |
| **Nicolás Bobadilla** | Spanish | Missionary and preacher |

### The Vows of Montmartre (15 August 1534)

On the morning of **15 August 1534**, the Feast of the Assumption of the Blessed Virgin Mary, these seven companions climbed the hill of **Montmartre** (the "Mountain of Martyrs") outside Paris. In the crypt of the Chapel of St. Denis (then part of a Benedictine priory), they made private vows of:

1. **Poverty** — to live in complete detachment from material goods.
2. **Chastity** — to observe perpetual celibacy.
3. **Apostolic Service** — to go to the Holy Land to minister to the souls of the faithful, and if this proved impossible, to place themselves at the disposal of the Pope for whatever mission he should assign.

St. Peter Faber, the only priest among them at that time, celebrated the Mass.

### Delays and the Italian Apostolate (1535–1537)

Unable to travel to the Holy Land due to the outbreak of war between Venice and the Ottoman Turks, the companions gathered in **Venice** in **1537**. Ignatius was ordained a priest on **24 June 1537** (the Feast of the Nativity of St. John the Baptist), along with Faber and Laínez.

They spent the remainder of 1537 in prayer and preaching throughout the towns of northern Italy.

### The Name "Company of Jesus"

The companions began to refer to themselves as the **"Company of Jesus"** (Latin: _Societas Iesu_; Spanish: _Compañía de Jesús_), signifying their identity as soldiers bound together under the banner of Christ the King and directly at the service of the Roman Pontiff.

### Papal Approval (1540)

In **1538**, Ignatius and his companions traveled to **Rome** to offer their service to Pope Paul III. They drew up a **Formula of the Institute** — a foundational document outlining their purpose and governance. On **27 September 1540**, Pope Paul III issued the bull **[_Regimini Militantis Ecclesiae_](https://www.papalencyclicals.net/paul03/p3regimi.htm)** ("To the Government of the Church Militant"), formally establishing the Society of Jesus as a religious order.

The key innovations of the Jesuits, as distinct from older religious orders, included:

- **No Choral Office:** Unlike monks and canons regular, Jesuits were not required to pray the Divine Office in common, freeing them for active ministry.
- **Direct Papal Obedience:** A special fourth vow of obedience to the Pope regarding missions.
- **Flexibility:** The order was designed for apostolic mobility, not monastic stability.
- **Rigorous Formation:** A long and demanding program of study in philosophy, theology, and spirituality.

## 7. Superior General and Government of the Order (1541–1556)

### Election as Superior General

On **8 April 1541**, the First Companions elected Ignatius as the first Superior General of the Society of Jesus. He initially refused, but the unanimous will of the companions prevailed, and he accepted the office, taking up residence in Rome near the Church of Santa Maria della Strada.

### Governance and Constitution

Over the next fifteen years, Ignatius governed the rapidly expanding order from a small room in Rome. He:

- **Drafted the Jesuit Constitutions** (1547–1550), a remarkably detailed and practical document that combined spiritual principles with administrative flexibility. St. Ignatius placed great emphasis on _discreta caritas_ (discerning charity) and the principle of _sentire cum Ecclesia_ (thinking with the Church).
- **Conducted an extensive correspondence**, writing over 7,000 letters to Jesuits and others across Europe and the missions. His letters are masterpieces of spiritual direction and practical governance.
- **Founded the German College** (1552) in Rome to train priests for the German lands, where the Church was hemorrhaging faithful to the Protestant Reformation.
- **Established the Roman College** (1551), which later became the **Pontifical Gregorian University**, a model for Jesuit education worldwide.

### Expansion of the Order

By the time of Ignatius's death, the Society of Jesus had grown from seven companions to over **1,000 members** in twelve provinces, with colleges and missions across Europe, India, Brazil, Ethiopia, and Japan.

### Relationship with the Roman Inquisition

St. Ignatius worked closely with the **Roman Inquisition** (the Holy Office), serving as a theological consultant and offering his spiritual discernment in matters of orthodoxy. Unlike many reformers of the period, he was deeply committed to the principle of obedience to the hierarchical Church and warned repeatedly against any "novelty" in doctrine.

## 8. The Spiritual Exercises

The **_Spiritual Exercises_ (_Exercitia Spiritualia_)** is St. Ignatius's most enduring and influential work. It is a manual of meditations, prayers, and contemplative practices designed to be directed by a retreat giver to a retreatant over approximately thirty days.

### Structure of the Exercises

The Exercises are organized into Four "Weeks" (which correspond not to seven-day periods but to stages of spiritual progress):

| Week | Theme | Duration |
|:----:|-------|:--------:|
| **First Week** | The Purpose of Life; Sin and its Consequences; Hell | ~10 days |
| **Second Week** | The Life of Christ from the Incarnation to Palm Sunday; The Call of the King; The Two Standards; Election | ~12 days |
| **Third Week** | The Passion of Christ | ~7 days |
| **Fourth Week** | The Resurrection and Ascension; The Contemplation on the Love of God | ~7 days |

### Key Doctrinal and Methodological Elements

1. **Principle and Foundation (Primo Principio y Fundamento):** The opening meditation establishes that human beings are created to praise, reverence, and serve God, and that all other things on the face of the earth are created to help in this purpose. Therefore, we ought to use creatures insofar as they help us toward our end and withdraw from them insofar as they hinder us.
2. **The Election:** The exercitant is guided to make a free and informed decision about one's state of life or a major life choice, without being swayed by disordered attachments.
3. **The Discernment of Spirits:** The Exercises include detailed rules for recognizing when one is moved by the good spirit (leading to consolation) versus the evil spirit (leading to desolation).
4. **The Contemplation on the Love of God:** The Exercises conclude with a meditation eliciting a grateful and total self-offering to God—the famous "Suscipe" prayer: _Take, Lord, and receive all my liberty..._

### Ecclesial Approval

The _Spiritual Exercises_ were examined and approved by Pope Paul III in the brief **_Pastoralis Officii Cura_** (31 July 1548). They have been used by countless saints, including St. Francis de Sales, St. Charles Borromeo, and St. John Paul II.

## 9. Later Years and Death

### Declining Health

By the early 1550s, St. Ignatius's health was failing. He suffered from severe stomach ailments (likely ulcers and gallstones), which he had endured since his years of harsh penance at Manresa.

### Final Days

In the summer of **1556**, Ignatius fell gravely ill. On the morning of **31 July 1556**, after receiving the last sacraments, he died quietly in his small room in Rome. He was surrounded by only a few companions (many were away on missions). He was sixty-five years old.

### Burial and Canonization

- He was buried in the Church of Santa Maria della Strada in Rome.
- His body was later translated to the magnificent Church of the **Gesù** (the Mother Church of the Jesuits), where it rests in a lavish urn to this day.
- He was **beatified** on 27 July 1609 by Pope Paul V.
- He was **canonized** on 12 March 1622 (along with St. Teresa of Ávila, St. Francis Xavier, St. Isidore the Farmer, and St. Philip Neri) by Pope Gregory XV.
- Pope Pius XI declared him the **Patron of All Spiritual Retreats** in 1922.

## 10. Legacy and Influence

### The Jesuit Order

At the death of St. Ignatius, the Society of Jesus numbered about 1,000 members. At its peak before the suppression of 1773, it numbered over 23,000 members. Today (as of 2024), the Society of Jesus has approximately 14,000 members, making it the largest religious order of priests in the Catholic Church.

Jesuits have served as:

- Missionaries to every continent (St. Francis Xavier, St. Peter Claver, Matteo Ricci)
- Educators at every level (the Jesuits operate over 2,000 schools and universities worldwide)
- Theologians and scholars (Francisco de Suárez, St. Robert Bellarmine, Karl Rahner, Henri de Lubac, Bernard Lonergan)
- Spiritual directors and retreat masters
- Popes: **Pope Francis** (Cardinal Jorge Mario Bergoglio, S.J.) is the first Jesuit to be elected to the See of Peter.

### The Spiritual Exercises in the Life of the Church

The _Spiritual Exercises_ have profoundly shaped Catholic spirituality. They have been recommended and practiced by saints, popes, and countless lay faithful. Pope St. John Paul II, in his encyclical _Vita Consecrata_ (1996), called the Exercises "a school of the _sequela Christi_."

### Patronage

St. Ignatius of Loyola is the patron saint of:

- The Society of Jesus
- The Basque Country
- Guipúzcoa, Spain
- Spiritual exercises and retreats
- Soldiers and military personnel
- The Diocese of Bilbao

## 11. Key Writings

- **_The Spiritual Exercises_ (_Exercitia Spiritualia_, 1548)** — The manual of meditations and discernment.
- **_The Constitutions of the Society of Jesus_ (1550)** — The foundational legal and spiritual document of the Jesuits.
- **_The Autobiography of St. Ignatius of Loyola_ (_Acta Patris Ignatii_, 1553–1555)** — Dictated to his secretary, Father Luís Gonçalves da Câmara. Essential for understanding his spiritual journey.
- **_The Spiritual Diary_ (_Diario Espiritual_, 1544–1545)** — A private journal recording his mystical experiences during the composition of the Constitutions.
- **_Letters of St. Ignatius of Loyola_** — Over 7,000 letters on spiritual direction, governance, and missiology.
