# St. Joseph: Spouse of the Blessed Virgin Mary and Patron of the Universal Church

## 1. Introduction

**St. Joseph** (Hebrew: **_יוֹסֵף_**, _Yosef_, "God shall add"; Latin: _Josephus_) is the **spouse of the Blessed Virgin Mary**, the **foster father and guardian of Jesus Christ**, and the **Patron of the Universal Church**. Though not a single word of his is recorded in Scripture, his silent obedience and just character have made him the model of faithfulness, humility, and working-class holiness. His principal feast is celebrated on **19 March** (Solemnity of St. Joseph, Spouse of the Blessed Virgin Mary), and his patronage as **St. Joseph the Worker** is honoured on **1 May**.

> "Joseph, son of David, do not be afraid to take Mary as your wife, for the child conceived in her is from the Holy Spirit. She will bear a son, and you are to name him Jesus, for he will save his people from their sins."
> — The Angel of the Lord to St. Joseph (Matthew 1:20–21, NRSV-CE)

## 2. Biblical Portrait

### Lineage from David

The Gospels of Matthew and Luke both trace Joseph's lineage to **King David**, fulfilling the messianic prophecies that the Christ would be born of the House of David. Matthew's genealogy (Matthew 1:1–17) traces Joseph's descent through the royal line of David, while Luke's genealogy (Luke 3:23–38) traces it through Nathan, another son of David. The angel addresses Joseph as **"Joseph, son of David"** (Matthew 1:20), explicitly placing him in the Davidic messianic context.

The Catechism of the Catholic Church affirms that Jesus is "born of Joseph's spouse into the messianic lineage of David" (CCC 437).

### Betrothal to Mary

Joseph was betrothed to Mary when she "was found to be with child from the Holy Spirit" (Matthew 1:18). Joseph, being **"a righteous man"** (_dikaios_), planned to divorce her quietly to avoid exposing her to public disgrace. An angel of the Lord appeared to him in a dream, instructing him: "Do not be afraid to take Mary as your wife, for the child conceived in her is from the Holy Spirit" (Matthew 1:20).

Joseph's response was immediate and complete obedience: **"he did as the angel of the Lord commanded him; he took her as his wife"** (Matthew 1:24). This act of faith—accepting a pregnancy that he knew was not his own, trusting in a divine plan he could not fully comprehend—is the foundation of his sanctity.

### The Nativity

Joseph traveled with the pregnant Mary from Nazareth to **Bethlehem** for the census ordered by Caesar Augustus (Luke 2:1–5). Finding no room in the inn, Joseph secured shelter in a stable, where Jesus Christ, the Son of God, was born. Joseph was present at the **Adoration of the Shepherds** and the **Visit of the Magi**.

### The Presentation in the Temple

Forty days after the Nativity, Joseph and Mary brought Jesus to the **Temple in Jerusalem** to present Him to the Lord, according to the Law of Moses: "Every firstborn male shall be designated as holy to the Lord" (Luke 2:23; cf. Exodus 13:2, 12). They also offered the sacrifice of "a pair of turtledoves or two young pigeons" (Luke 2:24)—the offering prescribed for a poor family (Leviticus 12:8), indicating the humble circumstances of the Holy Family.

At the Temple, they encountered **Simeon**, a righteous and devout man who had been promised by the Holy Spirit that he would not see death before he had seen the Lord's Messiah. Taking the infant Jesus in his arms, Simeon proclaimed the **Nunc Dimittis** (Luke 2:29–32), declaring the Child to be "a light for revelation to the Gentiles and for glory to your people Israel." He then foretold to Mary: "This child is destined for the falling and the rising of many in Israel, and to be a sign that will be opposed so that the inner thoughts of many will be revealed—and a sword will pierce your own soul too" (Luke 2:34–35). The aged prophetess **Anna**, daughter of Phanuel, also gave thanks to God and spoke of the Child to all who were awaiting the redemption of Jerusalem (Luke 2:36–38). Joseph, though silent, stood at the centre of these events as the legal father and protector of the Messiah.

### The Flight into Egypt

Warned in a dream that **King Herod** sought to destroy the Child, Joseph took Mary and Jesus and **fled to Egypt** (Matthew 2:13–15). He remained there until Herod's death, when another angelic message prompted the return to Nazareth. This event fulfills the prophecy: "Out of Egypt I have called my son" (Hosea 11:1; Matthew 2:15).

### The Hidden Life at Nazareth

Returning to Nazareth, Joseph worked as a **carpenter** (_tekton_), teaching his trade to Jesus. The Gospels summarize this period with the profound statement: "The child grew and became strong, filled with wisdom; and the favour of God was upon him" (Luke 2:40). Joseph's hidden life—labouring in obscurity, providing for the Holy Family, and raising the Son of God—is the model of holiness in ordinary daily life.

### The Finding in the Temple

The only event from Jesus's childhood recorded in the Gospels is the **Finding in the Temple** (Luke 2:41–52). When Jesus was twelve, He remained in Jerusalem after the Passover feast, causing Mary and Joseph three days of anxious searching. Upon finding Him in the Temple, Mary said: "Child, why have you treated us like this? Look, your father and I have been searching for you in great anxiety." The Gospel explicitly calls Joseph **"his father"** —a title that reflects Joseph's true, if not biological, fatherhood.

### The Death of St. Joseph

Sacred Scripture does not record Joseph's death, but Catholic tradition, supported by the unanimous testimony of the Fathers and the liturgical tradition, holds that **Joseph died in the presence of Jesus and Mary**. It is universally held that Joseph died before the beginning of Jesus's public ministry, since he is not mentioned during the Passion, at the foot of the Cross, or among the disciples gathered in the Upper Room after the Ascension (Acts 1:14 mentions Mary but not Joseph).

The **Dormition of Joseph** (the "Falling Asleep" of St. Joseph) is described in the apocryphal _History of Joseph the Carpenter_ (5th–6th c.), which recounts that Joseph died at the age of 111, with Jesus and Mary at his bedside, and that Jesus Himself closed his eyes and commended his soul to the Father. While this text is not canonical Scripture, it enshrines the ancient Christian belief that Joseph was blessed to die with the Son of God and the Blessed Virgin at his side—hence his invocation as the **Patron of a Happy Death**. The Church has never defined the circumstances of Joseph's death as a matter of dogma, but the tradition of his peaceful passing in the presence of Jesus and Mary is universally received and liturgically honoured.

## 3. Apocryphal and Traditional Sources

Because the canonical Gospels say little about Joseph's life before the Annunciation or after the Finding in the Temple, a rich body of early Christian apocryphal literature and later pious tradition has supplied additional details. While these sources are not inspired Scripture and must be read with theological discretion, they have profoundly shaped the Church's iconography, liturgy, and popular devotion to St. Joseph.

### The Protoevangelium of James (2nd Century)

The **Protoevangelium of James** (c. 150 A.D.), one of the earliest and most influential apocryphal gospels, is the primary source for the tradition of Joseph's advanced age and his previous marriage. According to this text:

- Mary was raised in the Temple from the age of three.
- When she reached puberty, the priests sought a worthy guardian for her. They gathered the widowers of Israel, each bearing a staff.
- Joseph's staff **miraculously blossomed** and a dove descended upon it, signifying divine choice.
- Joseph, portrayed as an elderly widower with children from a prior marriage, reluctantly accepted the charge of the Virgin, fearing public ridicule.

This account, while not historical in every detail, entered the Byzantine liturgical tradition (where Joseph is commemorated as an elderly man) and influenced Western art for over a millennium. The **flowering staff** became Joseph's most recognizable attribute in iconography. The Protoevangelium also explains the New Testament references to the "brothers of the Lord" as Joseph's children from his first marriage—an interpretation widely held in the Eastern tradition and defended by many Latin Fathers (e.g., St. Epiphanius of Salamis, St. Jerome), though St. Jerome himself argued they were cousins.

### The History of Joseph the Carpenter (5th–6th Century)

The **History of Joseph the Carpenter**, a text composed in Coptic Egypt, presents a lengthy deathbed narrative in which Joseph, at age 111, recounts his life to Jesus. It describes:

- Joseph's first marriage and his four sons (Judas, Justus, James, Simon) and two daughters.
- His advanced age at his betrothal to Mary (approximately 90 years old).
- His death attended by Jesus and Mary, with Jesus promising paradise to all who honour St. Joseph.

This text is the source of much of the traditional material for the **Feast of the Patronage of St. Joseph** and the **Novena to St. Joseph** for a happy death.

### Other Early Sources

- **St. Epiphanius of Salamis** (c. 310–403), in his _Panarion_, defends the tradition that Joseph was an elderly widower, arguing that this best explains the Gospel references to Jesus's "brothers and sisters."
- **St. Jerome** (c. 347–420), in _Against Helvidius_, championed the perpetual virginity of Mary and argued that the "brothers of the Lord" were either children of Joseph by a previous marriage (following the Protoevangelium) or cousins, while firmly maintaining that Joseph himself remained perpetually celibate.
- **St. Augustine of Hippo** (354–430) affirmed the perpetual virginity of both Mary and Joseph, writing that "the good of marriage was realized in them" through their spiritual communion and their joint parenthood of Christ (_De Nuptiis et Concupiscentia_, I.11–12).
- The **Venerable Bede** (c. 673–735) transmitted the tradition that Joseph was thirty years old at his betrothal to Mary—an alternative to the "elderly widower" tradition that gained currency in the medieval West.

### Theological Assessment of the Apocrypha

The Church has never declared any of these apocryphal sources authoritative, nor has she defined the age, marital history, or family circumstances of St. Joseph as a matter of dogma. The Magisterium has consistently focused on the **canonical portrait**: Joseph is the just man, the Virgin's spouse, the foster father of Jesus, and the silent servant of the Incarnation. The apocryphal traditions, while venerable and liturgically influential, remain in the realm of **pious opinion** and are not binding on the faithful.

## 4. Theological Significance

### The Just Man

The Gospel's description of Joseph as **"a righteous man"** (_dikaios_) is rich in Old Testament resonance. In Hebrew thought, the _tsaddiq_ (righteous one) is one who lives in faithful covenant relationship with God and embodies the Law. Joseph's righteousness is not merely legalistic; it is a merciful righteousness that seeks to protect Mary even before he understands the divine plan.

### The Silence of Joseph

No words of Joseph are recorded in Scripture. The Fathers of the Church saw in this silence a profound spiritual lesson: Joseph listened to God, obeyed promptly, and fulfilled his mission without seeking recognition. His silence speaks louder than words, teaching the value of contemplative obedience.

### True Spouse of Mary

The Church teaches that Joseph's marriage to Mary was a **true marriage**, though never consummated. The CCC states: "Joseph's marriage to Mary is the juridical basis of his fatherhood" (CCC 437). Their marriage was a communion of life and love ordered to the service of the Incarnation.

### Foster Father of Jesus

Pope Leo XIII, in his encyclical _Quamquam Pluries_ (1889), taught that Joseph shared in a unique way in the mystery of the Incarnation as the **virgin father** of Jesus. His fatherhood was not biological but real, exercised through his loving care, protection, and provision for the Holy Family.

### Joseph as a Type of the Old Testament Patriarchs

The Fathers of the Church and the medieval exegetical tradition saw in St. Joseph a fulfilment of several Old Testament types:

- **Joseph the Patriarch** (son of Jacob): The Old Testament Joseph, likewise a dreamer and interpreter of dreams, was sold into Egypt and became the saviour of his family during famine. St. Joseph of Nazareth, guided by dreams, fled into Egypt to save the Holy Family and returned when the danger had passed. Both Josephs were instruments of divine providence in times of crisis.
- **David the Shepherd-King**: As a son of David, Joseph exercised a shepherd's care over the Holy Family, protecting them from harm, providing for their needs, and guiding them in obedience to the Law.
- **Moses the Guardian**: Like Moses, who led Israel out of Egypt, Joseph led the Holy Family into and out of Egypt, fulfilling the prophecy: "Out of Egypt I have called my son" (Hosea 11:1; Matthew 2:15).
- **Noah the Just Man**: As Noah was a "righteous man" who preserved life through the Flood, Joseph is "a righteous man" who preserved the life of the Messiah through the peril of Herod's slaughter.

### Joseph and the Virtue of Work

The hidden life of Nazareth presents Joseph as the **model of labour and craftsmanship**. Pope John Paul II, in _Redemptoris Custos_, taught that work was the daily expression of Joseph's love and the means by which he provided for the Holy Family:

> "Work was the daily expression of love in the life of the Carpenter of Nazareth. The Church, which from the beginning has preached the Gospel of grace and the dignity of the human person, also proclaims the Gospel of work, which she learned from the example of St. Joseph." — Pope John Paul II, _Redemptoris Custos_, 23

Pope Pius XII instituted the feast of **St. Joseph the Worker** on 1 May 1955, offering Joseph as the Christian counter-model to secularized labour ideologies. The Second Vatican Council (_Gaudium et Spes_, 67) and St. John Paul II's encyclical _Laborem Exercens_ (1981) both drew on the figure of St. Joseph to articulate a theology of work that sees labour as a participation in the creative work of God.

### Joseph as Protector of the Church

The proclamation of St. Joseph as Patron of the Universal Church (1870) was not merely a devotional act but a theological recognition that Joseph's role as guardian of the Holy Family extends to the entire Mystical Body of Christ. In his encyclical _Quamquam Pluries_, Pope Leo XIII explained that Joseph's heavenly protection mirrors his earthly protection of Jesus and Mary. St. John Henry Newman, in his _Meditations on the Litany of St. Joseph_, called Joseph the "Terror of Demons" because the demons fear the protector of the Holy Family, whose authority over the household of Nazareth prefigures the authority of the Church.

### Joseph and the Interior Life

The silence of Joseph has long been read by spiritual writers as a profound model of the **contemplative life**. St. Francis de Sales, St. Teresa of Ávila, and St. John of the Cross all wrote of Joseph as a master of the interior life—one who lived in constant communion with God through the ordinary duties of his daily labour. In _Patris Corde_, Pope Francis describes Joseph as a "father in the shadows" whose hiddenness mirrors the humility of God Himself, who works quietly and unobtrusively in the soul.

## 6. History of Veneration

The history of devotion to St. Joseph is marked by a remarkable growth from near-silence in the early Church to universal prominence in modern Catholicism. Unlike the Blessed Virgin Mary, whose cult began in the earliest Christian centuries, Joseph's public veneration developed slowly and reached its full flowering only in the sixteenth century and thereafter.

### Patristic Era (1st–5th Centuries)

In the first three centuries, Joseph appears in Christian writing almost exclusively in the context of defending the Incarnation and the virgin birth. **St. Ignatius of Antioch** (c. 108 A.D.), in his _Epistle to the Ephesians_ (19:1), refers to the "virginity of Mary and her childbearing" but does not mention Joseph. **St. Justin Martyr** (c. 155 A.D.), in his _Dialogue with Trypho_, discusses Joseph's Davidic lineage but says nothing of his person.

The first sustained theological reflection on Joseph appears in **St. Jerome** (c. 347–420), who in his treatise _Against Helvidius_ (383 A.D.) defended the perpetual virginity of Mary and argued that Joseph, like Mary, remained a virgin throughout his life. Jerome thus established the foundation for Joseph's cult as a model of chastity and the **Guardian of the Virgin**.

**St. Augustine** (354–430) contributed the crucial insight that Joseph and Mary's marriage was a true marriage because it possessed the three goods of matrimony: _proles_ (offspring — Jesus), _fides_ (fidelity — mutual chastity), and _sacramentum_ (indissolubility). This teaching provided the theological basis for viewing Joseph as Mary's true spouse.

### Early Medieval Period (6th–12th Centuries)

The **History of Joseph the Carpenter** (Coptic, 5th–6th c.) fueled popular devotion, especially in Egypt and the East. The **Protoevangelium of James** circulated widely in both Greek and Latin versions, shaping the popular imagination with the stories of the flowering staff, Joseph's old age, and his prior fatherhood.

In the East, Joseph was commemorated in the Byzantine liturgical calendar from at least the 8th century. The **Synaxis of St. Joseph** on the Sunday after Christmas became a fixed feast, honouring Joseph together with King David and St. James the "Brother of the Lord." Eastern hymnography from this period already calls Joseph the _mnestor_ (betrothed) of the Theotokos and the guardian of the Incarnate Word.

In the West, devotion to Joseph remained largely confined to monastic circles. The **Benedictine** and **Cluniac** monasteries observed 19 March as a feast of St. Joseph by the 9th century, but the feast was not universally observed. **St. Bernard of Clairvaux** (1090–1153), the last of the Fathers, preached on Joseph's role in the Incarnation in his homilies _Super Missus Est_, calling Joseph "the faithful and prudent servant whom the Lord set over His family."

### The Golden Age of Josephite Devotion (15th–18th Centuries)

The fifteenth century witnessed an extraordinary flourishing of devotion to St. Joseph, driven by several interrelated developments:

- **The Franciscan Tradition**: St. Bernardine of Siena (1380–1444) and St. John of Capistrano (1386–1456) were among the first to promote a formal cult of St. Joseph, preaching about his dignity and intercessory power.
- **St. Teresa of Ávila** (1515–1582) was the single most important figure in the spread of devotion to St. Joseph in the West. She attributed her healing from a childhood illness to St. Joseph, placed her reformed Carmelite monasteries under his patronage, and wrote extensively in her _Life_ (Chapter 6) of his powerful intercession: "I do not remember up to this day that I have ever asked anything of him that he has not granted."
- **Pope Sixtus IV** (1479) inserted the Feast of St. Joseph (19 March) into the Roman Missal, though as a lesser observance.
- **Pope Gregory XV** (1621) elevated 19 March to a feast of precept, making it a holy day of obligation for the universal Church.
- **St. Francis de Sales** (1567–1622) placed his Visitation Order under the patronage of St. Joseph and promoted devotion to him as the model of the interior life.
- **St. Joseph of Cupertino** (1603–1663) and **St. André Bessette** (1845–1937) exemplified the power of Josephite intercession in the lives of simple, humble believers.

### 19th Century: From Local Devotion to Universal Patronage

The nineteenth century saw a dramatic intensification of Josephite devotion, culminating in the proclamation of St. Joseph as Patron of the Universal Church:

- **Pope Pius IX** (1870) proclaimed St. Joseph **Patron of the Universal Church** by the decree _Quemadmodum Deus_, at the very moment the Church was losing the Papal States and entering the modern era as a "spiritual" power.
- **Pope Leo XIII** (1889) issued the encyclical _Quamquam Pluries_, the first papal encyclical devoted entirely to St. Joseph, and added the **Prayer to St. Joseph** to the end of the Rosary during the month of October.
- The **Sisters of St. Joseph** and other religious congregations dedicated to Joseph multiplied across Europe and the Americas.
- The **Scala Sancta** and other sanctuaries dedicated to St. Joseph became centres of popular pilgrimage.

### 20th Century: Theological Deepening

- **Pope Benedict XV** (1920) inserted Joseph's name into the Litany of the Saints.
- **Pope Pius XII** (1955) instituted the feast of **St. Joseph the Worker** on 1 May, both to honour the dignity of labour and to provide a Christian counter-weight to the Communist May Day celebrations.
- **Vatican II** (1962–1965) inserted Joseph's name into the **Eucharistic Prayer** (Roman Canon), in the _Communicantes_ —a singular honour placing him alongside the Blessed Virgin and the Apostles. Pope John XXIII had already added his name to the Canon in 1962.
- **Pope John Paul II** (1989) issued the apostolic exhortation **_Redemptoris Custos_** ("The Guardian of the Redeemer"), the most comprehensive papal document ever written on St. Joseph, exploring his role in salvation history, his fatherhood, and his relevance to the modern world.
- **St. John Paul II** also included St. Joseph among the patrons of the **Great Jubilee of the Year 2000**.

### 21st Century: Recent Papal Teaching

- **Pope Francis** (8 December 2020) issued the Apostolic Letter **_Patris Corde_** ("With a Father's Heart") on the 150th anniversary of the proclamation of St. Joseph as Patron of the Universal Church. He declared a special **Year of St. Joseph** from 8 December 2020 to 8 December 2021.
- In _Patris Corde_, Pope Francis identified **seven aspects** of Joseph's fatherhood: beloved, tender and loving, obedient, welcoming, creatively courageous, working, and a father in the shadows.
- The Pope also granted special plenary indulgences for the Year of St. Joseph and encouraged devotion to St. Joseph especially among the elderly, the sick, and those suffering from the COVID-19 pandemic.
- The liturgical **Prefaces of St. Joseph** were enriched, and new texts were added to the Roman Missal.

## 7. Patronage of the Universal Church

On **8 December 1870**, Pope **Pius IX**, yielding to the desires and prayers of the whole Catholic world, issued the decree _Quemadmodum Deus_ formally proclaiming **St. Joseph as Patron of the Universal Church**. The decree states that the Church, "wishing to place herself under the powerful patronage of the holy Patriarch," declared him Patron of the Catholic Church.

Pope **John Paul II**, in his apostolic exhortation **_Redemptoris Custos_** ("The Guardian of the Redeemer," 1989), explained that this proclamation was rooted in Joseph's dignity as husband of Mary and guardian of the Holy Family, and that Joseph now protects and defends the Church of Christ by heavenly patronage.

Pope **Francis** dedicated the Year 2020–2021 as the **Year of St. Joseph** with the Apostolic Letter **_Patris Corde_** ("With a Father's Heart"), reflecting on Joseph as a beloved father, a tender and loving father, an obedient father, a welcoming father, a creatively courageous father, a working father, and a father in the shadows.

## 10. Devotions and Prayers

### The Litany of St. Joseph

Approved by Pope St. Pius X in 1909, the Litany of St. Joseph invokes him under many titles, including: **"Terror of Demons, Protector of the Holy Church, Patron of those who die."**

### The Novena to St. Joseph

Traditionally prayed from 10–18 March in preparation for the feast of 19 March.

### Consecration to St. Joseph

Following the model of consecration to Jesus through Mary, many saints (including St. Francis de Sales and St. John Paul II) promoted devotion to St. Joseph through acts of consecration.

## 11. In Eastern Catholic Tradition

The Eastern Catholic Churches honour St. Joseph with great reverence, particularly during the **Synaxis of St. Joseph** on the Sunday after Christmas, when he is commemorated alongside King David and St. James the Brother of the Lord. The Byzantine liturgical tradition emphasizes Joseph's role as the **protector of the Theotokos** and the **guardian of the Incarnate Word**.

Eastern iconography depicts Joseph as an elderly man, often shown with the Child Jesus, a lily (symbol of his purity), and sometimes a flowering staff—referencing the tradition that his staff blossomed to indicate God's choice of him as Mary's spouse.

## 12. In Art and Iconography

St. Joseph has been a subject of Christian art since the early Middle Ages, and his iconography has developed distinct conventions in both East and West.

### Western Iconography

In Western art, St. Joseph is typically portrayed as a robust, middle-aged man (reflecting the "young Joseph" tradition) or as an older, balding figure (reflecting the "elderly Joseph" tradition from the Protoevangelium of James). Common iconographic elements include:

- **The Lily**: Symbolizing Joseph's chastity and purity. Joseph is frequently depicted holding a white lily, especially in Counter-Reformation art. This attribute became so closely associated with him that the term "lily of St. Joseph" entered common usage.
- **The Carpenter's Square or Saw**: Representing his trade as a _tekton_ (carpenter/woodworker). Often shown alongside the Child Jesus holding a tool, teaching the lesson of work.
- **The Flowering Staff**: Depicting the miracle of Joseph's staff blossoming to reveal God's choice of him as Mary's spouse. This attribute is particularly common in medieval and Byzantine art.
- **The Child Jesus**: Joseph is frequently depicted holding the Christ Child or leading Him by the hand, emphasizing his role as foster father and guardian.
- **The Holy Family**: From the Renaissance onward, Joseph appears as the third member of the Holy Family, often shown working in his carpentry shop while Mary prays and Jesus assists. The "Holy Family" as a distinct artistic subject became popular in the 16th–17th centuries, promoted by the Franciscan and Carmelite orders.

Major works include **The Holy Family** by Michelangelo (1504–1506, Uffizi), **The Rest on the Flight into Egypt** by Caravaggio (1597, Doria Pamphilj Gallery), **The Carpenter's Shop** by Georges de La Tour (1645, Louvre), and countless paintings, frescoes, and stained-glass windows in churches throughout the world.

### Eastern Iconography

In the Byzantine and Eastern Catholic tradition, Joseph is almost invariably depicted as an elderly man with grey or white hair, a short beard, and a humble, stooped posture. This portrayal reflects the Protoevangelium of James tradition of Joseph as an elderly widower who received the Virgin as a sacred trust. He is typically shown:

- In the **Nativity** icon, seated apart from Mary and the Christ Child, often in a contemplative or troubled posture, reflecting his role as the "doubting" righteous man who was later reassured by the angel.
- In the **Flight into Egypt** icon, leading the donkey carrying Mary and Jesus.
- In the **Synaxis of St. Joseph** icon (commemorated the Sunday after Christmas), standing with King David and St. James the Brother of the Lord.

### Patronal Colors

In liturgical art, Joseph is traditionally vested in **brown** or **ochre** (representing his labour as a carpenter) and **white** or **gold** (representing his purity). In some modern depictions, he wears a green garment (the colour of hope) over a brown tunic.

### Devotional Images

The **Sacred Heart of St. Joseph** (analogous to the Sacred Heart of Jesus) is a distinct devotional image promoted by St. Joseph Marello (1844–1895), founder of the Oblates of St. Joseph. It depicts Joseph with his heart visible, burning with love for Jesus and Mary.

## 13. Patristic and Magisterial Citations

> "He was chosen by the eternal Father as the faithful guardian and protector of His greatest treasures, namely, His divine Son and Mary, Joseph's wife."
> — Pope Leo XIII, _Quamquam Pluries_ (1889)

> "Just as he once watched over the Holy Family of Nazareth, so now he protects and defends the Church of Christ by his heavenly patronage."
> — Pope John Paul II, _Redemptoris Custos_, 28

> "With a father's heart: that is how Joseph loved Jesus, whom all four Gospels refer to as 'the son of Joseph.'"
> — Pope Francis, _Patris Corde_ (2020)

> "Joseph, the just man, the faithful guardian of the divine mysteries, is the model of that hidden life which Christ Himself lived on earth in obedience and work."
> — Pope Paul VI, _Solemnity of St. Joseph_, 19 March 1969

> "The hidden life of Nazareth teaches us the value of daily work, family life, and silent obedience to God's will."
> — Catechism of the Catholic Church, CCC 564

> "What then was the extent of the grace conferred on Joseph? He was, as it were, the only-begotten son of the divine Father on earth, and he was the shadow of the eternal Father on earth, as the Son of God was subject to him."
> — Pope Pius IX, _Inclytum Patriarcham_ (1871)

> "I do not remember up to this day that I have ever asked anything of him that he has not granted."
> — St. Teresa of Ávila, _The Life of St. Teresa of Jesus_, Chapter 6

> "There is a general rule concerning the special graces of St. Joseph: he who cannot find a master to teach him how to pray, let him take St. Joseph for his master, and he will not go astray."
> — St. Teresa of Ávila

> "Among all the Saints, the glorious St. Joseph seems to have the special power of assisting souls in their last agony, for his own death was so happy, being attended by Jesus and Mary."
> — St. Alphonsus Liguori, _The Glories of St. Joseph_

> "He was indeed the faithful and prudent servant whom the Lord set over His family."
> — St. Bernard of Clairvaux, _Super Missus Est_

> "The silence of Joseph has a special eloquence, for thanks to that silence we can understand the truth of the Gospel's words: 'the just man lives by faith.'"
> — Pope Benedict XVI, Angelus Address, 19 March 2006
