# St. Thérèse of Lisieux: The Little Flower and Doctor of the Church

> **Note:** This document was composed with the assistance of Magisterium AI. Citations should be verified against primary sources where possible.

---

## 1. Introduction

**St. Thérèse of Lisieux** (French: *Thérèse de Lisieux*; Latin: *Teresia Lexoviensis*; 2 January 1873 – 30 September 1897), known as **St. Thérèse of the Child Jesus and of the Holy Face**, was a French Discalced Carmelite nun who is one of the most popular saints in the history of the Catholic Church. She is venerated as a **Doctor of the Church** (proclaimed by Pope John Paul II in 1997), the **patroness of missions**, and is affectionately known as **"The Little Flower"** (*La Petite Fleur*). Her feast day is **1 October**.

> "I will spend my heaven doing good on earth. I will let fall a shower of roses."
> — St. Thérèse of Lisieux, *Last Words*

---

## 2. Early Life

### Birth and Family

Marie-Françoise Thérèse Martin was born on **2 January 1873** in **Alençon, Normandy, France**, to **Louis Martin** and **Azélie-Marie (Zélie) Guérin**, both devout Catholics. She was the youngest of nine children, five of whom survived to adulthood (all four surviving sisters became nuns). Her parents were themselves canonized as saints by Pope Francis on **18 October 2015**—the first married couple to be canonized together.

### Childhood

Thérèse's childhood was marked by deep love for her family and an early sensitivity to spiritual things. At age two, her mother died of breast cancer, and the family moved to **Lisieux**. Thérèse developed a strong attachment to her eldest sister, Pauline, who became her "second mother."

### The Christmas Night Conversion

On **25 December 1886** (Christmas Eve), the twelve-year-old Thérèse experienced a profound grace she called her **"complete conversion."** After returning from Midnight Mass, her father, tired and irritated, remarked that he wished it were the last time the children opened their presents. Thérèse, usually extremely sensitive, felt no hurt and instead felt infused with strength and courage. She later wrote: "In an instant, Jesus, content with my good will, accomplished the work I had not been able to do for ten years" (*Story of a Soul*, Chapter V).

## 3. Entry to Carmel

### The Pilgrimage to Rome

At age fourteen, Thérèse felt called to enter the **Carmel of Lisieux**, but the prioress insisted she wait until age twenty-one. Determined, Thérèse accompanied her father on a pilgrimage to **Rome** in November 1887 for the Jubilee of Pope Leo XIII. At a general audience, she broke protocol by approaching the Pope directly and asking: "Holy Father, in honour of your Jubilee, permit me to enter Carmel at fifteen!" The Pope, struck by her boldness, said: "Well, my child, do what the superiors tell you."

Despite the Pope's non-committal response, the Bishop of Bayeux authorized her entry shortly after.

### Profession

On **9 April 1888**, Thérèse entered the Carmel of Lisieux at age fifteen. She received the habit in January 1889 and made her religious profession on **8 September 1890**, taking the name **Sister Thérèse of the Child Jesus and of the Holy Face**.

## 4. The Little Way

### Spiritual Childhood

Thérèse's spiritual doctrine, known as **"The Little Way"** (or "The Way of Spiritual Childhood"), is her most enduring contribution to Catholic spirituality. It is based on the Gospel injunction to become like little children (Matthew 18:3; Mark 10:15) and consists of:

1. **Recognizing one's own littleness and incapacity**: Acknowledging that we cannot earn heaven by our own efforts.
2. **Total trust in God's merciful love**: Abandoning oneself to God as a child abandons itself to its father.
3. **Doing small things with great love**: Finding holiness in the ordinary duties of daily life.
4. **Offering everything to God**: Transforming even failures and weaknesses into offerings of love.

> "It is confidence and nothing but confidence that must lead us to Love."
> — St. Thérèse, *Letter to Sister Marie of the Sacred Heart*

### The Act of Oblation to Merciful Love

On **9 June 1895**, during Mass, Thérèse composed and offered herself as a **"victim of holocaust to God's Merciful Love."** This act, which she wrote out and signed, expresses the heart of her spirituality: not seeking suffering for its own sake, but offering herself completely to Love.

## 5. Illness and Death

### Tuberculosis

In April **1896**, on Good Friday, Thérèse began coughing up blood—the first sign of the **tuberculosis** that would take her life. She continued her religious duties as long as possible, but by the summer of 1897, she was bedridden.

### The Trial of Faith

During her final months, Thérèse underwent a severe **trial of faith**. Despite her lifelong certainty of heaven, she was assailed by doubts about the existence of God and the afterlife. She later described this as "the thickest darkness" and "the voice of unbelievers." Yet she continued to profess her faith even when she felt nothing.

### Death

Thérèse died on **30 September 1897** at the age of twenty-four, after a prolonged and painful illness. Her final words were: **"My God, I love you!"**

## 6. Canonization and Doctorate

### Posthumous Fame

Thérèse was buried in the Carmelite cemetery in Lisieux. Her autobiography, ***Story of a Soul*** (*Histoire d'une Âme*), written under obedience, was published after her death and became an immediate worldwide sensation, translated into dozens of languages.

### Canonization

- **Beatified**: **29 April 1923** by Pope Pius XI.
- **Canonized**: **17 May 1925** by Pope Pius XI.
- **Declared patroness of missions** (co-equal with St. Francis Xavier) in **1927**.
- **Declared a Doctor of the Church**: **19 October 1997** by Pope **John Paul II**, making her—at the time—the youngest Doctor of the Church and the third woman so honoured (after St. Catherine of Siena and St. Teresa of Ávila).

St. Thérèse of Lisieux is the patron saint of:

## 9. The Shower of Roses

Thérèse promised on her deathbed: **"I will spend my heaven doing good on earth. I will let fall a shower of roses."** Countless testimonies of favours and miracles attributed to her intercession—often signalled by the appearance of roses or their scent—have been reported worldwide. Novenas to St. Thérèse are among the most popular in Catholic devotion.

> — Pope John Paul II, *Divini Amoris Scientia* (1997)

> "Thérèse of Lisieux is a living testimony that holiness is possible for everyone, because it is the work of God's grace."
> — Pope Benedict XVI, General Audience, 6 April 2011

> — Pope Francis, *Catechesis on St. Thérèse*, 2017
