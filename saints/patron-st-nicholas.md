# St. Nicholas of Myra: The Wonderworker and Patron of Children

> **Note:** This document was composed with the assistance of Magisterium AI. Citations should be verified against primary sources where possible.

## 1. Introduction

**St. Nicholas of Myra** (Greek: ***Ἅγιος Νικόλαος***; Latin: *Sanctus Nicolaus*; c. 270 – 6 December 343 A.D.) was a fourth-century Christian bishop of **Myra** (modern-day Demre, Turkey). Known as **"Nicholas the Wonderworker"** for the many miracles attributed to his intercession, he is one of the most popular saints in both Eastern and Western Christianity, venerated by Catholics, Eastern Orthodox, Oriental Orthodox, and many Protestants. His legendary generosity—particularly giving gifts to the poor—evolved into the figure of **Santa Claus**. His feast day is **6 December**.

> "The best way to show our gratitude to God is to accept everything, even our problems, with joy."
> — Attributed to St. Nicholas of Myra

---

## 2. Early Life

### Birth and Family

Nicholas was born around **270 A.D.** in **Patara**, a city in the Roman province of Lycia (modern-day Turkey), to wealthy and devout Christian parents. Following the deaths of his parents in an epidemic, Nicholas inherited a substantial fortune. He distributed his entire inheritance to the poor.

### Early Calling

From childhood, Nicholas showed signs of exceptional piety. According to tradition, he would abstain from his mother's milk on Wednesdays and Fridays—the traditional Christian fasting days.

## 3. Bishop of Myra

### The Election

After the death of the Bishop of Myra, the clergy and people gathered to choose a new bishop. According to tradition, the oldest among them had a revelation that the first person to enter the church the next morning should be consecrated bishop. That person was Nicholas.

### The Council of Nicaea

Nicholas is traditionally counted among the **318 fathers** of the **First Council of Nicaea** (325 A.D.), where he is said to have been a staunch defender of Nicene orthodoxy against Arianism. A popular tradition recounts that he was so incensed by Arius's denial of Christ's divinity that he **slapped the heretic** at the council, for which he was briefly imprisoned but later restored.

### Persecution Under Diocletian

During the **Diocletianic Persecution** (303–311), Nicholas was imprisoned for his faith. He was released after Constantine's Edict of Milan (313).

## 4. Miracles and Legends

### The Three Dowry Bags

The most famous legend of St. Nicholas tells of a poor man with three daughters who could not afford dowries for them, meaning they would be forced into prostitution. Nicholas, hearing of this, secretly threw **three bags of gold** through the man's window on three separate nights, providing dowries for all three daughters. This story is the origin of his patronage of children, gift-giving, and the modern image of Santa Claus.

### The Three Boys in the Pickle Barrel

Another legend recounts that three boys were murdered by an innkeeper who pickled their bodies in a barrel of brine. Nicholas, discovering this, made the sign of the cross over the barrel and **restored the boys to life**. This miracle made him the patron of children.

### The Storm at Sea

During a pilgrimage to the Holy Land, Nicholas calmed a violent storm through prayer, saving the ship and its crew. He is thus invoked as the patron of sailors and travellers.

### The Divine Intervention for Myra

During a famine, Nicholas obtained a shipment of grain from several ships in the port of Myra and distributed it to the starving people. The grain was miraculously replenished so that the ships could continue their journey.

## 5. Death and Relics

### Death

Nicholas died on **6 December 343 A.D.** in Myra and was buried in the cathedral there.

### The Translation of the Relics

In **1087**, Muslim Seljuk Turks controlled Myra, and Italian sailors from **Bari** (in Apulia) determined to rescue the relics. They broke open the tomb and carried the bones to Bari, where a magnificent basilica was built in his honour. The **Basilica of St. Nicholas** in Bari remains a major pilgrimage site and a centre of Catholic-Orthodox reconciliation.

## 8. The Santa Claus Tradition

The legend of St. Nicholas evolved through Dutch colonists in New Amsterdam (New York) into the figure of **Sinterklaas**, which became **Santa Claus**. The Dutch tradition of giving gifts on St. Nicholas Eve (5 December) merged with the Christmas celebration, transforming the historical bishop into the jolly red-suited gift-bringer.

---

## 9. Iconography

St. Nicholas is traditionally depicted as a **bearded bishop** in Eastern vestments (or Western bishop's robes), holding:

- **A Gospel book** (his preaching)
- **Three gold balls or coins** (the dowry miracle)
- **An omophorion** (Eastern bishop's stole) with crosses
- **A pastoral staff** (his episcopal office)
