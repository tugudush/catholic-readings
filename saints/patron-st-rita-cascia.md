# St. Rita of Cascia: Patron of Impossible Causes

> **Note:** This document was composed with the assistance of Magisterium AI. Citations should be verified against primary sources where possible.

---

## 1. Introduction

**St. Rita of Cascia** (Italian: *Rita da Cascia*; Latin: *Rita Casciensis*; 1381 – 22 May 1457), born **Margherita Lotti**, was an Italian Augustinian nun who is venerated as the **patron saint of impossible causes, desperate situations, and marital difficulties**. Her life—as a wife, mother, widow, and nun—is a remarkable witness to faith, forgiveness, and perseverance through suffering. Her feast day is **22 May**.

> "If God permits me to suffer, He will also give me the strength to bear it."
> — St. Rita of Cascia

---

## 2. Early Life

### Birth and Childhood

Rita was born in **1381** in the small village of **Roccaporena**, near **Cascia**, Umbria, Italy, to elderly parents, **Antonio Lotti** and **Amata Ferri**. They were known for their piety and charity; according to tradition, Rita's birth was considered a gift from God after many years of childlessness.

From childhood, Rita was drawn to religious life, but her parents, concerned for her future, arranged her marriage.

---

## 3. Married Life

### The Difficult Marriage

At age twelve or fourteen, Rita was married to **Paolo Mancini**, a wealthy, quick-tempered man involved in the violent political feuds of the time. Her marriage was extremely difficult: Paolo was abusive, unfaithful, and often absent. Rita bore two sons, **Giovanni** and **Antonio**.

### The Conversion of Her Husband

Through her patience, prayer, and suffering, Rita gradually brought about her husband's **conversion**. Paolo became more gentle and began to attend Mass with her. However, the feuds of the time caught up with him—he was **murdered** in a political vendetta.

### The Deaths of Her Sons

After their father's murder, Rita's sons swore revenge. Rather than see them commit murder and lose their souls, Rita **prayed that God would take them** rather than let them sin. Both sons died of natural causes within a year—reconciled to God through the sacraments before their deaths.

---

## 4. Entry into Religious Life

### The Three Attempts

After the deaths of her husband and sons, Rita sought to enter the **Augustinian Convent of St. Mary Magdalene** in Cascia. She was initially refused because of her status as a widow and her family's violent reputation.

According to tradition, Rita persisted through prayer, and through the intercession of her three patron saints—St. John the Baptist, St. Augustine, and St. Nicholas of Tolentino—she was miraculously transported into the convent at night. The sisters, recognizing God's will, accepted her.

### The Augustinian Nun

Rita lived as a faithful Augustinian nun for **forty years**, devoted to prayer, penance, and charity. She was known for her deep devotion to the **Passion of Christ** and to the **Blessed Sacrament**.

---

## 5. The Stigmata and the Rose

### The Wound on the Forehead

In about **1442**, while praying before an image of the crucified Christ, Rita experienced a mystical grace: a **thorn from Christ's crown** pierced her forehead, leaving an open, festering wound. This wound gave off an unpleasant odour, and Rita was forced to live in isolation from the other sisters. She bore this wound for the remaining fifteen years of her life.

### The Miracle of the Rose

In her final illness, Rita asked a cousin to bring her a rose from her family home in Roccaporena. It was **January**, and the cousin thought it impossible—but upon arriving, she found a **single blooming rose** on the withered bush. This miracle is the origin of the tradition of blessing roses on St. Rita's feast day.

---

## 6. Death

Rita died on **22 May 1457** at the Convent of St. Mary Magdalene in Cascia. Her body remained **incorrupt** and is preserved in the Basilica of St. Rita in Cascia, a major pilgrimage site.

### Canonization

- **Beatified**: 1626 by Pope Urban VIII.
- **Canonized**: **24 May 1900** by Pope Leo XIII.
- **Feast Day**: **22 May**.

## 7. Patronage

St. Rita of Cascia is the patron saint of:

| Patronage | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Impossible and Desperate Causes** | Her most famous patronage |
| **Abused and Battered Spouses** | Her own difficult marriage |
| **Widows and Single Mothers** | Her experience of widowhood |
| **Difficult Marriages** | Her intercession for troubled marriages |
| **Wounds and Injuries** | Her own mystical wound |
| **Parents** | Her prayer for her sons' souls |
| **Against Infertility** | Her own parents' long wait for a child |

## 9. The Blessing of Roses

On St. Rita's feast day (22 May), roses are traditionally blessed and distributed to the faithful in her honour, recalling the miraculous rose that bloomed in January. The rose has become her symbol, representing the beauty that can bloom from suffering.
