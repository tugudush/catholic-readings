# St. Jerome: Father and Doctor of the Church, Translator of the Vulgate

> **Note:** This document was composed with the assistance of Magisterium AI. Citations should be verified against primary sources where possible.

---

## 1. Introduction

**St. Jerome** (Latin: *Eusebius Sophronius Hieronymus*; Greek: ***Εὐσέβιος Σωφρόνιος Ἱερώνυμος***; c. 347 – 30 September 420 A.D.) was a Roman Catholic priest, theologian, and **Doctor of the Church** (one of the four Great Doctors of the Latin Church). He is best known for his translation of the Bible into Latin—the **Vulgate**—which became the authoritative biblical text for the Western Church for over a millennium. His feast day is **30 September**.

> "Ignorance of Scripture is ignorance of Christ."
> — St. Jerome

---

## 2. Early Life

### Birth and Education

Jerome was born around **347 A.D.** in **Stridon**, a town on the border of Pannonia and Dalmatia (modern-day Croatia/Slovenia). He was baptized as a youth but did not initially take the Christian faith seriously. He was sent to **Rome** for a classical education under the famous grammarian **Donatus**, where he studied Latin, Greek, and rhetoric.

### The Roman Years

In Rome, Jerome studied the Latin classics, amassing a library that he would later regret as "the love of Cicero." He frequented the Roman catacombs, which deepened his Christian sensibilities.

### Conversion

After his studies, Jerome traveled to **Trier** (modern-day Germany) and was deeply moved by the austere piety of the Christian monks there. Around **366 A.D.**, he experienced a profound conversion and determined to devote his life to God.

---

## 3. Monastic Life and the Dream of Cicero

### The Syrian Desert

After his conversion, Jerome lived as a **hermit in the Syrian desert** (near Antioch) for about **four years** (374–379). There he studied Hebrew, wrestled with temptations, and dedicated himself to prayer and penance. In a famous dream, Jerome was brought before the judgment seat of God and accused of being a "Ciceronian, not a Christian," because of his love for classical Latin literature.

> "I preferred Cicero to the Apostles, the profane writers to the sacred. But the Lord rebuked me, and from that time I read the Scriptures with more devotion than I had ever read Cicero."

---

## 4. Priesthood and Secretary to Pope Damasus

### Ordination

Jerome was ordained a priest in **Antioch** around **379 A.D.** by Bishop Paulinus.

### Secretary to Pope Damasus I

From **382–385 A.D.**, Jerome served as **secretary** to **Pope Damasus I** in Rome. At the Pope's request, he began the work of revising the Latin translations of the Gospels, comparing them with the Greek originals.

### The Roman Circle

Jerome gathered a group of noble Roman women—including **St. Paula, St. Eustochium, and St. Marcella**—whom he taught Scripture and guided in the ascetic life. This circle was the beginning of the monastic movement among Roman aristocratic women.

---

## 5. The Vulgate Translation

### The Commission

Pope Damasus commissioned Jerome to produce a reliable Latin translation of the Bible to replace the various and often inconsistent Old Latin (*Vetus Latina*) versions in circulation.

### Method

Jerome's translation method was groundbreaking:

- For the **Gospels**: He revised the Old Latin text against the Greek originals.
- For the **Old Testament**: He translated directly from the **Hebrew** (rather than the Greek Septuagint), consulting Jewish rabbis for difficult passages. This was controversial at the time, as most Christians considered the Septuagint the inspired version.
- For the **Psalms**: He produced three versions: the *Roman Psalter* (a light revision), the *Gallican Psalter* (a revision from the Septuagint), and the *Hebrew Psalter* (translated from Hebrew).

### Completion

Jerome completed his translation of the entire Bible around **405 A.D.**, after twenty years of labour. The **Vulgate** (from Latin *vulgatus*, "common/popular") became the standard Latin Bible and was declared the authentic Latin text of the Church by the **Council of Trent** (1546).

---

## 6. Bethlehem and the Monastery

### Journey to Bethlehem

After the death of Pope Damasus in 384, Jerome's enemies in Rome forced him to leave. With his brother Paulinian and the holy women of his circle (St. Paula and St. Eustochium), Jerome traveled to the **Holy Land** in **386 A.D.**, settling in **Bethlehem**.

### The Monasteries

Jerome established a **monastery** for men and **three convents** for women in Bethlehem, near the Church of the Nativity. He governed his monastery until his death.

### The Translations and Commentaries

In Bethlehem, Jerome continued his translation work and produced extensive biblical commentaries on nearly every book of the Bible, including *Commentaries on Isaiah, Jeremiah, Ezekiel, Daniel, Matthew, Galatians, Ephesians, Titus, and Philemon*.

---

## 7. Controversies

Jerome was involved in several major theological controversies:

- **Against Helvidius**: Defended the perpetual virginity of Mary.
- **Against Jovinian**: Defended the superiority of virginity, fasting, and asceticism.
- **Against Vigilantius**: Defended the veneration of relics and the monastic life.
- **Against Origenism**: With his friend St. Augustine, opposed the heresies of Origen, though he was initially an admirer of Origen's works.

---

## 8. Death

Jerome died on **30 September 420 A.D.** in Bethlehem, surrounded by his monks. His relics were later transferred to the **Basilica of St. Mary Major** in Rome.
