## 1. Introduction

**St. Lorenzo Ruiz** (Spanish: _Lorenzo Ruiz de Manila_; Chinese: 李乐伦, _Lǐ Yuèlún_; 28 November 1594 – 29 September 1637), also known as **San Lorenzo de Manila**, was a Filipino Catholic layman and member of the **Third Order of Saint Dominic** (T.O.S.D.). He is the **Protomartyr of the Philippines** — the first Filipino canonized saint — and is venerated as a martyr who died for the Catholic faith in Nagasaki, Japan, under the Tokugawa Shogunate's persecution of Christians.

He was beatified on **18 February 1981** by Pope John Paul II during the first beatification ceremony ever held outside the Vatican, and canonized on **18 October 1987** as one of the **16 Martyrs of Japan**. His feast day is **28 September**.

> "I am a Catholic and wholeheartedly do accept death for God; had I a thousand lives, all these to Him shall I offer."
> — St. Lorenzo Ruiz, at his death

---

## 2. Early Life

### Birth and Family

Lorenzo Ruiz was born on **28 November 1594** in **Binondo, Manila**, in the Captaincy General of the Philippines, Spanish Empire. He was the son of a **Chinese father** and a **Tagalog mother**, both devout Catholics. His father taught him Chinese, while his mother taught him Tagalog. This mixed heritage made him a _mestizo de sangley_ — a person of mixed Chinese and indigenous Filipino ancestry.

### Education and Vocation

As a boy, Lorenzo served as an **altar boy** at the Binondo Church, which was then under the pastoral care of the **Dominican Order**. The Dominican friars educated him, and he developed exceptional penmanship, earning the title of _escribano_ (scrivener or calligrapher). He became a lay member of the **Confraternity of the Most Holy Rosary** (_Cofradía del Santísimo Rosario_), a Dominican-sponsored devotional society, and eventually entered the **Third Order of Saint Dominic**.

### Married Life

Lorenzo married a native woman named **Rosario**, and the couple had three children — two sons and a daughter. The Ruiz family lived a peaceful, devout life in Binondo, with Lorenzo working as a clerk for the Binondo Church, supporting his family through his skills as a calligrapher and writer.

---

## 3. The Flight to Japan

### The Accusation

In **1636**, a tragic turn of events upended Lorenzo's life. While working as a clerk at the Binondo Church, he was **falsely accused of killing a Spaniard**. Historical records are unclear about the precise nature of the accusation, but it placed Lorenzo in grave danger under Spanish colonial law.

### Seeking Asylum

Fearing for his life and unable to prove his innocence through the colonial legal system, Lorenzo sought **asylum** aboard a ship bound for Japan. On board with him were three Dominican priests — **Fr. Antonio Gonzalez**, **Fr. Guillermo Courtet**, and **Fr. Miguel de Aozaraza** — a Japanese priest, **Fr. Vicente Shiwozuka de la Cruz**, and a lay leper named **Lázaro of Kyoto**. Together, these men would become the companions of Lorenzo's martyrdom.

### Arrival in Persecuting Japan

Lorenzo and his companions arrived in Japan during a time of intense persecution. The **Tokugawa Shogunate**, fearing Spanish colonial expansion disguised as religious mission, had outlawed Christianity and was systematically hunting down foreign missionaries and Japanese Christians. Upon arrival, the group was immediately arrested, and their ordeal began.

---

## 4. Martyrdom

### Imprisonment and Torture

Lorenzo and his companions were imprisoned and subjected to nearly **two years** of brutal torture designed to force them to renounce their faith. They were transferred to **Nagasaki**, the center of the Tokugawa persecution, to face formal trial by torture.

The methods of torture included:

- **Water torture**: Forced ingestion of large quantities of water followed by abdominal compression.
- **Strip beating**: Repeated lashing of the bare body.
- **Tsurushi** (also known as _horca y hoya_, "gallows and pit"): The victim was hung upside-down by the ankles over a pit, with one hand left free so that the victim could signal their desire to recant. This method caused excruciating pain from blood rushing to the head and eventual death by blood loss and suffocation.

### The Final Refusal

Throughout the torture, Lorenzo was repeatedly offered his freedom if he would renounce the Christian faith. His response has become one of the most celebrated declarations of faith in the history of Christian martyrdom:

> _"Ego Catholicus sum et animo prompto paratoque pro Deo mortem obibo. Si mille vitas haberem, cunctas ei offerrem."_
>
> ("I am a Catholic and wholeheartedly do accept death for God; had I a thousand lives, all these to Him shall I offer.")

### Death

Lorenzo Ruiz died by **tsurushi** on **29 September 1637** at Nishizaka Hill, Nagasaki, at the age of **42**. His body was **cremated** and his ashes **thrown into the sea** to prevent Christians from collecting his relics. His companions were martyred by various methods in the days surrounding his death.

## 5. Companions in Martyrdom

Lorenzo Ruiz was canonized alongside **fifteen companion martyrs** — missionaries and lay Christians who were executed in Japan between 1633 and 1637. The group, known as the **16 Martyrs of Japan**, includes:

| St. Antonio Gonzalez                                          | Spanish     | Dominican Priest |

These 16 martyrs were among the thousands of Christians who gave their lives during the Tokugawa persecution, a chapter of Japanese history that saw the near-total eradication of Christianity from Japan for over two centuries.

## 6. Beatification and Canonization

### The Rediscovery

The cause for Lorenzo Ruiz's beatification was advanced by the discovery of an **eyewitness account** written by two Japanese former Jesuit priests, preserved at the Jesuit Generalate archives in Rome. The Spanish Dominican historian **Fidel Villarroel, O.P.** , uncovered this crucial document, which provided clear evidence of Ruiz's martyrdom.

### Beatification in Manila

On **18 February 1981**, Pope John Paul II **beatified** Lorenzo Ruiz during his apostolic visit to the Philippines. The ceremony was held at **Rizal Park (Luneta)** in Manila and was the **first beatification ceremony ever held outside the Vatican**. This unprecedented act underscored both the significance of Lorenzo Ruiz as the protomartyr of the Philippines and the vitality of the Catholic faith in Asia.

### The Miracle for Canonization

In October **1983**, two-year-old **Cecilia Alegria Policarpio** of Calinog, Iloilo, was diagnosed with **brain atrophy (hydrocephalus)** and given little chance of survival. Her family and community prayed fervently for the intercession of Blessed Lorenzo Ruiz. The child was completely and inexplicably cured, with medical experts unable to provide a natural explanation. This miracle was approved by the Holy See.

### Canonization

On **18 October 1987**, Pope John Paul II **canonized** Lorenzo Ruiz and his fifteen companions in **Saint Peter's Basilica**, Vatican City, declaring them saints of the Catholic Church. Lorenzo Ruiz thus became the **first Filipino saint**.

## 7. Veneration and Legacy

### Feast Day

The feast of St. Lorenzo Ruiz is celebrated on **28 September** (the day before his death, adjusted to avoid conflict with the Feast of St. Michael the Archangel on 29 September). It is a **liturgical feast** observed in the Philippines and in many Filipino communities worldwide.

### Major Shrines

- **Minor Basilica and National Shrine of Saint Lorenzo Ruiz** (Binondo Church), Binondo, Manila, Philippines — the saint's home parish and the center of his cult.
- **Manila Cathedral**, Intramuros, Manila — houses a prominent statue of St. Lorenzo Ruiz.
- **Nishizaka Hill**, Nagasaki, Japan — the site of his martyrdom, now a memorial park.

### Patronage

St. Lorenzo Ruiz is invoked as patron of:

- The **Philippines** and the **Filipino people**
- **Overseas Filipino Workers (OFWs)** and **migrant workers**
- **Immigrants** and **separated families**
- The **Filipino youth**
- **Chinese-Filipinos**
- **Filipino altar servers**
- The **Archdiocese of Manila**
- **The poor** and those falsely accused

### Iconography

St. Lorenzo Ruiz is typically depicted wearing a **barong tagalog** or _camisa de chino_ with black trousers, holding a **rosary** in clasped hands, with a **gallows and pit** or a **palm of martyrdom** as his identifying attributes. He is sometimes shown with a **crucifix** and a **book** representing his work as a scrivener.

### Commemorations

- A **mosaic** of St. Lorenzo Ruiz is included in the **Communion of Saints Tapestries** at the Cathedral of Our Lady of the Angels in Los Angeles, California.
- Numerous schools, parishes, and chapels worldwide bear his name, particularly in areas with large Filipino diaspora communities.
- A statue of St. Lorenzo Ruiz is enshrined at **Saint Peter's Basilica** in Vatican City, among the altar mosaics.

## 8. Theological Significance

St. Lorenzo Ruiz embodies several key themes in Catholic theology and spirituality:

- **The Universality of Sanctity**: As a layman, husband, and father — not a cleric or religious — Lorenzo demonstrates that the call to holiness is universal, extending to all states of life.
- **The Lay Vocation to Martyrdom**: His witness affirms that the lay faithful, through baptism and confirmation, are called to bear witness to Christ even to the shedding of blood.
- **The Unity of the Church in Persecution**: Martyred alongside Spanish, French, and Japanese companions, Lorenzo Ruiz exemplifies the Catholic Church as a communion of diverse peoples united in faith.
- **The Filipino Catholic Identity**: As the protomartyr of the Philippines, he holds a unique place in the history of the Church in Asia, embodying the synthesis of indigenous and Catholic identity that characterizes Filipino Catholicism.

## 9. Cultural Impact

St. Lorenzo Ruiz has left an enduring mark on Filipino culture and the global Filipino diaspora:

- **Literature**: Multiple biographical works, including _To Die a Thousand Deaths_ by Celso Al. Carunungan and _Lorenzo de Manila: The Protomartyr of the Philippines_ by Fidel Villarroel, O.P.
- **Music and Theater**: The opera _Lorenzo Ruiz, Escribano_ (1994) by Jerry Amper Dadap; the musical _Lorenzo_ (2013) by Ryan Cayabyab.
- **Film**: _Ang Buhay ni Lorenzo Ruiz_ (1970); _Lorenzo Ruiz... The Saint... A Filipino!_ (1988).
- **Education**: Numerous schools named after him throughout the Philippines, including Lorenzo Ruiz Academy in Binondo and San Lorenzo Ruiz de Manila School in Cainta, Rizal.
