# St. Joan of Arc: The Maid of Orléans and Patron of France

> **Note:** This document was composed with the assistance of Magisterium AI. Citations should be verified against primary sources where possible.

---

## 1. Introduction

**St. Joan of Arc** (French: *Jeanne d'Arc*; Latin: *Ioanna Arcensis*; c. 1412 – 30 May 1431), known as **"The Maid of Orléans"** (*La Pucelle d'Orléans*), was a French peasant girl who, claiming divine guidance, led the French army to several important victories during the **Hundred Years' War**, paving the way for the coronation of **Charles VII**. She was executed by burning at the stake at age nineteen, was rehabilitated by the Church twenty-five years later, and was **canonized in 1920**. She is the **patron saint of France** and one of the most remarkable figures in Christian history. Her feast day is **30 May**.

> "I am not afraid; I was born to do this."
> — St. Joan of Arc

---

## 2. Early Life

### Birth and Family

Joan was born around **1412** in the village of **Domrémy** in the Duchy of Bar (modern-day Lorraine, France). Her father, **Jacques d'Arc**, was a peasant farmer and local tax collector; her mother, **Isabelle Romée**, was a devout and hardworking woman.

### A Pious Childhood

Joan was known as a pious and charitable child, devoted to prayer and to caring for the sick. She reportedly loved the Church and often received the sacraments. She was illiterate but had a deep knowledge of the faith taught by her mother.

### The Voices

At about **thirteen years of age** (c. 1425), Joan began to experience visions and hear voices, which she identified as **St. Michael the Archangel**, **St. Catherine of Alexandria**, and **St. Margaret of Antioch**. These voices told her that she was chosen by God to help the Dauphin Charles reclaim his throne and drive the English from France.

## 3. The Mission

### The Journey to Chinon

In **1428**, at age sixteen, Joan persuaded her uncle to take her to the nearby town of **Vaucouleurs**, where she appealed to the captain, **Robert de Baudricourt**, for an escort to the Dauphin. Initially dismissed, she persisted, and her perseverance, piety, and accurate prophecy of a French defeat near Orléans convinced de Baudricourt to send her to **Chinon**.

### The Recognition of the Dauphin

At Chinon, Joan met the Dauphin Charles—but she astonished the court by **identifying him** despite his attempt to hide among his courtiers. She conveyed a secret sign (a private prayer of Charles, known only to him) that convinced him of her divine mission.

### The Sword of Fierbois

Joan dictated a letter to the English, demanding their withdrawal. She also told the king of a hidden sword buried behind the altar of the Church of **St. Catherine of Fierbois**, which was found exactly as she described.

---

## 4. Military Campaign

### The Relief of Orléans

Clad in white armour and carrying a banner (bearing the names of Jesus and Mary, with a depiction of Christ in judgment), Joan joined the army, acting as a spiritual inspiration rather than a military commander. On **29 April 1429**, she entered Orléans. In a series of assaults over the following days, the French lifted the **Siege of Orléans**—a major turning point in the war.

### The Coronation at Reims

After the victory at Orléans, Joan urged Charles to march to **Reims** to be crowned, despite the city being deep in enemy territory. The French army captured Reims, and Charles was crowned **King Charles VII** on **17 July 1429**, with Joan standing by his side, holding her banner.

### The Failed Siege of Paris

Joan's attempt to liberate **Paris** in September 1429 failed due to a wound and lack of support from the King, who had begun peace negotiations.

---

## 5. Capture, Trial, and Martyrdom

### Capture at Compiègne

On **23 May 1430**, Joan was captured at **Compiègne** by Burgundian forces—French allies of the English. She was sold to the English for 10,000 livres.

### The Trial

Joan was brought to **Rouen** for an **Inquisitorial trial** on charges of heresy, orchestrated by the English who wanted to discredit Charles VII. The trial, presided over by Bishop **Pierre Cauchon**, was marked by procedural irregularities:

- Joan was denied legal counsel.
- She was kept in a secular prison (guarded by English soldiers), vulnerable to abuse.
- The charges were manipulated and reframed when she refuted them.

### The Articles of Accusation

Joan was accused of:
- **Heresy** and abandonment of proper feminine dress (cross-dressing, for wearing armour and men's clothing).
- **False claims of divine revelation**.
- **Blasphemy** (claiming to be directly sent by God).

### The Abjuration and Relapse

Under intense pressure, Joan signed an abjuration and agreed to wear women's clothing. She was sentenced to life imprisonment. Days later, she was found wearing men's clothing again (whether by her own choice or because her female clothing was stolen is debated). This constituted a **relapse** into heresy.

### Death at the Stake

On **30 May 1431**, nineteen-year-old Joan was burned alive at the stake in the **Old Market Square of Rouen**. As the flames rose, she called upon Jesus, Mary, and the saints. Her last word was: **"Jesus!"** Her ashes were thrown into the Seine.

## 6. Rehabilitation and Canonization

### The Nullification Trial (1456)

Twenty-five years after her death, following the end of the Hundred Years' War, Pope **Callixtus III** authorized a **nullification trial** that examined the original proceedings. The trial of 1456 declared the original verdict "null, void, without effect, and without value" and proclaimed Joan's innocence.

### Canonization

- **Beatified**: **18 April 1909** by Pope St. Pius X.
- **Canonized**: **16 May 1920** by Pope Benedict XV.
- **Feast Day**: **30 May**.
